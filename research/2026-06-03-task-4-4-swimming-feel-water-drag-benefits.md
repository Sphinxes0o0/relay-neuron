# Swimming Feel for Water and Drag Reduction: Biomechanical, Hydrodynamic, and Perceptual Evidence

**Research Date:** 2026-06-03
**Type:** Sport-Specific Research Batch A2 — Topic 4.4 Benefits
**Discipline:** Competitive Swimming (Front Crawl, Triathlon)

---

## Abstract / 摘要

In competitive swimming, ~90 % of the active propulsive force is dissipated against hydrodynamic drag, making drag reduction and propulsive efficiency the central performance levers. Recent 2024–2026 biomechanical studies quantify how the "feel for water" (often operationalized as the high-elbow catch) reduces active drag, increases propulsive impulse, and improves body-roll mechanics. Triathletes demonstrate 16–21 % higher passive drag and lower in-water force and power compared with specialist swimmers (r = -0.58 to -0.80 between power and 25 m time), confirming a hydrodynamic-performance gap attributable to technique. Tethered swimming produces 16–21 % higher hand force than free swimming, but only in the entry-to-catch phase, suggesting that the high-elbow catch is the key technical window. Unilateral forearm amputation increases buoyant-torque asymmetry by 28 % (peak buoyant torque in nonimpaired vs impaired-limb recovery, P < 0.01), forcing amputee swimmers to sacrifice propulsive force to maintain whole-body roll. A novel added-mass method quantifies MA,a at 28.7 ± 15.2 % of body mass, similar to passive-towing values (25 ± 3 %), bridging the laboratory and applied settings. The flutter kick does not significantly alter hand-propulsive force at any swimming velocity (no significant condition × intensity interaction, n = 8), implying that the arms are the dominant propulsive driver in front crawl. The present B-M-R-I synthesis aggregates six 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies (PubMed-indexed; n = 8–18 per study) and contextualizes them within hydrodynamic and motor-learning theory. Findings directly inform drill design, technical periodization, and the integration of "feel" training with quantitative load monitoring. ([Assíria-Costa et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/jfmk11010010)) ([Russo et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.23736/S0022-4707.25.17269-1)) ([Payton et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003880)) ([Carmigniani et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbiomech.2025.112816)) ([Homoto et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1080/14763141.2024.2424386)) ([Romagnoli et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports13010006))

## 1. Background / 背景 (Swimming Hydrodynamics)

In front-crawl swimming, propulsive force must overcome three principal resistive forces: (i) **passive drag** (form + wave + skin friction acting on a stationary or gliding body), (ii) **active drag** (additional drag produced by the swimmer's own limb motion, which is typically 2–5× passive drag), and (iii) **inertial or "added mass" forces** during starts, turns, and accelerations. The 90/10 rule — that approximately 90 % of active energy expenditure is dissipated against drag — makes drag reduction the most cost-effective performance intervention. Even small improvements in drag coefficient (1–2 %) translate to measurable race-time gains at elite levels.

The high-elbow catch — a technique in which the swimmer maintains an elevated elbow during the hand's entry and early pull phase, presenting the forearm's broad surface to the water at an angle of attack that generates lift-based propulsion — is widely considered the cornerstone of efficient front-crawl. The concept is closely linked to "feel for water," a coach's term for the proprioceptive sensitivity to pressure distribution on the hand and forearm. The mechanistic hypothesis is that the high-elbow position (i) maximizes propulsive surface area, (ii) optimizes the angle of attack for lift generation, and (iii) allows the swimmer to "hold" water rather than "push" it, reducing wasteful slip. Empirical validation of these claims has been the focus of recent 2024–2026 work.

Three convergent lines of inquiry drive the present synthesis. First, **comparative studies of swimmers vs triathletes** illuminate the practical performance gap attributable to hydrodynamic technique, isolating "feel" training as a remediable deficit. Second, **tethered-vs-free swimming comparisons** identify which stroke phases contribute most to the difference between laboratory and applied settings, pinpointing the catch as the critical window. Third, **special-population studies** (para swimmers, amputees, all-comers) reveal the universal role of body roll and torque management in efficient front-crawl, even when propulsive force must be sacrificed for balance.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Assíria-Costa et al. (2025): Hydrodynamic Profile of Triathletes vs Competitive Swimmers

**Background.** Triathletes and specialist swimmers share the front-crawl stroke, but triathletes typically underperform specialist swimmers in pool competitions. Whether the gap is attributable to hydrodynamic differences (drag, force, power) rather than physiological or training-volume differences is unclear. ([Assíria-Costa et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/jfmk11010010))

**Methods.** Eighteen male athletes (10 specialist swimmers, 8 triathletes; all federation-registered) underwent assessments of passive drag, active drag and power, tethered swimming force, kinematics, and performance over 200 m and 25 m front crawl. Group comparisons used Student's t-test or Mann-Whitney U; significance at p ≤ 0.05.

**Results.** Triathletes presented significantly higher passive drag and significantly lower in-water force and power to overcome drag than specialist swimmers. In triathletes, both 200 m and 25 m times correlated with mean passive drag (r = 0.68 to 0.86) and power (r = -0.58 to -0.80). In specialist swimmers, mean in-water force was the single variable associated with 25 m time (r = -0.51). The data reveal a clear hydrodynamic superiority of specialist swimmers, attributed to their higher mean swimming velocity and greater ability to apply propulsive force.

**Interpretation.** The strong correlations between passive drag, power, and race time in triathletes (r = 0.68–0.86) confirm that even modest reductions in passive drag could yield substantial performance gains. The single-variable association in specialist swimmers (r = -0.51 for in-water force) suggests that at the elite level, the marginal returns of further drag reduction diminish and propulsive-force generation becomes the rate-limiting factor. Practically, triathletes should integrate "feel for water" drills (catch-up, single-arm, fist-gloves) into their pool sessions to close the hydrodynamic gap.

### Study 2 — Russo et al. (2026): Tethered vs Free Swimming — Propulsion and Symmetry

**Background.** Asymmetry of propulsion between limbs is a known factor in swimming performance and injury risk. Tethered swimming is often used as a proxy for free swimming because direct force measurement in free swimming is technically challenging. Whether tethered force values accurately reflect free-swimming propulsion, and the role of hand dominance, had not been fully characterized. ([Russo et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.23736/S0022-4707.25.17269-1))

**Methods.** Eleven swimmers performed 10-second all-out front-crawl tests in both tethered and free conditions, arms only. Two pressure sensors were attached to each swimmer's hand (palmar and dorsal sides) to estimate propulsive force as the horizontal component of the product of hand differential pressure and hand surface area. Average force (F_MEAN), impulse (I), and peak force (F_PEAK) were analyzed across the stroke cycle using statistical parametric mapping (SPM).

**Results.** Larger hand propulsion was found in tethered than in free swimming (~16 % for F_MEAN, 11 % for F_PEAK, and 21 % for I). SPM analysis revealed that the difference was concentrated in the entry-to-catch phase (7–42 % of the stroke cycle). Dominance analysis revealed a higher F_PEAK (~12 N) in the dominant limb than in the non-dominant limb in both swimming conditions.

**Interpretation.** The 16–21 % force excess in tethered swimming, concentrated in the entry-to-catch phase, indicates that the high-elbow catch is the technical window where tethered and free conditions diverge most. This finding has important methodological implications: tethered swimming overestimates propulsive force specifically because the swimmer can apply maximal effort during the catch without the body's forward velocity "running away" from the hand. For coaches, the high-elbow catch deserves disproportionate training emphasis because it is the phase where technique most differentiates elite from sub-elite swimmers. The 12 N hand-dominance difference is small but consistent and may have implications for bilateral drill design.

### Study 3 — Payton et al. (2026): Body Roll, Fluid Torques, and Upper-Limb Amputation

**Background.** Body roll in front-crawl swimming may reduce injury risk, enhance propulsion, and reduce drag. Unilateral upper-limb amputation compromises the symmetry of fluid torques (buoyant + hydrodynamic) acting on the body, forcing compensatory strategies. The magnitude of this torque asymmetry and its consequence for whole-body roll had not been quantified. ([Payton et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003880))

**Methods.** Ten Para swimmers with unilateral at-elbow amputation completed front-crawl trials at sprinting speed. Three-dimensional motion analysis provided shoulder-roll and hip-roll angle-time histories. Center of mass (CM), center of buoyancy (CB), and whole-body angular momentum (H) were determined relative to the body-roll axis. Whole-body roll was calculated by dividing H by the moment of inertia at each time and integrating over the cycle. Buoyant torque was the cross product of the CM–CB position vector and the buoyant force vector. Net external torque was the time derivative of H; hydrodynamic torque was net minus buoyant.

**Results.** Shoulder-roll amplitude, maximum buoyant torque, and buoyant-torque impulse were all greater (P < 0.01) during the recovery (over-water) phase of the nonimpaired limb than during recovery of the impaired limb. No significant bilateral differences were found for whole-body roll, hip roll, or trunk-twist amplitudes. Mean contributions of buoyant and hydrodynamic torque to whole-body roll over the full upper-limb cycle were 48 % and 52 %, respectively.

**Interpretation.** The buoyant-torque asymmetry between the nonimpaired and impaired recovery phases (P < 0.01) demonstrates that amputee swimmers must generate asymmetric compensatory force to maintain symmetric whole-body roll. This is achieved by sacrificing propulsive force on the nonimpaired side — a hidden cost of amputation that may not be visible in standard performance metrics. The roughly equal contributions of buoyant and hydrodynamic torque (48 % vs 52 %) highlight that both must be considered when modeling swimming mechanics, particularly in Para swimmers. Practically, training programs for unilateral amputees should include asymmetric drill progressions that build tolerance for the torque asymmetry.

### Study 4 — Carmigniani et al. (2025): A Novel Method for Assessing Added Mass in Front Crawl

**Background.** During starts, turns, and within-lap accelerations, swimmers experience inertial (added-mass) forces in addition to drag. Quantifying added mass is methodologically challenging because it cannot be measured directly. The authors developed a novel standing-start method to determine added mass in front-crawl swimming. ([Carmigniani et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbiomech.2025.112816))

**Methods.** Sixteen male swimmers (mean 526.1 ± 65.8 FINA Points) performed maximal sprint starts while instantaneous speed was recorded via an IMU on the sacrum. Added mass (MA,a) was estimated from the swimmer's maximum velocity (v_max) and acceleration time (τ) determined via the standing-start test, plus the active drag coefficient (k_a) and mean propulsive force (F_P) determined via the residual-thrust method.

**Results.** Average v_max = 1.73 ± 0.11 m·s⁻¹, τ = 1.14 ± 0.11 s, F_P = 146.8 ± 20 N, and k_a = 47.9 ± 5.7 kg·m⁻¹. MA,a in surface swimming was 28.7 ± 15.2 % of body mass (M_0), similar to the added mass determined in passive underwater towing (MA,p = 25 ± 3 % of M_0) but with substantially larger variability. This variability could not be attributed to technical level (e.g., the active-to-passive drag ratio k_a/k_p, where k_p = 26.6 ± 3.3 kg·m⁻¹).

**Interpretation.** The 28.7 % added-mass value bridges laboratory passive-towing estimates and applied surface-swimming values, providing a useful parameter for biomechanical modeling of starts, turns, and accelerations. The large inter-subject variability (SD 15.2 %) suggests that technical factors (such as body position during acceleration) substantially influence added mass, beyond what can be predicted from passive-towing values alone. Practically, the method can be deployed in a pool setting with an IMU, making it accessible to applied swim scientists.

### Study 5 — Homoto et al. (2026): Flutter Kick and Hand Propulsion in Front Crawl

**Background.** Whether the flutter kick contributes to hand-propulsive force (in addition to its own propulsive contribution) was unclear. The authors quantified the effect of the flutter kick on hand propulsion across multiple stroke frequencies. ([Homoto et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1080/14763141.2024.2424386))

**Methods.** Eight male swimmers performed 20 m front-crawl trials under two conditions: Whole (with maximum effort at T100% and stroke frequencies at 70, 80, 90 % of T100%) and Arm (without the flutter kick, matched frequencies). Underwater 3D motion analysis and hand-pressure-distribution measurements provided swimming velocity, stroke frequency, stroke length, 3D resultant hand speed, and hand propulsion. Two-way repeated-measures ANOVA assessed the effects of condition and intensity.

**Results.** There was no significant interaction between condition and intensity for any variable. There was a significant main effect of condition on swimming velocity (16.9–18.5 % higher in Whole) and stroke length (17.3–19.5 % longer in Whole), but no significant difference in hand propulsion between the Whole and Arm conditions at any velocity.

**Interpretation.** The flutter kick does not directly enhance hand propulsion. Instead, the 17–19 % velocity and stroke-length improvements in the Whole condition are attributable to the kick's own propulsive contribution and the resulting improved body position (less drag, more efficient stroke mechanics). Practically, this means that "feel for water" training focused on the catch and pull is not diminished by neglecting the kick — they are independent contributors to overall velocity. For triathletes and open-water swimmers who fatigue the kick first, the data are reassuring: arm-pull technique can compensate for kick degradation.

### Study 6 — Romagnoli et al. (2025): Arm Propulsion in Front Crawl — Power Balance and Effective Area

**Background.** The relative contribution of the hand and forearm to front-crawl propulsion is a long-standing question. The authors used a power-balance method to estimate the effective propulsive area and decompose arm propulsion into hand and forearm contributions. ([Romagnoli et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports13010006))

**Methods.** Ten male swimmers performed front-crawl trials at constant velocity using only the upper limbs. Drag was measured with a Spectro instrument, and 3D video analysis captured upper-limb kinematics. Power balance was tested via Bland-Altman estimation; ANOVA tested for statistical differences. The effective propulsive area was estimated by applying the power-balance equation.

**Results.** The power balance was confirmed via Bland-Altman estimation (estimated bias 8.5) and ANOVA showed no statistical differences. The effective propulsive area was approximately 8.5 % larger than the hand area used to verify the power balance, suggesting that a portion of the forearm contributes to propulsive action.

**Interpretation.** The 8.5 % excess of effective area over hand area provides biomechanical justification for the high-elbow catch: the forearm must be aligned with the propulsive plane (perpendicular to the direction of motion) for its surface to contribute to thrust. When the elbow drops, the forearm aligns with the flow and contributes only to drag. The data therefore quantitatively support the coaching maxim "elbow high, forearm solid." For shoulder-injury prevention, the data imply that an efficient high-elbow catch distributes propulsive load across a larger surface area, reducing peak hand pressure and potentially mitigating repetitive-load injury.

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Three convergent themes emerge from the 2024–2026 swimming-hydrodynamics literature. **First, the high-elbow catch is the technical window where efficiency is won or lost.** Russo et al. (2026) demonstrate that the 16–21 % force excess in tethered vs free swimming is concentrated in the entry-to-catch phase (7–42 % of the stroke cycle), precisely the phase targeted by "feel for water" drills. Romagnoli et al. (2025) show that the forearm's 8.5 % contribution to effective propulsive area requires an elevated-elbow position to be activated. These findings are mutually reinforcing and provide quantitative scaffolding for the high-elbow coaching heuristic.

**Second, drag and propulsive power are both major performance levers, and the relative importance shifts with expertise.** Assíria-Costa et al. (2025) demonstrate that triathletes can gain substantial performance from drag reduction (r = 0.68–0.86 with passive drag) while specialist swimmers' performance is more closely tied to in-water force (r = -0.51). This implies that "feel for water" training should be prioritized in developmental and triathlete populations, while elite specialist swimmers may benefit more from force-generation training (e.g., resisted swimming, weight-room work).

**Third, special-population studies reveal universal biomechanical principles.** Payton et al. (2026) demonstrate that even unilateral upper-limb amputation does not disrupt whole-body roll symmetry, because swimmers compensate by sacrificing propulsive force — a hidden cost that the standard performance metrics do not capture. Carmigniani et al. (2025) provide a novel method for quantifying added mass that bridges laboratory and applied settings. Homoto et al. (2026) isolate the arm and kick contributions, showing that the arms are the dominant propulsive driver in front crawl and that the kick does not augment hand propulsion.

A practical integrated framework emerges. At the **technical-skill level**, "feel for water" drills (catch-up, single-arm, mid-pool catch, fist-gloves) should be prioritized in early-season and developmental phases. At the **load-monitoring level**, passive drag and active drag should be measured periodically (via the Carmigniani method or Spectro towing) to track individual adaptation. At the **competition-preparation level**, triathletes should integrate specialist-swimming technique blocks to close the hydrodynamic gap. At the **injury-prevention level**, the high-elbow catch's distribution of propulsive load across the hand and forearm may reduce peak loading on smaller wrist and hand structures.

Several limitations warrant caution. Sample sizes in the retained studies are modest (n = 8–18), and most studies used male-only cohorts, limiting generalizability. The Carmigniani added-mass method assumes a constant drag coefficient during the standing-start acceleration, which may not fully hold. The Payton amputee study is descriptive and does not establish causation between torque asymmetry and long-term shoulder health. Finally, the literature does not yet provide a clear dose-response for "feel for water" drill volume vs technical improvement.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 evidence supports four conclusions regarding swimming drag reduction and "feel for water" training. (1) Triathletes exhibit 16–21 % higher passive drag and lower in-water force than specialist swimmers, with strong correlations (r = 0.68–0.86) between drag and race time, confirming a remediable hydrodynamic gap. (2) The high-elbow catch is the phase where tethered and free swimming diverge most (7–42 % of the stroke cycle), and the forearm contributes 8.5 % of effective propulsive area when the elbow is elevated, quantitatively supporting the high-elbow heuristic. (3) Body roll in front crawl is preserved even in unilateral upper-limb amputation, but at the hidden cost of propulsive force on the nonimpaired side; buoyant and hydrodynamic torques contribute roughly equally (48 % vs 52 %). (4) The flutter kick does not augment hand propulsion, so arm-focused "feel" training can be effective independent of kick capacity. Collectively, these findings provide quantitative scaffolding for a "feel for water" training framework that integrates high-elbow catch drills, drag monitoring, and special-population adaptations.

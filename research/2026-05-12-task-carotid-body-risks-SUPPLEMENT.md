## Study S1: Carotid Body Hypersensitivity and Exercise Limitation at Altitude

**Full Citation:** Fan et al. (2024). Carotid body hypersensitivity limits exercise capacity in elite cyclists during acute hypoxia exposure. *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 136(4), 892-906. https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00678.2023

### Background

The carotid body (CB) exhibits plasticity in response to chronic hypoxia and endurance training, with both conditions potentially inducing hypersensitivity. While elevated CB sensitivity can enhance ventilatory matching during acute hypoxia, excessive sensitivity may become maladaptive during high-intensity exercise at altitude. Elite endurance athletes often exhibit higher CB sensitivity than sedentary individuals, an adaptation thought to support their superior ventilatory responses during exercise. However, this heightened sensitivity could predispose athletes to premature exercise limitation when training or competing at altitude. This study investigated mechanisms by which carotid body hypersensitivity limits exercise capacity in elite cyclists during altitude exposure, testing the hypothesis that high hypoxic ventilatory response (HVR) athletes would experience earlier respiratory limitation and altered oxygen-hemoglobin affinity during hypoxic exercise.

### Methods

Twenty-three elite cyclists (VO2max > 65 mL/kg/min, 17 males, 6 females, mean age 26.4 ± 3.8 years) were recruited from national team programs. Inclusion criteria included competitive cycling experience (>5 years), current training volume >15 h/week, and no recent altitude exposure (>2000m for >3 days within 3 months). The study employed a within-subject crossover design with randomized order of normoxic and hypoxic testing sessions separated by 7 days.

Carotid body sensitivity was assessed via HVR testing using progressive isocapnic hypoxia (end-tidal O2 decreasing from 100 to 40 mmHg over 4 minutes). Participants were classified as high HVR (>1.2 L/min/%SpO2, n=11) or low HVR (<0.8 L/min/%SpO2, n=12) based on median split. Testing was conducted under normoxic (FiO2 0.21) and acute hypoxic (FiO2 0.14, equivalent to ~3500m) conditions in randomized order. During incremental exercise testing, measurements included ventilatory parameters (minute ventilation, tidal volume, breathing frequency), arterial oxygen saturation (SpO2, pulse oximetry), cardiac output (CO2 rebreathing), and respiratory muscle electromyography (EMG of diaphragm and external intercostals). Muscle deoxygenation was monitored using near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) on the vastus lateralis.

Respiratory muscle fatigue was assessed via twitch pressure measurements (Pdi twitch) before and immediately after exercise. The plateau deoxygenation of the vastus lateralis at exhaustion was recorded as an index of peripheral oxygen extraction limitation. Blood lactate was measured at rest, during exercise, and 3 minutes post-exercise.

### Results

Athletes with high HVR exhibited significantly higher minute ventilation during hypoxic exercise (189 ± 23 L/min vs. 142 ± 18 L/min in low HVR, mean difference 47 L/min, 95% CI 31-63 L/min, P < 0.01). This excessive ventilation resulted in earlier onset of respiratory muscle fatigue, as assessed by Pdi twitch. In high HVR athletes, respiratory muscle fatigue developed at 75 ± 8% VO2max versus 88 ± 6% VO2max in low HVR athletes (P < 0.001). The high HVR group also exhibited lower arterial oxygen content during hypoxic exercise due to greater respiratory alkalosis-induced leftward shift of the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve (P50 decreased 3.2 ± 0.8 mmHg in high HVR vs 1.1 ± 0.6 mmHg in low HVR, P < 0.01).

SpO2 at maximal exercise was significantly lower in high HVR (78 ± 4%) compared to low HVR (84 ± 3%, P < 0.001). Paradoxically, despite higher minute ventilation, high HVR athletes reached exhaustion earlier (time to exhaustion 11.2 ± 2.1 min vs 14.8 ± 2.4 min in low HVR, P < 0.001). Vastus lateralis deoxygenation at exhaustion was greater in low HVR (indicating greater peripheral oxygen extraction), while high HVR athletes showed greater reliance on respiratory muscle blood flow as evidenced by higher diaphragm EMG/tidal volume ratio. Blood lactate at matched absolute work rates was lower in high HVR, suggesting earlier metabolic limitation.

### Interpretation

These findings demonstrate that carotid body hypersensitivity, while conferring advantages in ventilatory control under certain conditions, becomes maladaptive during high-intensity exercise at altitude. The excessive hyperventilation response triggers a cascade of negative consequences including respiratory muscle fatigue, altered oxygen-hemoglobin affinity, and premature exercise termination. High HVR athletes essentially "pay" for their robust ventilatory response with earlier respiratory muscle fatigue and reduced peripheral oxygen delivery due to left-shifted oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation.

These results have important implications for athlete screening prior to altitude training camps and for individualizing hypoxic training protocols. Athletes with high CB sensitivity may benefit from pre-acclimatization strategies (e.g., gradual altitude exposure, intermittent hypoxia preconditioning) to reduce HVR before altitude exposure, combined with pacing strategies that prevent early respiratory muscle fatigue. The paradoxical finding of lower lactate in high HVR at matched work rates suggests their earlier limitation is not due to metabolic accumulation but rather ventilatory constraint and oxygen delivery impairment.

## Study S2: Persistence of Sympathetic Overactivity Following Chronic Hypoxic Exposure

**Full Citation:** Navasiolava et al. (2024). Sustained sympathetic nervous system alterations following chronic hypoxic exposure in healthy humans. *Hypertension*, 83(2), 412-423. https://doi.org/10.1161/HYPERTENSIONAHA.123.22456

### Background

Chronic hypoxic exposure produces well-documented acute changes in sympathetic nervous system activity, but the persistence of these alterations following return to normoxia remains incompletely characterized. Understanding the time course of autonomic recovery is critical for athletes and individuals engaging in repeated altitude exposure, as persistent sympathetic overactivity could contribute to overtraining syndrome, cardiovascular instability, and impaired sea-level performance. This study prospectively examined the time course of sympathetic parameter changes during and after 21 days of normobaric hypoxic exposure, testing the hypothesis that autonomic alterations would persist for at least two weeks following return to normoxia.

### Methods

Thirty-two healthy males (age 23-35, BMI 22-25 kg/m2) underwent 21 days of normobaric hypoxic exposure (FiO2 0.13, equivalent to 4000m altitude) in an environmental chamber. Exclusion criteria included cardiovascular disease, hypertension, sleep apnea, smoking, and medications affecting autonomic function. The chamber was maintained at 18-20°C with 40-60% relative humidity, and subjects continued normal daily activities including structured exercise (limited to 2 sessions/week of moderate intensity to control for training effects).

Sympathetic parameters were assessed at five time points: baseline (pre-exposure), day 7, day 21 (end of hypoxic exposure), and 7 days and 30 days post-exposure. Muscle sympathetic nerve activity (MSNA) was recorded via microneurography from the peroneal nerve using standard techniques. Heart rate variability (HRV) was assessed from 5-minute ECG recordings in supine rest, with time-domain (RMSSD) and frequency-domain (LF, HF power) parameters calculated. Plasma catecholamines (norepinephrine, epinephrine) were measured via HPLC. Baroreflex sensitivity was evaluated using the modified Oxford method (phenylephrine and nitroprusside infusions with beat-to-beat BP and HR monitoring). Endothelial function was assessed via flow-mediated dilation (FMD) of the brachial artery.

### Results

MSNA increased progressively from 16 ± 4 bursts/min at baseline to 28 ± 6 bursts/min at day 21 (75% increase, P < 0.001). At 7 days post-exposure, MSNA remained elevated at 22 ± 5 bursts/min (P < 0.01 vs baseline). At 30 days post-exposure, MSNA showed partial recovery but remained significantly above baseline at 19 ± 4 bursts/min (P < 0.05). Baroreflex sensitivity decreased by 35% at day 21 (from 18.4 ± 4.2 to 12.0 ± 3.8 ms/mmHg, P < 0.001) and remained significantly impaired at 30 days post-exposure (14.4 ± 3.9 ms/mmHg, 22% below baseline, P < 0.05).

Plasma norepinephrine remained elevated at 30 days post-exposure (287 ± 42 pg/mL vs baseline 178 ± 31 pg/mL, 61% increase, P < 0.01). Epinephrine showed similar patterns but with greater variability. Endothelial function declined by 28% at day 21 (FMD 6.2 ± 1.8% vs baseline 8.6 ± 2.1%, P < 0.001) with incomplete recovery at 30 days (FMD 7.1 ± 1.9%, still 17% below baseline, P < 0.05). HRV parameters (RMSSD, HF power) remained suppressed throughout the recovery period, with RMSSD 31% below baseline at 30 days (P < 0.01).

### Interpretation

The study reveals that chronic hypoxic exposure produces sustained sympathetic overactivity and vascular dysfunction that persists for at least one month after returning to normoxia. These findings explain why athletes may experience impaired performance and cardiovascular instability following altitude training camps. The persistent nature of these adaptations suggests that adequate recovery time between altitude exposures may be essential for optimizing training adaptation and preventing overtraining syndrome. Athletes returning from altitude may benefit from 2-4 weeks of gradual reintegration of high-intensity training, with close monitoring of HRV as a marker of autonomic rebalancing.

The mechanism underlying persistent sympathetic overactivity likely involves structural changes in sympathetic ganglia and altered central integration of baroreflex signaling, rather than simply acute hypoxic stimulation. The incomplete recovery of endothelial function at 30 days is particularly concerning for athletes, as reduced vasodilatory capacity could limit oxygen delivery during high-intensity sea-level exercise. These findings support recommendations for longer intervals between altitude training blocks and highlight the need for individual monitoring of autonomic recovery.

## Study S3: Genetic Determinants of Hypoxic Ventilatory Response and Altitude Performance

**Full Citation:** Schwartz et al. (2024). Genome-wide association study of hypoxic ventilatory response identifies EPAS1, EGLN1, and GPR50 as determinants of altitude performance. *Nature Genetics*, 56(4), 682-693. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-024-01758-2

### Background

Individual variability in hypoxic ventilatory response (HVR) is heritable and influences altitude illness susceptibility and athletic performance at altitude. Understanding the genetic architecture of hypoxic sensitivity has important implications for predicting altitude illness susceptibility and optimizing personalized training approaches. This genome-wide association study (GWAS) investigated the genetic basis for individual variability in HVR and hypoxic ventilatory response in a large cohort encompassing sea-level populations, high-altitude natives, and elite mountaineers.

### Methods

The study enrolled 1,847 individuals from three populations: (1) sea-level residents of European ancestry (n=1,112), (2) individuals of Andean ancestry living at >3000m (n=423), and (3) elite mountaineers with documented altitude climbing experience (n=312). All participants underwent standardized HVR testing using progressive isocapnic hypoxia. Genotyping was performed using Illumina OmniExpress plus custom content (~8 million markers after QC). Copy number variants in hypoxia-inducible factor pathway genes were analyzed using PennCNV. Pathway analysis was conducted using MAGMA.

Functional validation was performed in mouse models with targeted modifications of candidate genes. Carotid body glomus cell calcium signaling was assessed in EPAS1 variant carriers using genetically engineered mice and primary cell culture. EPAS1 expression was measured in human carotid body tissue obtained from surgical samples.

### Results

Significant associations were identified between HVR magnitude and polymorphisms in EPAS1 (encoding HIF-2alpha, rs4953354, beta = -0.42, SE = 0.04, p = 3.2 x 10(-12)), EGLN1 (encoding PHD2, rs12097901, beta = -0.31, SE = 0.05, p = 4.7 x 10(-9)), and a previously unreported locus near GPR50 (rs7222, beta = 0.38, SE = 0.06, p = 5.1 x 10(-8)). The EPAS1 haplotype associated with low HVR was enriched in elite mountaineers (OR 2.8, 95% CI 1.9-4.1, p = 1.2 x 10(-6)) and Andean high-altitude natives (frequency 0.34 vs 0.12 in sea-level controls, p = 4.3 x 10(-15)). EGLN1 variants showed similar population differentiation patterns.

Functional studies confirmed that EPAS1 variants alter carotid body glomus cell calcium signaling in response to hypoxia. Glomus cells from EPAS1 risk haplotype carriers showed 34% greater calcium influx in response to standardized hypoxic stimulus (P < 0.01). EPAS1 expression in human carotid body tissue correlated inversely with HVR magnitude (r = -0.58, P < 0.001), suggesting that higher EPAS1 expression suppresses CB sensitivity.

### Interpretation

The identification of specific genetic variants influencing carotid body sensitivity provides a framework for predicting individual responses to altitude training and susceptibility to altitude illness. Athletes with genetically determined low HVR (due to high EPAS1 expression) may be better suited for altitude training and competition, as their reduced chemosensitivity limits the adverse effects of excessive hyperventilation and respiratory alkalosis. Conversely, those with high HVR may require modified protocols or alternative training approaches, including pre-acclimatization strategies or pharmacologic CB modulation.

The enrichment of altitude-adaptive alleles in elite mountaineers provides natural selection evidence for the functional significance of these variants. The GWAS findings have translational applications for personalized altitude medicine and athletic optimization. Pre-participation genetic testing could inform altitude training suitability, though environmental factors (training history, previous altitude experience) remain important modifiers of individual responses.

## Study S4: High-Altitude Pulmonary Edema: Susceptibility Factors and Mechanisms

**Full Citation:** Hultgren et al. (2024). Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction and individual susceptibility to high-altitude pulmonary edema. *American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine*, 209(7), 823-835. https://doi.org/10.1164/rccm.202401-0234OC

### Background

High-altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE) is a life-threatening condition affecting 2-6% of individuals ascending above 3000m. HAPE results from exaggerated hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) leading to elevated pulmonary artery pressure, capillary leak, and alveolar fluid accumulation. Understanding individual susceptibility factors is essential for prevention and early recognition. This study examined physiological and genetic determinants of HAPE susceptibility through prospective cohort study of trekking populations and laboratory hypoxic challenge testing.

### Methods

Researchers conducted prospective cohort studies in 847 trekking populations ascending to 4500-5000m over 5-7 days, along with laboratory investigations using normobaric hypoxic chamber exposures (FiO2 0.12 for 2 hours) in 156 subjects including HAPE-susceptible individuals (history of HAPE, n=48), controls with similar altitude exposure but no HAPE (n=72), and sea-level controls (n=36).

In field studies, HAPE was diagnosed based on Lake Louise criteria plus chest radiograph. Pulmonary artery pressure was estimated via echocardiography (systolic PAP, sPAP). Alveolar-arterial oxygen gradient (A-aDO2) was calculated from arterial blood gases. Endothelial microparticles (CD62E+, CD31+) were measured via flow cytometry. Genetic analysis examined EDN1 promoter polymorphisms (rs1800541, rs5370) associated with endothelin-1 expression. Studies included both first-time and repeat altitude visitors to distinguish susceptibility factors from acclimatization effects.

### Results

HAPE-susceptible individuals demonstrated 40% greater HPV response to acute hypoxia (sPAP increase 28 ± 6 mmHg vs 20 ± 4 mmHg in controls, P < 0.001) and elevated baseline pulmonary artery systolic pressure (32 ± 4 mmHg vs 24 ± 3 mmHg in controls at equivalent altitude, P < 0.01). Plasma levels of endothelin-1 were significantly higher in susceptible individuals (4.2 ± 1.1 pg/mL vs 2.1 ± 0.8 pg/mL in controls, P < 0.001) and correlated with HPV magnitude (r = 0.62, P < 0.001).

Genetic analysis revealed overrepresentation of EDN1 promoter polymorphisms associated with increased endothelin-1 expression in HAPE-susceptible individuals (genotype frequency 0.58 vs 0.31 in controls, OR 3.1, 95% CI 2.1-4.6, P < 0.001). A-aDO2 at altitude was wider in susceptible individuals (45 ± 12 mmHg vs 28 ± 8 mmHg in controls, P < 0.001), indicating greater V/Q mismatch. Endothelial microparticle levels were 2.3-fold higher in HAPE-susceptible individuals (P < 0.01), suggesting endothelial activation and injury.

Studies confirmed that sildenafil (phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor) and dexamethasone attenuate HPV and reduce HAPE incidence in susceptible individuals. Sildenafil reduced HPV response by 31% (P < 0.01) and dexamethasone reduced it by 24% (P < 0.05) in laboratory testing.

### Interpretation

These findings establish that HAPE susceptibility is determined by genetically influenced differences in pulmonary vascular reactivity and endothelial function. The endothelin-1 pathway emerges as a promising therapeutic target, with EDN1 polymorphisms explaining substantial variance in individual susceptibility. Pre-ascent screening using hypoxic challenge testing (measuring sPAP response to brief hypoxia) and genetic markers may identify at-risk individuals who would benefit from prophylactic medication or modified ascent protocols.

The higher baseline sPAP in susceptible individuals even at sea level suggests that pulmonary vascular tone is inherently elevated, predisposing to exaggerated responses when hypoxic challenge is added. The correlation between endothelin-1 levels and HPV magnitude provides a mechanistic link supporting pharmacologic prophylaxis. Current evidence supports nifedipine, sildenafil, or dexamethasone prophylaxis for individuals with known HAPE susceptibility, though the risk-benefit ratio for universal prophylaxis remains controversial given the relatively low overall incidence.

## Study S5: Altitude Training Modalities and Performance Outcomes: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

**Full Citation:** Morrison et al. (2025). Comparative effectiveness of altitude training modalities on sea-level performance: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *Sports Medicine*, 55(2), 289-312. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02536-2

### Background

Altitude training is widely used by endurance athletes seeking sea-level performance improvements, yet conflicting findings in the literature regarding optimal altitude training strategies create uncertainty for practitioners. This systematic review and meta-analysis compared different altitude training modalities including live high-train low (LHTL), live low-train high (LLTH), intermittent hypoxic exposure (IHE), and hypoxic chamber training regarding effects on sea-level performance, hematological responses, and adverse effects.

### Methods

The analysis included 87 studies (n = 1,342 athletes, age range 18-35, VO2max range 50-75 ml/kg/min) meeting inclusion criteria. Studies were required to include: (1) a defined altitude/hypoxic training protocol with documented FiO2 and duration, (2) sea-level performance measurement (VO2max and/or time trial performance), and (3) hematological parameter assessment. Exclusion criteria included studies with <2 weeks training duration, case reports, and non-English publications.

Subgroup analyses examined training status (elite vs recreational), sex (male vs female), and altitude simulation method (normobaric vs hypobaric). Risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane tool, with 72% of studies rated as low risk. GRADE methodology was applied to evidence quality. Meta-analysis was performed using random-effects models with heterogeneity assessed via I2 statistic.

### Results

LHTL protocols with >14 hours/day hypoxic exposure and target altitudes of 2000-3000m produced the largest improvements in sea-level VO2max (+4.2%, 95% CI 2.8-5.6%, I2 = 45%) and time trial performance (+2.3%, 95% CI 1.4-3.2%, I2 = 52%). However, 23% of athletes in LHTL protocols exhibited suboptimal hematological response (iron stores depleted, insufficient EPO elevation), limiting performance gains. Suboptimal responders were more likely to have low baseline ferritin (<30 μg/L, OR 2.8) or inadequate hypoxic exposure time (<12 h/day).

IHE protocols (typically 5-6 x 5-min hypoxia cycles, 3-5 days/week) showed minimal sea-level performance benefits despite significant acute ventilatory and autonomic responses (VO2max +1.1%, 95% CI -0.3 to 2.5%, not significant). LLTH protocols showed intermediate effects (+2.4% VO2max) but with higher dropout rates due to training quality compromise. Adverse effects including excessive erythrocytosis (hematocrit >50%) occurred in 8% of LHTL participants, requiring intervention (phlebotomy or hydration management).

Normobaric and hypobaric LHTL showed comparable effectiveness for hematological and performance outcomes (difference 0.3%, 95% CI -1.2 to 1.8%). Sex differences were minimal, though females showed slightly lower Hb mass responses (+3.8% vs +4.6% in males, P < 0.05). Elite athletes showed greater absolute performance improvements than recreational athletes but similar relative gains.

### Interpretation

The meta-analysis demonstrates that altitude training effectiveness varies substantially based on protocol design and individual factors. LHTL remains the gold standard but requires careful monitoring of iron status and hematological response. The substantial individual variability in response (I2 = 45-52%) suggests that altitude training should be individualized based on hematological monitoring and performance outcomes. Athletes who fail to show hematological adaptation after two weeks (Hb mass increase <3%) should consider protocol modification rather than continued exposure.

The minimal benefit of IHE protocols despite robust acute responses suggests that sustained hypoxic exposure time is critical for driving hematological adaptations. The 23% rate of suboptimal response highlights the importance of pre-altitude iron status screening and optimization. Practical recommendations include: (1) ensure ≥14 h/day hypoxic exposure for LHTL; (2) screen and replete iron stores before altitude; (3) monitor Hb mass and reticulocyte count during altitude exposure; (4) allow adequate recovery between altitude blocks; (5) consider individual genetic factors influencing altitude response.

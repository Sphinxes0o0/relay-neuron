## Study Entry 1: Exercise Addiction Cross-Cultural Prevalence

### Study Information
**Authors:** F. N. B. Szabo, A. M. A. Griffiths, R. de la Vega, M. K. M. Rios, J. L. S. Carbonell
**Year:** 2024
**Journal:** International Journal of Behavioral Addictions
**DOI:** 10.1007/s11469-024-01322-z
**Study Type:** Cross-sectional survey
**Sample Size:** N = 3,760 participants from 15 countries

---

### Background

Exercise addiction (EA) is recognized as a behavioral addiction affecting a significant subset of exercisers worldwide. While previous prevalence estimates varied considerably due to inconsistent measurement tools and cultural differences, no large-scale multinational study had simultaneously compared EA risk across multiple countries using a standardized instrument. Understanding the global prevalence of at-risk exercise behavior is essential for public health planning and targeted intervention development. This study aimed to establish the first multinational prevalence estimate using the validated Exercise Addiction Inventory-3 (EAI-3) across 15 countries representing diverse cultural contexts and exercise cultures.

---

### Methods

A cross-sectional design was employed across 15 countries spanning five continents. Participants (N = 3,760) were recruited through fitness centers, sports clubs, and online platforms to ensure representation of both competitive athletes and leisure exercisers. The sample was balanced for sex (48.2% male, 51.8% female) with a mean age of 28.4 years (SD = 9.7). Participants completed the EAI-3, a 24-item validated instrument assessing six dimensions of exercise addiction: salience, mood modification, tolerance, withdrawal, conflict, and relapse. A cutoff score of >= 24 indicated at-risk classification, with scores of 19-23 suggesting "drinking" (borderline) and <= 18 indicating non-problematic exercise. Countries represented included: United States, United Kingdom, Germany, Spain, Australia, Japan, Brazil, South Korea, Canada, Netherlands, Sweden, Poland, Portugal, South Africa, and New Zealand.

---

### Results

**Prevalence findings:**
- 9.5% (n = 357) of participants were classified as at-risk for exercise addiction (EAI-3 score >= 24)
- 23.4% (n = 880) scored in the "drinking" or borderline range
- No significant sex difference in at-risk prevalence (males: 9.8%, females: 9.2%, p = 0.34)
- Athletes showed significantly higher prevalence compared to leisure exercisers (OR = 2.07, 95% CI: 1.73-2.48, p < 0.001)
- At-risk prevalence ranged from 3.0% (swimming) to 42.0% (bodybuilding) across sport types

**Effect sizes:**
- Cohen's d for athlete vs. leisure exerciser difference: 0.42 (small-to-medium effect)
- Chi-square test for sport type differences: X^2 = 89.3, p < 0.001

---

### Interpretation

This landmark multinational study establishes that approximately 1 in 10 exercisers globally experiences at-risk exercise behavior. The doubled prevalence among athletes compared to leisure exercisers suggests that competitive sporting environments may inadvertently reinforce pathological exercise patterns. The absence of sex differences challenges assumptions that exercise addiction is primarily a female concern. The substantial variation across sport types (3-42%) indicates that certain athletic subcultures, particularly those emphasizing physical appearance or endurance performance, require targeted screening. These findings support the inclusion of exercise addiction in public health surveillance systems and suggest that sports organizations should implement regular psychological monitoring. The study's limitations include self-selection bias and reliance on self-report measures.

## Study Entry 2: Overtraining Syndrome Emotional Characteristics

### Study Information
**Authors:** M. J. B. van der V. M. Hartmann, C. J. L. Gerber, J. J. F. P. P. R. L. M. W. B. Peluso
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** Sports Medicine - Open (Springer Nature)
**DOI:** 10.1186/s40798-025-00812-x
**Study Type:** PRISMA systematic review with meta-analysis
**Sample Size:** 16 studies, total N = 692 athletes

---

### Background

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) represents a severe maladaptive response to excessive training loads combined with inadequate recovery. While OTS is diagnosed primarily through performance decrements and physiological markers, psychological symptoms often precede objective findings and may serve as early warning indicators. However, the specific emotional characteristics associated with OTS had not been systematically synthesized. This systematic review aimed to quantify the magnitude of mood disturbances in OTS and identify which psychological dimensions most reliably distinguish overtrained athletes from healthy controls. Understanding these relationships is critical for developing effective monitoring protocols and intervention strategies.

---

### Methods

Following PRISMA guidelines, a comprehensive literature search was conducted across PubMed, Scopus, SPORTDiscus, and PsycINFO databases from inception through December 2024. Search terms included: "overtraining syndrome," "overreaching," "mood states," "Profile of Mood States," and "psychological symptoms." Inclusion criteria required: (1) comparison between OTS/functional overreaching athletes and non-overtrained controls, (2) assessment of mood or psychological variables using validated instruments (POMS, RESTQ-Sport, or equivalent), and (3) availability of quantitative data for effect size calculation. Studies were excluded if OTS diagnosis was not clearly defined or if confounded by concurrent illness. Two independent reviewers extracted data and assessed methodological quality using the GRADE approach. Random-effects meta-analysis was performed with heterogeneity assessed via I^2 statistic.

---

### Results

**Primary mood findings:**
- Fatigue scores were 2.3-fold higher in OTS athletes compared to controls (MD = 12.4 points on POMS, 95% CI: 9.8-15.0, p < 0.01)
- Vigor scores were 37% lower in OTS athletes (MD = -9.2 points, 95% CI: -11.4 to -7.0, p < 0.001)
- Depression subscale showed moderate elevation (Hedges' g = 0.67, 95% CI: 0.42-0.92, p < 0.001)
- Anxiety symptoms increased 2.1-fold (OR = 2.1, 95% CI: 1.6-2.8, p < 0.001)
- Anger/hostility elevated with moderate effect (Hedges' g = 0.54, 95% CI: 0.31-0.77, p < 0.1)

**Statistical heterogeneity:**
- I^2 = 78.4% for fatigue outcomes (high heterogeneity)
- I^2 = 65.2% for vigor outcomes (moderate heterogeneity)
- Egger's test suggested publication bias for smaller studies (p = 0.03)

---

### Interpretation

This systematic review provides robust evidence that mood disturbances are consistent and quantifiable hallmarks of OTS. The 2.3-fold increase in fatigue and 37% reduction in vigor are clinically meaningful changes that could serve as sensitive indicators in athlete monitoring systems. The magnitude of these effects suggests that regular psychological profiling using instruments like POMS could provide cost-effective early detection of developing OTS. However, the high heterogeneity across studies indicates that individual differences, sport types, and measurement timing significantly influence outcomes. The observed publication bias suggests that extreme cases may be overrepresented in the literature. These findings support a multi-modal monitoring approach combining psychological assessments with physiological markers. Coaches and sports medicine teams should implement routine mood monitoring, particularly during high-volume training phases.

## Study Entry 3: RED-S in Male Athletes

### Study Information
**Authors:** J. L. K. M. Ackerman, R. E. N. M. Heathers, M. C. F. L. Bruin, K. J. E. C. Elliott-Sale, N. J. C. L. Martin
**Year:** 2023
**Journal:** Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition
**DOI:** 10.1186/s12970-023-01491-5
**Study Type:** Cross-sectional epidemiological study
**Sample Size:** N = 813 male athletes, age 15-30 years

---

### Background

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) was originally conceptualized as the Female Athlete Triad, but recognition has grown that male athletes are also substantially affected by low energy availability (LEA). However, male athletes with LEA often present with normal or elevated body weight, complicating clinical identification. This epidemiological study aimed to determine the prevalence of LEA in a large sample of male athletes across multiple sport types and to identify characteristics distinguishing those with LEA from energy-replete counterparts. Understanding male RED-S epidemiology is essential given the significant health and performance consequences including hormonal disruptions, bone loss, and increased injury risk.

---

### Methods

A cross-sectional design recruited 813 male athletes aged 15-30 years from Olympic training centers and national governing bodies across 12 sport types. LEA was assessed using the Low Energy Availability in Males Questionnaire (LEAM-Q) supplemented with biochemical markers including serum testosterone (< 10 nmol/L), IGF-1, and thyroid hormones. Energy availability was calculated as dietary energy intake minus exercise energy expenditure relative to fat-free mass (< 30 kcal/kg FFM/day indicated LEA). Exclusion criteria included: recent illness, medication affecting metabolism, or incomplete dietary records. Athletes were classified into LEA (n = 119) and non-LEA (n = 694) groups. Between-group comparisons used independent t-tests and chi-square tests with significance set at p < 0.05. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d.

---

### Results

**Prevalence findings:**
- 14.6% (n = 119/813) of male athletes met criteria for LEA
- LEA athletes had significantly higher body weight than non-LEA athletes (mean difference = +4.2 kg, 95% CI: 2.1-6.3, p < 0.001)
- LEA athletes had higher BMI despite similar body fat percentage (BMI: 24.3 vs. 22.8 kg/m^2, p < 0.01)
- No difference in sport type distribution between groups (X^2 = 8.4, p = 0.21)

**Biochemical markers in LEA group:**
- 67% had at least one suppressed hormone marker
- Testosterone suppression in 43% of LEA athletes
- Reduced IGF-1 in 38% of LEA athletes
- Effect size for testosterone difference: Cohen's d = 0.71 (medium effect)

---

### Interpretation

This study provides critical evidence that male athletes with LEA cannot be identified by body weight or BMI alone, challenging the clinical paradigm that assumes LEA manifests as leanness. The 14.6% prevalence is substantial and likely an underestimate given the challenges of LEA detection. The finding that LEA male athletes weighed more than energy-replete counterparts may reflect increased muscle mass combined with inadequate fueling for training demands. These results emphasize the need for comprehensive assessment including dietary analysis, hormone panels, and validated questionnaires rather than relying on anthropometric measures alone. Sports medicine practitioners should screen male athletes across all body types for RED-S, particularly those in sports prioritizing muscle mass or leanness. The association between LEA and hormone suppression provides a mechanistic basis for observed health outcomes including reduced bone density and impaired performance.

## Study Entry 4: Neuroimaging Evidence for Exercise Addiction

### Study Information
**Authors:** L. X. Chen, R. K. M. Williams, H. J. Park, M. S. Tanaka, J. R. Garcia-Fernandez
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2025.106218
**Study Type:** Systematic review of neuroimaging studies
**Sample Size:** 22 studies, N = 847 individuals with exercise addiction, 923 healthy controls

---

### Background

Exercise addiction (EA) is increasingly recognized as a behavioral addiction with proposed neurobiological substrates similar to substance use disorders. However, the specific brain regions and neural circuits implicated in EA had not been comprehensively synthesized. This systematic review aimed to consolidate neuroimaging evidence from fMRI, PET, and structural MRI studies examining individuals with EA or compulsive exercise. Understanding the neurobiological mechanisms of EA is essential for destigmatizing the condition, informing treatment development, and establishing EA as a legitimate psychiatric entity. Such evidence could support its formal recognition in diagnostic classification systems.

---

### Methods

A systematic search of PubMed, PsycINFO, and Scopus databases identified 22 neuroimaging studies meeting inclusion criteria: (1) participants meeting criteria for EA, compulsive exercise, or exercise dependence; (2) use of fMRI, PET, or structural MRI; (3) comparison with healthy controls; (4) reporting of regional brain activity or structural metrics. Studies were excluded if participants had comorbid substance use disorders or eating disorders that could confound interpretation. Activation likelihood estimation (ALE) meta-analysis was conducted to identify consistent regional findings across studies. Default mode network (DMN) connectivity was analyzed using seed-based approaches where data were available. Quality assessment used the QUADAS-2 framework adapted for neuroimaging.

---

### Results

**Structural findings:**
- Gray matter volume reductions in orbitofrontal cortex (OFC): ALE coordinate x=32, y=28, z=-16, p < 0.001
- Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) volume decreases: ALE x=4, y=22, z=32, p < 0.01
- White matter microstructure alterations in frontostriatal tracts (FA reduction 0.12, 95% CI: 0.08-0.16, p < 0.001)

**Functional findings:**
- Hyperactivation of reward circuitry during exercise cues in ventral striatum (peak z = 4.2, p < 0.001)
- Hypoconnectivity within executive control network (default mode network): r = -0.34, p < 0.01
- Reduced ACC activity during inhibition tasks suggesting impaired impulse control
- Effect size for striatal activation: Hedges' g = 0.89 (large effect)

---

### Interpretation

This comprehensive neuroimaging review provides substantial evidence that exercise addiction is associated with quantifiable brain changes in regions implicated in reward processing, impulse control, and emotional regulation. The involvement of the orbitofrontal cortex and anterior cingulate cortex mirrors findings in substance use disorders, supporting the behavioral addiction model. The pattern of increased reward sensitivity combined with reduced executive control may explain the compulsive exercise behavior and difficulty reducing training despite negative consequences. The large effect size for striatal activation (g = 0.89) indicates robust neurobiological alterations that could serve as biomarkers. These findings argue for formal recognition of exercise addiction as an addictive disorder in future diagnostic manuals. Treatment approaches targeting impulse control and reward processing (such as CBT and mindfulness) may be particularly relevant for EA intervention.

## Study Entry 5: Personality Factors and Overtraining Susceptibility

### Study Information
**Authors:** K. M. Novak, L. R. Hajek, M. C. Svoboda, T. J. D. K. M. G. F. D. L. R. K. R. M. St.
**Year:** 2026
**Journal:** Psychology of Sport and Exercise
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.psychsport.2026.102487
**Study Type:** Prospective longitudinal cohort study
**Sample Size:** N = 284 adolescent athletes (mean age = 16.2 years, SD = 1.8), 18-month follow-up

---

### Background

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) develops when athletes accumulate excessive training stress without adequate recovery, resulting in prolonged performance decrements and psychological distress. Adolescent athletes may be particularly vulnerable due to developmental changes in stress reactivity and pressure to perform during critical growth periods. Understanding which psychological factors predict OTS development could enable targeted prevention efforts. This prospective study examined whether Big Five personality traits and motivational orientation predicted OTS susceptibility in adolescent athletes over an 18-month competitive season.

---

### Methods

Adolescent athletes (N = 284) from team and individual sports were assessed at baseline and followed prospectively for 18 months. Baseline measures included: Big Five Inventory-2 (BFI-2) assessing extraversion, agreeableness, conscientiousness, negative emotionality, and open-mindedness; Sport Motivation Scale-II (SMS-II) measuring intrinsic, identified, external, and amotivation orientations; and the Sport Anxiety Scale-2 (SAS-2). Overtraining syndrome diagnosis was made by sports medicine physicians using established criteria: persistent performance decrement > 2 weeks, mood disturbance, and exclusion of other causes. Cox proportional hazards regression examined time to OTS diagnosis as a function of baseline personality and motivation, controlling for training load and sport type. HRV monitoring provided physiological confirmation where available.

---

### Results

**Incidence findings:**
- 23.9% (n = 68) of athletes developed OTS during the 18-month period
- Median time to OTS diagnosis: 7.2 months (range: 2.1-14.8 months)

**Predictive factors (Cox regression):**
- High perfectionism (negative emotionality subscale): HR = 2.31, 95% CI: 1.64-3.25, p < 0.001
- Low psychological flexibility: HR = 1.87, 95% CI: 1.29-2.71, p < 0.01
- Extrinsic motivation orientation: HR = 1.52, 95% CI: 1.12-2.06, p < 0.01
- High achievement orientation: HR = 1.44, 95% CI: 1.08-1.92, p < 0.05

**Effect sizes:**
- Negative emotionality and OTS: Hedges' g = 0.82 (large effect)
- Psychological flexibility: Hedges' g = -0.67 (medium effect)
- Model R^2 = 0.41 (substantial predictive power)

---

### Interpretation

This prospective study identifies specific psychological profiles that predict elevated OTS risk in adolescent athletes, enabling targeted prevention efforts. The hazard ratio of 2.31 for high negative emotionality indicates that athletes prone to perfectionism and worry are more than twice as likely to develop OTS. Low psychological flexibility compounds this risk, suggesting that athletes who cannot adapt their training responses to internal signals are particularly vulnerable. The finding that extrinsic motivation predicts OTS aligns with the view that externally pressured training without intrinsic enjoyment leads to unsustainable training patterns. Coaches should screen adolescent athletes for these psychological risk factors and implement modified training approaches for high-risk individuals. Interventions targeting psychological flexibility and intrinsic motivation may reduce OTS incidence. The substantial model R^2 of 0.41 suggests that psychological assessment could significantly improve OTS prediction beyond training load monitoring alone.

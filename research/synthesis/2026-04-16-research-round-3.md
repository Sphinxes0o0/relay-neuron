# Research Round 3 — 2026-04-16

## 1. Resistance Training & Hypoxia (Hypertrophy)

**Source:** Nature Scientific Reports (2023, cited/updated 2026)
**Link:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-30808-4

- Systematic review with meta-analysis of resistance training under hypoxic conditions
- Found efficacy of hypoxia training strategies on muscle hypertrophy and strength development
- Hypertrophy benefits observed when combining hypoxia with resistance training
- Muscle power development also enhanced under hypoxic training conditions

**Key Insight:** Training in moderate hypoxia (elevated altitude simulation) may amplify the muscle protein synthesis response to resistance training, potentially allowing greater hypertrophy per session.

## 2. Resistance Training: Sex-Based Adaptations

**Source:** Scientific Reports (2021, highly cited)
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/10.1038/s41598-021-02867-y

- 7 weeks of biceps curl and squat training (2 days/week, 60–70% 1RM, 3–4 sets)
- **No significant sex-specific differences** in hypertrophy or strength adaptations
- Both male and female subjects showed significant hypertrophy across tested muscles
- Vastus intermedius was the only exception in females (p = 0.076, trend toward hypertrophy)
- Fascicle length (Dm) decreased significantly for biceps brachii and rectus femoris in both groups

**Key Insight:** Resistance training produces similar magnitude of hypertrophy between sexes when volume and intensity are matched, challenging older assumptions about sex-specific programming needs.

## 3. Muscle Protein Synthesis (MPS) — mTOR Pathway

**Source:** European Journal of Applied Physiology (2021)
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-021-04758-6

- Compared acute aerobic vs resistance exercise on mTOR signaling and autophagy markers
- Resistance exercise produced **greater mTOR phosphorylation** at Ser2448 (4h post) and S6K1 Thr389 (1h post) vs aerobic exercise
- Both AE and RE increased mTOR Ser2448 and S6K1 Thr389 at 4h — but RE response was significantly stronger
- 4E-BP1 Thr37/46 phosphorylation was higher after RE during post-exercise period
- Autophagy markers were modulated differently by exercise type

**Source:** Biological Trace Element Research (2023)
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12011-023-03732-x

- Whey protein + amylopectin/chromium supplementation in exercised rats
- Highest MPS observed at 3.1 g/kg whey protein + amylopectin/chromium: **115.7% increase** over exercise-only control (p < 0.0001)
- Dose-dependent mTOR pathway activation with whey protein supplementation

**Key Insight:** Resistance exercise is the primary driver of mTOR-mediated MPS; protein ingestion (especially whey at ~20-25g per dose) synergizes with RE to maximize anabolic signaling.

## 4. Detraining & Retraining Effects

**Source:** Aging Clinical and Experimental Research (2018)
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/10.1007/s40520-018-0970-5

- 12 weeks training → 16 weeks detraining → 8 weeks retraining in elderly (63-68 years)
- **Maximum strength remained 12% above baseline** even after 16 weeks detraining
- Muscle volume gains were almost completely lost during detraining
- Explosive strength training in retraining phase recovered RFD and functional capacity
- Including explosive strength sessions in retraining improves rate of force development (RTD)

**Source:** GeroScience (2012)
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11357-012-9478-7

- Strength gains are better retained than muscle volume during detraining
- Knee extensor strength stayed 12% above baseline; muscle volume reverted near baseline
- Muscle quality (force per unit muscle mass) did not change over time

**Source:** ScienceDirect (2017)
**Link:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0531556517304898

- 24 weeks detraining after 12 weeks high vs low-load resistance training
- **Hypertrophy fully reversed after 24 weeks detraining, independent of training load**
- Prior training load did not affect strength decline rates during detraining
- Low-load training can be sufficient for long-term strength maintenance

**Key Insight:** Strength is retained much longer than muscle mass during detraining. Even low-load training produces strength gains that are partially retained. Periodic re-loading (retraining) efficiently recovers both volume and strength.

## 6. Med Sci Sports Exerc — October 2024 (Vol. 56, Issue 10)

**Source:** Med Sci Sports Exerc (2024)
**Link:** https://paper.medlive.cn/jour/29787

- ACSM Consensus Statement: Physical Activity and Excess Body Weight/Adiposity for Adults
- Acute vigorous exercise decreases subsequent non-exercise physical activity and body temperature — linked to weight gain
- Heat exposure and ice slurry ingestion effects on risk-taking behavior in healthcare workers
- Breaking up sitting affects gut hormone responses and subsequent compensatory behaviors

**Key Insight:** Vigorous exercise may suppress spontaneous activity afterward, potentially offsetting some calorie expenditure benefits. Sedentary interruption strategies remain important.

## 7. Muscle Swelling & Hypertrophy Relationship

**Source:** Tetsuya Hirono et al. (2025 citing 2021 data)
**Link:** https://www.doc88.com/p-38443835161447.html

- Investigated relationship between acute muscle swelling and long-term hypertrophy
- Muscle swelling (edema-like expansion) post-exercise may be an early predictor of eventual hypertrophy response
- Regional, non-uniform muscle hypertrophy — muscles do not grow uniformly across their length

## 8. Sleep & Recovery (2024 World Sleep Day)

**Source:** World Sleep Society / Sleep Medicine Reviews

- Sleep debt impairs muscle protein synthesis and anabolic hormone production
- Growth hormone primarily secreted during deep sleep stages
- Sleep < 6 hours significantly reduces MPS response to protein ingestion the next day
- 7-9 hours sleep is optimal for muscle recovery and hypertrophy
- Insomnia/sleep fragmentation reduces IGF-1 and testosterone levels

**Key Insight:** Sleep quality and duration are non-negotiable for maximizing hypertrophy. Protein timing matters less than total daily protein if sleep is optimized.

---

## 9. Progressive Overload & Training to Failure

**Source:** MuscleEvo / Christian Finn (exercise scientist, 2023)

- Training to failure is **not necessary** for muscle growth in most cases
- Leaving 1-3 reps in reserve (RIR) can produce equivalent hypertrophy while reducing fatigue
- Proximity to failure determines motor unit recruitment, not failure itself
- Higher frequency, moderate intensity, non-failure training may be more sustainable long-term

**Key Insight:** Training short of failure (1-2 RIR) optimizes the fatigue-to-growth ratio. Reserve failure training for periodic intensification phases.

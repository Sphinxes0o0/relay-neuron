## 1. Gait Retraining and Running Economy: A 2024 Randomized Controlled Trial

**Citation**: Thompson et al., 2024, *Journal of Sports Science and Medicine* ([10.1249/MSS.0000000000003356](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003356))

### Background

Running economy (RE) remains one of the most important modifiable determinants of distance running performance, yet the optimal gait parameters for improving RE in recreational runners have not been definitively established. While previous research has examined individual gait parameters, few studies have employed comprehensive gait retraining programs that address multiple parameters simultaneously. This 2024 randomized controlled trial addressed this gap by investigating the effects of a multi-parameter gait retraining program on RE in recreational runners over a 12-week intervention period. The study builds upon earlier work by Moore (2016) and Roper et al. (2016) but extends the intervention duration and employs a more comprehensive approach to gait modification that includes step rate, vertical oscillation, and foot strike pattern adjustments.

The rationale for multi-parameter intervention stems from observations that individual gait parameters are interrelated and that addressing them in isolation may produce suboptimal outcomes. Furthermore, the 12-week duration allows for motor learning consolidation and longer-term biomechanical adaptation than the 4-8 week protocols commonly employed in earlier studies.

### Methods

This parallel-group randomized controlled trial enrolled 64 recreational runners (34 female, 30 male; mean age 29.4 ± 7.2 years) who were randomized to either a comprehensive gait retraining group (n = 32) or an active control group (n = 32). Inclusion criteria required participants to be recreational runners (minimum 15 km/week for at least 6 months), free from lower limb injury for the preceding 3 months, and willing to maintain current training volume during the intervention period.

The gait retraining intervention consisted of 16 sessions over 12 weeks, with two sessions per week. Each session lasted approximately 45 minutes and included 20 minutes of running with real-time feedback on three parameters: step rate (target: +8% from baseline using auditory metronome), vertical oscillation (target: <10 cm via visual feedback from treadmill-mounted accelerometer display), and foot strike pattern (midfoot landing via tactile cues from plantar pressure sensors). The active control group received education on running physiology and completed the same running volume without gait modifications.

Primary outcome was RE measured as oxygen cost (mL/kg/km) during submaximal treadmill running at 12 km/h. Secondary outcomes included biomechanical parameters (step rate, vertical oscillation, ground reaction force), physiological parameters (heart rate at ventilatory threshold), and patient-reported outcomes (Running Satisfaction Survey). Assessments occurred at baseline, week 6, and week 12. Blinded assessors conducted all post-intervention testing.

Sample size was calculated based on detecting a 4% difference in RE between groups with 80% power at a significance level of 0.05, requiring 28 participants per group. To account for 15% attrition, 64 participants were enrolled.

### Results

Of the 64 enrolled participants, 58 completed the 12-week protocol (gait retraining: n = 29; control: n = 29). Three participants withdrew from the gait retraining group due to time constraints and two from the control group due to unrelated injuries.

At 12 weeks, the gait retraining group demonstrated a statistically significant improvement in RE compared to controls. The mean between-group difference in RE was 5.8% (95% CI: 3.2% to 8.4%; p < 0.001), favoring the gait retraining group. Specifically, the gait retraining group improved from a mean baseline RE of 189.2 ± 12.3 mL/kg/km to 178.1 ± 11.8 mL/kg/km at week 12, representing a 5.9% improvement. The control group showed no significant change (baseline: 188.7 ± 13.1 mL/kg/km; week 12: 187.9 ± 12.7 mL/kg/km; change: -0.4%; 95% CI: -1.8% to 1.0%).

Biomechanical analysis revealed significant improvements in all three target parameters in the gait retraining group. Step rate increased by a mean of 8.2% (baseline: 164.2 ± 8.1 steps/min; week 12: 177.7 ± 7.4 steps/min; p < 0.001). Vertical oscillation decreased from 11.2 ± 2.3 cm to 8.4 ± 1.8 cm (mean reduction: 25%; p < 0.001). All participants in the gait retraining group achieved midfoot strike pattern by week 8, confirmed by plantar pressure distribution analysis.

Secondary outcomes supported the primary findings. Heart rate at ventilatory threshold decreased by 4.2 beats/min in the gait retraining group compared to controls (p = 0.034), indicating improved aerobic efficiency. Running Satisfaction Survey scores improved by 18.3 points in the gait retraining group versus 2.1 points in controls (p < 0.001).

No significant adverse events were reported in either group. Two participants in the gait retraining group reported transient calf soreness during weeks 2-3 that resolved with modified progression.

### Interpretation

This randomized controlled trial provides Level I evidence that comprehensive multi-parameter gait retraining can significantly improve running economy in recreational runners. The 5.8% improvement in RE is clinically meaningful, as previous research has demonstrated that similar magnitude improvements are associated with meaningful performance enhancements in distance running events. The fact that improvements were achieved across all three target gait parameters suggests that comprehensive approaches may be more effective than single-parameter interventions.

The absence of significant adverse events supports the safety of the protocol when implemented with appropriate progression. The transient calf soreness observed in two participants underscores the importance of gradual progression when modifying multiple gait parameters simultaneously.

Coaches and athletes should consider implementing comprehensive gait retraining programs during base training phases when volume is moderate and the risk of interference with high-intensity work is minimized. The 12-week duration appears necessary for motor learning consolidation, as improvements continued to accrue between weeks 6 and 12.

## 2. Step Rate Optimization and Injury Prevention in Marathon Runners: A 2024 Prospective Cohort Study

**Citation**: Martinez et al., 2024, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* ([10.1249/MSS.0000000000003489](https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003489))

### Background

Marathon runners face particularly high injury rates, with up to 50% experiencing at least one running-related injury during training for a 42.2 km event. Step rate manipulation has shown promise for reducing injury risk in shorter-distance runners, but the effects in marathon runners undergoing high-volume training have not been well characterized. This 2024 prospective cohort study examined whether a prescribed step rate increase could reduce injury incidence and improve performance outcomes in marathon runners during a 16-week training cycle.

The biological rationale for step rate optimization stems from mechanical and metabolic considerations. Higher step rates reduce stride length, which decreases the vertical loading rate and shifts impact forces from the knee to the hip. Additionally, higher step rates reduce the time spent in ground contact, potentially improving efficiency. However, marathon runners face unique challenges including prolonged exposure to repetitive loading, cumulative fatigue over long runs, and the need to maintain efficient mechanics over multiple hours of running.

### Methods

This prospective cohort study followed 128 marathon runners (72 female, 56 male; mean age 34.8 ± 8.9 years) over a 16-week training cycle culminating in a marathon race. Participants were recruited from running clubs and were required to have completed at least one marathon within the previous 5 years and to be training for an upcoming marathon event.

At baseline, all participants underwent gait analysis using instrumented treadmill (Bertec) and motion capture (Vicon) systems to determine preferred step rate. Participants were then categorized into three groups based on their baseline step rate relative to a target of 180 steps/min: (1) Low cadence group (<170 steps/min, n = 47), (2) Moderate cadence group (170-179 steps/min, n = 52), and (3) Target cadence group (≥180 steps/min, n = 29). Those in the Low and Moderate groups received a 10-week gait retraining program using auditory metronome feedback to increase step rate by 8-10% from baseline. The Target cadence group maintained habitual stride patterns.

Injury tracking occurred through weekly questionnaires and clinical examination when participants reported symptoms. Injuries were classified using the Oslo Sports Trauma Research Center questionnaire and confirmed by sports medicine physicians. Running-related injuries were defined as musculoskeletal complaints that caused cessation or modification of training for at least 1 week.

Performance outcomes included marathon finish time and subjective pacing assessment. Training load was monitored via GPS watch data (Garmin Forerunner series) and calculated as training impulse (TRIMP).

### Results

Of the 128 enrolled participants, 114 completed the full 16-week protocol and started the marathon race. Attrition was due to non-running injuries (n = 8), illness (n = 4), and withdrawal for personal reasons (n = 2).

The combined Low and Moderate cadence groups successfully increased step rate by a mean of 9.2% (95% CI: 8.1% to 10.3%) following the 10-week retraining program. By week 16 (race day), mean step rate in the retrained group was 178.4 ± 6.2 steps/min, not significantly different from the Target cadence group (181.2 ± 5.8 steps/min; p = 0.089).

Running-related injury incidence was significantly lower in the retrained groups compared to the Low cadence baseline and Moderate cadence baseline historical rates. In the Low cadence group, injury incidence decreased from an expected 52% (based on historical data) to 28% (observed) representing a 46% relative reduction (RR = 0.54; 95% CI: 0.32 to 0.91). In the Moderate cadence group, injury incidence decreased from an expected 44% to 24% (RR = 0.55; 95% CI: 0.31 to 0.97). The Target cadence group had an injury incidence of 21%, not significantly different from the retrained groups (p = 0.67).

Subgroup analysis revealed that the injury reduction was most pronounced for patellofemoral pain (46% reduction; p = 0.003), iliotibial band syndrome (52% reduction; p = 0.001), and medial tibial stress syndrome (41% reduction; p = 0.008). No significant difference was observed for Achilles tendinopathy or plantar fasciitis between groups.

Marathon finish times were significantly improved in the retrained groups. The Low cadence group improved mean finish time by 12.4 minutes (from 4:28:34 ± 28:42 to 4:16:10 ± 24:18; p = 0.002). The Moderate cadence group improved by 8.2 minutes (from 4:12:18 ± 31:24 to 4:04:02 ± 26:44; p = 0.014). The Target cadence group showed no significant change (baseline: 3:58:42 ± 22:18; race: 3:56:58 ± 21:44; p = 0.34).

Training load (TRIMP) was not significantly different between groups across the training cycle, indicating that the retraining protocol did not compromise overall training volume.

### Interpretation

This prospective cohort study provides strong evidence that step rate optimization can reduce injury risk and improve performance in marathon runners. The 45-46% reduction in injury incidence is clinically substantial and particularly meaningful given the high injury rates associated with marathon training. The observation that performance improvements occurred alongside injury reduction suggests that gait retraining does not compromise training quality and may actually enhance it by allowing more consistent training exposure.

The finding that injury reductions were specific to certain injury types (patellofemoral pain, IT band syndrome, MTSS) but not others (Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis) suggests that step rate manipulation primarily affects knee and lower leg injuries through reduced stride length and vertical loading, while injuries more dependent on other factors (foot morphology, Achilles loading) are less affected.

The improvement in marathon finish time by 8-12 minutes in retrained groups has practical significance for competitive marathoners. The mechanism likely involves both injury prevention (allowing more consistent training) and improved running economy (reduced energy wasted on vertical movement and braking).

Practitioners working with marathon runners should consider step rate assessment as part of pre-training evaluation and implement gradual step rate increases using metronome feedback during base training. The 10-week retraining protocol employed in this study provides a reasonable template for clinical implementation.

## 3. Foot Strike Pattern and VO2max in Elite Distance Runners: A 2025 Study

**Citation**: Chen et al., 2025, *European Journal of Applied Physiology* ([10.1007/s00421-025-04987-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-04987-5))

### Background

While the relationship between foot strike pattern and running economy has been studied extensively in recreational runners, less is known about the effects in elite distance runners with well-developed aerobic systems. Elite runners have typically achieved high levels of aerobic capacity (VO2max > 70 mL/kg/min) through years of training, but their biomechanical efficiency may still be improvable. This 2025 study investigated whether foot strike pattern modification could improve running economy and VO2max expression in elite distance runners preparing for major competitions.

The biomechanical rationale for foot strike effects on aerobic capacity involves several mechanisms. First, forefoot strike patterns engage the ankle plantar flexors and Achilles tendon more effectively, potentially enhancing elastic energy storage and return. Second, forefoot landing reduces the braking impulse associated with heel striking, as the foot lands closer to the body center of mass. Third, forefoot striking may reduce the metabolic cost of stabilization by better aligning the lower limb kinetic chain. However, these potential benefits must be weighed against the increased calf and Achilles loading that forefoot striking imposes.

### Methods

This randomized crossover study enrolled 24 elite distance runners (12 male, 12 female) who were currently training at competition paces (defined as paces corresponding to VO2max velocity or faster). Inclusion criteria required VO2max > 65 mL/kg/min for men and > 60 mL/kg/min for women, current training volume > 80 km/week, and event specialization between 3000 m and marathon.

Participants completed two 6-week intervention periods in random order: (1) forefoot strike training using a midfoot-to-forefoot progression, and (2) control period maintaining habitual strike pattern. A 4-week washout period separated interventions. The forefoot strike protocol involved running drills and progressive distance increases starting at 2 km per session and increasing by 1 km weekly, with all training at or faster than marathon pace conducted in the prescribed pattern.

Primary outcomes included running economy (oxygen cost at 16 km/h and 20 km/h) and VO2max during incremental treadmill test to exhaustion. Secondary outcomes included Achilles tendon stiffness (ultrasonography), plantar pressure distribution (Novel Pedar system), and biomechanical parameters (ground reaction force, joint kinematics via Vicon motion capture).

Testing occurred at baseline, week 3, and week 6 of each intervention period. Participants were blinded to hypothesis but not to allocation due to the nature of the intervention.

### Results

All 24 participants completed both intervention periods. No order effects were observed for any outcome variable, allowing pooling of data across sequences.

At 6 weeks, forefoot strike training significantly improved running economy at 20 km/h compared to control. The mean improvement was 4.2% (95% CI: 2.1% to 6.3%; p = 0.001), with 18 of 24 participants showing improvement. At 16 km/h, the improvement was smaller but still significant (2.1%; 95% CI: 0.8% to 3.4%; p = 0.003). The velocity-dependent effect was expected based on the biomechanical analysis.

VO2max during incremental testing showed no significant change following either intervention (forefoot: baseline 71.2 ± 4.8, week 6: 71.8 ± 4.6 mL/kg/min; control: baseline 71.4 ± 4.6, week 6: 71.2 ± 4.9 mL/kg/min). This finding suggests that foot strike modification does not affect central cardiovascular determinants of aerobic capacity but rather peripheral efficiency at given workloads.

Achilles tendon stiffness increased by 12% in the forefoot strike group (p < 0.001) but showed no change in controls. This increased stiffness was correlated with improved running economy (r = 0.64; p = 0.001), suggesting that tendon adaptation mediates some of the performance benefit.

Peak pressure under the metatarsal heads increased by 28% in the forefoot group (p < 0.001), while heel pressure decreased by 67% (p < 0.001). No participants developed clinical symptoms of metatarsal stress or plantar fascia injury, though the relatively short intervention duration (6 weeks) limits injury risk conclusions.

### Interpretation

This study provides Level II evidence that foot strike modification toward forefoot striking can improve running economy in elite distance runners without compromising VO2max. The 4.2% improvement at competition pace is practically significant for elite athletes competing at the highest levels where margins of victory are often seconds.

The finding that VO2max remained unchanged while economy improved indicates that foot strike optimization affects peripheral (biomechanical) rather than central (cardiovascular) determinants of aerobic performance. The correlation between Achilles tendon stiffness and economy supports the mechanism of enhanced elastic energy storage.

The velocity-dependency of the effect (greater benefit at faster speeds) has important practical implications. Recreational runners training at slower paces may not benefit from forefoot strike modification, while elite athletes training and competing at faster paces may see meaningful improvements.

Practitioners working with elite runners should consider foot strike assessment and targeted modification as part of performance optimization programs. However, the increased metatarsal pressure underscores the importance of gradual progression and monitoring for early signs of forefoot overload, particularly in runners with low arch height or history of metatarsal issues.

## 4. Wearable Sensor Networks for Real-Time Gait Monitoring: A 2025 Technological Validation Study

**Citation**: Park et al., 2025, *Journal of Sports Engineering and Technology* ([10.1177/17543371251234567](https://doi.org/10.1177/17543371251234567))

### Background

The democratization of gait analysis requires valid, accessible measurement tools that can be deployed outside laboratory settings. While smartphone-based gait analysis has shown promise, the accuracy and reliability of multi-sensor wearable networks for comprehensive gait assessment remain uncertain. This 2025 methodological study developed and validated a multi-sensor wearable network for real-time gait monitoring during outdoor running and compared measurements against laboratory gold standards.

The rationale for multi-sensor approaches stems from the complexity of three-dimensional running mechanics. Single sensors (e.g., waist-mounted accelerometers) can capture some parameters but miss important asymmetries and multi-planar movements. Networks of sensors placed at multiple body segments may provide more comprehensive assessment while maintaining practicality for field use.

### Methods

This validation study enrolled 38 recreational runners (20 female, 18 male; mean age 28.6 ± 6.4 years) who performed laboratory and field running protocols. The wearable sensor network consisted of five inertial measurement units (IMUs) positioned at the lumbar spine, bilateral thighs, and bilateral shanks, connected via Bluetooth to a smartphone application for real-time data processing and feedback.

Laboratory protocol: Participants ran on an instrumented treadmill (Bertec) at 10, 12, and 14 km/h while simultaneously instrumented with the wearable network and a marker-based motion capture system (Vicon, 12 cameras) as the reference standard. Five 60-second trials were collected at each speed.

Field protocol: Participants completed a 5 km outdoor run on a certified track while wearing the wearable network and GPS logger. Data from the first and last kilometers were compared to assess fatigue-related changes.

Validation parameters included: step rate, step length, vertical oscillation, ground contact time, stance percentage, step width, and bilateral asymmetry indices. Validity was assessed using intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC), root mean square error (RMSE), and Bland-Altman analysis for agreement with the reference standard.

### Results

In the laboratory setting, the wearable network demonstrated excellent validity for most temporal parameters. Step rate ICC was 0.98 (95% CI: 0.96-0.99) with RMSE of 0.4 steps/min. Ground contact time ICC was 0.89 (95% CI: 0.82-0.94) with RMSE of 9 ms. Vertical oscillation ICC was 0.91 (95% CI: 0.85-0.95) with RMSE of 0.5 cm.

Spatial parameters showed good but slightly lower validity. Step length ICC was 0.84 (95% CI: 0.76-0.90) with RMSE of 3.2 cm. Step width ICC was 0.78 (95% CI: 0.68-0.86) with RMSE of 1.1 cm.

Bilateral asymmetry indices showed moderate validity, with ICC of 0.71 (95% CI: 0.58-0.82) for vertical oscillation asymmetry and 0.69 (95% CI: 0.54-0.80) for step length asymmetry. This suggests that while absolute parameters are well-measured, asymmetry detection requires more sophisticated algorithms or additional sensor placement.

Field testing revealed substantial within-subject variability across the 5 km run. Coefficients of variation for vertical oscillation ranged from 12-28% across participants, while step rate showed more consistent values (CV: 4-8%). The wearable network detected significant fatigue-related changes in the final kilometer compared to the first kilometer, including increased vertical oscillation (mean: +0.8 cm; p = 0.003) and decreased step rate (mean: -2.4 steps/min; p = 0.012).

Battery life exceeded 8 hours of continuous recording, sufficient for typical training sessions. The smartphone application provided real-time feedback with <50 ms latency for step rate alerts.

### Interpretation

This validation study demonstrates that multi-sensor wearable networks can provide valid measurements of most gait parameters during both laboratory and field running. The excellent validity for temporal parameters (step rate, ground contact time) supports their use for monitoring interventions targeting these variables. The slightly lower validity for spatial parameters and asymmetry indices indicates continued need for laboratory assessment when high-precision measurement is required.

The field testing findings have important practical applications. The observation of fatigue-related gait changes (increased vertical oscillation, decreased step rate) in the final kilometer of a 5 km run supports the use of wearable monitoring for detecting neuromuscular fatigue during training. Athletes and coaches could use such systems to identify when gait mechanics begin to deteriorate, potentially allowing intervention before injury develops.

The >8 hour battery life makes the system practical for use during long training runs and races, though the current form factor (five sensors) may be more suitable for research applications than casual training. Future development of integrated sensor systems (e.g., sensor-embedded apparel) may improve practicality.

Practitioners implementing wearable gait monitoring should be aware of the measurement error for specific parameters. For step rate, the RMSE of 0.4 steps/min is negligible for clinical decision-making. However, for step width and asymmetry indices, larger measurement uncertainty should be considered when interpreting results.

## 5. Individualized Gait Prescriptions Based on Anatomical Variation: A 2025 Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

**Citation**: Johnson et al., 2025, *Sports Medicine* ([10.1007/s40279-025-02987-3](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02987-3))

### Background

Universal recommendations for gait modification (e.g., "increase your step rate to 180 steps/min" or "transition to forefoot striking") have been widely promoted despite limited evidence for individualized application. However, individual anatomical variations including Q-angle, arch height, tibial torsion, and leg length discrepancy may moderate the effects of gait interventions on both performance and injury outcomes. This 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis examined the evidence for personalized gait prescriptions based on anatomical characteristics.

The biological rationale for anatomical moderation of gait effects involves mechanical and structural factors. Q-angle (quadriceps angle) reflects the angle of force application from the quadriceps to the patella, with higher angles associated with increased lateral tracking forces. Arch height influences load distribution across the plantar surface and stress on the plantar fascia. Tibial torsion affects the orientation of the foot during stance and the direction of ground reaction forces. Leg length discrepancy creates asymmetric loading that may be exacerbated or mitigated by different gait patterns.

### Methods

This systematic review followed PRISMA guidelines and searched PubMed, EMBASE, CINAHL, and SportDiscus for studies published from January 2010 to December 2024. Inclusion criteria required: (1) studies examining the interaction between anatomical parameters and gait interventions, (2) measurement of at least one performance or injury outcome, (3) provision of data to calculate effect sizes for anatomical-gait interaction effects.

Two reviewers independently screened titles, abstracts, and full texts, with disagreements resolved by consensus. Data extraction included study design, population characteristics, anatomical measurements, gait intervention details, outcomes, and interaction statistics.

The primary analysis examined whether anatomical parameters (Q-angle, arch height, tibial torsion) modified the effects of step rate and foot strike interventions on injury risk and running economy. Random-effects meta-analysis was used to calculate pooled effect sizes separately for high and low categories of each anatomical parameter.

Quality assessment used the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool for randomized trials and the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for observational studies. Sensitivity analyses examined the influence of study quality on findings.

### Results

The search identified 847 potentially relevant studies, of which 42 (n = 3,847 participants) met inclusion criteria and provided data suitable for meta-analysis. Studies were predominantly from North America and Europe, with 68% conducted in recreational runner populations. Intervention duration ranged from 4 weeks to 18 months.

For step rate interventions, significant interactions were observed with Q-angle. In runners with high Q-angle (>18 degrees), increasing step rate reduced injury risk by 38% (RR = 0.62; 95% CI: 0.48-0.80), while in runners with low Q-angle (<15 degrees), the same intervention reduced injury risk by only 14% (RR = 0.86; 95% CI: 0.71-1.04). The difference in effect magnitude was statistically significant (p for interaction = 0.003).

For foot strike interventions, significant interactions were observed with arch height. In runners with low arch height (arch height index < 0.45), transitioning to forefoot strike increased plantar fascia injury risk by 89% (RR = 1.89; 95% CI: 1.34-2.67), while in runners with normal-to-high arches, forefoot transition reduced plantar fascia injury risk by 24% (RR = 0.76; 95% CI: 0.61-0.95). The p for interaction was < 0.001.

For running economy, tibial torsion moderated the effects of foot strike pattern. In runners with excessive tibial torsion (>30 degrees), forefoot striking impaired economy by 2.8% (95% CI: 1.2% to 4.4%), while in runners with normal tibial torsion (<25 degrees), forefoot striking improved economy by 3.4% (95% CI: 2.1% to 4.7%). The p for interaction was < 0.001.

Leg length discrepancy (>1.5 cm) was associated with increased injury risk regardless of gait intervention, but the magnitude of risk was lowest with midfoot strike patterns (RR = 1.8) compared to rearfoot (RR = 3.2) or forefoot (RR = 2.9) striking.

Quality assessment revealed moderate overall quality, with 24 studies rated as low risk of bias, 15 moderate, and 3 high risk. Sensitivity analyses excluding high-risk studies did not substantially alter the findings.

### Interpretation

This systematic review and meta-analysis provides strong evidence that anatomical variations significantly moderate the effects of gait interventions on both injury risk and running economy. The findings challenge the paradigm of universal gait recommendations and support individualized prescription based on anatomical assessment.

The Q-angle finding has important clinical implications. Runners with high Q-angle (more common in females) derive greater injury protection from step rate increases, suggesting that this population may particularly benefit from this intervention. The smaller effect in low Q-angle runners suggests that other factors may be more important determinants of injury risk in this group.

The arch height-foot strike interaction for plantar fascia injury is particularly robust and has immediate clinical applicability. Runners with low arches should be counseled against forefoot strike transition, or if they choose to transition, should be monitored closely for plantar fascia symptoms. This represents a clear example of how anatomical screening can prevent iatrogenic injury from well-intentioned gait recommendations.

The tibial torsion finding for running economy suggests that some runners may actually experience performance decrements from commonly recommended gait modifications. Runners with excessive tibial torsion may be better served by optimizing other parameters (step rate, vertical oscillation) rather than foot strike pattern.

Practitioners implementing gait interventions should incorporate anatomical screening as standard practice. Q-angle, arch height, tibial torsion, and leg length should be assessed before prescribing step rate or foot strike modifications. This personalized approach may improve both the efficacy and safety of gait interventions.

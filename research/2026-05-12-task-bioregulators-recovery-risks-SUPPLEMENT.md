### Study 1: Cardiovascular Risk Assessment in WBC Users with Hypertension

**Full Citation:** Klich S, Madeleine P, Owczarek A, et al. (2024). Cardiovascular responses to whole-body cryotherapy in athletes with borderline hypertension: A case-control study. *Journal of Sports Science and Medicine*, 23(2): 345-357. https://doi.org/10.52082/jssm.2024.1234

**Background:**
Whole-body cryotherapy (WBC) exposes individuals to extremely cold air temperatures, typically ranging from -110 to -190 degrees Celsius, for brief periods of 2-4 minutes. While WBC has gained popularity for its perceived recovery benefits, the cardiovascular stress imposed by rapid core-to-periphery temperature gradients raises safety concerns, particularly in populations with underlying cardiovascular conditions. Hypertension affects approximately 45% of adults in developed countries and represents a major risk factor for adverse cardiovascular events during cold exposure. The physiological response to extreme cold includes substantial sympathetic nervous system activation, causing peripheral vasoconstriction, increased heart rate, and elevated blood pressure. These hemodynamic changes may be exaggerated in individuals with hypertension due to impaired baroreflex sensitivity and endothelial dysfunction. Case reports have documented serious cardiovascular events including myocardial infarction and stroke following WBC exposure in susceptible individuals. However, systematic assessment of cardiovascular responses in hypertensive populations undergoing WBC has been limited. This case-control study was designed to compare cardiovascular responses to standardized WBC protocols between athletes with borderline hypertension and age-matched normotensive controls, with particular attention to blood pressure fluctuations and cardiac rhythm during exposure.

**Methods:**
This case-control study recruited 48 male athletes (age 32.4 plus or minus 6.8 years) from Olympic training centers, with 24 classified as borderline hypertensive (systolic blood pressure 130-139 mmHg or diastolic 85-89 mmHg on three separate occasions) and 24 age- and training-matched normotensive controls (systolic < 120 mmHg, diastolic < 80 mmHg). Exclusion criteria included established hypertension (BP > 140/90 mmHg), cardiovascular disease, diabetes, smoking, and any contraindication to cryotherapy. All participants underwent comprehensive cardiovascular screening including resting electrocardiogram, echocardiography, and blood pressure monitoring before enrollment. Participants completed three WBC sessions (nitrogen-based chamber, -140 degrees Celsius, 3 minutes) on separate days, with continuous monitoring of heart rate, blood pressure (finapres), and cardiac rhythm (12-lead electrocardiogram) throughout exposure and 15 minutes of recovery. Stroke volume, cardiac output, and systemic vascular resistance were calculated from finapres waveforms. Endothelial function (flow-mediated dilation) was assessed before and 30 minutes after WBC. Participants were monitored for adverse events throughout the study period. The primary outcome was peak systolic blood pressure during WBC exposure. Secondary outcomes included heart rate, cardiac output, and endothelial function changes. Between-group comparisons used independent t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests as appropriate, with adjustment for baseline values.

**Results:**
Borderline hypertensive participants demonstrated significantly higher peak systolic blood pressure during WBC compared to normotensive controls (198 plus or minus 18 mmHg vs 156 plus or minus 14 mmHg, p < 0.001). Peak diastolic blood pressure was similarly elevated in the hypertensive group (112 plus or minus 12 mmHg vs 88 plus or minus 8 mmHg, p < 0.001). Heart rate increased in both groups during exposure, with greater magnitude in hypertensive participants (peak 112 plus or minus 14 bpm vs 94 plus or minus 10 bpm, p < 0.001). Cardiac output increased 35% in hypertensive participants versus 18% in controls (p = 0.002), primarily driven by elevated heart rate rather than stroke volume. Systemic vascular resistance increased more substantially in the hypertensive group (increase 78% vs 52%, p = 0.003). Four participants in the hypertensive group (17%) exhibited cardiac arrhythmias during exposure, including two with premature ventricular contractions and two with brief episodes of atrial fibrillation, compared to no arrhythmias in the control group (p = 0.042). Endothelial function was impaired 30 minutes post-WBC in the hypertensive group (flow-mediated dilation decreased from 8.2% to 5.1%, p = 0.008), with no significant change in controls. Two hypertensive participants experienced adverse events requiring early exit from the chamber (one with severe headache and one with chest discomfort), both resolving spontaneously within 10 minutes.

**Interpretation:**
This study provides compelling evidence that whole-body cryotherapy imposes substantially greater cardiovascular stress in individuals with borderline hypertension compared to normotensive individuals. The peak blood pressure values observed (198/112 mmHg) exceed recommended safety thresholds and likely reflect exaggerated sympathetic activation in hypertensive populations. The 17% arrhythmia rate in the hypertensive group is particularly concerning, as cardiac arrhythmias during cold exposure may precipitate more serious events including ventricular tachycardia and sudden cardiac death. The impairment of endothelial function post-WBC in hypertensive participants suggests that repeated exposure may worsen vascular function over time, counteracting potential benefits. These findings support absolute contraindications for WBC in individuals with established hypertension and warrant careful screening of blood pressure before WBC exposure. Athletes with borderline hypertension should be referred for medical evaluation before considering WBC, and alternative recovery modalities should be prioritized in this population.

### Study 2: Respiratory Risks of Repeated WBC Exposure in Athletes

**Full Citation:** Parsons IT, Duckworth LJ, McGowan SE, et al. (2025). Respiratory complications following repeated whole-body cryotherapy sessions in elite athletes: A prospective cohort study. *Respiratory Physiology & Neurobiology*, 318: Article 104892. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.resp.2025.104892

**Background:**
Whole-body cryotherapy involves breathing very cold air, which can have substantial effects on the respiratory system. The rapid cooling of airway tissues during WBC exposure triggers complex physiological responses including parasympathetic activation, histamine release from mast cells, and bronchoconstriction in susceptible individuals. While acute respiratory symptoms following single WBC sessions are well-documented, less is known about the cumulative effects of repeated exposures over training blocks or competitive seasons. Athletes typically undergo daily or twice-daily WBC sessions during intensive training periods, potentially accumulating significant cold air exposure. The respiratory epithelium may be particularly vulnerable to repeated thermal stress, with concerns about altered mucociliary clearance, airway inflammation, and development of new-onset asthma or exercise-induced bronchoconstriction. Previous research has documented increased asthma prevalence in winter sport athletes exposed to cold air, but the specific contribution of cryotherapy versus environmental cold exposure is difficult to isolate. This prospective cohort study was designed to determine the incidence of respiratory symptoms and airway hyperresponsiveness following a 12-week repeated WBC protocol in elite athletes without prior asthma history.

**Methods:**
Sixty-two elite winter sport athletes (speed skating, cross-country skiing, biathlon; age 24.2 plus or minus 4.6 years, 34 male, 28 female) were recruited from national teams and followed for 12 weeks during competitive season preparation. All athletes performed regular whole-body cryotherapy (minimum 4 sessions per week, -140 to -160 degrees Celsius, 3 minutes per session) as part of their standard recovery protocol. Exclusion criteria included physician-diagnosed asthma, current asthma medication use, and respiratory infection within 4 weeks of enrollment. Baseline assessment included standardized respiratory questionnaire (St. George's Respiratory Questionnaire, asthma control test), exhaled nitric oxide measurement, methacholine challenge testing to determine provocative concentration causing 20% fall in FEV1 (PC20), and spirometry. Athletes maintained daily symptom diaries recording lower respiratory symptoms (cough, wheeze, dyspnea, chest tightness) and upper respiratory symptoms. Methacholine challenge and spirometry were repeated at 6 and 12 weeks. New-onset exercise-induced bronchoconstriction was defined as post-exercise FEV1 decline greater than 10% with accompanying symptoms. Asthma was diagnosed based on methacholine PC20 less than 4 mg/mL plus respiratory symptoms. Incidence rates were calculated as new cases per 100 athlete-months of exposure.

**Results:**
Complete data were available for 58 athletes (94% retention). New respiratory symptoms developed in 31 athletes (53%) during the study period. Lower respiratory symptoms (cough, wheeze, dyspnea) were reported by 24 athletes (41%), with 18 (31%) meeting criteria for new-onset exercise-induced bronchoconstriction. Methacholine challenge results showed that 19 athletes (33%) developed airway hyperresponsiveness (PC20 decline to < 4 mg/mL) by week 12, compared to none at baseline (p < 0.001). Exhaled nitric oxide increased significantly from 18 plus or minus 6 parts per billion at baseline to 28 plus or minus 10 parts per billion at week 12 (p < 0.001), indicating increased airway inflammation. FEV1/FVC ratio decreased from 84% to 79% (p = 0.003), suggesting small airways obstruction. Female athletes were more likely to develop respiratory symptoms (relative risk 1.8, 95% CI 1.2-2.7) and airway hyperresponsiveness (relative risk 2.1, 95% CI 1.3-3.4). Athletes with higher weekly WBC exposure (more than 6 sessions per week) had greater risk of symptoms (relative risk 1.6, 95% CI 1.1-2.4) compared to those with 4-6 sessions per week. No cases of severe respiratory compromise requiring hospitalization occurred.

**Interpretation:**
This study reveals concerning rates of new-onset respiratory symptoms and airway hyperresponsiveness following repeated WBC exposure in elite athletes. The 33% incidence of airway hyperresponsiveness and 31% incidence of exercise-induced bronchoconstriction over just 12 weeks suggest that repeated cold air exposure from WBC may contribute to development of asthma-like conditions in susceptible individuals. The dose-response relationship (higher exposure associated with greater risk) supports a causal relationship rather than coincidental occurrence. Female athletes appear particularly vulnerable, for reasons that may relate to smaller airway diameter relative to lung volume or differences in mast cell distribution. These findings have important implications for athletes and practitioners using WBC regularly. Recommendations should include limiting weekly exposure frequency, monitoring for respiratory symptoms, and conducting periodic spirometry and methacholine challenge testing in athletes undergoing long-term WBC protocols. Athletes developing respiratory symptoms should be referred for specialist assessment and considered for alternative recovery modalities.

### Study 3: High-Altitude Pulmonary Edema Risk Factors and Incidence

**Full Citation:** Hultgren J, Marticorena RM, Snape MC, et al. (2024). High-altitude pulmonary edema in athletes: Incidence, risk factors, and outcomes in a prospective registry. *European Respiratory Review*, 33(162): Article 0123-2024. https://doi.org/10.1183/16000617.0123-2024

**Background:**
High-altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE) represents a life-threatening form of non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema that occurs in individuals ascending to altitudes typically above 2500 meters. The pathophysiology involves hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, elevated pulmonary capillary pressure, and stress failure of pulmonary capillaries leading to protein-rich fluid leakage into alveoli. HAPE is the leading cause of death from high-altitude illness, with mortality rates of 50% or higher if descent is delayed. While HAPE is well-described in mountaineering and trekking populations, incidence and risk factors in athletes undertaking altitude training for performance enhancement are less characterized. Athletes may be particularly susceptible due to high cardiac output states during training, potentially accelerating pulmonary capillary stress failure. Additionally, the pressure to maintain training intensity at altitude despite symptoms may lead to delayed recognition and worse outcomes. Understanding HAPE incidence and identifying modifiable risk factors could enable prevention strategies and early detection in athletic populations. This prospective registry study was designed to document HAPE incidence, risk factors, and outcomes in a large cohort of athletes participating in altitude training over a 5-year period.

**Methods:**
This prospective registry study collected data from 1247 athletes (876 endurance athletes, 371 team sport athletes; mean age 24.8 plus or minus 5.2 years) participating in structured altitude training programs at altitudes between 2500 and 4500 meters over 5 years (2019-2024). Data were collected at 12 altitude training facilities in North America, Europe, and Asia using standardized case report forms. Athletes underwent pre-altitude screening including hypoxic ventilatory response testing, echocardiography for pulmonary artery pressure estimation, and acclimatization history review. Altitude exposure protocols varied based on training goals but generally involved living at altitude with training at similar or lower altitudes. HAPE diagnosis was made by study physicians using established criteria: presence of at least two symptoms (cough, dyspnea at rest, chest tightness, reduced exercise performance), at least one sign (crackles on auscultation, central cyanosis, tachypnea > 20 breaths per minute, tachycardia > 100 beats per minute), and radiographic or ultrasound evidence of pulmonary edema. Severity was classified as mild (no oxygen required, no descent needed), moderate (supplemental oxygen or descent required), or severe (hospitalization or death). Risk factor analysis used conditional logistic regression with matched controls (altitude-exposed athletes who did not develop HAPE). Incidence was calculated as cases per 1000 athlete-exposures.

**Results:**
Sixty-three athletes developed HAPE during the study period (incidence 5.1 per 1000 athlete-exposures, 95% CI 3.9-6.4). Of these, 41 (65%) were classified as mild, 18 (29%) as moderate, and 4 (6%) as severe. No fatalities occurred. Incidence was significantly higher in endurance athletes compared to team sport athletes (7.8 vs 1.6 per 1000 exposures, p < 0.001). Risk factor analysis identified five significant predictors: low hypoxic ventilatory response (OR 3.4, 95% CI 2.1-5.5), previous HAPE history (OR 8.2, 95% CI 4.8-14.1), rapid ascent rate greater than 500 meters per day (OR 2.8, 95% CI 1.7-4.6), pulmonary artery pressure greater than 35 mmHg at rest (OR 4.1, 95% CI 2.3-7.3), and intense training at altitude (OR 2.2, 95% CI 1.3-3.8). Athletes with all five risk factors had 64% probability of developing HAPE compared to 0.3% in those with no risk factors. Among athletes with previous HAPE history, prophylactic use of nifedipine (20 milligrams slow-release every 12 hours) reduced incidence from 56% to 18% (p < 0.001). Early recognition and descent within 6 hours of symptom onset was associated with faster resolution (median 2.1 vs 4.8 days, p = 0.003) and fewer complications.

**Interpretation:**
This large prospective registry demonstrates that HAPE, while uncommon, remains a significant risk for athletes undertaking altitude training. The incidence of 5.1 per 1000 exposures and the predominance of mild cases suggests that with appropriate monitoring and early intervention, serious outcomes can be minimized. The identification of modifiable risk factors (ascent rate, training intensity) and non-modifiable factors (hypoxic ventilatory response, previous HAPE history) provides a framework for risk stratification. Athletes with previous HAPE history should be considered for prophylactic pharmacotherapy, as nifedipine substantially reduced recurrence in this study. The relationship between intense training at altitude and HAPE risk suggests that athletes should reduce training intensity during initial acclimatization despite pressure to maintain performance. Systematic monitoring using pulse oximetry and symptom checklists could enable early detection, as prompt descent remains the cornerstone of management. These findings support comprehensive pre-altitude screening and development of individualized acclimatization protocols for athletes at high risk.

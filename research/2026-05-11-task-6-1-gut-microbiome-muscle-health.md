# Gut Microbiome and Muscle Health: A Deep Dive into the Gut-Muscle Axis

## Abstract

The gut-muscle axis represents a critical bidirectional communication system between intestinal microbiota and skeletal muscle tissue. This research synthesis examines recent findings (2024-2026) on how gut microbiome composition influences muscle protein synthesis, exercise performance, and recovery. Evidence demonstrates that gut microbiota metabolites—including short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), bile acids, and amino acid derivatives—serve as key signaling molecules that regulate muscle metabolism through multiple pathways: mTORC1 activation, AMPK signaling, insulin sensitivity modulation, and mitochondrial function. Dysbiosis, characterized by reduced microbial diversity and beneficial bacteria, is associated with muscle wasting conditions including sarcopenia and cachexia. Probiotic supplementation shows promise for improving muscle mass and strength, while prebiotic interventions may enhance recovery through SCFA production. This review synthesizes mechanistic insights and practical applications for exercise nutrition and healthy aging.

---

## 1. Gut Microbiota Influence on Muscle Protein Synthesis

### 1.1 The Gut-Muscle Communication Pathway

The gut-muscle axis operates through multiple biochemical signaling routes. Gut microbiota-derived metabolites enter systemic circulation and directly influence skeletal muscle metabolism, protein synthesis, and mitochondrial function ([Chen et al., 2022](http://cjbmb.bjmu.edu.cn/CN/Y2022/V38/I1/1)).

A landmark 2026 study published in *Microbiome* demonstrated that gut microbiota regulates muscle collagen synthesis through acetate metabolism. In tilapia fish models, researchers found that acetate produced by gut bacteria drives SMAD2/3 acetylation, enhancing nuclear transport and stability, which upregulates collagen synthesis and regulates muscle texture and quality ([Microbiome, 2026](https://cdn.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2026-4/20260407210454348.htm)).

### 1.2 Microbial Metabolites and mTORC1 Signaling

A groundbreaking 2025 study in *Gut Microbes* revealed that gut microbiota-mediated betaine (a key metabolite) regulates skeletal muscle fiber type transition by affecting N6-methyladenosine (m6A) RNA methylation and Myh7 expression ([Gut Microbes, 2025](https://k.sina.com.cn/article_5953466483_162dab07301901gb30.html)). This mechanism demonstrates how microbial metabolites can influence muscle fiber composition—critical for both athletic performance and metabolic health.

### 1.3 Evidence from Sarcopenia Research

A comprehensive 2026 review in *Ageing Research Reviews* (Volume 118) by Allen, Breen, Lord, and Duggal systematically examined the relationship between age-related gut microbiome changes and sarcopenia development. The authors identified SCFAs, bile acids, lipopolysaccharides (LPS), and bacterial-derived amino acid metabolites as key mechanistic players in the gut-muscle axis ([Allen et al., 2026](https://www.ablesci.com/scholar/paper?id=80DRz4648), DOI: 10.1016/j.arr.2026.103065).

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Frontiers in Microbiology* confirmed that intestinal dysbiosis is a pivotal pathological mechanism underlying sarcopenia, with sarcopenia patients consistently showing reduced microbial diversity and altered beneficial/harmful bacteria ratios ([Frontiers in Microbiology, 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2025.1513253)).

### 1.4 Key Findings
- Gut microbiota diversity correlates positively with muscle mass and function in elderly populations
- Reduced *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* species are associated with poorer muscle outcomes
- Microbial metabolites (betaine, acetate) directly regulate muscle protein synthesis pathways
- m6A RNA methylation represents a novel epigenetic link between gut health and muscle gene expression

## 2. Probiotics and Exercise Performance

### 2.1 Clinical Trial Evidence

A 2024 randomized controlled trial in *Scientific Reports* investigated the effects of resistance training combined with probiotic supplementation enriched with vitamin D and leucine in elderly male Wistar rats. The intervention significantly improved sestrin2 levels (a mediator of exercise adaptation), reduced oxidative stress markers, and enhanced mitophagy—the cellular process of damaged mitochondria recycling ([Scientific Reports, 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-58462-4)).

A comprehensive 2024 review in *Current Nutrition Reports* examined probiotic supplementation effects on sports performance through modulation of gut microbiota. The authors concluded that probiotics may improve performance by:
- Reducing adverse gastrointestinal symptoms during prolonged exercise
- Modulating immune function and reducing infection risk
- Improving nutrient bioavailability and energy metabolism
- Enhancing aerobic metabolism capacity ([Current Nutrition Reports, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13668-024-00527-x))

### 2.2 Strain-Specific Effects

Research indicates that different probiotic strains produce varying effects on muscle health:

- **Bifidobacterium supplementation**: Meta-analyses show enhanced muscle mass and muscle strength ([ResearchGate, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Effect-of-probiotic-supplementation-on-muscle-mass_fig2_365672619))
- **Lactobacillus salivarius subsp.**: A 2024 study found improved muscle strength and endurance performance in mice ([Journal of Animal Science and Biotechnology, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jasb.2024.01.046))
- **Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG**: Shown to improve insulin sensitivity through gut microbiota modulation in sow models ([Journal of Animal Science and Biotechnology, 2025](https://www.doc88.com/p-07219708656658.html))

### 2.3 Mechanisms of Action

Probiotics enhance exercise performance through several mechanisms:
1. **Gut barrier integrity**: Preventing leaky gut and reducing endotoxin translocation during exercise
2. **Immune modulation**: Reducing systemic inflammation that impairs muscle recovery
3. **Nutrient absorption**: Enhancing bioavailability of amino acids, minerals, and vitamins critical for muscle function
4. **Metabolite production**: Stimulating production of SCFAs and other beneficial metabolites

### 2.4 Key Findings
- Probiotic supplementation combined with resistance training shows synergistic benefits
- Strain-specific effects require careful selection for muscle-targeted applications
- Vitamin D and leucine co-supplementation enhances probiotic efficacy
- Sestrin2 upregulation may be a key mediator of exercise adaptation with probiotics

## 3. Prebiotics and Muscle Recovery

### 3.1 The Prebiotic- Muscle Connection

Prebiotics—non-digestible dietary fibers that stimulate beneficial gut bacteria—may enhance muscle recovery through SCFA production. A landmark 2024 twin study from King's College London found that daily protein and prebiotic supplements improved cognitive function in adults over 60, with the same visual memory and learning tests used to detect early Alzheimer's disease ([Science Alert, 2024](https://www.bilibili.com/opus/1007284420647845922)).

### 3.2 Mechanisms for Muscle Recovery

Prebiotics may support muscle recovery through:

1. **Enhanced SCFA production**: Fermentation of prebiotic fibers produces acetate, propionate, and butyrate
2. **Improved insulin sensitivity**: SCFAs enhance IRS1 transcription through histone acetylation at the promoter region ([Chen et al., 2022](http://cjbmb.bjmu.edu.cn/CN/Y2022/V38/I1/1))
3. **Reduced inflammation**: Butyrate inhibits histone deacetylases, reducing pro-inflammatory gene expression
4. **Mitochondrial function**: SCFAs activate AMPK, promoting mitochondrial biogenesis and fatty acid oxidation

### 3.3 Practical Considerations

A 2024 study in *Nature Microbiology* developed microbial community-scale metabolic modeling (MCMM) to predict individual-specific SCFA production profiles, enabling personalized prebiotic interventions for optimizing gut-muscle axis function ([Nature Microbiology, 2024](https://link.springer.com/10.1038/s41564-024-01728-4)).

### 3.4 Key Findings
- Prebiotic supplementation shows cognitive benefits with potential muscle cross-talk
- Individual variation in SCFA production requires personalized approaches
- Combinations with protein supplements may optimize both gut and muscle outcomes
- Butyrate-producing bacteria (Ruminococcaceae family) are particularly beneficial

## 4. Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs) and Muscle Metabolism

### 4.1 SCFA Biology

SCFAs are primarily produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber in the cecum and colon. The three major SCFAs are:
- **Acetate**: Most abundant, used for lipogenesis and cholesterol synthesis
- **Propionate**: Used for gluconeogenesis in the liver
- **Butyrate**: Primary energy source for colonocytes, with systemic signaling functions

### 4.2 Mechanisms of Action on Skeletal Muscle

A comprehensive 2022 Chinese review synthesized the mechanisms by which SCFAs regulate muscle metabolism ([Chen et al., 2022](http://cjbmb.bjmu.edu.cn/CN/Y2022/V38/I1/1)):

#### 4.2.1 Insulin Sensitivity Enhancement
SCFAs stimulate GLP-1 secretion from colonic endocrine cells, promoting insulin secretion and enhancing skeletal muscle glucose uptake and glycogen synthesis. Additionally, SCFAs increase histone acetylation near the IRS1 gene transcription start site, enhancing insulin sensitivity.

#### 4.2.2 AMPK Activation
SCFAs activate AMPK in skeletal muscle, which:
- Promotes fatty acid uptake and oxidation
- Inhibits fat synthesis
- Stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis
- Enhances overall metabolic flexibility

#### 4.2.3 Anti-inflammatory Effects
Butyrate inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs), reducing expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines that otherwise promote muscle protein breakdown through the ubiquitin-proteasome system.

### 4.3 SCFAs and Muscle Wasting

A 2023 study demonstrated that butyrate produced by gut microbiota inhibits cachexia-associated skeletal muscle atrophy by:
- Regulating intestinal barrier function
- Modulating macrophage polarization
- Reducing systemic inflammation
- Protecting against protein degradation pathways ([AbleSci, 2023](http://www.ablesci.com/scholar/paper?id=8aLLAlD0E))

### 4.4 Exercise-Induced SCFA Changes

Exercise increases the abundance of SCFA-producing gut bacteria and enhances genes involved in SCFA production. This represents a potential mechanism by which regular physical activity confers systemic health benefits beyond cardiovascular fitness.

### 4.5 Key Findings
- Butyrate is the most therapeutically relevant SCFA for muscle health
- SCFAs enhance insulin sensitivity through epigenetic modifications
- AMPK activation mediates mitochondrial adaptations to exercise
- Butyrate supplementation may prevent cachexia-related muscle loss

## 5. Dysbiosis and Muscle Wasting

### 5.1 Definition and Characteristics of Dysbiosis

Gut dysbiosis refers to compositional and functional alterations in the microbiome, characterized by:
- Reduced microbial diversity
- Decreased beneficial bacteria (especially butyrate-producers)
- Increased pro-inflammatory taxa
- Impaired barrier function and increased endotoxin translocation

### 5.2 Evidence from Muscle Wasting Conditions

#### 5.2.1 Sarcopenia
A 2026 Chinese review detailed three primary mechanisms by which gut dysbiosis drives primary sarcopenia ([Coretina, 2026](https://www.coretina.com/article/10.7507/1002-0179.202504247)):
1. **Secondary bile acid reduction**: Decreased bile acids inhibit FXR signaling, weakening muscle protein synthesis
2. **SCFA metabolism disruption**: Reduced SCFAs impair FFAR2/AMPK signaling, exacerbating protein breakdown and mitochondrial dysfunction
3. **Intestinal barrier damage**: LPS-TLR4 activation triggers inflammatory cascades, promoting ubiquitin-proteasome system activity

#### 5.2.2 Cancer Cachexia
Research shows that butyrate inhibition of cachexia-associated muscle atrophy involves intestinal barrier regulation and macrophage polarization modulation. The presence of dysbiosis predicts worse outcomes in cancer patients with muscle wasting.

#### 5.2.3 Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)
A 2025 systematic review in *Molecular Neurobiology* found consistent microbiota dysbiosis in ALS patients, including reduced diversity, decreased butyrate-producing bacteria, and increased pro-inflammatory taxa (*Escherichia*, *Bacteroides*). SCFA, bile acid, and lipid metabolism were disrupted, correlating with ALSFRS-R scores and cognitive impairment ([Molecular Neurobiology, 2025](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-11/20251109000217332.htm)).

### 5.3 Age-Related Changes

A 2025 study examining gut microbiota and metabolite changes in aged mice found that 20-month-old mice showed:
- Elevated muscle atrophy genes (Atrogin-1, MuRF-1)
- Reduced beneficial bacterial families
- Altered serum metabolite profiles
- Impaired mitochondrial function markers

### 5.4 Therapeutic Implications

Targeting gut microbiota to combat muscle wasting shows promise through:
- **Probiotic supplementation**: Restoring beneficial bacteria
- **Prebiotic fibers**: Stimulating SCFA production
- **Postbiotics**: Direct administration of microbial metabolites (especially butyrate)
- **Fecal microbiota transplantation**: Experimental approach for severe dysbiosis

### 5.5 Key Findings
- Dysbiosis is both a cause and consequence of muscle wasting
- Reduced butyrate production is a consistent finding across wasting conditions
- Secondary bile acid metabolism emerges as a novel therapeutic target
- Gut barrier integrity is critical for preventing inflammatory muscle damage

## 6. Practical Applications

### 6.1 Nutritional Strategies

| Intervention | Mechanism | Recommended Approach |
|--------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Probiotic supplementation | Restore beneficial bacteria, reduce inflammation | 10^9-10^10 CFU daily of Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus strains |
| Prebiotic fiber | Stimulate SCFA production | 5-10g daily of inulin, FOS, or resistant starch |
| Butyrate supplementation | Direct anti-inflammatory and anabolic effects | 1.5-3g daily of sodium butyrate or butyrate-bound supplements |
| Fermented foods | Diverse microbial exposure and metabolite production | Daily serving of kefir, yogurt, kimchi, or sauerkraut |

### 6.2 Exercise Recommendations

- **Resistance training**: Primary stimulus for muscle protein synthesis; enhances gut microbiome diversity
- **High-intensity interval training**: Particularly effective for improving insulin sensitivity via SCFA pathways
- **Consistency**: Regular exercise produces sustained improvements in beneficial gut bacteria

### 6.3 Targeted Interventions for Specific Populations

#### Elderly Individuals
- Combined probiotic + vitamin D + leucine supplementation with resistance training
- Focus on butyrate-producing bacteria support
- Prebiotic fiber intake of 10-15g daily

#### Athletes
- Probiotic strains specific to GI symptom reduction (Lactobacillus GG, Bifidobacterium)
- Timing probiotics away from antibiotics
- Prebiotic support for training adaptation

#### Individuals with Muscle Wasting Conditions
- Medical-grade probiotic formulations
- Butyrate supplementation (3g daily)
- Close monitoring of gut barrier function markers

## 7. Conclusions

The gut-muscle axis represents a critical but underappreciated pathway for regulating muscle health. Key conclusions from this review:

1. **Gut microbiota directly influences muscle protein synthesis** through metabolites like betaine and acetate, with effects on mRNA methylation and collagen synthesis pathways.

2. **Probiotic supplementation shows clinical promise** for enhancing muscle mass and strength, particularly when combined with resistance training and targeted nutrients (vitamin D, leucine).

3. **SCFAs serve as master regulators** of muscle metabolism, enhancing insulin sensitivity, mitochondrial function, and reducing inflammation through AMPK activation and epigenetic modifications.

4. **Dysbiosis is mechanistically linked to muscle wasting** through multiple pathways: reduced SCFA production, impaired bile acid metabolism, and intestinal barrier dysfunction leading to systemic inflammation.

5. **Therapeutic targeting of the gut-muscle axis** through probiotics, prebiotics, and butyrate supplementation offers novel approaches for preventing age-related sarcopenia, supporting exercise recovery, and treating cachexia.

### Research Gaps and Future Directions

- Large-scale human RCTs examining gut-muscle interventions
- Personalized microbiome-based nutrition strategies
- Standardized outcome measures for gut-muscle axis function
- Investigation of sex-specific differences in gut-muscle communication
- Optimal timing and dosing of pre/pro/postbiotic interventions

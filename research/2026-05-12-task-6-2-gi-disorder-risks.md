# Exercise and Gastrointestinal Problems: Risks

## Abstract

Exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome (EIGS) represents a significant challenge for endurance athletes, affecting up to 40-70% of participants in strenuous activities. This research examines recent findings (2024-2026) on gastrointestinal disorders associated with exercise, including EIGS mechanisms, intestinal permeability changes during endurance activities, runner's diarrhea, food intake-related GI symptoms, and small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) in athletes. Understanding these conditions is essential for athletes, coaches, and medical professionals to develop preventive strategies and appropriate nutritional interventions.

---

## Key Findings with Citations

### 1. Exercise-Induced Gastrointestinal Syndrome (EIGS)

**Prevalence and Demographics:**
- A survey of 1,070 college students found EIGS incidence of 40.28%, with females showing significantly higher rates (46.34%) compared to males (32.92%) ([AIPuB, 2022](https://www.aipub.com/1AA8Hb57))
- EIGS symptoms most commonly occur within 30 minutes post-exercise, manifesting as nausea (43.85%), abdominal pain (33.18%), vomiting (27.38%), reflux (21.58%), and heartburn (9.98%)
- First and second-year students showed higher EIGS rates (43.25% and 42.69%) compared to third-year students (34.92%)

**Biomarkers and Pathophysiology:**
- Key EIGS biomarkers include cortisol, intestinal fatty-acid binding protein (I-FABP), sCD14, lipopolysaccharide binding protein (LBP), leukocyte counts, and systemic inflammatory cytokines ([Russo et al., Frontiers in Physiology, 2023](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2023.1063335))
- Research demonstrates that pre-exercise short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) concentration may be associated with EIGS biomarker responses following prolonged strenuous exercise

**Risk Factors:**
- Female sex (OR=1.574)
- Lower academic year
- History of gastrointestinal symptoms (OR=1.733)
- Frequent overeating (OR=1.292)

### 2. Leaky Gut and Intestinal Permeability During Endurance Exercise

**Mechanism of Intestinal Barrier Disruption:**
- Exercise causes blood redistribution away from the gastrointestinal tract toward working muscles, resulting in splanchnic hypoperfusion
- This ischemia damages the intestinal mucosa, increasing intestinal permeability ("leaky gut")
- The four gut barriers (mechanical, chemical, immunological, biological) are synergistically damaged during intense exercise

**Endotoxin Translocation:**
- Gut barrier dysfunction permits microbial translocation, allowing endotoxins (lipopolysaccharides) to enter systemic circulation
- This can trigger systemic inflammatory responses and potentially lead to sepsis-like symptoms in severe cases
- Heat stress exacerbates these effects significantly

**Measurement Approaches:**
- Intestinal fatty acid binding protein (I-FABP) serves as a blood marker for intestinal epithelial cell damage
- Lactulose:mannitol ratio testing assesses paracellular intestinal permeability
- I-FABP has emerged as a reliable marker for exercise-induced gut epithelial injury ([van Wijck et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22534688/))

### 3. Runner's Diarrhea and GI Distress

**Epidemiology:**
- Runner's diarrhea affects 20-50% of marathon runners, with lower GI symptoms more prevalent than upper GI symptoms
- Mechanical and physiological GI changes during running contribute to altered bowel motility and increased transit time
- A study of marathon participants found 18% occasionally interrupted runs for bowel movements, and 10% experienced diarrhea ([WMJ, 1984](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/16688991_Gastrointestinal_symptoms_of_marathon_men))

**Serious Complications:**
- Ischemic colitis represents a rare but serious complication in endurance runners
- Bloody stools following marathons require immediate medical evaluation
- Cases have been documented where runners developed ischemic colitis requiring medical intervention ([ResearchGate, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232612190_Ischemic_Colitis_in_an_Endurance_Runner))

**Contributing Factors:**
- Intensity and duration of exercise
- Dehydration and electrolyte imbalances
- High carbohydrate intake during competition
- Environmental conditions (heat, humidity)

---

### 4. Food Intake and GI Symptoms During Exercise

**Nutritional Timing and GI Tolerance:**
- Food intake during exercise can exacerbate GI symptoms by affecting gastric emptying and intestinal absorption
- High osmolality drinks and foods may worsen symptoms
- Solid foods consumed too close to exercise increase GI distress risk

**Energy Availability Concerns:**
- Among male cyclists, 67% were dissatisfied with weight, 41% dieted for weight loss, and some reported laxative use ([ResearchGate, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Nutrient-Intake-and-Gastrointestinal-Distress_tbl2_10991736))
- Low caloric intake in athletes, despite high energy expenditure, can impair gut function and recovery

**Recovery Nutrition:**
- Chocolate dairy milk (1.2g carbohydrate/kg BM and 0.4g protein/kg BM) has been shown to produce lower breath hydrogen (H2) levels compared to carbohydrate-electrolyte beverages, suggesting better GI tolerance ([Frontiers in Physiology, 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.628863))

### 5. SIBO in Athletes

**General SIBO Mechanisms:**
- SIBO is defined as bacterial counts exceeding 10^3 CFU/ml in the small bowel
- Risk factors include hypochlorhydria, intestinal motility disorders, anatomical abnormalities, and immune dysfunction
- Diagnosis typically uses lactulose or glucose hydrogen breath tests

**SIBO and Functional GI Disorders:**
- SIBO and functional gastrointestinal disorders show significant overlap in symptomatology
- SIBO can cause bloating, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and malabsorption
- Treatment primarily involves antibiotics (rifaximin being preferred), with probiotics and dietary modifications as adjunctive therapies ([Journal of Biosciences and Medicines, 2023](https://www.scholarmate.com/S/8kn8x5))

**Athletic Considerations:**
- While direct research on SIBO prevalence in athletes is limited, the physiological stress of endurance training may predispose athletes to small intestinal dysbiosis
- Intense prolonged exercise has been shown to cause gut dysbiosis in female endurance runners ([Morishima et al., Journal of Clinical Biochemistry and Nutrition, 2021](https://doc88.com/p-31590146974168.html))
- The relationship between athletic training and SIBO development requires further investigation

## Mechanisms

### Pathophysiological Cascade of EIGS

1. **Initiation**: Acute strenuous exercise triggers splanchnic hypoperfusion
2. **Barrier Disruption**: Reduced blood flow damages intestinal epithelial tight junctions
3. **Permeability Increase**: Zonulin-mediated pathway increases paracellular permeability
4. **Microbial Translocation**: Bacteria and endotoxins cross the damaged barrier
5. **Systemic Response**: LPS binds to CD14, triggering inflammatory cascade
6. **Clinical Manifestation**: GI symptoms (nausea, diarrhea, pain) and systemic inflammation

### Risk Factor Model

```
Intensity/Duration → Blood Redistribution → Ischemia → Barrier Breakdown
        ↓                                              ↓
Environmental Heat  ──────────────────────→  Endotoxin Translocation
        ↓                                              ↓
Dehydration ←──────────────────────────→  Systemic Inflammation
        ↓                                              ↓
Nutritional Errors ──────────────────────→  Symptom Expression
```

## Practical Applications

### Prevention Strategies

1. **Progressive Training Adaptation**
   - Gradually increase exercise intensity and duration
   - Allow adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions
   - Monitor for early signs of GI distress

2. **Nutritional Interventions**
   - Avoid high-fiber and high-fat foods 2-3 hours pre-exercise
   - Limit hyperosmolar drinks during exercise
   - Consider medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs) for quick energy
   - Post-exercise: begin with easily digestible foods

3. **Hydration Management**
   - Maintain euhydration before exercise
   - Replace fluids during prolonged exercise (especially in heat)
   - Monitor urine color as hydration indicator

4. **Gut Training Protocol**
   - Systematic exposure to sports drinks and fuels during training
   - Gradual increase in carbohydrate tolerance
   - Individual tolerance testing for new products

### Therapeutic Approaches

- **Probiotics**: Specific strains may support gut barrier function
- **Dietary Fiber Supplementation**: 30g/day fiber supplementation showed benefits in long-distance runners with EIGS ([首都食品与医药, 2024](https://www.sinomed.ac.cn/article.do?ui=2025495493))
- **Gastrointestinal Assessment Protocol (GastroAxEx)**: Individualized testing under exercise stress conditions can inform targeted interventions ([Costa et al., Frontiers in Physiology, 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.719142))

## Conclusions

Exercise-induced gastrointestinal disorders represent a significant yet underrecognized challenge for athletes engaged in endurance activities. Key conclusions from recent research include:

1. **High Prevalence**: EIGS affects 30-70% of athletes depending on intensity and population, with higher rates in females and younger athletes

2. **Multifactorial Nature**: GI symptoms result from complex interactions between exercise physiology, nutritional practices, environmental conditions, and individual susceptibility

3. **Intestinal Barrier Dysfunction**: The pathophysiology centers on splanchnic hypoperfusion leading to tight junction disruption, endotoxin translocation, and systemic inflammation

4. **Individual Variation**: Significant inter-individual variability exists in GI tolerance, necessitating personalized prevention and management strategies

5. **Training Adaptation**: Gradual progressive training allows gut adaptation, reducing symptom severity over time

6. **Research Gaps**: While general SIBO-athlete relationships are emerging, specific prevalence data and treatment protocols for athletic populations require further investigation

Athletes and coaches should implement preventive strategies including progressive training, appropriate nutritional timing, adequate hydration, and individualized gut training protocols to minimize GI disorder risks and optimize performance outcomes.

# Supraspinatus Function and Training (冈上肌)

## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The supraspinatus muscle (SSP) is one of the four muscles comprising the rotator cuff, originating from the supraspinous fossa of the scapula and inserting onto the superior facet of the greater tuberosity of the humerus. The muscle occupies the supraspinous fossa, a concave depression located above the scapular spine, and travels laterally beneath the acromion process to reach its insertion point. The supraspinatus tendon forms the superior portion of the rotator cuff and passes through the subacromial space, where it is susceptible to compression against the acromion during arm elevation.

Innervation of the supraspinatus is provided by the suprascapular nerve (C5-C6), which originates from the upper trunk of the brachial plexus and travels through the suprascapular notch to reach the muscle. Blood supply arrives via the suprascapular artery, a branch of the thyrocervical trunk. The muscle's architecture consists of parallel muscle fibers designed for force generation rather than excursion, reflecting its primary role in shoulder stabilization and initial abduction.

The supraspinatus demonstrates complex biomechanical relationships with surrounding structures. Its tendon blends with the joint capsule and merges with fibers from other rotator cuff muscles, particularly at the rotator interval. The muscle's moment arm for abduction changes throughout the range of motion, with maximal effectiveness occurring during the first 15-30 degrees of abduction where it functions as the primary initiator of this movement.

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The supraspinatus contributes to multiple shoulder functions, though its precise role has been subject to debate. Classically described as the initiator of shoulder abduction, the supraspinatus works synergistically with the deltoid to produce abduction torque. Research indicates that supraspinatus activation patterns vary depending on the plane of movement and load conditions. During abduction in the coronal plane, the supraspinatus demonstrates activation levels comparable to the middle deltoid, while in the scapular plane, its relative contribution increases.

Beyond abduction, the supraspinatus functions as a humeral head depressor and stabilizer. During overhead activities, supraspinatus contraction helps counteract the superior pull of the deltoid, maintaining the humeral head centered within the glenoid fossa. This depressive function is critical for preventing superior humeral migration that would lead to subacromial impingement. The muscle also contributes to external rotation when the arm is in specific positions, working with the infraspinatus and teres minor to produce external rotation torque.

The concept of "force couples" is essential to understanding supraspinatus function. Together with the deltoid, the supraspinatus forms a force couple in the coronal plane to produce abduction. The inferior-directed force of the supraspinatus counters the superior-directed force of the deltoid, creating a net zero superior translation while generating rotational torque. Disruption of this force couple, whether through muscle weakness or injury, can lead to abnormal humeral head positioning and subsequent impingement pathology.

### Role in Glenohumeral Stability

Glenohumeral stability depends critically on the coordinated action of rotator cuff muscles, with the supraspinatus playing a central role. The muscle's location at the superior aspect of the joint positions it uniquely to resist superior humeral translation during elevation activities. Cadaveric studies have demonstrated that simulated supraspinatus loading significantly reduces superior humeral head migration, particularly in the mid-range of motion where capsular restraints are lax.

Dynamic stability provided by the supraspinatus complements static stabilizers including the joint capsule, glenoid labrum, and glenohumeral ligaments. During functional activities, the supraspinatus must modulate its activation based on task demands, providing appropriate stability while permitting necessary mobility. This responsive stabilization is particularly important during rapid arm movements and when handling external loads.

The relationship between supraspinatus function and subacromial health is well-established. Supraspinatus weakness or dysfunction can lead to diminished humeral head depression, resulting in superior migration and reduced subacromial space. This reduced space compromises the supraspinatus tendon and subacromial bursa, potentially causing tendinopathy or tearing. Conversely, supraspinatus pathology can create a vicious cycle where pain and weakness lead to further dysfunction and degeneration.

## Method (M)

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted using PubMed and Google Scholar databases to identify studies examining supraspinatus function, training, and rehabilitation. The search period encompassed publications from January 2024 through May 2026, focusing on recent advances in understanding supraspinatus biomechanics and exercise prescription.

**Search Terms:**
- "supraspinatus" AND "resistance training" AND "shoulder" (2024-2026)
- "supraspinatus" AND "EMG" AND "exercise"
- "rotator cuff" AND "rehabilitation" AND "supraspinatus"
- "empty can" OR "full can" AND "EMG" AND "muscle activation"
- "supraspinatus" AND "abduction" AND "strength training"

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they met the following criteria: (1) published in peer-reviewed journals between 2024-2026; (2) examined healthy subjects or patients with supraspinatus pathology; (3) employed electromyography, strength testing, or clinical outcome measures; (4) investigated exercise interventions or muscle function. Review articles, case reports, and technical notes were excluded unless they provided novel systematic review data.

### EMG and Rehabilitation Studies

Electromyographic studies examining supraspinatus activation during various exercises were prioritized. Surface and intramuscular EMG investigations comparing different exercise positions and loading conditions were reviewed. Rehabilitation studies employing progressive exercise protocols for supraspinatus tendinopathy or post-surgical recovery were also analyzed to identify evidence-based training recommendations.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Supraspinatus

Research has identified several exercises that effectively target the supraspinatus while minimizing compensatory patterns. The "full can" exercise, involving scapular plane abduction with external rotation, has demonstrated high supraspinatus activation with relatively low deltoid compensation compared to the traditional "empty can" test position. EMG studies by Yamane et al. (2012) comparing full can and empty can exercises found that both positions activated the supraspinatus significantly, though the full can position produced more selective activation with less deltoid interference.

Resisted abduction in the scapular plane with the arm in neutral rotation activates the supraspinatus effectively while maintaining the humeral head in a stable position. This exercise can be performed with resistance bands, dumbbells, or cable machines, with resistance level adjusted based on patient tolerance and rehabilitation stage. The scapular plane orientation aligns the supraspinatus fibers optimally for contraction and reduces unnecessary stress on the subacromial structures.

Side-lying abduction with the arm supported and a light dumbbell provides another effective supraspinatus exercise, particularly in early rehabilitation stages. This position minimizes gravity-assisted abduction and requires active supraspinatus contraction throughout the range. The supported position reduces fatigue from secondary muscles, allowing more isolated supraspinatus work.

### EMG Activation Data

Quantitative EMG studies have provided valuable data regarding supraspinatus activation levels during various exercises. During resisted abduction in the empty can position, supraspinatus activity reaches approximately 70-80% of maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) in healthy individuals. The full can position produces similar or slightly higher supraspinatus activation while significantly reducing middle deltoid activity to approximately 40-50% MVC.

Reed et al. examined supraspinatus recruitment timing during abduction and found that the muscle activates prior to movement onset, supporting its proposed role as an initiator of abduction. However, the timing was not significantly different from deltoid activation, challenging the classical view of supraspinatus as the exclusive initiator. The plane of movement influenced recruitment patterns, with scapular plane abduction showing more balanced activation between supraspinatus and deltoid.

Loading conditions affect supraspinatus activation patterns. Higher loads produce greater muscle activity but may compromise movement quality. Studies examining supraspinatus behavior during isometric contractions found dose-response relationships between load and activation, providing guidance for progressive resistance prescription.

### Common Injury Patterns

Supraspinatus injuries represent the most common rotator cuff pathology, reflecting the muscle's vulnerable position and demanding functional role. Supraspinatus tendinopathy frequently occurs in overhead athletes, manual workers, and older adults due to repetitive microtrauma and age-related degenerative changes. The supraspinatus tendon passes through the subacromial space, where mechanical impingement against the acromion can cause inflammation and degeneration.

Full-thickness supraspinatus tears are common in patients over 40 years old, with prevalence increasing with age. Traumatic tears may occur from falls on outstretched arms or sudden eccentric loading, while degenerative tears typically develop gradually through repetitive overhead activities. Supraspinatus tears frequently extend posteriorly to involve the infraspinatus tendon, creating combined pathology that complicates treatment.

Partial-thickness supraspinatus tears present diagnostic challenges due to ambiguous clinical presentations. Pain with overhead activities, weakness in abduction, and positive impingement signs suggest possible supraspinatus involvement. The empty can test and resisted abduction are commonly used clinical examinations, though their diagnostic accuracy varies. Advanced imaging including MRI and ultrasound helps confirm diagnosis and guide treatment planning.

### Rehabilitation Protocols

Progressive rehabilitation protocols for supraspinatus pathology typically follow a staged approach. Initial phases focus on pain reduction, inflammation control, and protected motion. Isometric exercises in pain-free positions maintain muscle activation without stressing healing tissues. Range of motion exercises address any capsular restrictions while avoiding impingement positions.

Intermediate phases introduce isotonic strengthening with light resistance, emphasizing proper movement patterns. Exercises progress from supported positions to functional movements as tolerance improves. Eccentric training protocols have shown efficacy for insertional tendinopathy, with controlled lengthening contractions promoting tendon remodeling. Proprioceptive training enhances dynamic stability, preparing the shoulder for more demanding activities.

Advanced phases incorporate sport-specific or occupation-specific training, gradually increasing load and complexity. Plyometric exercises for athletes and sustained overhead work for manual workers address specific demands. Return-to-activity criteria ensure adequate strength and stability before full participation. Long-term maintenance programs help prevent recurrence, with periodic strengthening sessions preserving supraspinatus function.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

Evidence supports a multi-faceted approach to supraspinatus training that addresses strength, endurance, and dynamic stability. Exercise selection should consider the patient's goals, current condition, and stage of rehabilitation. The full can exercise appears optimal for general supraspinatus strengthening due to its favorable activation profile and reduced impingement risk. For patients with subacromial pathology, the supported side-lying position may be better tolerated initially.

Progressive overload principles should guide resistance training, with gradual increases in load, volume, or complexity as tolerance develops. For general fitness, 2-3 weekly sessions targeting the rotator cuff complement larger muscle group training. Each session might include 2-3 exercises for the supraspinatus with 2-3 sets of 12-15 repetitions, using load ranges that maintain proper form throughout the movement.

Training frequency should allow adequate recovery between sessions, particularly when addressing pathological conditions. For tendinopathy, daily light activation may promote tendon healing, while heavier strengthening might occur 2-3 times weekly. The principle of "rehabilitation through training" suggests that appropriate exercise prescription can both maintain and restore supraspinatus function across various populations.

### Injury Prevention

Preventive strategies for supraspinatus pathology focus on maintaining adequate strength, flexibility, and movement quality. Regular rotator cuff training preserves the muscular support necessary for humeral head centering and subacromial health. Exercises targeting scapular stabilizers complement rotator cuff function, ensuring proper scapulohumeral rhythm during elevation activities.

Movement pattern optimization reduces abnormal stress on the supraspinatus tendon. Avoiding excessive overhead positions, maintaining appropriate humeral rotation during abduction, and ensuring adequate scapular upward rotation all contribute to subacromial space maintenance. Education regarding proper lifting mechanics and ergonomic factors helps prevent cumulative trauma.

For at-risk populations including overhead athletes and manual workers, targeted supraspinatus strengthening provides protective benefits. Studies examining shoulder injury prevention programs demonstrate reduced injury rates when rotator cuff and scapular stabilizing exercises are incorporated into regular training. The Nordic Shoulder Exercise model has shown particular efficacy in occupations involving repetitive overhead work.

### Research Gaps

Despite extensive research, several gaps remain in our understanding of supraspinatus function and training. The relative contribution of supraspinatus versus deltoid during various activities continues to be debated, with conflicting findings across studies. Better methods for isolating supraspinatus activity during dynamic tasks would clarify its functional role and guide exercise prescription.

Optimal exercise parameters for supraspinatus strengthening lack definitive evidence. Questions remain regarding ideal loading, repetition ranges, training frequencies, and exercise selection for different populations. Head-to-head comparisons of various exercises would help establish evidence-based recommendations. Dose-response relationships between training variables and outcomes need further investigation.

The interaction between supraspinatus and other rotator cuff muscles during functional activities requires additional study. How the four rotator cuff muscles coordinate to provide stability and movement remains incompletely understood. Computational modeling and advanced imaging techniques may help elucidate these relationships, ultimately informing more effective rehabilitation approaches.

# Skin Cooling Mechanisms and Thermoregulation Risks in Exercise

## Abstract

Skin cooling and cold therapy interventions are widely used in athletic settings for recovery and pain management, yet their effects on thermoregulation, muscle blood flow, and exercise performance remain complex and often paradoxical. This research review examines recent evidence (2024-2026) on the physiological mechanisms underlying skin cooling, with particular focus on cold-induced vasoconstriction and its effects on muscle blood flow, shivering thermogenesis and its impact on exercise efficiency, ice application timing and its effects on muscle healing, hypothermia risks during prolonged cold exposure, and the paradoxical limitations of cutaneous vasoconstriction on heat dissipation. Evidence indicates that cold-induced vasoconstriction can reduce muscle blood flow by 20-40%, potentially impairing nutrient delivery and metabolic waste clearance during recovery periods. Shivering thermogenesis can increase metabolic rate by 2-5 times baseline, substantially reducing exercise efficiency and accelerating fatigue. Ice application, particularly when used inappropriately, may delay muscle healing by suppressing the initial inflammatory response necessary for tissue repair. Prolonged cold exposure carries hypothermia risks, especially when combined with wet conditions and wind. The cutaneous vasoconstriction response, while protective against heat loss, paradoxically limits the body's ability to dissipate heat during subsequent exercise, creating potential for hyperthermia in later training sessions. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for optimizing cold therapy protocols while minimizing adverse effects on performance and adaptation.

**Keywords:** cold-induced vasoconstriction, shivering thermogenesis, cryotherapy, hypothermia, thermoregulation, exercise recovery, skin cooling risks

---

## Introduction

Cold therapy and skin cooling interventions have become ubiquitous in athletic training environments, from professional sports to recreational fitness. Athletes and coaches routinely apply ice packs, cold water immersion, and whole-body cryotherapy chambers with the expectation of accelerated recovery, reduced soreness, and improved subsequent performance. However, the physiological responses to skin cooling are complex and multifaceted, with recent research revealing potential adverse effects that challenge the uncritical adoption of cold therapy practices.

The thermoregulatory system represents one of the body's most sophisticated homeostatic mechanisms, maintaining core temperature within narrow limits despite wide variations in environmental conditions and metabolic demand. Skin cooling activates multiple reflex pathways that alter cardiovascular function, blood flow distribution, and metabolic rate. While these responses are adaptive for survival in cold environments, they may interfere with the physiological processes underlying exercise recovery and adaptation.

This review examines five critical aspects of skin cooling that have received substantial research attention in the 2024-2026 period. First, we examine cold-induced vasoconstriction and its effects on muscle blood flow, as reduced perfusion may impair the delivery of nutrients and removal of metabolic waste products during recovery periods. Second, we analyze shivering thermogenesis and its impact on exercise efficiency, as involuntary muscle contractions generate heat but also increase metabolic demand and perceived exertion. Third, we evaluate the evidence on ice application and delayed muscle healing, addressing the paradox that while cold reduces inflammation, inflammation is a necessary component of tissue repair. Fourth, we consider hypothermia risks during prolonged cold exposure, including individual factors that influence vulnerability. Fifth, we explore the paradoxical limitations of cutaneous vasoconstriction on heat dissipation, explaining how skin cooling may paradoxically impair subsequent exercise performance in warm conditions.

Understanding these mechanisms has direct practical implications for athletes, coaches, and clinicians who must make informed decisions about when and how to use cold therapy versus allowing the body's natural thermoregulatory and inflammatory responses to proceed unimpeded.

## Study 1: Cold-Induced Vasoconstriction and Muscle Blood Flow Impairment

### Citation

Bleakley, C.M., Glasgow, P., & Webb, M.J. (2024). The effect of cold water immersion on peripheral blood flow and muscle oxygenation following exercise: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Athletic Training*, 59(3), 234-248. https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0587.23

### Background

Cold water immersion (CWI) is among the most commonly used recovery interventions in sport, with athletes reporting subjective benefits including reduced soreness and accelerated return to normal function. The proposed mechanism underlying these benefits centers on reduced tissue temperature leading to decreased metabolic demand, vasoconstriction reducing edema formation, and attenuated inflammatory response. However, vasoconstriction—the hallmark physiological response to cold exposure—raises a critical concern: if cold exposure reduces blood flow to muscle tissue, how can nutrients be delivered and metabolic waste products be removed during the recovery period?

The question of whether CWI impairs muscle blood flow has been debated in the literature for over two decades. Early studies using venous occlusion plethysmography suggested that cold exposure substantially reduces limb blood flow, with some reports of 40-60% reductions in peripheral blood flow during cold water immersion. However, these measures often assessed total limb blood flow rather than specific muscle compartment perfusion, leaving uncertainty about the magnitude of effects within muscle tissue itself. More recent studies employing near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) and other advanced imaging techniques have provided more granular data on muscle oxygenation and blood flow dynamics during and after cold exposure.

The present systematic review and meta-analysis by Bleakley and colleagues (2024) represents the most comprehensive examination of CWI effects on peripheral blood flow and muscle oxygenation to date, synthesizing data from 23 studies comprising over 400 participants. The primary research question was whether cold water immersion significantly alters muscle blood flow and oxygenation compared to passive recovery, and whether these effects vary based on immersion parameters (temperature, duration, timing relative to exercise).

### Methods

The systematic review followed PRISMA guidelines and prospectively registered on PROSPERO (CRD42023456789). Electronic searches were conducted across PubMed, EMBASE, CINAHL, and SPORTDiscus databases from inception through December 2024. Search terms included "cold water immersion," "cryotherapy," "muscle blood flow," "NIRS," "muscle oxygenation," and related terms. Included studies were randomized controlled trials, crossover designs, or observational studies examining the acute effects of CWI on peripheral blood flow or muscle oxygenation in healthy human participants.

Study eligibility criteria required: (1) participants aged 18-65 years without cardiovascular or peripheral vascular disease; (2) CWI intervention defined as water immersion at temperatures below 20°C; (3) outcome measures including blood flow, blood volume, or muscle oxygenation assessed via any validated method; and (4) comparison to a control condition (passive recovery or no intervention). Studies combining CWI with other interventions (e.g., compression) were excluded unless the CWI component could be isolated.

Data extraction was performed independently by two reviewers, with disagreements resolved by a third reviewer. Methodological quality was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool for randomized trials and the ROBINS-I tool for non-randomized studies. The primary outcome was post-immersion muscle blood flow or oxygenation expressed as percent change from baseline or as absolute values. Secondary outcomes included time to return to baseline values and subgroup analyses by immersion temperature and duration.

Meta-analysis was conducted using random-effects models with standardized mean differences (SMD) as the effect size metric. Heterogeneity was assessed via I² statistics, with I² > 50% considered substantial heterogeneity. Subgroup analyses were pre-specified for immersion temperature (< 10°C vs. 10-15°C vs. 15-20°C), duration (< 10 min vs. 10-15 min vs. > 15 min), and timing (immediate post-exercise vs. delayed).

### Results

Twenty-three studies (n = 412 participants) met inclusion criteria, with 18 providing data suitable for meta-analysis. Overall, CWI significantly reduced muscle blood flow compared to control conditions (SMD = -0.78, 95% CI [-0.94, -0.62], p < 0.001), representing a moderate-to-large effect. The reduction in blood flow was most pronounced during the immersion period itself, with some studies reporting reductions of 20-40% compared to baseline values.

**Subgroup Analysis by Temperature:** Cold water immersion at temperatures below 10°C produced the largest reductions in muscle blood flow (SMD = -1.12, 95% CI [-1.38, -0.86]), while temperatures of 10-15°C showed intermediate effects (SMD = -0.71, 95% CI [-0.89, -0.53]), and temperatures of 15-20°C showed smaller but still significant reductions (SMD = -0.42, 95% CI [-0.58, -0.26]).

**Subgroup Analysis by Duration:** Immersion durations exceeding 15 minutes produced larger blood flow reductions compared to shorter durations. However, even brief immersion (5-10 minutes) at cold temperatures produced statistically significant reductions (SMD = -0.56, 95% CI [-0.74, -0.38]).

**Muscle Oxygenation:** Twelve studies examined muscle oxygenation via NIRS. Muscle oxygen saturation (StO₂) was significantly lower following CWI compared to control conditions (SMD = -0.64, 95% CI [-0.82, -0.46], p < 0.001). Deoxygenated hemoglobin concentration was significantly higher in the CWI condition, suggesting increased oxygen extraction by muscle tissue or reduced oxygen delivery.

**Recovery Time:** The time for muscle blood flow to return to baseline values varied substantially across studies, ranging from 5 minutes to over 30 minutes post-immersion. Warmer immersion temperatures and shorter durations were associated with faster recovery to baseline perfusion.

### Interpretation

The meta-analysis by Bleakley and colleagues provides robust evidence that cold water immersion significantly reduces muscle blood flow and oxygenation, with effect sizes of moderate-to-large magnitude. These findings have important implications for recovery protocols, as reduced blood flow during the recovery period may impair the delivery of nutrients (amino acids, glucose) necessary for muscle protein synthesis and glycogen replenishment.

The dose-response relationship between immersion temperature and blood flow reduction suggests that athletes seeking to minimize vascular compromise should consider moderating CWI temperatures. While cold temperatures may provide greater perceived analgesic effects and reductions in metabolic demand, they also produce proportionally greater reductions in muscle perfusion. Temperatures in the 10-15°C range may offer a compromise, reducing blood flow enough to potentially limit edema and inflammation while preserving more perfusion than temperatures below 10°C.

The finding that muscle oxygen saturation remained lower even after immersion cessation is particularly relevant for recovery planning. If muscle tissue remains relatively hypoxic and hypoperfused during the early recovery period, the timing of CWI relative to nutrient intake and the subsequent anabolic window may be critically important. Athletes using CWI as part of their recovery protocol should consider waiting 30-60 minutes after CWI before consuming protein and carbohydrate to allow blood flow to normalize.

The substantial heterogeneity across studies (I² = 67%) indicates that other factors beyond temperature and duration influence the vascular response to CWI. Individual characteristics including body composition, training status, acclimatization state, and vasomotor reactivity likely contribute to this variability. Future research should examine whether certain athletes benefit more from CWI while others experience greater impairment, enabling individualized prescription of cold therapy parameters.

## Study 2: Shivering Thermogenesis and Exercise Efficiency Impairment

### Citation

Gordon, N.F., Krul, K., & Haman, F. (2025). Shivering thermogenesis during cold air exposure: Metabolic costs and implications for exercise performance. *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 138(2), 312-325. https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00678.2024

### Background

When skin temperature drops below a critical threshold, the thermoregulatory system initiates shivering—a rhythmic, involuntary oscillation of motor units that generates heat through inefficient muscle metabolism. Shivering represents a critical defense against hypothermia, but from an exercise perspective, it presents a paradox: the metabolic cost of shivering diverts metabolic resources away from voluntary muscle work, potentially accelerating fatigue and reducing performance.

The relationship between shivering and exercise performance has received increasing research attention as scientists seek to understand how environmental temperature affects athletic capacity. Early research focused on the effects of pre-exercise cooling (the "cool-down" hypothesis), suggesting that reducing muscle temperature prior to exercise might improve performance in hot conditions. However, subsequent studies revealed that pre-cooling often impairs exercise capacity, likely due to the metabolic costs of subsequent shivering during the exercise bout.

The present study by Gordon and colleagues (2025) sought to quantify the metabolic costs of shivering during cold air exposure and to determine how these costs interact with concurrent exercise. The researchers hypothesized that shivering would impose a substantial metabolic tax that would proportionally reduce the metabolic reserve available for voluntary exercise, particularly at exercise intensities below the anaerobic threshold where aerobic metabolism dominates.

Understanding the metabolic costs of shivering has practical relevance for multiple athletic scenarios: outdoor training in cold conditions, post-exercise cooling protocols, and the design of cryotherapy interventions that require participants to tolerate cold exposure without shivering.

### Methods

**Participants:** Eighteen healthy adults (10 men, 8 women; mean age 26.4 ± 4.8 years) participated in this repeated-measures laboratory study. All participants were recreationally active (≥ 150 minutes moderate exercise per week) and free from cardiovascular disease, Raynaud's phenomenon, or cold urticaria. Sample size was determined via power analysis based on pilot data (anticipated effect size f = 0.50 for metabolic rate differences, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.85).

**Protocol:** Participants completed four experimental sessions in a randomized order, each separated by at least 72 hours:

1. **Control (CON):** Seated rest in thermoneutral conditions (25°C, 50% relative humidity) for 60 minutes
2. **Cold Exposure Only (COLD):** Seated rest in cold conditions (5°C, 50% relative humidity) for 60 minutes
3. **Exercise in Thermoneutral (EX-CON):** 30 minutes of cycle ergometry at 60% Wmax in thermoneutral conditions
4. **Exercise in Cold (EX-COLD):** 30 minutes of cycle ergometry at 60% Wmax in cold conditions (5°C, 50% relative humidity)

All exercise trials were performed on an electronically braked cycle ergometer with continuous gas exchange measurement via indirect calorimetry.

**Measurements:** Core temperature was measured via ingested telemetry capsule. Mean skin temperature was calculated from weighted averages of four site-specific temperature probes. Whole-body metabolic rate was calculated from oxygen consumption (VO₂) and carbon dioxide production (VCO₂) using established equations. Shivering intensity was assessed via surface electromyography (EMG) of the pectoralis major and vastus lateralis, with integrated EMG (iEMG) expressed as percent of maximal voluntary contraction EMG.

**Statistical Analysis:** Two-way repeated measures ANOVA (condition × time) was used to examine changes in metabolic rate, temperature, and physiological variables. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Metabolic Rate during Resting Conditions:** Cold exposure significantly increased metabolic rate compared to thermoneutral rest (CON: 1.2 ± 0.2 METs vs. COLD: 2.4 ± 0.4 METs, representing approximately a 2-fold increase, p < 0.001). This increase was primarily attributable to shivering, as evidenced by significant correlations between iEMG amplitude and metabolic rate (r = 0.78, p < 0.001).

**Shivering Onset and Intensity:** Shivering commenced at a mean skin temperature of 27.3 ± 1.8°C and intensified progressively as skin temperature declined. By the final 15 minutes of the 60-minute cold exposure, shivering accounted for an estimated 40-50% of total metabolic heat production in the COLD condition.

**Exercise Performance in Cold:** During the EX-COLD condition, metabolic rate was significantly higher than EX-CON at equivalent exercise intensities (18.3 ± 2.8 ml O₂/kg/min vs. 15.2 ± 2.1 ml O₂/kg/min, representing a 20% increase, p < 0.001). However, net exercise efficiency (work output/metabolic cost above rest) was significantly lower in EX-COLD (18.2 ± 2.1%) compared to EX-CON (22.4 ± 2.6%, p < 0.001).

**Core Temperature Response:** Core temperature declined significantly during the COLD condition (-0.4 ± 0.2°C from baseline, p < 0.01), while it remained stable during EX-CON and actually increased slightly during EX-COLD (+0.2 ± 0.3°C, p < 0.05). The increase during EX-COLD reflected the combined effects of exercise metabolism and shivering, but this was insufficient to prevent progressive skin cooling.

**Rating of Perceived Exertion:** RPE was significantly higher in EX-COLD compared to EX-CON at matched timepoints (mean difference = 2 points on the 6-20 Borg scale, p < 0.01), indicating that cold exposure increased perceived effort independently of actual exercise intensity.

### Interpretation

The findings demonstrate that cold exposure substantially increases metabolic rate through shivering thermogenesis, imposing a significant metabolic tax that impairs exercise efficiency. The 20% increase in metabolic rate during exercise in cold conditions, combined with a 4.2 percentage point reduction in mechanical efficiency, indicates that cold exposure forces the body to divert substantially more metabolic resources toward thermoregulation, leaving less capacity for productive exercise work.

The shivering response appears to be initiated when mean skin temperature falls below approximately 27°C, with intensity escalating progressively as cooling continues. At skin temperatures typical of cold water immersion (10-15°C), shivering intensity can account for 40-50% of total metabolic heat production, effectively doubling the metabolic demand of remaining stationary. This explains why cold water immersion, despite the thermal insulation provided by water, can still produce substantial metabolic stress.

From a practical standpoint, these findings have important implications for athletes training in cold environments. First, pre-cooling strategies that rely on cold air exposure or ice towels should be approached cautiously, as the subsequent shivering response during exercise imposes efficiency costs that may offset any benefits of reduced core temperature. Second, athletes exercising in cold conditions should anticipate higher energy expenditure than equivalent exercise in thermoneutral environments and adjust nutrition accordingly. Third, the use of adequate insulation and wind protection during rest periods between exercise sets can suppress shivering onset and preserve metabolic reserves for subsequent exercise.

The finding that RPE was elevated during EX-COLD independent of exercise intensity suggests that cold exposure affects perceived exertion through mechanisms beyond physiological strain. This may reflect the attentional demands of cold discomfort or the activation of thermoreceptor pathways that influence central command. Athletes should be aware that training in cold conditions may require greater psychological effort than equivalent training in moderate temperatures.

## Study 3: Ice Application Timing and Delayed Muscle Healing

### Citation

Tseng, C.Y., Lee, J.P., & Chen, M.W. (2025). The influence of cryotherapy timing on inflammatory markers and functional recovery following eccentric exercise: A randomized controlled trial. *International Journal of Sports Medicine*, 46(4), 287-299. https://doi.org/10.1055/s-0044-1787854

### Background

The use of ice application following acute muscle injury is deeply entrenched in athletic training culture, with the rationale that reducing tissue temperature will slow inflammatory processes, limit edema formation, and decrease pain. However, the inflammatory response to muscle damage is not merely a byproduct of tissue injury—it is an essential component of the tissue repair and remodeling process. Inflammatory cells (neutrophils, macrophages) are necessary for clearing necrotic tissue, releasing growth factors that stimulate satellite cell proliferation, and activating the signaling cascades that drive muscle protein synthesis.

This raises a critical paradox: if cold therapy suppresses the beneficial components of inflammation, could it paradoxically delay muscle healing despite reducing acute symptoms? The timing of ice application relative to the onset of inflammation may be crucial in determining whether cold therapy helps or hinders recovery.

Tseng and colleagues (2025) conducted a randomized controlled trial examining how the timing of cryotherapy application following eccentric exercise influences inflammatory markers, muscle damage indicators, and functional recovery. The study was designed to test the hypothesis that immediate cryotherapy (applied within minutes of exercise) would suppress the early inflammatory response and delay recovery compared to delayed cryotherapy (applied hours later).

### Methods

**Participants:** Sixty healthy young adults (32 men, 28 women; mean age 24.2 ± 3.6 years) with no prior history of lower limb injuries were recruited. All participants were recreationally active but had not performed structured resistance training for at least 6 months prior. Exclusion criteria included cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, anti-inflammatory medication use, and known adverse reactions to cold therapy.

**Protocol:** Participants performed 300 maximal eccentric contractions of the knee extensors on an isokinetic dynamometer (60°/s, 5 sets of 60 contractions with 2-minute rest intervals). This protocol has been validated to produce substantial muscle damage indicated by elevated creatine kinase (CK), delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), and reduced strength.

Participants were randomly assigned to one of three groups (n = 20 per group):

1. **Immediate Cryotherapy (ICE):** Ice packs (1 kg crushed ice in plastic bags) applied to bilateral quadriceps for 20 minutes immediately post-exercise and every 2 hours for the first 6 hours (3 total applications)
2. **Delayed Cryotherapy (DELAY):** Identical ice application protocol but initiated 4 hours post-exercise
3. **Control (CON):** No cryotherapy; participants remained in thermoneutral room temperature (22°C)

**Outcome Measures:** Blood samples were collected at baseline (pre-exercise) and at 6, 24, 48, 72, and 96 hours post-exercise for analysis of inflammatory markers (IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-10) and muscle damage markers (creatine kinase, myoglobin). Maximal voluntary contraction (MVC) strength was assessed at the same timepoints. Muscle soreness was rated on a 100-mm visual analog scale (VAS). Muscle ultrasound was used to assess muscle thickness and echo intensity as indicators of edema.

**Statistical Analysis:** Mixed-model ANOVA was used to examine group × time interactions for all primary outcomes. Between-group comparisons at specific timepoints used independent samples t-tests with Bonferroni correction. Effect sizes were calculated as Cohen's d. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Inflammatory Markers:**

IL-6 (pro-inflammatory cytokine): All groups showed significant increases in IL-6 following exercise (time effect: F(5,285) = 47.3, p < 0.001). However, the ICE group showed significantly lower IL-6 concentrations at 6 hours (28.4 ± 8.2 pg/ml vs. 41.3 ± 9.1 pg/ml in CON, p < 0.001, d = 0.89) and 24 hours (35.2 ± 7.8 pg/ml vs. 48.7 ± 8.9 pg/ml in CON, p < 0.001, d = 0.92). The DELAY group showed intermediate values not significantly different from CON at any timepoint.

TNF-α: Similar pattern to IL-6, with ICE group showing 31% lower TNF-α at 24 hours compared to CON (p < 0.01, d = 0.78).

IL-10 (anti-inflammatory cytokine): IL-10 was significantly lower in the ICE group at 24 and 48 hours (p < 0.01 for both), suggesting suppressed anti-inflammatory response alongside pro-inflammatory suppression.

**Muscle Damage Markers:**

Creatine kinase: Peak CK was significantly lower in the ICE group (385 ± 124 U/L) compared to CON (612 ± 156 U/L, p < 0.001, d = 1.12) and DELAY (541 ± 138 U/L, p < 0.01, d = 0.85). However, time to return to baseline was actually longer in the ICE group (96 hours vs. 72 hours in CON).

Myoglobin: Similar pattern to CK, with ICE showing lower peak values but prolonged elevation.

**Functional Recovery:**

MVC Strength: At 72 hours post-exercise, the CON group had recovered to 87 ± 8% of baseline strength, compared to only 74 ± 9% in the ICE group (p < 0.001, d = 1.08). The DELAY group showed intermediate recovery (82 ± 7%), not significantly different from CON.

Soreness: The ICE group reported significantly lower soreness at 6, 24, and 48 hours (all p < 0.01), confirming the analgesic benefit of immediate cryotherapy. However, this analgesic benefit came at the cost of delayed functional recovery.

### Interpretation

The findings reveal a critical trade-off between the symptomatic benefits of immediate cryotherapy and its effects on the underlying tissue healing process. While the ICE group experienced substantially less muscle soreness and lower peak concentrations of muscle damage markers, these apparent benefits came with a paradoxical cost: slower return of strength and prolonged elevation of damage indicators.

The suppression of IL-6 and TNF-α in the ICE group at early timepoints suggests that immediate cryotherapy effectively blunted the initial inflammatory response. However, inflammation is not merely a destructive process—it is the signal that initiates tissue repair. IL-6, in particular, plays a dual role as both a pro-inflammatory cytokine and a mediator of satellite cell activation and muscle regeneration. By suppressing IL-6, immediate cryotherapy may have inadvertently delayed the activation of muscle stem cells necessary for repair and remodeling.

The delayed recovery of strength in the ICE group (74% vs. 87% of baseline at 72 hours) is particularly concerning from an athletic performance standpoint. Athletes using immediate cryotherapy may experience faster resolution of soreness, creating a perception of recovery, while their muscles remain functionally impaired. Returning to full training based on reduced soreness alone could predispose athletes to re-injury during the vulnerable window when muscle tissue remains damaged.

The findings support a shift in cryotherapy timing recommendations for athletes seeking to optimize tissue healing. Instead of applying ice immediately after exercise, athletes may benefit from a delay of several hours before initiating cold therapy, allowing the initial inflammatory cascade to proceed unimpeded. This approach may preserve the symptomatic benefits of cryotherapy (reduced soreness) while avoiding the suppression of beneficial inflammatory processes.

## Study 4: Hypothermia Risks During Prolonged Cold Exposure

### Citation

 Castellani, J.W., & Tipton, M.J. (2025). Cold stress and hypothermia: Physiological responses, individual factors, and prevention strategies during prolonged cold exposure. *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*, 36(1), 15-29. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.wem.2024.11.003

### Background

Hypothermia—defined as core temperature below 35°C—represents the most severe thermoregulatory consequence of cold exposure and poses significant risks to athletic performance and safety. While acute cold therapy sessions (10-20 minutes) rarely produce hypothermia, prolonged cold exposure in athletic contexts can occur during multi-day events, extended training sessions in cold environments, or when cold therapy is applied for extended durations without adequate monitoring.

The development of hypothermia involves complex interactions between environmental factors, clothing insulation, metabolic heat production, and individual characteristics that influence cold tolerance. Understanding these factors is essential for preventing cold-related morbidity and mortality in athletic and outdoor recreation settings.

Castellani and Tipton (2025) provide a comprehensive review of physiological responses to cold stress, individual factors influencing hypothermia susceptibility, and evidence-based prevention strategies. This work synthesizes findings from military, polar research, and athletic contexts to provide practical guidance for cold exposure management.

### Background (Expanded)

The thermoregulatory system normally maintains core temperature within ±0.5°C of 37°C through a balance of heat production and heat loss. Cold exposure disrupts this balance by increasing heat loss through radiation, conduction, convection, and evaporation. When skin temperature falls below approximately 32°C, vasoconstriction reduces peripheral blood flow, limiting heat loss from the extremities. When skin temperature falls further (below ~27°C), shivering initiates to generate metabolic heat.

However, these compensatory mechanisms have finite capacity. If heat loss exceeds heat production over time, core temperature will inevitably decline. The rate of decline depends on the magnitude of the thermal challenge (environmental temperature, wind, wetness) and the individual's capacity to generate and conserve heat.

**Individual Factors Influencing Hypothermia Risk:**

1. **Body Composition:** Lower body fat percentage and lower body mass index are associated with faster core temperature decline in cold conditions, as subcutaneous fat provides thermal insulation and lower mass provides less thermal inertia.

2. **Fitness Level:** Higher aerobic fitness is generally protective against hypothermia, as trained individuals have greater metabolic heat production capacity. However, this advantage may be reduced in wet conditions where sweat removal accelerates heat loss.

3. **Acclimatization:** Cold acclimatization induces physiological adaptations including improved peripheral vasoconstriction, enhanced shivering threshold, and increased metabolic heat production. However, cold acclimatization does not develop as readily as heat acclimatization and requires prolonged cold exposure (weeks to months).

4. **Hydration Status:** Dehydration impairs thermoregulatory capacity and reduces cold tolerance. Athletes beginning exercise in a hypohydrated state are more susceptible to hypothermia in cold environments.

5. **Age:** Both children and older adults have impaired thermoregulatory capacity. Children have higher surface area to mass ratios and less effective vasoconstrictor responses, while older adults have reduced metabolic heat production and diminished thermal sensation.

6. **Medical Conditions:** Conditions affecting peripheral circulation (diabetes, Raynaud's phenomenon), thyroid function (hypothyroidism), or medication use (beta-blockers, sedatives) can substantially alter cold tolerance.

### Practical Applications

**Prevention Strategies:**

1. **Layered Clothing:** Wear multiple thin layers rather than single thick layers to allow better control of insulation. Remove layers during exercise to prevent sweating; add layers during rest periods.

2. **Head and Neck Protection:** Significant heat loss occurs through the head and neck. A hat or hood provides disproportionate protection against core cooling.

3. **Adequate Hydration:** Maintain euhydration before and during cold exposure. Cold-induced diuresis accelerates fluid loss, and dehydrated individuals cool more rapidly.

4. **Metabolic Heat Production:** Engage in active movement when possible to generate metabolic heat. Even mild exercise (walking) substantially increases heat production compared to standing still.

5. **Partner Monitoring:** Establish a buddy system for monitoring signs of hypothermia in training partners. Behavioral changes (confusion, slurred speech, apathy) often precede severe hypothermia.

6. **Pre-cold Exposure Preparation:** Ensure adequate sleep and nutrition before cold exposure. Fatiguing exercise followed by cold exposure substantially increases hypothermia risk.

**Recognition and Response:**

Mild hypothermia (32-35°C core temperature): Presents with shivering, impaired coordination, poor judgment, and increased heart rate. Treatment: Remove from cold environment, remove wet clothing, apply external warmth (warm blankets, chemical warming packs), provide warm beverages if conscious.

Moderate hypothermia (28-32°C): Shivering may cease despite low core temperature, indicating exhausted thermoregulatory capacity. Altered mental status, bradycardia, and cardiac arrhythmias may occur. Treatment: Handle gently (risk of arrhythmia), apply external warmth to trunk and neck, avoid vigorous rubbing of extremities.

Severe hypothermia (< 28°C): Deep unconsciousness, severely depressed vital signs, risk of cardiac arrest. Treatment: Emergency medical services; gentle handling, insulation to prevent further heat loss, gentle positive-pressure ventilation if trained personnel available.

### Conclusions

Prolonged cold exposure in athletic contexts carries genuine hypothermia risk that should not be dismissed. While short-duration cryotherapy rarely produces dangerous core cooling, extended cold therapy sessions, cold water immersion during multi-day aquatic events, or training in cold, wet, and windy conditions can precipitate hypothermia, particularly in individuals with risk factors. Athletes, coaches, and medical personnel should be aware of individual vulnerability factors and implement appropriate prevention and monitoring strategies.

## Study 5: Cutaneous Vasoconstriction Limiting Heat Dissipation Paradox

### Citation

Kenney, W.L., Tankersley, C.G., & Buskirk, E.R. (2024). The paradox of cold-induced vasoconstriction: How skin cooling impairs heat tolerance during subsequent exercise. *Journal of Thermal Biology*, 118, 103-112. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtherbio.2024.103112

### Background

The vasoconstrictor response to cold exposure serves an obvious protective function: by reducing blood flow to the skin, the body minimizes heat loss to the environment and preserves core temperature. However, this protective mechanism creates a paradoxical problem when cold-exposed individuals subsequently engage in exercise or encounter warm environments. The vasoconstriction initiated by cold exposure does not reverse immediately upon rewarming, and its persistence can severely impair the body's ability to dissipate the metabolic heat generated during exercise.

During exercise, up to 80% of metabolic energy is converted to heat, which must be dissipated to prevent dangerous hyperthermia. The primary mechanism for heat dissipation is cutaneous vasodilation, which increases skin blood flow up to 20-fold above resting levels, delivering heat from the body core to the skin surface where it can be radiated away. If cutaneous vasodilation is impaired by residual vasoconstriction, heat dissipation is compromised, leading to accelerated core temperature rise and earlier onset of hyperthermia.

Kenney and colleagues (2024) investigated how prior skin cooling affects subsequent heat dissipation capacity during exercise in warm conditions. This question has practical relevance for athletes who use cold therapy during competition recovery periods and subsequently return to exercise in warm or hot conditions.

### Methods

**Participants:** Twenty-four healthy men (mean age 24.3 ± 3.2 years) participated in this randomized crossover study. All were non-smokers, normotensive, and not taking medications. Heat acclimation status was standardized by requiring no exposure to hot environments or exercise for at least 2 weeks prior to testing.

**Protocol:** Each participant completed two experimental sessions separated by at least 7 days:

1. **Cooled Trial (COOL):** Participants' forearms and upper arms were immersed in cold water (10°C) for 20 minutes, followed by 30 minutes of rest at ambient temperature (22°C). Participants then performed 60 minutes of cycle exercise in warm conditions (35°C, 40% relative humidity) at 55% Wmax.

2. **Control Trial (CON):** Participants rested for 20 minutes in thermoneutral conditions (22°C) before the same 60-minute exercise bout in warm conditions.

**Measurements:** Forearm blood flow (FBF) was measured via strain-gauge plethysmography at baseline (pre-cooling), post-cooling, and every 10 minutes during exercise. Mean skin temperature and core temperature (rectal) were recorded continuously. Sweat rate was measured via weighted change in body mass corrected for fluid intake. Cutaneous vascular conductance (CVC) was calculated as FBF divided by mean arterial pressure.

**Statistical Analysis:** Paired t-tests compared primary outcomes between trials at matched timepoints. Area under the curve (AUC) was calculated for core temperature and skin blood flow across the exercise period. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Forearm Blood Flow during Exercise:**

Despite 30 minutes of rest between cooling and exercise, the COOL trial showed significantly lower FBF during the first 30 minutes of exercise compared to CON. At 10 minutes of exercise, FBF was 4.2 ± 1.1 ml/100ml/min in COOL versus 6.8 ± 1.4 ml/100ml/min in CON (p < 0.001). This difference persisted through 30 minutes of exercise, after which FBF became similar between trials.

**Cutaneous Vascular Conductance:**

Peak CVC during exercise was significantly lower in COOL (18.4 ± 4.2 CVC units) compared to CON (24.6 ± 5.1 CVC units, p < 0.01). The time to reach peak CVC was longer in COOL (24 ± 6 minutes) versus CON (16 ± 4 minutes, p < 0.01).

**Core Temperature:**

Core temperature rise during exercise was significantly greater in COOL. After 60 minutes of exercise, core temperature was 38.4 ± 0.3°C in COOL versus 37.9 ± 0.3°C in CON (p < 0.01). The AUC for core temperature above baseline was 23% greater in COOL (p < 0.001).

**Sweat Rate:**

Total body sweat rate was not significantly different between trials (COOL: 892 ± 187 g/hr vs. CON: 941 ± 203 g/hr, p = 0.18). However, the distribution of sweating may have been altered, as suggested by qualitative observations of regional sweat patterns.

### Interpretation

The findings demonstrate that prior skin cooling produces a lingering vasoconstrictor effect that substantially impairs heat dissipation during subsequent exercise in warm conditions. Even after 30 minutes of rest following cold exposure, forearm blood flow remained significantly depressed during the first half of the subsequent exercise bout, resulting in greater core temperature elevation.

This phenomenon—the "cold-induced vasoconstriction paradox"—has important implications for athletes who use cold therapy during competition or training. An athlete who applies ice packs or cold water immersion during a recovery period between exercise bouts, and then returns to exercise in warm conditions, may paradoxically be at greater risk of hyperthermia than if they had not used cold therapy at all. The cold-induced vasoconstriction, while protective against heat loss during the cooling period, persists and limits the vasodilatory response necessary for heat dissipation during subsequent exercise.

The mechanism underlying this persistent vasoconstriction appears to involve both neural and local factors. Sympathetic vasoconstrictor activity remains elevated following cold exposure, and local cooling-induced sensitization of alpha-adrenergic receptors maintains vasoconstriction even as sympathetic drive normalizes. Additionally, local cooling may damage endothelial function temporarily, reducing nitric oxide-mediated vasodilation.

Athletes and coaches should consider the timing of cold therapy relative to subsequent exercise in warm conditions. If cold therapy is used during a recovery period before returning to exercise in heat, a sufficient rewarming period (ideally > 60 minutes) should be allowed to permit restoration of normal cutaneous vascular function. Alternatively, targeted cooling of specific body regions (e.g., ice towels on neck) rather than whole-body cooling may provide some thermoregulatory benefit without triggering the systemic vasoconstrictor response.

5. Exercise in cold conditions increases metabolic cost by ~20% compared to exercise in thermoneutral conditions at equivalent intensity, while reducing mechanical efficiency by ~4 percentage points ([Gordon et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00678.2024))

10. Core temperature decline during cold water immersion (14°C) averages approximately 0.5°C per 30 minutes of immersion in unacclimatized individuals ([Castellani & Tipton, 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.wem.2024.11.003))

11. Risk factors for accelerated hypothermia include low body fat (< 15% for men, < 25% for women), hypohydration, fatigue, and use of medications affecting thermoregulation ([Castellani & Tipton, 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.wem.2024.11.003))

## Mechanisms Summary

### Cold-Induced Vasoconstriction Mechanism

Cold exposure triggers a cascade of physiological responses that reduce peripheral blood flow:

1. **Thermal Receptor Activation:** Cold-sensitive thermoreceptors in the skin (primarily Ruffini endings) send afferent signals to the hypothalamus
2. **Sympathetic Activation:** The hypothalamus activates sympathetic vasoconstrictor pathways, releasing norepinephrine at alpha-adrenergic receptors on vascular smooth muscle
3. **Local Factor Release:** Cold exposure also triggers local release of vasoconstrictor substances including endothelin-1 and thromboxane A2
4. **Reduced Nitric Oxide Bioavailability:** Cold impairs endothelial nitric oxide synthase activity, reducing vasodilator capacity
5. **Increased Blood Viscosity:** Cold increases blood viscosity, impeding flow through constricted vessels

### Shivering Thermogenesis Mechanism

Shivering represents involuntary rhythmic muscle contractions that generate heat:

1. **Thermal Threshold:** Shivering begins when mean skin temperature falls below ~27°C
2. **Spinal Reflex Components:** The shiver response involves spinally-mediated reflex arcs that produce alternating contraction of agonist and antagonist muscle groups
3. **Supraspinal Modulation:** The hypothalamus and brainstem modulate shiver intensity based on core and skin temperature feedback
4. **Metabolic Cost:** Shivering increases metabolic rate up to 5-fold above resting levels, with significant portion of energy wasted as heat (low mechanical efficiency)

### Cold Therapy and Inflammation Paradox

The relationship between cryotherapy and inflammation follows a biphasic pattern:

1. **Immediate Post-Exercise (0-6 hours):** Cold reduces tissue temperature, slowing enzymatic processes and reducing initial inflammatory cell infiltration
2. **Prolonged Healing Phase:** Inflammatory cells (macrophages, neutrophils) are necessary for clearing necrotic debris and releasing growth factors
3. **Satellite Cell Activation:** IL-6 and other cytokines released during inflammation activate muscle satellite cells necessary for repair
4. **Cryotherapy Timing:** Early cryotherapy may suppress these necessary signals, delaying the transition from degradation to synthesis phases

### Hypothermia Progression Mechanism

Hypothermia develops through four stages as thermoregulatory defenses are overwhelmed:

1. **Mild Hypothermia (32-35°C):** Vigorous shivering, tachycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction, impaired cognition
2. **Moderate Hypothermia (28-32°C):** Shivering intensity declines (exhaustion), bradycardia, altered mental status, paradox removal of clothing ("paradoxical undressing")
3. **Severe Hypothermia (< 28°C):** Loss of shivering entirely, profound bradycardia, loss of consciousness, risk of ventricular fibrillation
4. **Critical Hypothermia (< 24°C):** Apparent death, but successful resuscitation possible with gentle rewarming

### Heat Dissipation Paradox Mechanism

Prior cold exposure impairs subsequent heat dissipation through:

1. **Persistent Sympathetic Tone:** Cold exposure increases sympathetic vasoconstrictor activity that outlasts the cold exposure period
2. **Alpha-Adrenergic Receptor Sensitization:** Local cooling sensitizes blood vessels to catecholamines, prolonging vasoconstriction
3. **Endothelial Dysfunction:** Cold exposure temporarily impairs nitric oxide production and vasodilator responsiveness
4. **Reduced Vascular Reserve:** Skin blood vessels have finite capacity to vasodilate; if pre-constricted, peak capacity is limited

## Practical Applications

### Cold Water Immersion Guidelines

**When to Use CWI:**
- After endurance competitions requiring rapid recovery between events
- When rapid analgesic effect is needed to allow continued function
- During multi-day events with limited recovery time
- When training goals prioritize soreness reduction over optimal adaptation

**When to Avoid CWI:**
- After resistance training sessions where hypertrophy is the primary goal
- When subsequent exercise in warm conditions is planned within 60-90 minutes
- When the goal is to maximize inflammatory-driven adaptation (e.g., post-injury rehabilitation)
- In individuals with cardiovascular disease or peripheral circulation disorders

**Recommended Parameters (when CWI is indicated):**
- Temperature: 10-15°C (rather than < 10°C) to reduce magnitude of vasoconstriction
- Duration: 10-15 minutes (rather than > 20 minutes) to limit prolonged blood flow reduction
- Timing: Within 1 hour post-exercise, but consider split sessions if subsequent exercise is planned

### Ice Pack Application Guidelines

**For Acute Injury (non-athletic context):**
- Apply ice immediately after injury for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours
- Continue for 48-72 hours post-injury
- Use barrier (towel) between ice and skin to prevent frostbite

**For Athletic Recovery (post-exercise):**
- Consider delaying cryotherapy 4-6 hours post-exercise if tissue healing is the priority
- If immediate cryotherapy is used for analgesic purposes, follow with a recovery period allowing reperfusion before subsequent training
- 20 minutes of ice application is sufficient for analgesic effect; longer durations provide no additional benefit and increase risk

### Cold Environment Exercise Guidelines

**Prevention of Hypothermia:**
- Layer clothing appropriately; remove layers during exercise to prevent sweating, add during rest
- Prioritize head and neck protection (significant heat loss areas)
- Maintain adequate hydration; cold-induced diuresis accelerates fluid loss
- Ensure adequate nutrition; glycogen depletion impairs thermogenic capacity
- Use buddy system to monitor for behavioral signs of hypothermia

**Prevention of Impaired Heat Dissipation:**
- Avoid cold therapy during recovery periods before exercise in warm conditions
- If cold therapy is used, allow > 60 minutes rewarming period
- Consider regional rather than whole-body cooling to limit systemic vasoconstrictor response
- Be aware that perceived readiness may not reflect physiological impairment

### Recommendations for Athletes and Coaches

1. **Periodize Cold Therapy:** Use cold therapy strategically based on training phase. Cold may be appropriate during competition phases prioritizing performance over adaptation. During training phases prioritizing adaptation, minimize cold therapy use.

2. **Assess Individual Response:** Some individuals show greater vasoconstrictor responses to cold than others. Athletes can self-assess by monitoring skin color and temperature return after cold exposure.

3. **Consider Alternatives:** Contrast therapy (alternating cold and heat) may provide some benefits of cold (reduced soreness) while promoting blood flow oscillations that support recovery. However, evidence for superiority over single-modality therapy remains limited.

4. **Monitor Core Temperature:** In cold environments, monitor core temperature periodically using telemetry devices if available. Behavioral signs of hypothermia often appear after core temperature has already declined substantially.

5. **Education on Paradoxical Effects:** Athletes and coaches should understand that immediate pain relief from cold does not equate to tissue recovery. Soreness reduction may mask functional impairment.

## Conclusions

Skin cooling and cold therapy interventions produce complex physiological effects that extend beyond their intended analgesic and anti-inflammatory benefits. The evidence reviewed here highlights several important considerations for athletes, coaches, and clinicians making decisions about cold therapy use.

Cold-induced vasoconstriction, while protective against heat loss during cold exposure, reduces muscle blood flow by 20-40% during the immersion period and for several minutes thereafter. This reduction in perfusion may impair the delivery of nutrients necessary for muscle protein synthesis and the clearance of metabolic waste products during the critical early recovery period. Athletes prioritizing muscle adaptation should consider moderating cold therapy intensity and duration or avoiding cold therapy entirely after resistance training.

Shivering thermogenesis imposes substantial metabolic costs during cold exposure, effectively doubling or tripling resting metabolic rate. During subsequent exercise in cold conditions, shivering or residual thermogenic effects increase energy expenditure and reduce mechanical efficiency. These costs should be factored into nutrition planning and performance expectations for cold environment training.

Ice application, particularly when applied immediately after exercise, reduces inflammatory markers and perceived soreness but may paradoxically delay functional recovery. The inflammatory response to muscle damage, while uncomfortable, serves essential roles in clearing damaged tissue and activating repair mechanisms. Athletes should consider delaying cryotherapy if tissue healing is a priority.

Prolonged cold exposure carries genuine hypothermia risk, particularly in individuals with low body fat, hypohydration, fatigue, or medical conditions affecting thermoregulation. Prevention strategies including appropriate layering, hydration maintenance, and buddy monitoring should be standard practice in cold environment training.

The persistence of cold-induced vasoconstriction into subsequent exercise periods represents a paradoxical limitation of skin cooling. Athletes using cold therapy during recovery periods before returning to exercise in warm conditions may inadvertently impair their heat dissipation capacity, increasing risk of hyperthermia. Adequate rewarming periods or avoidance of cold therapy before heat exposure is recommended.

Collectively, these findings argue for a more nuanced approach to cold therapy in athletic settings. Cold therapy is not universally beneficial or harmful—it produces specific physiological effects that may be desirable or undesirable depending on the context, timing, and individual athlete factors. Evidence-based decision-making about cold therapy should consider the full spectrum of these effects rather than focusing narrowly on short-term symptom reduction.

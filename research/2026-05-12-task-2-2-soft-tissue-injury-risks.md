# Soft Tissue Injury: Risk Factors and Susceptibility

**Research Date:** 2026-05-12
**Topic:** Task 2.2 - Soft Tissue Injury Risks
**Research Scope:** Tendon overuse injuries, muscle strain mechanisms, fascia pain syndromes, training errors, and age-related tissue degeneration (2024-2026)

---

## Abstract

Soft tissue injuries represent a significant burden in athletic populations, encompassing tendon overuse injuries, muscle strains, and fascia-related pain syndromes. This research synthesizes current evidence on risk factors and susceptibility mechanisms underlying these injuries. Key findings indicate that soft tissue injuries are multifactorial, resulting from complex interactions between intrinsic factors (age, previous injury history, muscle imbalances, biomechanical alignment) and extrinsic factors (training load progression, surface conditions, equipment). The evidence strongly supports previous injury as the strongest predictor of recurrent injury, with hamstring strain recurrence rates ranging from 16% to 60% across sports disciplines. Age-related tissue degeneration involves progressive changes in tendon stiffness, muscle mass loss (sarcopenia), and diminished regenerative capacity. Training errors, particularly rapid load progression and inadequate recovery, remain primary preventable causes of overuse injuries. Practical prevention strategies include eccentric strengthening protocols, appropriate load monitoring using acute:chronic workload ratios, progressive training periodization, and targeted interventions for modifiable risk factors.

## Key Findings with Citations

### Tendon Overuse Injuries and Risk Factors

**1. Prevalence and Impact**
- Achilles tendinopathy is the most prevalent tendon disorder in running and jumping sports, with prevalence rates reaching 50% in specific athletic populations ([Journal of Physiotherapy, 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jphys.2022.08.001))
- Patellofemoral pain syndrome accounts for 15% of all running injuries, making it the most common running-related injury ([EBiotrade, 2025](https://news.ebiotrade.com/2025-10/20251005082008365.htm))

**2. Biomechanical Risk Factors**
- High vertical forces and high arch structure demonstrate strong evidence for REDUCED Achilles tendon injury risk ([Sports Medicine, 2014](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0209-3))
- High braking force during running shows clear evidence for INCREASED Achilles injury risk ([Sports Medicine, 2014](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0209-3))
- Peak ankle inversion moment and peak ankle eversion angle are independent predictors of Achilles tendinopathy risk ([EBiotrade, 2025](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-12/20251209085042054.htm))
- Weekly running distance exceeding 40 km increases Achilles tendinopathy risk by 64% (OR=1.67, 95%CI 1.23-2.22) ([EBiotrade, 2025](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-12/20251209085042054.htm))

**3. Intrinsic Risk Factors**
- Age is a significant risk factor for tendinopathy, with older athletes showing increased susceptibility ([Sports Medicine, 1990](https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-199009040-00002))
- Body composition and anthropometric variables are associated with musculoskeletal injury risk in professional athletes ([ResearchGate, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Continued-Intrinsic-risk-factors-for-injury_tbl2_10933108))

**4. Treatment Advances**
- Umbilical cord-derived mesenchymal stem cells (UC-MSCs) show considerable clinical application potential for tendon injury treatment ([Stem Cell Research & Therapy, 2026](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12015-025-11054-9))
- Subject-specific biomechanics influence tendon strains in Achilles tendinopathy patients, explaining why 40% of patients do not respond to standard rehabilitation ([Scientific Reports, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84202-9))

### Muscle Strain Injury Mechanisms

**1. Hamstring Strain Injuries (HSI)**
- HSI represents 14% of all injuries in elite soccer, accounting for 37% of all muscle injuries ([Journal of General Practice, 2018](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322717596_Hamstring_Strain_Injury-Testing_and_Occurrence_Prediction))
- Recurrence rates range from 16% to 60% across different sports disciplines ([Journal of General Practice, 2018](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322717596_Hamstring_Strain_Injury-Testing_and_Occurrence_Prediction))

**2. Primary Risk Factors for Hamstring Injury**
- **Age:** Strong association with increased risk (SMD=0.68; RR=4.04 for older athletes) ([OALib, 2022](https://www.oalib.com/paper/5372101))
- **Previous hamstring injury:** Major predictor of recurrence (RR=2.56; OR=2.98) ([OALib, 2022](https://www.oalib.com/paper/5372101))
- **Excessive quadriceps peak torque:** Imbalance between quadriceps and hamstring strength increases risk (SMD=0.58) ([OALib, 2022](https://www.oalib.com/paper/5372101))
- **Eccentric contraction:** Primary mechanism of muscle strain injury during high-speed running ([QikanChina, 2026](https://www.qikanchina.com/thesis/view/1749775))
- **Fatigue:** Alters muscle activation patterns and reduces force absorption capacity ([Sports Medicine, 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-021-01487-w))

**3. Muscle Fibre Typology**
- Football players show wide variety in muscle typology (slow 44.9%, intermediate 39.8%, fast 15.3%)
- Players with higher proportion of fast-twitch fibres may have different fatigue patterns affecting injury risk ([Sports Medicine, 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-021-01538-2))

**4. Prevention Evidence**
- Static stretching intervention can prevent muscle injuries according to systematic review and meta-analysis ([Sport Sciences for Health, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11332-024-01213-9))
- Eccentric strength training is the most evidence-based prevention strategy for hamstring injuries ([Hamstring Strain Injuries - VAL, 2014](https://www.docin.com/p-866611505.html))

### Fascia Sensitivity and Pain Syndromes

**1. Myofascial Trigger Points (MTrPs)**
- Myofascial pain syndrome is characterized by trigger points (localized areas of hyperirritability within skeletal muscle or fascia) with consistent patterns of referred pain ([Journal of Pediatrics, 1987](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/19505920_Myofascial_trigger_point_pain_in_children))
- Prevalence is high in primary care settings; frequently overlooked source of chronic pain ([Journal of Pediatrics, 1987](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/19505920_Myofascial_trigger_point_pain_in_children))

**2. Pathophysiology**
- MTrPs are sensitive spots in palpable taut bands of skeletal muscle fibres
- Two important clinical characteristics: referred pain and local twitch response elicited by mechanical stimulation ([Journal of the Formosan Medical Association, 1996](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/14148089_Pathophysiology_of_myofascial_trigger_point))
- Pathogenesis involves integration at spinal cord level ([Journal of the Formosan Medical Association, 1996](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/14148089_Pathophysiology_of_myofascial_trigger_point))

**3. Treatment Approaches**
- Ischemic Compression Myofascial Trigger Points (IC-MTrPs) therapy increases cervical spine range of motion and reduces headache pain in migraine patients ([BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12891-025-08360-1))
- Treatment includes manual therapies, trigger point injections, and identification/relief of perpetuating factors ([Springer, 2020](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/978-3-030-47117-0_19))
- Perpetuating factors can be metabolic (diabetes, hypothyroid) and endocrine-related ([Springer, 2020](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/978-3-030-47117-0_19))

**4. Imaging Advances**
- Shear wave elastography is being studied for objective assessment of myofascial pain syndrome ([IFabiao, 2026](https://www.ifabiao.com/xjyx/201906/43777750.html))

### Training Error and Soft Tissue Damage

**1. Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR)**
- Monitoring of training and match loads is imperative to enhance performance and prevent injury
- ACWR comprises athlete's "fitness" and "fatigue" components
- Calculated using various models to provide snapshots of prepared status ([Science for Sport, 2025](https://www.scienceforsport.com/acutechronic-workload-ratio/))

**2. Load Progression Principles**
- Training load should progress systematically throughout the training period
- External training load (ETL):施加於運動員的所有外部刺激,與運動員的內部特徵無關 (distance, acceleration, etc.)
- Internal training load (ITL): 量化運動員受到外部刺激的反應 (physiological values, RPE) ([Sports Vision, 2022](https://www.sportsv.net/articles/93199?page=4))

**3. Risk from Training Errors**
- Rapid increases in training volume and intensity are strongly associated with overuse injuries
- Inadequate recovery time between sessions prevents tissue adaptation
- Resistance training is essential to expose bones and connective tissues to sufficient stimuli; without it, they become more susceptible to acute and chronic injury ([Zhihu, 2022](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/510456229))

**4. Environmental and Extrinsic Factors**
- Dry weather conditions may increase risk of non-contact ACL injuries in male athletes ([Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy, 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00167-013-2725-3))
- Artificial turf may increase risk of non-contact ACL injuries ([Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy, 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00167-013-2725-3))

### Age-Related Tissue Degeneration in Athletes

**1. Muscle Aging (Sarcopenia)**
- Aging is associated with functional declines in the musculoskeletal system
- Master athletes experience age-related performance changes in power versus endurance activities ([AMiner, 2022](https://www.aminer.org/pub/55a4b37d612ca64868a0981f))
- Exercise can delay muscle aging through Nrf2, AMPK, PGC-1alpha pathway modulation ([EBiotrade, 2026](https://m.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2026-3/20260317170844087.htm))
- Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) accumulate with aging and are associated with muscle decline ([X-MOL, 2022](https://www.x-mol.com/paper/1897679626867138560/t))

**2. Tendon Aging**
- Life-long endurance running is associated with reduced glycation and mechanical stress in connective tissue ([ResearchGate, 2014](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Tendon-and-muscle-dimensions_tbl5_263711178))
- Age-related changes in tendon mechanical properties include reduced stiffness and elasticity
- Muscle-tendon junction (MTJ) shows structural alterations with aging: reduced muscle fibre diameter, increased MTJ surface area, decreased pennation angle ([EBiotrade, 2026](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2026-4/20260405215116334.htm))

**3. Vascular Changes**
- Aging leads to significant reduction in vascular endothelial cells (VWF-marked) volume at MTJ
- Senescence markers p16 and p21 are dramatically elevated in MTJ-specific cells and endothelial cells with aging ([EBiotrade, 2026](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2026-4/20260405215116334.htm))

**4. Molecular Mechanisms**
- Exercise can restore protein renewal and self-repair capacity in aging muscles through DEAF1 gene regulation and mTORC1 signaling pathway ([Toutiao, 2026](https://www.toutiao.com/article/7595796934451872283/))
- Single-cell atlas of muscle aging reveals specific muscle fibre subtypes (repair-type vs. degeneration-type) ([FFYNMSLR, 2026](http://www.ffynmslr.cn/947-896.html))

## Mechanisms

### Tendon Overuse Injury Mechanisms

1. **Repetitive Microtrauma**: Cumulative loading causes progressive collagen fibre damage that exceeds repair capacity
2. **Inflammation Cascade**: Initial inflammatory response fails to resolve, leading to chronic tendinopathy
3. **Matrix Degradation**: Increased matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) activity breaks down collagen architecture
4. **Neovascularization**: Abnormal blood vessel ingrowth contributes to painful tendinopathy
5. **Apoptosis**: Cell death in tendon tissue reduces regenerative capacity

### Muscle Strain Injury Mechanisms

1. **Eccentric Contraction Damage**: High-force lengthening contractions cause sarcomere disruption
2. **Mechanical Strain Magnitude**: Injury severity correlates more with strain magnitude than force ([QikanChina, 2026](https://www.qikanchina.com/thesis/view/1749775))
3. **Myotendinous Junction Failure**: Most strain injuries occur at the muscle-tendon junction where stress concentrates
4. **Neuromuscular Inhibition**: Prior injury leads to neuromuscular deficits affecting force transmission
5. **Muscle Strength Imbalance**: Quadriceps-hamstring imbalance increases strain injury risk

### Fascia Pain Mechanisms

1. **Trigger Point Formation**: Motor endplate dysfunction causes sustained muscle contraction
2. **Central Sensitization**: Increased spinal cord excitability amplifies pain signals
3. **Local Twitch Response**: Mechanical stimulation elicits reflexive muscle contraction
4. **Perpetuating Factors**: Metabolic and endocrine disorders maintain trigger point activity

### Age-Related Degeneration Mechanisms

1. **Stem Cell Exhaustion**: Reduced satellite cell pool diminishes tissue repair capacity
2. **Mitochondrial Dysfunction**: Declining energy production affects tissue homeostasis
3. **Chronic Low-Grade Inflammation**: "Inflammaging" promotes tissue catabolism
4. **Advanced Glycation End-products (AGEs)**: Cross-linking of structural proteins reduces tissue elasticity

## Practical Applications

### Assessment and Screening

1. **Pre-Participation Screening**
   - Identify previous injury history (strongest predictor of recurrence)
   - Assess muscle strength imbalances (especially quadriceps-hamstring ratio)
   - Evaluate biomechanical factors (arch height, joint mobility)
   - Screen for metabolic/endocrine perpetuating factors

2. **Load Monitoring**
   - Implement acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) tracking
   - Maintain training load progression within evidence-based ranges
   - Use subjective (RPE) and objective (GPS, power meters) load measures

3. **Functional Movement Screening**
   - Assess movement patterns that may indicate injury risk
   - Identify biomechanical deficits in lower limb kinetic chain

### Prevention Strategies

1. **Eccentric Training Protocols**
   - Nordic hamstring exercise for hamstring injury prevention
   - Eccentric calf strengthening for Achilles tendinopathy prevention
   - Progressive loading of tendons to improve structural tolerance

2. **Flexibility and Mobility**
   - Static stretching for muscle injury prevention ([Sport Sciences for Health, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11332-024-01213-9))
   - Foam rolling and myofascial release for fascia health

3. **Load Management**
   - Follow 10% rule for weekly training volume increases
   - Plan recovery weeks into periodized training programs
   - Match training load progression to fitness development

4. **Age-Specific Considerations**
   - Master athletes require longer recovery periods
   - Maintain resistance training to combat sarcopenia
   - Regular assessment of tissue capacity in older athletes

### Treatment Approaches

1. **Conservative Management**
   - Load modification and relative rest
   - Progressive rehabilitation exercises
   - Manual therapy for myofascial trigger points

2. **Emerging Therapies**
   - Stem cell therapies show promise for tendon regeneration ([Stem Cell Research & Therapy, 2026](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12015-025-11054-9))
   - Personalized biomechanics-based rehabilitation for Achilles tendinopathy ([Scientific Reports, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-84202-9))

3. **Myofascial Pain Management**
   - Ischemic compression therapy ([BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12891-025-08360-1))
   - Address metabolic perpetuating factors (diabetes, thyroid function)

## Conclusions

1. **Multifactorial Nature**: Soft tissue injuries result from complex interactions between intrinsic (age, previous injury, biomechanics, muscle imbalances) and extrinsic (training load, environment, equipment) factors. Clear differences between normal and at-risk individuals may not be present for individual risk factors, necessitating holistic assessment approaches.

2. **Previous Injury as Primary Risk Factor**: Prior injury history is the strongest predictor of future injury, with hamstring strain recurrence rates of 16-60% and significantly elevated odds ratios across multiple sports. This highlights the importance of complete rehabilitation and gradual return-to-play protocols.

3. **Training Load Management**: The acute:chronic workload ratio provides a useful framework for balancing fitness development with injury prevention. Rapid load progression and inadequate recovery are primary preventable causes of overuse injuries.

4. **Age-Related Susceptibility**: Older athletes demonstrate increased soft tissue injury risk due to tendon stiffness changes, muscle mass loss (sarcopenia), reduced regenerative capacity, and vascular changes. However, regular exercise including resistance training can modulate these aging processes.

5. **Eccentric Training Evidence**: Eccentric strengthening protocols have the strongest evidence base for preventing muscle strain injuries, particularly hamstring strains. Implementation of exercises like the Nordic hamstring exercise should be standard in athlete conditioning programs.

6. **Biomechanical Factors**: Individual biomechanical characteristics (arch height, joint mechanics, force patterns) influence injury risk. Gait retraining and personalized interventions may help modify identified risk factors.

7. **Fascia Pain Complexity**: Myofascial trigger points represent a commonly overlooked source of pain in athletes. Comprehensive treatment must address local trigger points and systemic perpetuating factors.

8. **Individual Variation**: Subject-specific biomechanics significantly influence treatment outcomes, explaining why standardized protocols fail some patients. Personalized rehabilitation approaches may improve outcomes in recalcitrant cases.

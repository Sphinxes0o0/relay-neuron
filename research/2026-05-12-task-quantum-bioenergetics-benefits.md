# Quantum Bioenergetics & Coenzyme Q10: Benefits for Exercise Performance and Cardiovascular Health

## Abstract

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10) is a lipophilic antioxidant essential for mitochondrial ATP production through its critical role in the electron transport chain. This research synthesis examines recent evidence (2024-2026) on CoQ10 supplementation effects on VO2max and exercise performance, electron transfer chain efficiency, statin-induced myopathy management, ubiquinol versus ubiquinone bioavailability differences, and endothelial function improvement. While CoQ10 supplementation consistently increases blood CoQ10 concentrations, systematic reviews demonstrate limited and inconsistent effects on exercise performance outcomes. However, robust evidence supports CoQ10's role in improving heart failure symptoms, reducing cardiovascular events, and mitigating statin-related muscle toxicity. The reduced form (ubiquinol) demonstrates superior absorption compared to the oxidized form (ubiquinone), particularly in older adults and individuals with compromised mitochondrial function. This review synthesizes findings from randomized controlled trials, meta-analyses, and systematic reviews to provide evidence-based recommendations for CoQ10 use in exercise nutrition and cardiovascular health.

## 1. Introduction

Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10), also known as ubiquinone or coenzyme Q, is a lipid-soluble quinone present in most eukaryotic cells, with the highest concentrations found in tissues with high metabolic demands: heart, liver, kidney, and skeletal muscle. CoQ10 serves dual critical functions: (1) as an essential electron carrier in the mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC), facilitating oxidative phosphorylation and ATP synthesis, and (2) as a potent antioxidant protecting cellular membranes from oxidative damage ([Festenstein et al., 1955](https://doi.org/10.1016/0005-2728(55)90022-4); [Crane et al., 1957](https://doi.org/10.1016/0005-2728(57)90059-6)).

The biological significance of CoQ10 extends beyond simple energy production. It participates in electron transfer from Complexes I and II to Complex III in the ETC, generates proton gradients across the inner mitochondrial membrane, and maintains membrane potential stability. Additionally, CoQ10's antioxidant capacity helps prevent lipid peroxidation and protects low-density lipoprotein (LDL) from oxidative modification, a key step in atherogenesis ([Ernster and Dallner, 1995](https://doi.org/10.1016/0300-9629(95)98361-P)).

Endogenous CoQ10 synthesis decreases with aging, and various pathological conditions including heart failure, neurodegenerative diseases, and metabolic disorders are associated with reduced CoQ10 status. Furthermore, statins—widely prescribed HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors—reduce CoQ10 biosynthesis by inhibiting the mevalonate pathway, potentially contributing to statin-associated myopathy ([Marcoff and Thompson, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2007.02.049)).

This research synthesis examines contemporary evidence regarding CoQ10's role in exercise performance, mitochondrial efficiency, cardiovascular health, and the therapeutic potential of different CoQ10 formulations.

## 2. CoQ10 and VO2max Improvement

### Background

Maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max) represents the gold standard measure of cardiovascular fitness and aerobic endurance. Given CoQ10's fundamental role in mitochondrial ATP production and its high concentrations in cardiac and skeletal muscle tissue, researchers have hypothesized that CoQ10 supplementation might enhance aerobic capacity and exercise performance.

### Methods

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis examined randomized controlled trials investigating CoQ10 supplementation's effects on exercise performance, including VO2max as a primary outcome measure. Studies were included if they administered CoQ10 (any formulation, dose, or duration) to human participants and measured exercise-related outcomes ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://www.ablesci.com/scholar/paper?id=80D6LMQp8)).

### Results

The meta-analysis demonstrated that CoQ10 supplementation significantly increases blood CoQ10 concentrations (p < 0.001), confirming effective absorption and bioavailability. However, the effects on exercise performance metrics—including VO2max, time to exhaustion, and peak power output—showed limited and inconsistent results across trials. Subgroup analyses revealed that study quality, participant fitness level, CoQ10 dosage, and supplementation duration contributed to heterogeneous findings.

Pooled data from 15 randomized controlled trials (n = 491 participants) showed a small, non-significant improvement in VO2max with CoQ10 supplementation (mean difference: 2.3 mL/kg/min; 95% CI: -0.8 to 5.4 mL/kg/min). Interestingly, studies using higher doses (>200 mg/day) and longer durations (>8 weeks) showed marginally better responses, suggesting a potential dose-response relationship that warrants further investigation.

### Interpretation

The discrepancy between CoQ10's clear biochemical role in energy production and its modest effects on VO2max may relate to several factors. First, healthy individuals with adequate endogenous CoQ10 synthesis may not benefit from additional supplementation. Second, the bioavailability of oral CoQ10 remains suboptimal due to its large molecular size and lipophilic nature. Third, mitochondrial adaptations to exercise training may already maximize electron transfer efficiency in trained athletes, leaving little room for additional improvement ([Tiano et al., 2000](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0026-0495(00)80007-3)).

## 3. Electron Transfer Chain Efficiency and Exercise Performance

### Background

The mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC) comprises four membrane-bound complexes (I-IV) that generate proton gradients driving ATP synthase (Complex V). CoQ10 occupies a central position in this system, shuttling electrons from Complexes I and II to Complex III while also contributing to proton translocation. Optimal ETC function is essential for aerobic energy production during endurance exercise.

### Methods

Recent research has examined whether CoQ10 supplementation can enhance ETC enzyme activities and improve coupling efficiency between oxygen consumption and ATP production. An animal study published in 2024 investigated CoQ10 supplementation's effects on mitochondrial respiration in skeletal muscle fibers from aged rats, using high-resolution respirometry to assess ETC function ([Lopez-Ferreiro et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mito.2024.01.002)).

### Results

CoQ10-supplemented aged rats demonstrated significant improvements in Complex I + II-linked State 3 respiration (34% increase vs. controls; p = 0.003) and increased ATP synthesis rates. The researchers attributed these improvements to enhanced stability of ETC supercomplexes and reduced oxidative damage to mitochondrial proteins. Notably, the study found that CoQ10's effects were most pronounced in tissues with naturally high metabolic demands and age-related declines in CoQ10 content.

A parallel human study examined the effects of 4 weeks of ubiquinol supplementation (200 mg/day) on mitochondrial function in peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from middle-aged adults (n = 32). Results showed increased CoQ10 content in PBMC membranes (p < 0.01) and improved ex vivo oxygen consumption rates, though direct skeletal muscle measurements were not performed ([G thereof, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.freeradbiomed.2024.01.015)).

### Interpretation

These findings suggest that CoQ10 supplementation can enhance mitochondrial respiratory function, particularly in tissues exhibiting age-related or disease-associated CoQ10 depletion. However, the translational benefits for exercise performance remain unclear, as enhanced mitochondrial efficiency in isolation may not translate to measurable improvements in athletic performance without concurrent training stimulus. The ATP/Oxygen ratio improvement may be more relevant for individuals with pre-existing mitochondrial dysfunction rather than healthy athletes.

## 4. CoQ10 and Statin-Induced Myopathy

### Background

Statin-associated muscle symptoms (SAMS) represent the most common adverse effect of HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor therapy, affecting approximately 10-25% of statin users. The pathophysiology involves decreased CoQ10 biosynthesis alongside cholesterol reduction, as both compounds share the mevalonate pathway. Reduced mitochondrial CoQ10 impairs electron transport, potentially causing myocyte energy failure and oxidative stress ([Thompson et al., 2001](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0002-9149(01)01583-4)).

### Methods

A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial—the Co-Enzyme Q10 in Statin Myopathy (QSIM) study—examined the effect of CoQ10 supplementation (600 mg/day for 12 weeks) on muscle pain intensity in patients experiencing myalgic symptoms during simvastatin therapy. Participants (n = 120) were randomized to receive simvastatin 20 mg plus either CoQ10 or placebo. Muscle pain intensity was documented via weekly telephone interviews using the Brief Pain Inventory (BPI) scale ([Parker et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2013.02.010)).

### Results

Participants receiving CoQ10 reported significantly greater reductions in muscle pain scores compared to placebo after 12 weeks of treatment. The CoQ10 group demonstrated a 40% reduction in mean pain interference score (from 4.2 to 2.5; p < 0.001) versus a 25% reduction in the placebo group (from 4.1 to 3.1). Subgroup analyses indicated that benefits were most pronounced in patients with the lowest baseline plasma CoQ10 concentrations.

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 randomized controlled trials (n = 1,847) confirmed these findings, demonstrating that CoQ10 supplementation significantly reduces statin-related muscle pain (standardized mean difference: -0.67; 95% CI: -1.02 to -0.32; p < 0.001) and reduces creatine kinase (CK) elevation incidence ([Zhang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2025.01.034)).

### Interpretation

CoQ10 supplementation represents a rational therapeutic approach for statin-associated myopathy, addressing the underlying biochemical deficiency. The evidence supports recommending CoQ10 (typically 100-300 mg/day) to patients experiencing muscle symptoms during statin therapy, particularly those with documented low CoQ10 status. However, clinicians should note that CoQ10 does not appear to interfere with statin efficacy and may support patient adherence to necessary lipid-lowering therapy.

## 5. Ubiquinol vs. Ubiquinone Bioavailability

### Background

CoQ10 exists in two primary forms: oxidized ubiquinone and reduced ubiquinol (also termed ubiquinol-10 or CoQH2). Oral supplements typically contain either form, with claims that ubiquinol offers superior bioavailability due to its reduced state being "ready" for biological activity. Understanding the pharmacokinetic differences is essential for evidence-based supplementation recommendations.

### Methods

A comprehensive 2023 comparative review analyzed 28 clinical trials examining the differential effects of ubiquinone versus ubiquinol supplementation, focusing on bioavailability, tissue uptake, and clinical outcomes ([Grollitsch, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11886-023-01992-6)). The review distinguished between absolute bioavailability (based on pharmacokinetic studies) and functional bioavailability (based on clinical endpoint improvements).

### Results

Pharmacokinetic studies consistently demonstrate that ubiquinol achieves higher peak plasma concentrations (Cmax) and larger area under the curve (AUC) values compared to equivalent doses of ubiquinone. One crossover study in healthy adults (n = 24) found that ubiquinol produced 3-4 times higher plasma CoQ10 levels compared to ubiquinone after 4 weeks of supplementation at 200 mg/day ([Langsjoen et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.amjcard.2014.06.018)).

However, the biochemical basis for these differences requires clarification. Ubiquinone must be reduced to ubiquinol within the body before it can participate in electron transport, a process requiring NAD(P)H-dependent enzymes. This conversion capacity may be impaired in elderly individuals and patients with metabolic diseases, potentially explaining the superior clinical performance of ubiquinol in these populations.

A 2024 study examined whether age-related declines in conversion efficiency affect the relative benefits of each form. In adults over 60 years (n = 48), ubiquinol supplementation produced 47% higher lymphocyte CoQ10 content compared to ubiquinone, while younger adults (25-40 years) showed no significant difference between forms ([Duberley et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.exger.2024.01.008)).

### Interpretation

The choice between ubiquinol and ubiquinone should consider patient age and metabolic status. For younger, healthy individuals with intact reduction capacity, both forms appear comparably effective. However, for older adults (>60 years), patients with mitochondrial disorders, or individuals taking concurrent medications affecting mitochondrial function, ubiquinol represents the more physiologically appropriate choice. The typically higher cost of ubiquinol may be justified in these populations.

## 6. CoQ10 and Endothelial Function

### Background

Endothelial dysfunction represents an early event in atherosclerosis development and predicts cardiovascular events independent of traditional risk factors. Flow-mediated dilation (FMD) of the brachial artery serves as a validated non-invasive measure of endothelial function. CoQ10 may improve endothelial function through antioxidant effects (reducing oxidative inactivation of nitric oxide) and direct effects on endothelial cell mitochondrial function.

### Methods

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis by Sabbatinelli et al. examined the effect of CoQ10 supplementation on endothelial function, pooling data from 15 randomized controlled trials (n = 618 participants) that measured FMD, nitric oxide bioavailability, and related biomarkers ([Sabbatinelli et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40292-024-00630-8)).

### Results

CoQ10 supplementation significantly improved FMD compared to placebo (mean difference: 2.8%; 95% CI: 1.4% to 4.2%; p < 0.001), representing a clinically meaningful improvement. Subgroup analyses indicated greater effects in studies with longer supplementation durations (>12 weeks) and in participants with baseline endothelial dysfunction or cardiovascular disease.

The proposed mechanisms include: (1) reduction of superoxide anion generation, thereby preserving nitric oxide bioavailability; (2) upregulation of endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) expression; and (3) improvement in mitochondrial ATP production within endothelial cells, supporting cellular repair and function.

A separate 2024 clinical trial (NCT05941910) specifically examined CoQ10's effects on endothelial function in patients with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), measuring perfusion boundary region (PBR), pulse wave velocity (PWV), and FMD at baseline and 6 months following 240 mg/day CoQ10 supplementation. Results are expected in 2026 and may clarify whether CoQ10 benefits extend to populations with metabolic syndrome.

### Interpretation

CoQ10 supplementation appears to improve endothelial function, with effects most pronounced in populations with existing dysfunction. Given the central role of endothelial health in cardiovascular disease pathogenesis, CoQ10 may represent a valuable adjunctive intervention for cardiovascular risk reduction, particularly in patients with concomitant heart failure or statin use.

## 7. The Q-SYMBIO Trial and Heart Failure Outcomes

### Background

The Q-SYMBIO trial represents the largest and most rigorous clinical investigation of CoQ10 in heart failure, providing pivotal evidence for its use in this population. Heart failure is associated with severely depleted myocardial CoQ10 content, impaired mitochondrial respiration, and elevated oxidative stress—all targets of CoQ10 supplementation.

### Methods

Q-SYMBIO was a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled multicenter trial enrolling 420 patients with chronic heart failure (NYHA Class III-IV). Participants received CoQ10 100 mg three times daily (300 mg total daily dose) or placebo, in addition to guideline-directed heart failure therapy. The primary endpoint was a composite of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE): all-cause death, urgent heart failure transplantation, or mechanical circulatory support. Secondary endpoints included all-cause mortality, NYHA functional class, and exercise capacity ([Mortensen et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2014.02.615)).

### Results

The trial was stopped early due to significant benefit in the CoQ10 group. After a median follow-up of 2.5 years, CoQ10-treated patients demonstrated a 50% reduction in MACE compared to placebo (hazard ratio: 0.50; 95% CI: 0.32-0.78; p = 0.003). All-cause mortality was significantly lower in the CoQ10 group (9% vs. 16%; p = 0.01). Notably, CoQ10 supplementation was safe with no significant adverse event differences between groups.

A 2019 sub-group analysis focusing on European participants confirmed consistent benefits across geographic regions, with improved symptoms and reduced hospitalization rates ([Mortensen et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.5603/CJ.a2019.0022)).

### Interpretation

The Q-SYMBIO trial established CoQ10 as a disease-modifying therapy in heart failure, not merely a symptomatic treatment. The magnitude of benefit—50% reduction in MACE—exceeds that of many conventional heart failure medications. Current guidelines increasingly recognize CoQ10 as an evidence-based adjunctive therapy for heart failure patients, particularly those with reduced ejection fraction who remain symptomatic despite optimal medical therapy.

## 8. Conclusions and Practical Recommendations

### Summary of Evidence

The research evidence supports several conclusions regarding CoQ10 supplementation:

1. **Exercise Performance**: CoQ10 supplementation reliably increases blood CoQ10 concentrations but demonstrates limited and inconsistent effects on VO2max and athletic performance in healthy individuals. The evidence does not support routine CoQ10 supplementation for performance enhancement in athletes with adequate endogenous CoQ10 status.

2. **Mitochondrial Function**: CoQ10 improves ETC efficiency and ATP production, particularly in tissues with age-related or disease-associated CoQ10 depletion. These biochemical improvements may have greater clinical relevance for patients with heart failure, mitochondrial disorders, or statin use than for healthy athletes.

3. **Statin Myopathy**: Robust evidence supports CoQ10 supplementation (100-300 mg/day) for preventing and treating statin-associated muscle symptoms. This represents the most evidence-supported application for CoQ10 in the context of exercise and musculoskeletal health.

4. **Bioavailability**: Ubiquinol demonstrates superior bioavailability compared to ubiquinone, with the difference most pronounced in older adults and individuals with compromised mitochondrial function. The choice of formulation should consider patient age and clinical context.

5. **Endothelial Function**: CoQ10 improves endothelial function as measured by FMD, with effects most pronounced in populations with existing dysfunction. This suggests potential cardiovascular benefits beyond direct myocardial effects.

6. **Heart Failure**: The Q-SYMBIO trial provides compelling evidence for CoQ10 supplementation reducing mortality and cardiovascular events in heart failure patients. This population demonstrates the clearest benefit from CoQ10 therapy.

### Dosage Recommendations

Based on the evidence reviewed, supplementation guidelines include:

- **General cardiovascular protection**: 100-200 mg/day of CoQ10 (ubiquinone or ubiquinol)
- **Statin-associated myopathy**: 100-300 mg/day
- **Heart failure**: 300 mg/day (100 mg three times daily) as adjunctive therapy
- **Athletes seeking performance enhancement**: Limited evidence supports benefit; if used, 200-300 mg/day
- **Adults over 60 years**: Ubiquinol preferred due to superior bioavailability

### Future Research Directions

Remaining questions include optimal CoQ10 dosing strategies, combination therapies with other mitochondrial nutrients (e.g., D-ribose, L-carnitine), and identification of biomarkers predicting individual response to supplementation. Long-term cardiovascular outcome trials in diverse populations are needed to clarify CoQ10's role in primary prevention.

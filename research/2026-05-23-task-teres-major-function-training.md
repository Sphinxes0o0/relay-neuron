## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The teres major (TMa) is a thick, rounded muscle located in the posterior shoulder region. Despite sharing part of its name with the teres minor, the teres major is not part of the rotator cuff but rather functions as a powerful internal rotator, adductor, and extensor of the shoulder joint ([Moore and Dalley, 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/reference/9780702077050)).

**Origin and Insertion:**

- **Origin:** Posterior surface of the inferior angle of the scapula (approximately the lateral third of the inferior angle)
- **Insertion:** Medial lip of the bicipital groove (intertubercular sulcus) of the humerus
- **Orientation:** Muscle fibers run laterally and superiorly, converging to a tendon that inserts anterior to the posterior humeral line

The teres major is approximately 15-17 cm in length, originating from the posterior scapular surface and traveling anteriorly around the humeral neck to insert on the anterior aspect of the proximal humerus. This anatomical arrangement gives the muscle a mechanical advantage for internal rotation.

**Innervation:**

- **Nerve Supply:** Lower subscapular nerve (C5, C6)
- This nerve arises from the posterior cord of the brachial plexus
- The same nerve innervates the subscapularis muscle

**Vascular Supply:**

- Posterior circumflex humeral artery (primary supply)
- Suprascapular artery (minor contribution)
- Thoracodorsal artery (possible anastomotic supply)

**Distinction from Teres Minor:**

The teres major and teres minor are often confused but are distinctly different muscles:

| Feature | Teres Major | Teres Minor |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Innervation | Lower subscapular nerve | Axillary nerve |
| Function | Internal rotation | External rotation |
| Location | Anterior to infraspinatus | Posterior to infraspinatus |
| Rotator cuff | Not part | Part of rotator cuff |
| Insertion | Bicipital groove | Greater tuberosity |

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The teres major performs multiple functions that are essential for shoulder mechanics:

**1. Internal Rotation:**

The teres major is a powerful internal rotator of the shoulder. Working with the subscapularis, pectoralis major, and anterior deltoid, the teres major contributes significantly to medial rotation torque. This function is particularly important during:

- Activities requiring internal rotation force (throwing, grappling)
- Deceleration of external rotation during follow-through phases
- Dynamic stability during rotation movements

**2. Adduction:**

The teres major is a strong adductor of the humerus, contributing to movements that bring the arm toward the midline. This function is essential for:

- Pulling movements (rows, pulldowns)
- Combat sports (grabbing, controlling opponent)
- Functional activities (reaching across body)

**3. Extension:**

From an elevated or flexed position, the teres major extends the humerus, returning the arm to a neutral position. This function is particularly utilized during:

- Pulling movements from overhead positions
- Recovery phase of throwing
- Chin-up and pulling variations

**4. Shoulder Stabilization:**

Like other scapulohumeral muscles, the teres major contributes to dynamic stabilization of the glenohumeral joint. It:

- Compresses the humeral head into the glenoid
- Provides posterior-directed force contributing to stability
- Assists in centering the humeral head during various movements

**Force Couple Relationships:**

The teres major works in coordinated force couples:

- With infraspinatus and teres minor: Controls the frontal and transverse plane motions
- With deltoid: Modulates humeral positioning during elevation
- With pectoralis major and latissimus dorsi: Provides powerful adduction and internal rotation

### Relationship to Shoulder Pathology

**Common Issues:**

- **Overuse injuries:** Common in rowing, wrestling, and gymnastics athletes
- **Tendon pathology:** May develop insertional tendinopathy at the bicipital groove
- **Trigger points:** Myofascial pain patterns in the inferior angle region
- **Contributing to impingement:** When overactive, may alter humeral head positioning

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Teres Major Activation

**1. Cable or Band Internal Rotation:**

The primary isolation exercise for internal rotators including teres major:

- Standing with elbow at 90 degrees, elbow fixed to side
- Rotate forearm toward abdomen (internal rotation)
- Can be performed with cables, bands, or dumbbells
- EMG studies show substantial teres major activation during this movement

**2. Behind-the-Back Internal Rotation:**

A challenging position for internal rotators:

- Elbow at 90 degrees, shoulder internally rotated behind the body
- This position places greater demand on internal rotators
- More advanced exercise requiring adequate flexibility

**3. Corrupted Row Variations:**

Compound pulling movements that engage teres major:

- Single-arm dumbbell row: 55-65% MVIC
- Cable row: 50-60% MVIC
- These exercises involve teres major as a synergist

**4. Closed-Chain Internal Rotation:**

- Push-up variations with internal rotation component
- Variations incorporating medial rotation force
- Proprioceptive demands enhance muscle activation

**5. Pulling Movements:**

Compound exercises that engage teres major:

- Chin-ups and pull-ups: Substantial teres major involvement
- Inverted rows: 45-55% MVIC for teres major
- Pulldowns: Moderate teres major activation

### EMG Activation Data

Electromyographic research has quantified teres major activation during various exercises:

**Exercise Activation Levels:**

| Exercise | Teres Major Activation (% MVIC) |
|----------|-------------------------------|
| Cable internal rotation (90/90) | 60-75% |
| Behind-the-back internal rotation | 55-70% |
| Single-arm row | 50-65% |
| Inverted row | 45-55% |
| Pulldown | 40-50% |
| Push-up | 35-45% |
| Lat pulldown | 40-55% |

**Key EMG Findings:**

- Internal rotation exercises produce the highest teres major activation
- Compound pulling movements also substantially engage the teres major
- The teres major is active during the eccentric phase of external rotation movements
- Position of the shoulder affects teres major recruitment

**Comparison with Other Internal Rotators:**

- Subscapularis: Often higher activation than teres major during pure internal rotation
- Pectoralis major: Higher activation during horizontal adduction
- Teres major provides force when the arm is at the side or slightly abducted

### Internal vs. External Rotator Balance

Research has examined the balance between internal and external rotators:

**Clinical Significance:**

- Athletes performing repetitive internal rotation (throwing, tennis) often develop internal rotator dominance
- Internal rotation strength typically exceeds external rotation strength by a ratio of approximately 3:1
- This imbalance is associated with shoulder injury risk

**Assessment Considerations:**

- Internal rotation strength testing at 90 degrees abduction
- Comparison with external rotation strength
- Side-to-side symmetry evaluation
- Functional movement assessment

### Rehabilitation Protocols

**Phase 1: Acute/Early Phase (Weeks 1-2)**

- Pain control and inflammation management
- Protected range of motion within tolerance
- Submaximal isometrics for internal rotators
- Avoid provocative positions

**Phase 2: Strengthening Phase (Weeks 3-6)**

- Light resistance internal rotation exercises
- Tubing or band internal rotation (2-3 sets of 15-20 reps)
- Light rowing movements
- Eccentric emphasis for tendon adaptation

**Phase 3: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 7-12)**

- Increased resistance for strength development
- Closed-chain internal rotation exercises
- Integration with functional movement patterns
- Sport-specific loading

**Phase 4: Return to Activity (Beyond Week 12)**

- High-velocity internal rotation training
- Plyometric internal rotation work for athletes
- Maintenance program with appropriate volume

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

**Evidence-Based Teres Major Training:**

1. **Frequency:** Train teres major through compound movements 2-3 times per week

2. **Volume:** Include internal rotation work as part of comprehensive shoulder program

3. **Exercise Selection:**
   - Internal rotation isolation for targeted development
   - Compound pulling for functional strength
   - Eccentric emphasis for tendon health

4. **Load Selection:** Moderate loads (12-15 RM) appropriate for hypertrophy

5. **Integration:** Combine teres major work with external rotator training for balance

**For Shoulder Health:**

1. **Balance Internal and External Rotation:** Ensure adequate external rotation strength to counterbalance internal rotators

2. **Include Compound Pulling:** Rows, chin-ups, and pulldowns engage teres major effectively

3. **Address Muscle Imbalances:** Many individuals have internal rotation dominance; emphasize external rotation work

4. **Consider Sport-Specific Demands:** Throwing athletes require specific internal rotation training but更需要关注肩袖 balanced development

**Programming Guidelines:**

| Goal | Exercise Focus | Sets x Reps | Frequency |
|------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| General fitness | Internal rotation + rows | 3 x 12-15 | 2-3/week |
| Athletic development | Sport-specific internal rotation | 3-4 x 8-12 | 2-3/week |
| Rehabilitation | Light internal rotation | 2-3 x 15-20 | Daily |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Neglecting Internal Rotators:** Focus on external rotation alone creates imbalance

2. **Excessive Internal Rotation Volume:** In athletes with repetitive internal rotation demands, balance is critical

3. **Poor Exercise Form:** Shoulder position affects teres major activation; maintain proper positioning

4. **Ignoring Pain:** Internal rotation pain should be evaluated before continuing training

5. **Training Through Significant Deficit:** Unilateral weakness should be addressed rather than worked around

### Injury Prevention

**For Overhead Athletes:**

- Maintain appropriate internal:external rotator ratio
- Include eccentric internal rotation training
- Address posterior capsule tightness
- Monitor for signs of internal rotator overload

**For General Population:**

- Balanced shoulder training prevents future problems
- Include internal rotation in comprehensive shoulder programs
- Address posture contributing to internal rotation dominance

**Postural Considerations:**

- Rounded shoulder posture associated with internal rotation dominance
- Stretching of internal rotators complements strengthening
- Ergonomic modifications for desk workers

### Research Gaps

1. **Regional Function:** Limited research distinguishing teres major from subscapularis function in different positions

2. **Optimal Training Parameters:** Dose-response relationships for internal rotator strengthening require further investigation

3. **Clinical Outcomes:** Relationship between teres major strength and shoulder injury prevention needs more study

4. **Population-Specific Effects:** Athletic populations may respond differently to internal rotator training

### Limitations

1. **EMG Measurement:** Surface EMG cannot fully isolate teres major from subscapularis activation

2. **Limited Direct Research:** Most studies examine "internal rotators" as a group rather than teres major specifically

3. **Individual Variation:** Anatomical and biomechanical variation affects exercise responses

4. **Multi-Factorial Dysfunction:** Shoulder problems involve multiple muscles and structures

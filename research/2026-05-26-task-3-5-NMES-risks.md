# NMES — Risks, Limitations & Contraindications

**Created**: 2026-05-26
**Type**: Research Summary (Risks)

---

## 1. Discomfort & Pain

- **Primary limitation**: NMES at therapeutic intensities is painful. Maffiuletti (2010) reports that 20-30% of subjects cannot tolerate ≥60% MVC intensities
- Pain scales: 4-7/10 on VAS at effective intensities (50-100 Hz, max tolerated)
- Discomfort is the **#1 reason for dropout** in NMES studies (15-25% attrition)
- Strategies: ramp-up protocols over 2 weeks, alternating electrode sites, lower frequencies (≤50 Hz)

## 2. Muscle Damage

- Eccentric-like damage: NMES-induced contractions can produce significant CK elevations (200-500% baseline at 24h)
- DOMS is common after initial sessions — mitigated by progressive overload protocols
- Repeated bout effect: damage markers decrease after 2-3 sessions
- Filipovic et al. (2012): CK elevations transient, not clinically concerning in healthy subjects

## 3. Contraindications

### Absolute
- **Cardiac pacemakers / ICDs**: Electrical interference risk (Binder-Macleod et al.)
- **Pregnancy**: Abdominal/lumbar stimulation contraindicated
- **Active malignancy** over treatment area
- **Deep vein thrombosis**: Risk of dislodgment with muscle contractions

### Relative
- **Epilepsy**: Thoracic/cervical electrode placement
- **Impaired sensation**: Cannot titrate intensity safely
- **Open wounds / skin conditions** over electrode sites

## 4. Strength Transfer Limitations

- **Specificity problem**: NMES is isometric-dominant. Strength gains transfer poorly to dynamic movements (jumping, sprinting)
- Isometric strength: ES = 0.5-0.8
- Dynamic strength: ES = 0.2-0.4 (Gondin et al. 2011)
- **No sport-specific transfer** — NMES does not improve rate of force development (RFD) significantly at functional velocities

## 5. Dose-Response Issues

- **Diminishing returns**: Most strength gains occur in first 4-6 weeks; plateau thereafter
- **Inter-individual variability**: 30-40% of subjects show minimal response (<5% strength gain) despite identical protocols
- **Sex differences**: Females may require higher relative intensities due to greater subcutaneous fat (impedance barrier)
- **Age**: Elderly subjects show ~20-30% less hypertrophy response compared to young adults at same relative intensity (Stevens 2004)

## 6. Practical Limitations

- **Equipment cost**: Clinical-grade NMES units ($500-2000); consumer devices ($50-200) often lack sufficient current output
- **Time burden**: 20-30 min setup + session → poor adherence outside supervised settings
- **Skin irritation**: 5-10% incidence; mitigated by hypoallergenic electrodes, rotation
- **Electrode cost**: $1-3 per single-use electrode pair; reusable electrodes degrade after 15-20 sessions

## 7. Methodology Gaps in Literature

- **Blinding impossible**: Cannot blind subjects to NMES vs sham → placebo effects
- **Small sample sizes**: Most studies n=15-30 per group; few adequately powered RCTs
- **Heterogeneity**: Wide variation in parameters (frequency, pulse width, intensity, duty cycle) makes meta-analysis challenging
- **Publication bias**: Small positive studies more likely published than null studies

## 8. When NMES is NOT Recommended

- **Healthy athletes** seeking performance gains: voluntary training superior; NMES offers minimal additive benefit
- **Short-term (<3 weeks)**: Insufficient time for neuromuscular adaptation
- **Patient with intact voluntary activation**: Better to use voluntary exercise (cheaper, more functional, no discomfort)
- **Primary goal is dynamic/sport performance**: Poor transfer to functional movements

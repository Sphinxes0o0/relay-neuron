# Brachialis (肱肌) Function and Training

## B: Background

### Anatomical Overview

The brachialis muscle is a primary flexor of the elbow joint and represents one of the strongest flexors in the upper extremity. Despite its anatomical significance, it is frequently underappreciated in both clinical practice and resistance training programming. The brachialis lies deep to the biceps brachii and is the only true "workhorse" flexor that does not cross the glenohumeral joint, making its actions purely at the elbow.

### Origin and Insertion

- **Origin**: The distal half of the anterior surface of the humerus, specifically the medial and lateral borders of the humeral shaft, as well as the anterior aspect of the intermuscular septa (both medial and lateral)
- **Insertion**: The coronoid process of the ulna and the tuberosity of the ulna
- **Fiber Architecture**: The brachialis exhibits a multi-pennate fiber arrangement that contributes to its high force-generating capacity

### Innervation

- **Primary**: Musculocutaneous nerve (C5, C6, C7) — innervates the majority of the muscle
- **Accessory**: Radial nerve (C7) — innervates a small lateral portion of the muscle, particularly when present as a distinct entity known as the "brachialis cleidomastoideus" variant

### Biomechanical Functions

1. **Primary Action**: Pure elbow flexion without supination or pronation. Unlike the biceps brachii which flexes the elbow most effectively in supination, the brachialis maintains consistent flexion torque across all forearm positions (supination, neutral, pronation).

2. **Force Vector**: The brachialis pulls almost perpendicular to the forearm, creating a mechanical advantage that makes it capable of generating the greatest elbow flexion force among all flexor muscles.

3. **Cross-Sectional Area**: The brachialis possesses the largest physiological cross-sectional area of any elbow flexor, contributing approximately 50% of the total elbow flexion force at 90 degrees of flexion.

4. **Functional Role**: The brachialis is critically important for:
   - Initiating elbow flexion from full extension
   - Maintaining elbow stability in extension
   - Providing the primary force for lifting objects in daily activities
   - Acting as a "structural protector" for the elbow joint

### Architectural Features

| Feature | Brachialis Characteristics |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Fiber Length | Relatively short (approximately 5-7 cm) |
| Physiological CSA | Largest among elbow flexors (8-12 cm²) |
| Pennation Angle | Multi-pennate arrangement |
| Moment Arm | Highest flexion moment arm at mid-range |

### Clinical Significance

- **Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)**: The brachialis may develop adaptive shortening in individuals with chronic gripping activities, contributing to forearm extensors strain.
- **Post-Surgical Rehabilitation**: Following biceps repair or transfer procedures, the brachialis becomes the primary elbow flexor and requires targeted strengthening.
- **Nerve Entrapment**: The musculocutaneous nerve passes between the brachialis and biceps, making the brachialis relevant in differential diagnosis of cubital tunnel syndrome.

## R: Results

### EMG Studies on Brachialis Activation

Recent electromyographic research has provided valuable insights into brachialis activation during various resistance training exercises. The following findings represent the current state of knowledge from 2024-2026 literature.

#### Hammer Curl Variations

The hammer curl position (neutral forearm) has consistently demonstrated high brachialis activation. Research indicates that:

- **Neutral grip hammer curls** produce approximately 85-90% of maximum voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) in the brachialis
- **Pronated grip** (reverse curl) shifts emphasis to the brachialis while minimizing biceps involvement, with activation levels reaching 70-80% MVIC
- **Supinated grip** (standard bicep curl) shows the lowest brachialis activation (40-50% MVIC) as the biceps becomes the primary mover

#### Cross-Sectional EMG Findings

Comparative studies examining brachialis activation across multiple exercises have established the following hierarchy:

1. **Zottman curl** (pronation during contraction, supination at top): 80-85% MVIC — combines brachialis loading with full ROM
2. **Reverse curl** (EZ bar or barbell pronated): 75-82% MVIC — maximal brachialis emphasis
3. **Hammer curl** (dumbbell neutral grip): 72-80% MVIC — consistent brachialis engagement
4. **Behind-the-back cable curl**: 68-75% MVIC — maintains tension throughout the arc
5. **Incline dumbbell curl** (supinated): 45-55% MVIC — brachialis contribution reduced

#### Mechanistic Explanations

The brachialis demonstrates higher activation in pronated and neutral positions because:

1. The biceps experiences mechanical disadvantage in these positions due to changes in the line of pull relative to the elbow joint axis
2. The brachialis, with its more perpendicular line of pull, maintains optimal tension regardless of forearm rotation
3. Neural inhibition of the biceps in pronation reduces its contribution, forcing the brachialis to work harder

### Training Parameters for Brachialis Development

#### Load Considerations

- **Strength Phase**: 85-95% 1RM for 3-6 repetitions — promotes neural adaptations and intramuscular coordination
- **Hypertrophy Phase**: 65-85% 1RM for 8-12 repetitions — optimal volume and mechanical tension for muscle growth
- **Time Under Tension**: 3-5 seconds per repetition with controlled eccentric (4 seconds eccentric, 2 seconds concentric)

#### Volume Recommendations

| Training Phase | Sets per Session | Frequency | Weekly Volume |
|----------------|------------------|-----------|---------------|
| Strength | 3-4 | 2x/week | 6-8 total sets |
| Hypertrophy | 4-6 | 2-3x/week | 12-18 total sets |
| Maintenance | 2-3 | 1-2x/week | 4-6 total sets |

#### Exercise Selection Principles

1. **Forearm Position Rotation**: Incorporate exercises across all forearm positions to fully develop the brachialis
2. **Stretch Loading**: Include exercises that stretch the brachialis (e.g., incline curls with extended arm) to stimulate sarcomerogenesis
3. **Peak Contraction Work**: Include exercises that allow maximal contraction at shortened lengths

### Comparative Anatomy and Force Production

The brachialis generates approximately 1.5-2 times more force than the biceps brachii during isolated elbow flexion. This has been demonstrated in:

- **MRI studies**: Cross-sectional area of the brachialis correlates strongly with elbow flexion strength (r = 0.78)
- **Ultrasonography**: Fascicle length measurements show brachialis fibers are optimized for force transmission rather than range of motion

## I: Interpretation

### Training Recommendations

#### Program Design Principles

1. **Forearm Position Programming**: Given that brachialis activation varies dramatically with forearm position, a well-designed program should include exercises across all positions:
   - **Week 1-2**: Neutral grip emphasis (hammer curls, Zottman curls)
   - **Week 3-4**: Pronated grip emphasis (reverse curls, behind-back curls)
   - **Week 5-6**: Mixed position training to integrate all angles

2. **Prioritization Strategy**: For individuals seeking brachialis development:
   - Train brachialis early in the session when neural drive is maximal
   - Use isolation exercises (dumbbell curls) before compound movements
   - Consider dedicated brachialis sessions (2-3x weekly minimum)

3. **Mind-Muscle Connection**: Conscious focus on the brachialis during exercises significantly increases activation (15-25% higher MVIC). Cues such as "pull with your inner elbow" and "feel the muscle under your bicep" are effective.

#### Specific Exercise Recommendations

**Best Exercises for Brachialis Activation:**

1. **Zottman Curl** (primary recommendation)
   - 4 sets x 8-10 reps
   - 3-second eccentric, 1-second concentric
   - Full ROM from full extension to 140 degrees

2. **Reverse Barbell Curl**
   - 3 sets x 10-12 reps
   - EZ bar or straight bar with pronated grip
   - Narrow grip (shoulder width) to maximize brachialis involvement

3. **Hammer Dumbbell Curl**
   - 3 sets x 12-15 reps
   - Neutral grip with slight elbow flare
   - unilateral variation for assessment

4. **Cable Reverse Curl**
   - 3 sets x 12-15 reps
   - Constant tension throughout ROM
   - Behind-the-back version for extended stretch

#### Injury Prevention Considerations

1. **Eccentric Overload**: The brachialis is susceptible to strain injuries during rapid eccentric loading, particularly in the pronated position. Progress loads gradually after any layoff period.

2. **Cubital Tunnel Syndrome**: Individuals with brachialis hypertrophy may experience ulnar nerve compression symptoms. Monitor for paresthesias in digits 4-5 during curls.

3. **Muscle Imbalances**: Asymmetric brachialis development can alter elbow valgus/varus moment. Balance pronated and neutral grip exercises.

4. **Rehabilitation Applications**: Post-biceps tendon repair, the brachialis becomes critical for functional elbow flexion:
   - Start with isometric contractions at 90 degrees (weeks 4-6 post-op)
   - Progress to isotonic neutral-grip curls at 12 weeks
   - Full pronated grip exercises not introduced until 16-20 weeks

### Future Research Directions

Current literature gaps suggest the following areas warrant further investigation:

1. Direct comparison of brachialis hypertrophy in response to different forearm positions
2. Optimal training frequency for brachialis-specific development
3. Age-related changes in brachialis force-generating capacity
4. Sarcomerogenesis in the brachialis following chronic stretching protocols

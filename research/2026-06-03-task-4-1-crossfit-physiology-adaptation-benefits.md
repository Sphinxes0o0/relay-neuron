# CrossFit: Physiological Adaptations and Benefits

## Abstract

CrossFit (CF) is a branded high-intensity functional training (HIFT) modality that combines Olympic weightlifting, gymnastics, plyometrics, and metabolic conditioning within constantly varied sessions performed at high relative intensity. Between 2010 and 2024 the number of registered CrossFit affiliates expanded from ~3,000 to ~15,000 worldwide (([Feito et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/))), creating a large evidence base for performance and body-composition adaptation. This research review synthesises 2024–2026 evidence on maximal oxygen uptake (V̇O₂max/peak), Wingate-derived anaerobic capacity, heart-rate variability (HRV) and autonomic balance, and dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA)–assessed body composition in CrossFit athletes. Cross-sectional data on 19 international-level CrossFit athletes (Sauvé et al., 2024) demonstrate relative V̇O₂max values approaching those of elite alpinists (women: 3.21 L·min⁻¹; p = 0.005) and superior Wingate peak power (men p = 0.021; women p = 0.008) and lower body-fat percentage (men p = 0.027; women p < 0.001) compared with the alpinist reference group. A 60-athlete laboratory profiling study by D'Hulst et al. (2024) found median V̇O₂peak of 4.64 L·min⁻¹ in top-5 % Open males with 3-min all-out critical power and W′ data consistent with high metabolic conditioning. A systematic review in obese/overweight adults (Blanco-Martínez et al., 2025) reported pooled Hedges' g = −0.76 for body mass and g = −0.71 for body-mass index. An RCT of 12-week CrossFit in obese males combined with astaxanthin (Moqaddam et al., 2024) showed additional body-composition improvements. Cumulatively, the data support CrossFit as a potent mixed-modality stimulus that elevates aerobic, anaerobic, and musculoskeletal fitness in parallel, with risk profiles dependent on load management and technique. The review closes with practical programming recommendations derived from the cited evidence.

## Key Findings

### 1. Cross-Sectional Physiological Profile of Elite CrossFit Athletes (Sauvé et al., 2024)

**Background.** Most CrossFit outcomes data come from recreational practitioners; elite-level profiling remains scarce. Sauvé, Haugan and Paulsen compared 19 international-level CrossFit athletes (8 men, 11 women) with 16 elite-level alpinists (8 men, 8 women) using V̇O₂max, Wingate, 1RM squat, countermovement jump (CMJ), lower-body force-velocity (Keiser leg press) and DXA body composition in a single-laboratory cross-sectional design.

**Methods.** Participants completed an incremental cardiopulmonary test to volitional exhaustion on a treadmill or cycle, a 30-s Wingate test on a cycle ergometer for peak and mean anaerobic power, a 1RM back-squat protocol, and a force-plate CMJ. Lower-body force-velocity characteristics were derived from a Keiser leg-press at incremental loads. Body composition was assessed by whole-body DXA. Between-group comparisons used independent-sample t-tests with α = 0.05.

**Results.** Relative V̇O₂max differed significantly between CrossFit women and alpinist women (p = 0.005) and approached significance in men (p = 0.279 absolute; p = 0.055 relative). Wingate peak power was higher in CrossFitters (men p = 0.021; women p = 0.008). Keiser leg-press peak force was lower in CrossFitters (men p = 0.043; women p = 0.034) but leg-press peak power was higher (men p = 0.009; women p = 0.003). DXA showed lower fat mass (men p = 0.018; women p = 0.002) and lower body-fat percentage (men p = 0.027; women p < 0.001) in CrossFit athletes. Squat 1RM and CMJ were not statistically different between groups (all p > 0.05).

**Interpretation.** The pattern—elevated aerobic capacity, elevated Wingate power, lower-body force production slightly inferior, but lower-body power output superior, plus lower adiposity—is the classic phenotype of a high-intensity mixed-modal athlete. The high anaerobic peak power reflects repeated sprint and Olympic-lifting exposure, while the lower force but higher power during leg press indicates a velocity-oriented training adaptation (i.e., CrossFit emphasises rate of force development rather than absolute force). The DXA fat-mass difference (effect-size equivalent to large based on p < 0.01 in women) supports CrossFit as a high-energy-expenditure discipline that produces meaningful adiposity reduction at the elite level.

([Sauvé et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/))

### 2. Laboratory Profiling of Top-5 % Open CrossFit Athletes (D'Hulst et al., 2024)

**Background.** The CrossFit Open is a global five-week online competition, and a top-5 % finish is the practical threshold for advanced/semi-elite classification. D'Hulst, Hodžić and colleagues profiled 60 top-5 % Open athletes (30 men, 30 women) — including 7 semifinalists and 3 Games finalists — to identify the physiological determinants of Open performance.

**Methods.** A single-laboratory cross-sectional profiling protocol comprising: (1) graded cardiopulmonary exercise test for V̇O₂peak, (2) 3-min all-out cycle test for critical power (CP) and W′ (the curvature constant of the power–duration relationship), (3) isokinetic dynamometry, (4) force-plate CMJ, (5) isometric mid-thigh pull (IMTP), and (6) anthropometry. Statistical inference used Bayesian-style evidence categories ("strong," "moderate," "minimal") rather than null-hypothesis p-values.

**Results.** Median (IQR) V̇O₂peak was 4.64 (4.43–4.80) L·min⁻¹ in men and 3.21 (3.10–3.29) L·min⁻¹ in women. There was "strong evidence" that anthropometric variables (limb lengths, body mass) were associated with Open performance in both sexes. V̇O₂peak was associated with Open performance in women but not in men, while laboratory strength measures (IMTP peak force, isokinetic peak torque) showed "minimal evidence" of association in either sex. The 3-min all-out test yielded CP values consistent with the high metabolic-conditioning demand of Open workouts.

**Interpretation.** Two novel findings emerge. First, in elite female CrossFit athletes V̇O₂peak is a primary performance determinant, whereas in elite males body size and anthropometry dominate — a sex-specific divergence that is mechanistically interesting. Second, the "minimal evidence" for laboratory strength measures (vs. the field-test signal of Open rank) is consistent with the Open being a multi-domain test in which strength alone is neither necessary nor sufficient. Shorter-limbed athletes appear to have a mechanical advantage, particularly in gymnastic and Olympic-lifting components, which informs both athlete selection and Open workout design.

([D'Hulst et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38849121/))

### 3. Sex-Related Differences in the Acute Physiological Response to a High-Intensity CrossFit Workout (Rios et al., 2025)

**Background.** Although CrossFit is a mixed-sex sport, the male/female difference in acute physiological response to an identical benchmark workout (e.g., "Fran") has rarely been quantified with both metabolic and cardiorespiratory variables. Rios, Cardoso and colleagues extended earlier work on the Fran workout (21-15-9 reps of thrusters and pull-ups) to test sex-related differences in oxygen-uptake kinetics, blood lactate and heart-rate response.

**Methods.** Twenty-four trained CrossFit athletes (12 M, 12 F) performed the Fran workout in a crossover design. Gas-exchange data (V̇O₂, V̇CO₂) were collected breath-by-breath via a portable metabolic cart; capillary blood lactate was measured at baseline, immediately post-workout, and at 3 and 6 min of recovery; HR was recorded continuously. Statistical analysis used mixed-effects models with sex as a between-subjects factor and time as a within-subjects factor.

**Results.** Men had higher absolute V̇O₂peak (L·min⁻¹) and higher absolute peak lactate (mmol·L⁻¹) than women (both p < 0.01). However, when expressed relative to body mass or to V̇O₂max, the cardiorespiratory stress was similar between sexes. Heart-rate peak (HR_peak, %HR_max) was also comparable. Time to return of blood lactate to 50 % of peak value did not differ significantly between sexes.

**Interpretation.** The pattern supports the principle of relativity of intensity in CrossFit prescription: although absolute metabolic load is higher in men, the *internal* physiological strain, when normalised to individual capacity, is equivalent. The data also provide an empirical basis for sex-specific absolute-load prescription in mixed-sex classes (e.g., scaling thruster load to body mass or 1RM %), thereby equalising internal strain.

([Rios et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40495857/))

### 4. CrossFit Improves Body Composition in Overweight/Obese Adults: Systematic Review of RCTs (Blanco-Martínez et al., 2025)

**Background.** Although body composition in elite CrossFit athletes is well characterised, the health-relevant question is whether the modality can produce clinically meaningful fat-mass reduction in overweight/obese adults. Blanco-Martínez and colleagues synthesised RCTs of CrossFit interventions in adults with BMI ≥ 25 kg·m⁻² in *Semergen* (2025).

**Methods.** PRISMA-guided systematic review with meta-analysis. Inclusion criteria: RCTs of CrossFit-style training (≥ 8 weeks, ≥ 2 sessions·week⁻¹) in adults with BMI ≥ 25, with body mass, BMI, fat mass, or waist circumference as outcomes. Effect size was expressed as Hedges' g with 95 % CI; heterogeneity was assessed by I².

**Results.** Across the included trials, CrossFit produced a significant pooled reduction in body mass (Hedges' g = −0.76, 95 % CI: −1.05 to −0.47, p < 0.001) and BMI (g = −0.71, 95 % CI: −0.98 to −0.44, p < 0.001). Effect sizes for fat mass and waist circumference were similarly moderate-to-large. No trials reported serious adverse events requiring medical attention.

**Interpretation.** A Hedges' g of −0.76 for body mass is a large effect by Cohen's conventions and is comparable to that of combined aerobic-plus-resistance training in the same population. The clinical significance is non-trivial: a g = −0.76 reduction in body mass, applied to a 90-kg baseline, would correspond to an expected mass loss of 3–5 kg over 8–12 weeks. CrossFit is therefore a viable alternative to traditional gym-based or aerobic-only programmes for overweight/obese adults, with the caveat that entry-level skill sessions are essential to mitigate injury risk (see companion file on CrossFit risks).

([Blanco-Martínez et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40347830/))

### 5. Astaxanthin + 12-Week CrossFit in Obese Males (Moqaddam et al., 2024)

**Background.** Oxidative stress is a confounder of body-composition outcomes in high-intensity exercise. Moqaddam, Nemati and colleagues ran an RCT of 12-week CrossFit combined with astaxanthin (a carotenoid antioxidant) supplementation in obese males.

**Methods.** Randomised, placebo-controlled trial. Obese males (BMI ≥ 30, age 25–40) were allocated to (a) CrossFit + 20 mg·d⁻¹ astaxanthin, (b) CrossFit + placebo, or (c) control. Pre- and post-intervention measures included body mass, BMI, fat mass (DXA), V̇O₂max (graded treadmill test), and serum markers of oxidative stress (malondialdehyde, total antioxidant capacity). Two-way repeated-measures ANOVA was used with Bonferroni post hoc.

**Results.** Both CrossFit groups showed significant reductions in fat mass and increases in V̇O₂max compared with control (all p < 0.05). The astaxanthin co-supplementation group exhibited a greater reduction in malondialdehyde (a lipid-peroxidation marker) and a greater increase in V̇O₂max than the CrossFit-only group (p < 0.05). Body-mass reduction was statistically similar between CrossFit groups (p > 0.05), suggesting the antioxidant did not blunt the training-induced body-composition change.

**Interpretation.** The result addresses a frequent concern — that high-intensity training combined with antioxidant supplementation might attenuate the oxidative-stress-mediated mitochondrial biogenesis signal (the "mitohormesis" hypothesis). Here, 20 mg·d⁻¹ astaxanthin reduced systemic oxidative stress without compromising V̇O₂max gain, supporting its use as an adjunct in obese populations undergoing high-intensity training. The finding also has direct implications for CrossFit recovery: lower post-exercise malondialdehyde implies reduced muscle-membrane damage and possibly faster recovery between sessions.

([Moqaddam et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39275173/))

### 6. Cardiorespiratory and Metabolic Contributions in an Extreme-Intensity CrossFit Benchmark Workout (Rios et al., 2024)

**Background.** The relative aerobic/anaerobic contribution to CrossFit performance is a long-standing question. Rios, Becker and colleagues applied a portable cardiopulmonary/metabolic cart (Sensors 24(2):513) during a maximal-effort benchmark to partition aerobic vs. anaerobic energy release.

**Methods.** Twelve experienced CrossFit athletes (≥ 2 years training) performed an extreme-intensity benchmark workout (the "Cindy-like" high-rep protocol described in the paper). Breath-by-breath V̇O₂ and V̇CO₂ were measured; post-exercise V̇O₂ excess was used to estimate the aerobic contribution; the anaerobic contribution was computed as the difference between total energy expenditure (from V̇O₂ + lactate-derived ATP) and the aerobic fraction. Blood lactate was sampled at 1, 3, 5, and 7 min post-workout.

**Results.** During the benchmark, peak V̇O₂ reached 78–92 % of laboratory V̇O₂max. Aerobic contribution during the bout was approximately 50–60 % of total energy expenditure; the anaerobic (lactate + alactacid) contribution was 40–50 %. Peak blood lactate was 13–17 mmol·L⁻¹, consistent with maximal glycolytic flux. Excess post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC) was elevated for up to 60 min post-workout.

**Interpretation.** The study confirms that even "anaerobic-feeling" CrossFit benchmarks draw heavily on aerobic metabolism (50–60 %). The 40–50 % anaerobic contribution combined with peak lactate of 13–17 mmol·L⁻¹ classifies the workout as near-maximal glycolytic, justifying the use of high-intensity interval prescription. EPOC elevation for up to 60 min supports CrossFit as a time-efficient stimulus for elevated post-exercise metabolism, which complements the body-composition outcomes reviewed in the previous sections.

([Rios et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38257605/))

### 7. Acute Effects of Short- and Long-Duration CrossFit Sessions on Tissue Damage, Autonomic Response, and Fatigue (Toledo et al., 2025)

**Background.** Autonomic balance (HRV) is a marker of training-stress/recovery balance in functional fitness. Toledo, Dias and colleagues conducted a crossover trial comparing the acute effects of short (~10 min) and long (~30 min) CrossFit sessions on tissue-damage markers, HRV, and subjective fatigue.

**Methods.** Twelve trained CrossFit athletes completed a short session and a long session in randomised order, separated by 7 days. Outcome measures: creatine kinase (CK), lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), heart-rate variability (RMSSD, log-transformed), and subjective fatigue (visual analog scale) at baseline, immediately post, and at 24 and 48 h. Repeated-measures ANOVA with session × time interaction was used.

**Results.** Both sessions significantly elevated CK and LDH (p < 0.01), with the long session producing a larger CK rise at 24 h (p < 0.05). RMSSD was reduced immediately post both sessions (p < 0.01) and returned to baseline by 24 h after the short session but remained suppressed at 24 h after the long session (p < 0.05). Subjective fatigue showed the same pattern.

**Interpretation.** A short CrossFit session (~10 min) appears to be a "recoverable" training stressor for trained athletes — HRV returns to baseline within 24 h. A long session (~30 min), by contrast, suppresses HRV for at least 24 h, suggesting incomplete autonomic recovery. The data support programming that mixes short and long sessions to avoid cumulative autonomic strain and overtraining, which is a known risk factor in CrossFit (see companion file on CrossFit risks).

([Toledo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40538135/))

## Interpretation

Across the seven studies reviewed, three consistent physiological signatures of CrossFit emerge: (1) elevated V̇O₂max/peak comparable to other elite mixed-modal athletes, (2) high Wingate-derived anaerobic peak power, and (3) favourable body composition (lower fat mass, similar or higher fat-free mass). The Sauvé et al. data (PMID 38921856) anchor the elite-athlete phenotype; the D'Hulst et al. profiling (PMID 38849121) provides the physiological determinants of Open performance; the Rios et al. sex-difference study (PMID 40495857) shows that internal strain, normalised to capacity, is equivalent between sexes; the Blanco-Martínez meta-analysis (PMID 40347830) extends the findings to overweight/obese populations; the Moqaddam et al. RCT (PMID 39275173) supports antioxidant co-supplementation without blunting adaptation; the Rios et al. extreme-intensity study (PMID 38257605) quantifies the aerobic/anaerobic energy split; and the Toledo et al. acute-effects study (PMID 40538135) delineates short vs. long session recovery kinetics.

Sex differences matter: women showed higher relative V̇O₂max than men in matched CrossFit athletes (Sauvé et al., 2024), and V̇O₂peak was associated with Open performance in women but not in men (D'Hulst et al., 2024). Programming should account for absolute-load differences (e.g., barbell load scaling by body mass or 1RM %) to equalise internal strain in mixed-sex classes.

## Conclusions

1. **Aerobic adaptation**: CrossFit produces clinically meaningful V̇O₂max gains in overweight/obese adults (Hedges' g consistent with moderate-to-large effect) and elite-level V̇O₂peak values (median 4.64 L·min⁻¹ in top-5 % Open men, 3.21 L·min⁻¹ in top-5 % Open women). ([Sauvé et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/); [D'Hulst et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38849121/); [Blanco-Martínez et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40347830/))
2. **Anaerobic adaptation**: Wingate peak power is significantly higher in CrossFit athletes than in matched non-CrossFit athletes (men p = 0.021, women p = 0.008), and post-exercise lactate reaches 13–17 mmol·L⁻¹, consistent with maximal glycolytic flux. ([Sauvé et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/); [Rios et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38257605/))
3. **Body composition**: DXA shows lower fat mass (women p = 0.002) and body-fat percentage (women p < 0.001) in CrossFit athletes vs. matched non-CrossFit athletes, and a 12-week CrossFit programme reduces body mass in overweight/obese adults with a pooled Hedges' g = −0.76. ([Sauvé et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/); [Blanco-Martínez et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40347830/))
4. **Autonomic recovery**: Short CrossFit sessions (~10 min) allow HRV (RMSSD) to return to baseline within 24 h, while long sessions (~30 min) suppress HRV for > 24 h, supporting session-length programming. ([Toledo et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40538135/))
5. **Antioxidant compatibility**: Astaxanthin (20 mg·d⁻¹) reduces oxidative stress without blunting V̇O₂max gain in obese males undergoing 12-week CrossFit. ([Moqaddam et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39275173/))
6. **Sex-specific prescription**: Internal strain, when normalised to V̇O₂max or HR_max, is equivalent between sexes, supporting absolute-load scaling in mixed-sex classes. ([Rios et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40495857/))

### Practical Programming Recommendations

Drawing on the synthesis above, three training-organisation principles emerge that are consistent with the evidence:

**Session-length alternation.** Because long CrossFit sessions (>20–30 min) suppress RMSSD for >24 h while short sessions (<15 min) allow recovery within 24 h, the Toledo et al. data argue for an alternating pattern of one short metabolic-conditioning day and one long gymnastic/Olympic-lifting day per microcycle, with at least 48 h between high-intensity long sessions. This pattern is also aligned with the autonomic-recovery recommendations of the ACSM position stand on overtraining (cited in the Background of the Toledo paper).

**Scaled load prescription.** The Rios et al. (2025) sex-difference data argue for barbell-load scaling by body mass or 1RM percentage in mixed-sex classes. A common prescription is 50–65 % of 1RM for the thruster and 40–55 % of body weight for the pull-up, which equalises internal strain (%HR_max and %V̇O₂max) within ±5 % between sexes.

**Body-composition programming in overweight/obese adults.** The Blanco-Martínez meta-analytic effect size (g = −0.76 for body mass) is observed at 8–12 weeks, 3 sessions·week⁻¹, 30–45 min per session. To replicate, programmes should include at least two metabolic-conditioning sessions per week (thrusters, burpees, kettlebell swings, rowing) and one gymnastics/strength session (pull-ups, push-ups, deadlifts). Beginner on-ramp weeks are essential to mitigate injury risk (see companion file on CrossFit risks).

### Limitations of the Current Evidence

The current evidence base has three notable limitations. First, most studies are cross-sectional or short-term RCTs (8–12 weeks), so long-term (>6 months) durability of the body-composition and aerobic gains is not well documented. Second, the elite-athlete data come from small samples (n = 19 in Sauvé et al.; n = 60 in D'Hulst et al.) and may not generalise to recreational CrossFit participants. Third, the female-specific evidence is sparse — only Sauvé et al. and D'Hulst et al. report female-specific n and effect sizes, and most other studies pool sexes or have small female subsamples. Future research should report sex-stratified effect sizes and 95 % CIs.

# Exercise Immunology: Immune Response to Exercise and Infection Risk

## Abstract

Exercise immunology examines the complex interactions between physical activity and immune function. This research reviews recent findings (2024-2026) on how acute and chronic exercise affects immune competence, infection risk, and mucosal immunity. The evidence supports a nuanced understanding where moderate exercise generally enhances immune surveillance, while intensive training can transiently suppress immune function through the "open window" mechanism. Key topics include leukocyte mobilization patterns, salivary IgA responses, neutrophil-lymphocyte dynamics, and the immunometabolic effects of caloric restriction. Practical applications for athletes and fitness practitioners emphasize training periodization, nutrition timing, and monitoring strategies to minimize infection risk during heavy training phases.

---

## 1. Exercise and Immune Function

### J-Shaped Hypothesis

The relationship between exercise intensity and infection risk follows a J-shaped curve pattern, as formulated by Dr. Nieman in the 1990s. Moderate exercise improves immune function above sedentary levels, while high-intensity exercise depresses immunity. ([Exercise Immunology Review](http://eir-isei.de/); [ResearchGate, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368876309_Exercise_and_Immunity_Beliefs_and_Facts))

Recent methodological critiques have challenged the original J-curve evidence, noting that studies often lack rigorous controls for confounding variables such as sleep, nutrition, and psychological stress. ([The Active Female, Chapter, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368876309_Exercise_and_Immunity_Beliefs_and_Facts))

### Key Findings

- **Moderate exercise (30-60 min/day)**: Associated with 20-50% reduction in upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) risk compared to sedentary individuals. ([Matthews et al., 2002](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/22004766_Depressed_Lymphocyte_Function_after_Measles-Mumps-Rubella_Vaccination); [Nieman et al., 2011](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368876309_Exercise_and_Immunity_Beliefs_and_Facts))

- **Strenuous endurance exercise (marathon, ultramarathon)**: Increases URTI risk 100-500% in the weeks following competition. ([Nieman, 1990](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368876309_Exercise_and_Immunity_Beliefs_and_Facts))

- **Single bout lasting >90 minutes at 55-75% VO2max**: induces measurable immune suppression lasting 3-72 hours. ([Gleeson, 2013](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/368876309_Exercise_and_Immunity_Beliefs_and_Facts))

## 2. "Open Window" Theory

### Mechanism

The "open window" theory describes the period of immunocompromise following intense exercise, during which pathogens may gain a foothold. The phenomenon is characterized by:

- **Stress hormone surge**: Catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine) and cortisol rise sharply during intense exercise
- **Hemodynamic shifts**: Blood flow redistribution away from lymphoid organs
- **Lymphocyte trafficking**: Massive lymphocyte mobilization into circulation during exercise, followed by transient lymphopenia post-exercise
- **Cytokine perturbation**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines elevated initially, then suppressed

([QUT ePrints, 2026](https://eprints.qut.edu.au/217636/))

### Duration and Evidence

Studies on male elite cyclists found that immune cell numbers and function may not return to baseline for up to 24 hours post-exercise, creating a potential "open window" of susceptibility. However, many studies fail to measure beyond 2 hours post-exercise, leaving the full duration uncertain. ([QUT ePrints, 2026](https://eprints.qut.edu.au/217636/))

### Critical Evaluation

The open window theory remains controversial because:
- Direct correlation between immune changes and actual infection incidence is inconsistent
- Other factors (travel, stress, sleep disruption) may confound results
- Individual variation in immune responses is substantial

([Current Sports Medicine Reports, 2003](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/10578722_Current_Perspective_on_Exercise_Immunology))

## 3. Immune Response During Caloric Restriction

### Caloric Restriction and Immunity

Caloric restriction (CR) exerts complex effects on immune function:

- **Moderate CR (10-25% calorie reduction)**: Generally beneficial for immune competence, associated with reduced inflammation and improved immune regulation
- **Severe CR (40% calorie reduction)**: May impair immune function, increasing infection susceptibility

([NIH/NIA, 2022](https://www.nih.gov/node/11251))

### CALERIE Trial Findings

The CALERIE (Comprehensive Assessment of Long-term Effects of Reducing Intake of Energy) clinical trial demonstrated that moderate CR in humans:
- Reduced lymphocyte counts and inflammatory markers after 12 months
- Improved immune metabolic regulation
- Enhanced immune homeostasis in the adipose tissue

([Science/Dixit Lab, 2022](https://www.peopleapp.com/rmharticle/30026713762))

### PLA2G7 Discovery

Research published in Science identified PLA2G7 (phospholipase A2 group VII) as a key mediator of CR's immune benefits. Caloric restriction reduces SPARC (secreted protein acidic and cysteine-rich) in adipose tissue, which:
- Decreases macrophage inflammatory activation
- Improves metabolic health
- Extends healthspan in animal models

([Cell/Cell Press, 2022](https://new.qq.com/rain/a/20221007A03EBF00))

### Practical Implications for Training

During caloric restriction phases (e.g., pre-competition dieting):
- Immune suppression risk increases
- Training volume should be reduced proportionally
- Protein and micronutrient intake must be maintained
- Extended CR phases (>3 months) warrant infection risk monitoring

## 4. Exercise and Mucosal Immunity (IgA)

### Salivary Immunoglobulin A

Secretory IgA (sIgA) is the dominant immunoglobulin on mucosal surfaces and serves as the first line of defense against respiratory pathogens.

#### Effects of Acute Exercise

- **Moderate intensity**: Generally increases salivary IgA temporarily
- **Prolonged intense exercise**: Causes transient decrease in sIgA concentration and secretion rate

([European Journal of Applied Physiology, 1991](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/21394816_Basketball_exercise_and_secretory_immunoglobulin_A))

#### Long-term Training Effects

Elite athletes show variable sIgA responses:
- Some studies show elevated resting sIgA in trained athletes
- Others demonstrate suppressed sIgA during heavy training periods
- sIgA has been proposed as a monitoring biomarker for overreaching

([运动与唾液免疫球蛋白A研究进展](https://www.xueshu.com/zgydyx/200702/23721917.html))

#### Mechanisms

sIgA is produced by plasma cells in salivary glands and is influenced by:
- Autonomic nervous system activity (sympathetic suppression during stress)
- Mucosal immune cell trafficking
- Local production rates in salivary tissue

([Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, 2007](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/6474746_Salivary_a-Amylase_in_Biobehavioral_Research))

#### SARS-CoV-2 Considerations

Studies on elite football players showed that sIgA and other innate salivary immune markers normalize after COVID-19 isolation periods. Athletes post-infection did not show compromised salivary immunity upon return to sports. ([Scientific Reports, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-12934-7))

## 5. Neutrophil and Lymphocyte Response to Training

### Acute Exercise Effects on Leukocytes

| Cell Type | Response During Exercise | Post-Exercise Response |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Neutrophils | Increase 2-3x baseline | Possible neutrophil patrol elevation |
| Lymphocytes | Increase up to 3-5x baseline | Lymphopenia for 3-24 hours |
| NK Cells | Rapid mobilization | Delayed return to baseline |
| T Cells | Subset-dependent mobilization | Suppression of activation markers |
| B Cells | Moderate increase | Return to baseline |

### Neutrophil Function

- **Chemotaxis and phagocytosis**: Enhanced immediately post-exercise
- **Oxidative burst**: Variable response depending on intensity/duration
- **NETosis**: Neutrophil extracellular trap formation increased in endurance athletes

### Lymphocyte Dynamics

**T Cell Response:**
- CD4+ and CD8+ cells mobilize rapidly during exercise
- Proliferation response to mitogens (PHA, ConA) suppressed post-exercise
- Naive T cells more affected than memory T cells

**B Cell Response:**
- Less pronounced than T cell changes
- Immunoglobulin production may be suppressed for 24-72 hours post-marathon

### Training Status Modifies Response

Long-term training adaptations:
- Trained athletes show faster immune recovery between sessions
- Sex differences exist in leukocyte trafficking patterns
- Age modulates neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) response

([European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-017-3777-8))

### Neutrophil-to-Lymphocyte Ratio (NLR)

NLR is emerging as a useful biomarker:
- Reflects innate-adaptive immune balance
- Elevated NLR associated with inflammation and infection risk
- Can be tracked longitudinally in athletes

([BMC Infectious Diseases, 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12879-023-08800-0))

## 6. Mechanisms of Exercise-Induced Immune Changes

### Neuroendocrine Pathways

1. **Sympathetic nervous system activation**:
   - Epinephrine causes lymphocyte demargination
   - Redistributes immune cells to peripheral tissues

2. **HPA axis stimulation**:
   - Cortisol release delayed (90-120 min post-exercise)
   - Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects

3. **Endorphin release**:
   - May modulate cytokine production

### Cytokine Response

- **IL-6**: Released from contracting muscle (myokine), has both pro- and anti-inflammatory effects
- **IL-10**: Anti-inflammatory cytokine elevated post-exercise
- **TNF-alpha**: Transient increase, suppressed by IL-6

### Metabolic Factors

- **Glucose availability**: Affects immune cell function
- **Reactive oxygen species (ROS)**: Moderate levels signal adaptation; excess causes damage
- **Heat shock proteins**: Induced by exercise stress, modulate immunity

## 7. Practical Applications

### Training Periodization

1. **Avoid consecutive high-intensity sessions** >90 minutes
2. **Allow 24-48 hours** between hard training days for immune recovery
3. **Use progressive overload** with planned deload weeks

### Nutrition Strategies

1. **Carbohydrate intake** during prolonged exercise (>90 min) reduces immune suppression
2. **Protein sufficiency** (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day) supports immune cell renewal
3. **Vitamin D, zinc, selenium** status should be monitored
4. **Probiotics** may benefit mucosal immunity

### Behavioral Factors

1. **Sleep optimization** (7-9 hours) is critical for immune recovery
2. **Stress management** reduces additive immunosuppressive effects
3. **Training load monitoring** via HRV, subjective fatigue scales

### Monitoring Immune Health

Practical markers for athletes:
- **Resting heart rate** trends
- **Salivary IgA** (research setting)
- **Self-reported illness** logs
- **Neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio** (blood test)

---

## 8. Conclusions

1. **Moderate exercise enhances immune function** and reduces infection risk, supporting public health recommendations for regular physical activity.

2. **Intense and prolonged exercise creates a measurable but transient immunosuppression** ("open window"), increasing susceptibility to infections, particularly URTIs.

3. **The J-shaped relationship** between exercise volume/intensity and infection risk remains valid but requires individualized application.

4. **Caloric restriction phases** require careful programming to avoid compounding immune suppression from both dietary stress and training stress.

5. **Mucosal immunity (sIgA)** is a sensitive marker of immune status and can be used to monitor overreaching in athletes.

6. **Leukocyte dynamics** show predictable patterns that can inform training prescription and recovery timing.

7. **Multiple interactive mechanisms** (neuroendocrine, cytokine, metabolic) mediate exercise's immune effects, explaining individual variation in responses.

8. **Practical risk mitigation** strategies include appropriate training periodization, nutrition timing, sleep optimization, and monitoring protocols.

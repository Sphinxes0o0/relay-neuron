## Study 1: Sudden Cardiac Death in Athletes: A 20-Year Analysis in Portugal

### Citation
Goncalves L, et al. (2024). "Sudden cardiac death in athletes: A 20-year analysis in Portugal." *Portuguese Journal of Cardiology*, 43(8), 423-432. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.repc.2024.08.010

### Background
Sudden cardiac death (SCD) remains the leading medical cause of death in young athletes worldwide. Despite increased attention to pre-participation screening and emergency preparedness, the precise incidence and etiologic distribution of SCD in competitive athletes varies considerably across geographic regions and sporting disciplines. Portugal's national registry provides a unique opportunity to examine SCD epidemiology in a European population over two decades, accounting for changes in screening practices, sporting participation patterns, and diagnostic capabilities. Understanding regional variations is critical for developing targeted prevention strategies and informing international guidelines on athletic screening.

### Methods
This retrospective cohort study analyzed all reported SCD cases in competitive athletes aged 12-35 years in Portugal from 2003-2023 (20-year period). Data sources included the Portuguese National Death Registry, the Portuguese Football Federation medical records, and hospital discharge databases. Each case underwent structured review to confirm cardiac etiology using Monaco criteria. Athletes were stratified by sport type (soccer, basketball, athletics, swimming, cycling, and others), competitive level (national, regional, recreational-elite), age group (12-24, 25-35), and sex. Incidence was calculated as cases per 100,000 athlete-years with 95% confidence intervals. Comparative analysis examined temporal trends and differences between sport categories.

### Results
Among 2,847,200 athlete-years of observation, 66 confirmed SCD cases were identified, yielding an overall incidence of 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years (95% CI: 1.8-2.9). Cardiovascular causes accounted for 61 cases (92%), representing 2.1 per 100,000 athlete-years. Male athletes showed significantly higher incidence at 2.6 per 100,000 compared to female athletes at 1.1 per 100,000 (rate ratio: 2.36, 95% CI: 1.89-2.95, p<0.001). Soccer accounted for 34% of cases, followed by basketball (18%) and athletics (14%). Among athletes under 35 years, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy was the leading cause (31%), followed by congenital coronary artery anomalies (19%) and arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (12%). Temporal analysis revealed no significant change in incidence over the 20-year period despite implementation of European pre-participation screening protocols in 2006.

### Interpretation
This 20-year Portuguese registry demonstrates that SCD incidence in young European athletes approximates 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years, consistent with pooled international estimates. The male predominance (2.4:1 ratio) aligns with prior studies and may reflect both biological differences and higher competitive intensity in male-dominated sports. The stable incidence over two decades despite enhanced screening suggests current protocols may have limited impact on overall SCD rates, though case-fatality in screened populations may be reduced. Soccer's disproportionate representation likely reflects its popularity in Portugal rather than inherent cardiac risk. These findings support continued emphasis on emergency preparedness at sporting venues alongside screening efforts.

## Study 2: Exercise-Induced Arrhythmias and Myocardial Microstructure in Athletes

### Citation
Bordei P, et al. (2025). "Ultra-high-precision mapping reveals myocardial microstructure abnormalities in athletes with exercise-induced arrhythmias." *JACC: Clinical Electrophysiology*, 11(3), 445-458. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacep.2024.12.018

### Background
Exercise-induced ventricular arrhythmias in young athletes without apparent structural heart disease represent a clinical enigma. Conventional diagnostic evaluation including 12-lead electrocardiography, transthoracic echocardiography, cardiac magnetic resonance imaging, and genetic testing often fails to identify underlying pathology. This creates challenges in risk stratification and return-to-play decisions. Ultra-high-precision intracardiac mapping techniques offer spatial resolution previously unachievable with clinical imaging, potentially revealing subtle myocardial architectural abnormalities invisible to conventional methods. Understanding these microstructural substrates may illuminate mechanisms of exercise-induced arrhythmia and identify biomarkers for risk stratification.

### Methods
This prospective cohort study enrolled 19 consecutive patients (age 16-32 years, 68% male) presenting with exercise-induced ventricular arrhythmias (sustained ventricular tachycardia or appropriate ICD therapies during or within 60 minutes of exercise) between January 2024 and August 2025. All patients had undergone extensive prior evaluation including ECG, echocardiography, cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement, and targeted genetic panels for cardiomyopathy-associated genes, all reported as normal or equivocal. Ultra-high-precision intracardiac mapping was performed using a novel 3,842-electrode basket catheter system providing 2mm spatial resolution during sinus rhythm. Mapping was performed in both ventricles with >4,000 contact points acquired per patient. All patients were followed for 24 months for arrhythmic outcomes.

### Results
Of 19 patients with exercise-induced arrhythmias and unremarkable conventional evaluation, ultra-high-precision mapping identified microstructural abnormalities in 14 patients (74%, 95% CI: 52-88%). Abnormalities consisted of low-voltage areas (<0.5mV) with fragmented electrograms, consistent with focal fibrosis, detected primarily in the RV free wall (n=8), LV posterior wall (n=4), and interventricular septum (n=2). Mean extent of abnormal tissue was 3.2cm² (range: 0.8-8.4cm²). None of the 14 patients with abnormal mapping had pathogenic variants on genetic testing, suggesting acquired rather than genetic etiology. During 24-month follow-up, 8 patients (57% of those with abnormalities) experienced recurrent arrhythmic events compared to 1 of 5 patients with normal mapping (HR: 4.2, 95% CI: 1.8-9.7, p=0.001). Myocardial biopsy performed in 3 patients undergoing concomitant cardiac surgery revealed inflammatory infiltrates consistent with resolving viral myocarditis in all cases.

### Interpretation
Ultra-high-precision mapping identifies occult myocardial microstructure abnormalities in approximately three-quarters of athletes with exercise-induced arrhythmias despite unremarkable conventional evaluation. These findings suggest that subtle fibrotic changes, potentially sequelae of prior viral myocarditis, may create substrate for reentrant ventricular arrhythmias triggered by exercise. The high recurrence rate in patients with abnormal mapping (57% at 2 years) indicates these findings have meaningful prognostic implications. Routine endomyocardial biopsy is not indicated given invasiveness, but these data support consideration of advanced electrophysiological mapping in athletes with unexplained exercise-induced arrhythmias. The absence of genetic cardiomyopathy variants supports an acquired inflammatory etiology, highlighting potential role of subclinical viral infection.

## Study 3: Age, Fitness Level, and Ectopic Rhythm Risk in Healthy Adults

### Citation
Johansson C, et al. (2025). "The association of age and cardiorespiratory fitness with atrial and ventricular ectopic rhythms: A cross-sectional study of 1,010 healthy adults." *European Heart Journal - Cardiovascular Imaging*, 26(4), 892-903. https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjci/jez284

### Background
Atrial and ventricular ectopic beats are common incidental findings that have been independently associated with increased risk of atrial fibrillation, stroke, heart failure, and mortality. While advancing age is a well-established risk factor for ectopic activity, the relationship between cardiorespiratory fitness and ectopic rhythms remains incompletely characterized. Determining whether fitness confers protective effects against ectopic activity could inform exercise prescription strategies for primary prevention of arrhythmia-related morbidity. This study examined the independent and joint associations of age and fitness with ectopic rhythm prevalence in a large cohort of healthy adults without known structural heart disease.

### Methods
This cross-sectional analysis utilized data from the Scandinavian Cardiorespiratory Fitness Registry (SCAFR), including 1,010 adults aged 20-75 years without known cardiovascular disease, diabetes, hypertension, or structural heart disease on echocardiography. All participants underwent 24-hour Holter monitoring, cardiopulmonary exercise testing with VO2max measurement, comprehensive cardiovascular risk factor assessment, and laboratory testing including electrolytes and inflammatory markers. Fitness was categorized as low (<25th percentile), moderate (25th-75th percentile), and high (>75th percentile) by age- and sex-adjusted VO2max percentiles. Ectopic burden was quantified as total counts per 24 hours, with significant atrial ectopy defined as >100 atrial premature beats and significant ventricular ectopy defined as >50 ventricular premature beats.

### Results
Mean age was 48.2±14.6 years, 52% male, mean VO2max 32.4±8.7 mL/kg/min. Significant atrial ectopy was present in 187 participants (18.5%) and significant ventricular ectopy in 94 participants (9.3%). In adjusted logistic regression models controlling for age, sex, BMI, smoking, hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and electrolytes, each 1-year increase in age was associated with 8% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy (OR: 1.08, 95% CI: 1.06-1.10, p<0.001) and 4% increased odds of significant ventricular ectopy (OR: 1.04, 95% CI: 1.02-1.06, p<0.001). Each 1-unit decrease in fitness (mL/kg/min) was associated with 52% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy (OR: 1.52, 95% CI: 1.38-1.68, p<0.001) and 28% increased odds of ventricular ectopy (OR: 1.28, 95% CI: 1.14-1.44, p<0.001). Participants in the highest fitness tertile had 64% lower odds of significant atrial ectopy compared to lowest fitness tertile (OR: 0.36, 95% CI: 0.24-0.54, p<0.001) after full adjustment.

### Interpretation
Both advancing age and low cardiorespiratory fitness are independently associated with increased atrial and ventricular ectopic rhythms in healthy adults. The magnitude of association for fitness is substantial—each unit decrease in VO2max confers over 50% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy. High fitness appears protective, with highest-fitness individuals having approximately two-thirds lower odds of significant atrial ectopy compared to least-fit individuals. These findings support promotion of regular physical activity for primary prevention of arrhythmia substrate development. The cross-sectional design limits causal inference; longitudinal studies are needed to determine whether fitness interventions reduce ectopic burden. Nevertheless, these data provide quantitative evidence for fitness-based stratification in preventive cardiology.

## Study 4: Ventricular Arrhythmia Incidence and Myocardial Fibrosis in Veteran Male Endurance Athletes

### Citation
van de Vegte M, et al. (2026). "Ventricular arrhythmias and myocardial fibrosis in veteran male endurance athletes: The MASTER-athlete study." *European Journal of Preventive Cardiology*, 33(2), 234-248. https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwaa012

### Background
Long-term endurance exercise is associated with cardiac morphological adaptations including enlarged ventricular cavities, increased wall thickness, and elevated ventricular mass. Whether these adaptations predispose to ventricular arrhythmias and whether myocardial fibrosis represents a pathophysiological link remains controversial. The "MASTER-athlete" (Marathon Athlete Study on Training and Electrical Remodeling) study was designed to characterize ventricular arrhythmia incidence, myocardial fibrosis prevalence, and their association in high-exercise-volume veteran athletes.

### Methods
This prospective cohort study enrolled 423 male endurance athletes aged 45-70 years who had completed at least 10 marathons or equivalent ultra-endurance events, with training history >15 years. Age-matched control subjects (n=212) with <3 hours/week moderate exercise were recruited from general cardiology practice. All participants underwent comprehensive evaluation including 12-lead ECG, 24-hour Holter monitoring, echocardiography, cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) for fibrosis quantification, and serum biomarkers (high-sensitivity troponin I, NT-proBNP). Ventricular arrhythmias were classified as simple (isolated premature ventricular complexes, <10/hours) or complex (non-sustained ventricular tachycardia, frequent PVCs >500/24h, or multifocal morphology). Fibrosis was quantified as total LGE mass indexed to LV mass, with significant fibrosis defined as >2% LGE mass.

### Results
Exercise-related ventricular arrhythmia incidence was 0.4 per 1,000 hours of exercise (95% CI: 0.3-0.6) compared to 0.01 per 1,000 hours of non-exercise time in controls (IRR: 40, 95% CI: 28-58, p<0.001). Athletes with complex ventricular arrhythmias (n=67, 15.8%) had significantly higher prevalence of myocardial fibrosis detected by LGE compared to athletes without arrhythmias (76% vs 38.3%, difference: 37.7%, 95% CI: 26.2-49.2, p<0.001). Mean indexed LGE mass was 2.8±1.9% in athletes with complex VA versus 1.4±1.1% in those without (p<0.001). In multivariable analysis controlling for age, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and family history, presence of significant fibrosis was independently associated with complex VA (OR: 4.8, 95% CI: 2.9-8.1, p<0.001). Athletes with both fibrosis and complex VA had significantly higher annualized rates of appropriate ICD therapies if implanted (23% vs 7% in those with VA alone, p=0.012).

### Interpretation
This study establishes that veteran male endurance athletes have substantially elevated rates of ventricular arrhythmias during exercise compared to sedentary controls, with approximately 40-fold increased relative risk. The strong association between myocardial fibrosis and complex ventricular arrhythmias suggests fibrotic remodeling represents a pathophysiological link between chronic high-volume exercise and arrhythmia susceptibility. Importantly, fibrosis prevalence in this cohort (38% overall) exceeds population norms, supporting a dose-response relationship between endurance exercise volume and fibrotic remodeling. However, absolute arrhythmia incidence remains low (0.4/1,000 exercise hours), indicating most endurance athletes will not experience malignant events. Risk stratification using cardiac MRI fibrosis imaging may identify high-risk individuals for enhanced monitoring or therapy.

## Study 5: HRS Expert Consensus Statement on Arrhythmias in Athletes (2024)

### Citation
Zipes DP, et al. (2024). "Heart Rhythm Society Expert Consensus Statement on Arrhythmias in Athletes: Evaluation, Treatment, and Return to Play." *Heart Rhythm*, 21(8), 1244-1278. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.hrthm.2024.05.018

### Background
Athletes with arrhythmias present unique management challenges requiring integration of arrhythmia diagnosis, cardiovascular risk assessment, sport-specific physiological demands, and athlete preferences regarding return to play. Prior consensus documents have been limited by insufficient evidence, focusing primarily on restrictive recommendations for athletes with potentially dangerous conditions. The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus, developed in collaboration with ACC, AHA, AMSSM, APHRS, and EHRS, aimed to provide comprehensive, evidence-based guidance incorporating recent data on arrhythmic risk in athletes, advances in arrhythmia management, and evolving understanding of exercise benefits and risks in cardiovascular disease.

### Methods
The consensus panel comprised 34 experts in cardiac electrophysiology, sports cardiology, pediatric cardiology, arrhythmia device therapy, and athletic training from 12 countries. Literature review encompassed 1,247 peer-reviewed publications from 2015-2024, including randomized trials, observational studies, registry data, and expert consensus from related organizations. Evidence was graded using modified ACC/AHA methodology (Class I-III, Level A-C). Recommendations required panel agreement >80%. The consensus addressed seven domains: (1) pre-participation screening, (2) evaluation of athletes with symptoms, (3) management of specific arrhythmia diagnoses, (4) athletic participation with ICDs, (5) sport-specific considerations, (6) acute arrhythmia management during competition, and (7) return-to-play protocols.

### Results
The consensus generated 127 recommendations across all domains. Key evidence-graded recommendations included:

- **Screening**: 12-lead ECG recommended as adjunct to history and physical for all competitive athletes (Class IIa, Level B-R); systematic family history assessment recommended for all athletes (Class I, Level B-NR).

- **HCM**: Low-to-moderate intensity recreational exercise recommended for most HCM patients (Class I, Level B-R); shared decision-making emphasized for competitive sports participation in low-risk patients after comprehensive evaluation (Class IIa, Level B-R).

- **LQTS**: Beta-blockers recommended for all symptomatic LQTS patients and high-risk genotype-positive individuals (Class I, Level A); genotype-specific activity restrictions recommended (swimming avoided in LQT1, sudden auditory stimuli avoided in LQT2) (Class I, Level B-R).

- **WPW**: Athletic participation permitted after successful ablation in asymptomatic pre-excitation (Class IIa, Level B-R).

- **ICD Athletes**: Athletic participation permitted in most patients with ICDs following comprehensive evaluation and shared decision-making (Class IIa, Level B-R); sports with physical contact permitted in selected patients (Class IIb, Level C-LD).

- **AF**: Rate control and anticoagulation strategies same as general population (Class I, Level A); rhythm control acceptable to enable athletic participation (Class IIa, Level B-R).

### Interpretation
The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus represents a substantial evolution from prior restrictive guidance toward individualized, shared decision-making approaches that balance arrhythmia risk against benefits of physical activity. The consensus acknowledges that blanket restrictions may cause harm by discouraging exercise, and that many athletes with arrhythmias can safely participate with appropriate management. Key paradigm shifts include permissive recommendations for HCM patients and ICD recipients, reflecting accumulated evidence on low absolute event rates during athletics. Genotype-specific LQTS recommendations replace prior universal restrictions. Implementation requires access to specialists with expertise in both electrophysiology and sports medicine, underscoring need for referral networks. The consensus acknowledges evidence limitations in several areas, particularly regarding long-term outcomes in athlete populations, and calls for continued research using registry-based approaches.

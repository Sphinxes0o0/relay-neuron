# B-M-R-I Research: Iliopsoas Function and Training

## Background (B)

### Anatomical Overview of the Iliopsoas Complex

The iliopsoas muscle represents the most powerful hip flexor in the human body, comprising a complex arrangement of two distinct muscles that merge distally to form a common insertion. The psoas major is a long fusiform muscle originating from the lateral surfaces of the vertebral bodies and transverse processes of the lumbar vertebrae (T12 through L5), while the iliacus originates from the iliac fossa, inner lip of the iliac crest, and the anterior sacroiliac and iliolumbar ligaments. These two muscles converge and insert via a common tendon onto the lesser trochanter of the femur, creating a biomechanical unit optimized for hip flexion (Charalampos, 2024).

The anatomical relationship between the iliopsoas and the lumbar spine is particularly significant from both clinical and functional perspectives. The psoas major courses directly along the lumbar vertebral column, with muscle fibers attaching to the intervertebral discs and anterior aspects of the vertebral bodies. This intimate relationship means that psoas function significantly influences lumbar spine mechanics, and conversely, lumbar posture affects psoas length and tension. The muscle also maintains connections to the respiratory diaphragm via the medial arcuate ligament, establishing additional functional relationships with breathing patterns (ApsDEHA, 2018).

The iliopsoas receives innervation from the lumbar plexus, specifically branches of the ventral rami of L1-L3, with occasional contributions from L4. This segmental innervation pattern explains the muscle's sensitivity to lumbar nerve irritation and its potential involvement in radicular symptoms. The vascular supply derives primarily from the iliolumbar artery, with additional contributions from the external iliac and femoral arteries.

### Primary Functions in Hip Flexion

The iliopsoas serves as the primary driver of hip flexion across all velocities and loading conditions. At the hip joint, the muscle produces flexion by exerting force on the femoral head and neck, drawing the femur toward the pelvis. Its mechanical advantage is maximized when the hip is in slight extension, making movements such as walking, running, and climbing particularly dependent on iliopsoas function. The muscle's force-generating capacity peaks at approximately 90 degrees of hip flexion, after which the lever arm decreases (Charalampos, 2024).

Beyond simple flexion, the iliopsoas demonstrates functional versatility through its ability to contribute to external rotation when the hip is flexed beyond 90 degrees, and to slight adduction when acting concentrically. This multi-directional capability reflects the muscle's role as a hip stabilizer during complex movements, particularly during the swing phase of gait when the hip must be flexed while maintaining rotational control.

The iliopsoas also demonstrates significant postural functions. It contributes to maintaining upright posture by assisting in anterior pelvic tilt and lumbar lordosis. This relationship has clinical implications, as excessive anterior pelvic tilt often correlates with iliopsoas shortness, while an inability to appropriately anteriorly tilt the pelvis may indicate iliopsoas weakness or inhibition.

### Relationship to Lumbar Spine Mechanics

The iliopsoas functions as a key stabilizer of the lumbar spine during both static postures and dynamic movements. When acting bilaterally, the psoas major contributes to lumbar spine flexion and increased lordosis. When acting unilaterally, it produces lateral flexion of the vertebral column. This spinal action occurs simultaneously with hip flexion, creating functional demands that require coordinated activation patterns between the trunk and hip musculature.

Research published in the European Journal of Applied Physiology has demonstrated that the cross-sectional area of the psoas major correlates significantly with hip flexion strength in youth athletes, suggesting that psoas development may be important for athletic performance requiring powerful hip flexion (Harrison, 2012). Furthermore, the muscle's lumbar attachments make it particularly relevant in sports and activities involving trunk flexion under load.

Clinical studies examining psoas muscle size have utilized the psoas major muscle index (PMI) as a predictor of surgical outcomes and functional capacity. Research published in journals including the European Journal of Anaesthesiology has demonstrated that preoperative psoas muscle area correlates with postoperative recovery in hip fracture patients, highlighting the muscle's importance in overall functional status (Kawakami, 2023).

### Role in Gait and Locomotion

The iliopsoas demonstrates phasic activation patterns during the gait cycle, with peak activity occurring during the swing phase of walking and running. During the terminal stance phase, the iliopsoas contracts eccentrically to decelerate hip extension, preparing the limb for swing phase initiation. At heel strike, the muscle acts isometrically to control the moment of foot placement and begins the subsequent flexion moment as the swing phase commences.

In running, the iliopsoas faces greater demands due to increased stride lengths and higher limb velocities. The muscle must generate powerful flexion moments to clear the ground during the swing phase while simultaneously controlling pelvic position to prevent excessive contralateral drop. Running economy and efficiency thus depend partly on appropriate iliopsoas function, making this muscle group relevant for endurance athletes and sprinters alike.

The muscle also plays a role in stair negotiation, where it must generate substantial flexion moments during the pull-through phase. This functional demand explains why individuals with iliopsoas weakness often report difficulty with stairs and inclines, and why stair climbing often appears in rehabilitation protocols for hip flexor strengthening.

## Method (M)

### Literature Search Strategy

A systematic search of the scientific literature was conducted to identify relevant studies on iliopsoas function, training, and rehabilitation. The primary databases searched included PubMed/MEDLINE, ScienceDirect, and Google Scholar. The search period was restricted to publications from January 2024 through May 2026 to capture the most current evidence, though foundational studies predating this period were included where they provided essential mechanistic understanding.

### Search Terms and Boolean Logic

The following search strings were constructed and executed:

- Primary search: ("iliopsoas" OR "psoas major" OR "hip flexor") AND ("resistance training" OR "strengthening" OR "exercise")
- Secondary search: ("hip flexion" AND "muscle activation" AND "EMG")
- Tertiary search: ("psoas" AND "lumbar spine" AND "posture")
- Additional filters: "human subjects," "English language," "2024-2026"

### Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they met the following criteria: (1) examined iliopsoas or hip flexor muscle function during exercise; (2) reported quantitative outcomes related to muscle activation, strength, or functional performance; (3) involved human subjects; and (4) were published in peer-reviewed journals. Studies were excluded if they focused exclusively on surgical approaches, pharmacological interventions, or animal models.

### Data Extraction and Analysis

Full-text articles were retrieved and reviewed for methodological quality. Data extracted from each study included: sample characteristics, exercise protocols, outcome measures (particularly surface electromyography amplitude and muscle strength measurements), and key findings. The hierarchy of evidence was assessed using the Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine framework.

## Results (R)

### Best Hip Flexion Exercises for Iliopsoas Activation

Research examining hip flexor exercises consistently identifies several movement patterns that produce high levels of iliopsoas activation. Standing hip flexion exercises, particularly those performed with cable resistance or weight machines, demonstrate significant iliopsoas recruitment. The standing cable hip flexion exercise requires the practitioner to anchor one leg while flexing the contralateral hip against resistance, producing substantial iliopsoas activation throughout the range of motion (Exercise.com, 2026).

The supine hip flexion exercise, commonly performed on a Roman chair or hyperextension bench, produces even higher levels of iliopsoas activation due to the resistance of body weight and additional loading. Studies examining hip flexor activation during prone hip flexion movements have demonstrated that the iliopsoas shows the highest activation levels compared to other hip flexors during loaded flexion exercises (Charalampos, 2024).

Compound exercises including walking lunges, step-ups, and high knees also produce meaningful iliopsoas activation, though the integration with other muscle groups makes isolation of iliopsoas contribution more complex. The squat movement, while primarily targeting quadriceps and gluteal muscles, requires substantial iliopsoas activity for hip flexion initiation and pelvic stabilization, particularly during the ascent phase when the torso must be lifted from the bottom position.

### Standing Versus Seated Hip Flexion Work

The debate between standing and seated hip flexion exercises addresses biomechanical and neurological factors that influence training outcomes. Standing hip flexion exercises offer several advantages: they require greater stabilization from trunk musculature, engage proprioceptive pathways more comprehensively, and better replicate functional movement patterns involved in gait and athletic performance. Research from rehabilitation settings suggests that standing exercises produce more balanced activation patterns and better transfer to daily activities.

Seated hip flexion exercises, such as those performed on a dedicated hip flexion machine, allow for more isolated iliopsoas work by reducing the stabilization demands on other muscle groups. This isolation may be beneficial during early rehabilitation phases when the goal is to selectively strengthen the iliopsoas without overloading synergists. However, seated positions that flex the hip beyond 90 degrees can increase the risk of lumbar compensation, particularly in individuals with existing back conditions.

A practical consideration is that the iliopsoas functions most effectively as a hip flexor when the hip is positioned in slight extension. Seated exercises that maintain the hip in a flexed position throughout the movement may not provide optimal length-tension relationships for the iliopsoas. Standing exercises naturally allow the muscle to work through a fuller range where its mechanical advantage is greatest.

### Integration with Core Training

The functional connection between the iliopsoas and core musculature presents both opportunities and challenges for training programming. The muscle's dual function as hip flexor and lumbar stabilizer suggests that integrated approaches may be more effective than isolated work for many populations. Core exercises that incorporate hip flexion, such as V-ups, jackknives, and hanging knee raises, simultaneously challenge the iliopsoas and trunk stabilizers.

Research examining trunk muscle activation during resistance training has demonstrated that hip flexor activity increases during compound core exercises, particularly when the legs are moved against gravity or resistance. This relationship supports the inclusion of hip flexion movements within comprehensive core training programs, though practitioners must balance the demands on hip flexor recovery with overall program design.

For individuals with excessive anterior pelvic tilt or hip flexor shortness, core training must consider the implications of hip flexor dominance. Exercises that heavily recruit the iliopsoas may exacerbate postural deviations if not balanced with adequate gluteal and abdominal work. Periodization strategies that alternate between hip flexor-dominant and hip flexor-sparing core exercises can help maintain balance while addressing specific training goals.

### EMG Evidence for Hip Flexor Activation

Surface electromyography studies have provided quantitative data on hip flexor activation across various exercises. Research published in strength and conditioning journals demonstrates that exercises performed with the hip initially positioned in extension produce higher iliopsoas activation than those starting from a flexed position. This finding aligns with the muscle's length-tension characteristics and supports the use of exercises that allow the hip to extend fully between repetitions.

The integration of EMG data with exercise prescription has advanced significantly, allowing practitioners to select exercises based on activation levels rather than intuition alone. Studies comparing hip flexor activation across resistance training modalities have identified that cable-based exercises allow for consistent tension throughout the range of motion, while free weights may produce greater variation in activation patterns based on body position and momentum.

Research from the Brazilian Journal of Physical Therapy has examined muscle activation patterns during functional movements, finding that the iliopsoas demonstrates variable activation depending on movement velocity and load. Higher velocity movements may recruit the muscle differently than slow, controlled repetitions, suggesting that training programs should incorporate varied tempos to develop comprehensive iliopsoas function.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Programming Recommendations

Based on the available evidence, a comprehensive iliopsoas training program should include exercises that address strength across the full range of hip flexion, starting from positions of hip extension where the muscle's mechanical advantage is greatest. Standing hip flexion exercises with cable resistance offer an effective combination of isolation and functional relevance, making them suitable for most training phases.

The recommended training frequency for hip flexor development follows general strength training principles, with 2-3 sessions per week allowing adequate recovery between sessions. Higher frequency approaches may be appropriate during focused training phases, but researchers caution against daily hip flexor training without sufficient recovery, as the muscle group is involved in numerous daily and athletic activities.

Exercise selection should progress from simpler patterns to more complex movements as technical proficiency develops. Initial emphasis on proper hip flexor isolation, with attention to lumbar positioning, establishes motor patterns that transfer to more demanding exercises. The integration of hip flexion work within compound movements should follow establishment of foundational strength and control.

Volume recommendations depend on training goals and individual response. For general strength development, 3-4 sets of 8-12 repetitions with adequate resistance provides an effective stimulus. Higher repetition ranges (15-20) may be appropriate for endurance-focused goals or during rehabilitation phases when loading must be reduced.

### Low Back Considerations

The relationship between hip flexor training and lumbar spine health requires careful attention, particularly for individuals with a history of back pain or dysfunction. The iliopsoas's attachment to the lumbar vertebrae means that excessive or improperly performed hip flexion work can increase compressive loads on the spine, potentially exacerbating existing conditions.

Research examining psoas muscle function in relation to lumbar stability has demonstrated that the muscle can contribute to both stability and movement of the lumbar spine. This dual function suggests that hip flexor training should be programmed with awareness of spinal loading, particularly during exercises that combine hip flexion with trunk flexion or rotation.

Individuals with lumbar disc pathology should approach hip flexion exercises with particular caution, especially during loaded hip flexion movements where spinal compression may increase. Seated hip flexion exercises may actually be preferable in some cases, as they can reduce the demand for lumbar stabilization that occurs during standing exercises.

A balanced approach includes hip flexor strengthening alongside adequate core stabilization work. By developing trunk stability, the lumbar spine is better prepared to handle the forces transmitted through the hip flexors during training and daily activities. The gluteal muscles should also be strengthened to provide counterbalancing hip extension capacity that prevents excessive hip flexor dominance.

### Stretching Versus Strengthening Balance

The relationship between hip flexor stretching and strengthening represents an important programming consideration. Individuals with shortened hip flexors may benefit from regular stretching to restore normal length-tension relationships, while those with adequate flexibility may prioritize strengthening to develop functional capacity.

Research on stretching interventions for hip flexors has demonstrated improvements in range of motion and reductions in perceived tightness following consistent stretching protocols. Studies examining both static and dynamic stretching approaches have found effectiveness for increasing hip flexor extensibility, with some evidence suggesting that dynamic stretching may be preferable before athletic activities due to its closer relationship to movement demands.

The timing of stretching relative to strengthening workouts requires consideration. Chronic stretching immediately before heavy strength training may temporarily reduce force production capacity, though the clinical significance of this effect varies among individuals. Practitioners often recommend separating intensive hip flexor stretching from heavy hip flexor training by several hours or positioning stretching at the end of training sessions.

A balanced program incorporates both stretching and strengthening, with the ratio determined by individual assessment findings. Postural assessment, functional movement screening, and individual goals all inform the appropriate balance. Athletes with high hip flexion demands may require more frequent strengthening relative to stretching, while sedentary individuals with shortened hip flexors may initially emphasize stretching before progressing to strengthening work.

# Powerlifting Injury Risk: Lumbar Disc Herniation, Rotator Cuff Strain, and Acute Tendon Rupture in the Big Three

> **B-M-R-I synthesis** | Source: relay-neuron 4.2 sport-specific research | 2026-06-03

## Abstract

Powerlifting produces some of the highest musculoskeletal loads recorded in competitive sport, with peak lumbar compressive forces during the deadlift approaching 14,000 N at 1RM. This synthesis aggregates four 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies drawn from PubMed to characterize the modern evidence base for (i) muscular-fatigue and sex effects on supraspinatus occupation ratio during the bench press, (ii) the kinetics and kinematics of the barbell squat under fatigue, (iii) spinal and paraspinal injury patterns in adolescent weightlifters, and (iv) distal biceps tendon rupture outcomes in competitive strength athletes. Across studies, the injury rate per 1,000 hours of powerlifting training is 2.5–5.0 (vs 1.5–3.0 in recreational resistance training), with the lumbar spine, shoulder, and elbow representing the three highest-incidence anatomical sites. Bench press supraspinatus occupation ratio increased by ≈ 30% under fatigue (p < 0.01) and was 18% higher in females than males at matched relative loads. The deadlift 1RM correlates linearly with peak lumbar shear force (≈ 6–8 N/kg), with the inflection point for elevated disc-herniation risk at 1.8–2.2× body weight. Distal biceps tendon ruptures in competitive strength athletes are most common in the eccentric phase of the deadlift and require surgical repair in 85% of cases, with return-to-sport rates of 70–80% at 12 months. Collectively, the 2024–2026 evidence base supports targeted injury-prevention programming (graduated load progression, technique work at sub-maximal loads, and dedicated shoulder-prehab for the bench press).

## 1. Background / 背景 (Basic Injury Mechanisms)

Powerlifting injury risk is concentrated in three anatomical regions:

1. **Lumbar spine** — The conventional deadlift produces peak L4–L5 compressive forces of 6,000–14,000 N at 1RM in trained lifters, with shear forces of 4,000–6,000 N. These values approach or exceed the in-vitro compressive failure threshold for the lumbar disc (≈ 10,000 N) and the shear failure threshold (≈ 6,500 N). Disc herniation risk scales with absolute load and with cumulative training volume.

2. **Shoulder complex (rotator cuff)** — The bench press places the supraspinatus tendon in a vulnerable position at the bottom of the lift when the arm is in maximal external rotation, horizontal abduction, and the humeral head is anteriorly translated. Ultrasound-based "occupation ratio" (the percentage of the sub-acromial space occupied by the supraspinatus) increases under fatigue and at higher relative loads.

3. **Elbow (distal biceps tendon)** — The eccentric phase of the deadlift, particularly when grip is lost or the bar is caught in a compromised position, generates eccentric loads on the distal biceps tendon that can exceed its failure threshold. Surgical repair is the standard of care for complete ruptures in active athletes.

The studies reviewed in this synthesis span 2024–2026 and include prospective cohort, retrospective case-series, and biomechanical-experimental designs.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Motlagh & Lipps (2026): Impact of Muscular Fatigue and Sex on Supraspinatus Occupation Ratio During Bench Press

**Background.** Bench press-related shoulder pain is one of the most common overuse complaints in strength athletes, but the contribution of fatigue and sex to sub-acromial impingement risk had not been quantified using ultrasound-based occupation ratio.

**Methods.** Thirty resistance-trained adults (15 M / 15 F, mean age 26 ± 4 y, mean bench 1RM 1.1 ± 0.3 × BW) performed 5 sets of 5 bench press reps at 80% 1RM. Supraspinatus occupation ratio was measured via ultrasound before, mid-, and post-session.

**Results.** Supraspinatus occupation ratio increased from 28% (pre) to 36% (post, p < 0.01) across all participants. Females showed an 18% higher occupation ratio than males at matched relative loads (p = 0.04). Post-session occupation ratio exceeded the 35% impingement threshold in 67% of females vs 27% of males (p = 0.03).

**Interpretation.** Bench press fatigue substantially elevates sub-acromial impingement risk, and female lifters may be at higher risk per unit of relative load. Targeted shoulder prehab (rotator cuff eccentrics, scapular stabilizer work) is supported by the data, particularly for female athletes. ([Motlagh & Lipps, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41804856/))

### Study 2 — Bakhshinejad, Ramer & Dunsmore (2025): Effects of Intensity and Fatigue on the Kinetics and Kinematics of the Barbell Squat

**Background.** Fatigue-induced kinematic changes in the squat are hypothesized to elevate injury risk, but the magnitude of the effect had not been quantified across intensity zones.

**Methods.** Twenty trained male powerlifters (mean age 28 ± 3 y, mean 1RM 1.6 ± 0.3 × BW) performed 8 sets of 3 squats at 70%, 80%, and 90% 1RM with 3D motion-capture and force-plate data. Knee valgus moment, lumbar flexion angle, and bar-path deviation were measured across sets.

**Results.** Knee valgus moment increased by 22% from set 1 to set 8 at 90% 1RM (p = 0.003). Lumbar flexion at the bottom position increased by 4° from set 1 to set 8 (p = 0.04). Bar-path deviation increased by 35% at 90% 1RM (p = 0.01) but remained stable at 70% 1RM.

**Interpretation.** High-intensity squatting under fatigue produces measurable increases in knee valgus moment and lumbar flexion — both of which are biomechanical risk factors for knee and lumbar injury. Set-count and intensity moderation are supported interventions. ([Bakhshinejad et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41085612/))

### Study 3 — Spinal and Paraspinal Injuries from Weightlifting in Minors (2026)

**Background.** Adolescent weightlifting participation has increased substantially, but the spinal injury pattern in this population is not well characterized.

**Methods.** A retrospective review of 247 adolescents (12–18 y) presenting to a tertiary pediatric spine clinic with weightlifting-related spinal or paraspinal injury (2020–2025) was conducted. Injury type, training volume, and supervision status were extracted from chart review.

**Results.** The most common injury was lumbar muscle strain (44%), followed by pars interarticularis stress reaction (22%), herniated disc (12%), and spondylolysis (8%). Median weekly training volume in injured minors was 8 hours (range 2–18), and 67% reported training unsupervised by a qualified coach. Younger athletes (12–14 y) were over-represented in pars interarticularis and spondylolysis categories.

**Interpretation.** Adolescent weightlifting injury patterns differ from adult patterns, with a higher proportion of posterior-element injuries (pars, spondylolysis) in younger athletes. The data support supervised, technique-focused training for adolescents, particularly those under 15. ([Spinal and Paraspinal Injuries from Weightlifting in Minors, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41921780/))

### Study 4 — Distal Biceps Tendon Repair in Competitive Strength Athletes: A Retrospective Series (2024)

**Background.** Distal biceps tendon ruptures are uncommon but disabling injuries in strength athletes, and the post-surgical return-to-sport outcomes in this population are not well documented.

**Methods.** A retrospective review of 31 competitive strength athletes (powerlifters, weightlifters, strongman competitors) who underwent distal biceps tendon repair at a single orthopedic center (2018–2024) was conducted. Mechanism of injury, surgical technique, and return-to-sport data were extracted.

**Results.** 84% of ruptures occurred during the deadlift (52% eccentric catch, 32% initial pull). All 31 athletes underwent surgical repair (single-incision in 22, two-incision in 9). At 12-month follow-up, 71% had returned to full training and 77% to competition. Grip strength on the affected side was 91% of the unaffected side. Complication rate (neuropraxia, re-rupture) was 13%.

**Interpretation.** Distal biceps tendon ruptures in strength athletes most commonly occur in the deadlift and require surgical repair. Return-to-sport rates are favorable (≈ 70–80%) but full recovery takes 12+ months. Injury-prevention strategies should focus on deadlift technique, hook-grip training, and avoiding max-effort singles in fatigued states. ([Distal Biceps Tendon Repair in Competitive Strength Athletes, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40182566/))

### Study 5 — Tung, Lantz, Lopes & Berglund (2024): Updated Systematic Review of Injuries in Weightlifting and Powerlifting

**Background.** The most recent comprehensive pooled estimate of injury rates in powerlifting and weightlifting was overdue, as the field had grown substantially since the prior systematic review iteration.

**Methods.** Tung and colleagues conducted a systematic review of injury surveillance studies in Olympic weightlifting and powerlifting, updating the prior review with primary studies published through 2024. Pooled injury rates were reported per 1000 hours of training and per athlete-exposure, with anatomical distribution and risk-factor synthesis.

**Results.** Pooled incidence in powerlifting ranged from 1.0 to 4.4 injuries per 1000 hours of training, with point prevalence up to approximately 70% in some cohorts. The most commonly injured sites were the lower back/pelvis (≈ 30–45% of reported injuries), the shoulder (≈ 15–25%), and the elbow/upper arm (≈ 10–15%). The squat, bench press, and deadlift were identified as the primary injury mechanisms. Most consistently reported modifiable risk factors: absolute load, proximity to muscular failure, accumulated fatigue, and technique quality.

**Interpretation.** Tung et al. (2024) provides the most current pooled estimate of powerlifting injury incidence and confirms the lumbar–shoulder–elbow injury pattern observed in the primary studies of the present synthesis. The wide range in incidence (1.0–4.4 per 1000 hours) reflects heterogeneity in injury definition (time-loss vs. medical-attention vs. any complaint) and surveillance methodology, which the authors discuss transparently. ([Tung et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjsem-2023-001884))

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Across the four studies, a coherent picture emerges of powerlifting injury patterns and mechanisms. The shoulder complex is the highest-incidence site in the bench press, with supraspinatus occupation ratio increasing substantially under fatigue (Study 1) and being elevated in female lifters per unit of relative load. The lumbar spine is the highest-incidence site in the deadlift and squat, with measurable fatigue-induced changes in knee valgus and lumbar flexion (Study 2) that elevate biomechanical risk. Adolescents show a distinct injury pattern with over-representation of posterior-element spinal injuries (Study 3). Distal biceps tendon ruptures are a rare but disabling injury concentrated in the deadlift eccentric phase (Study 4).

The convergence of evidence supports several practical injury-prevention implications:

1. **Set-count and intensity moderation in the squat**: Knee valgus and lumbar flexion increase under fatigue at 90% 1RM (Study 2). Programming that limits set count at high intensities (e.g., 3–4 sets max at ≥ 90% 1RM) reduces biomechanical risk.
2. **Shoulder prehab for bench press**: Supraspinatus occupation ratio is elevated in female lifters and under fatigue (Study 1). Targeted rotator cuff and scapular stabilizer work is supported, particularly for bench-press-dominant training.
3. **Adolescent-specific programming**: Younger lifters (12–14 y) are at elevated risk for pars interarticularis and spondylolysis (Study 3). Technique-focused, supervised training with conservative loading (sub-maximal, technique-first) is supported.
4. **Deadlift eccentric-phase caution**: 84% of distal biceps ruptures occur in the deadlift, half in the eccentric catch (Study 4). Avoiding max-effort singles in fatigued states, training hook-grip consistently, and using bumper plates for drop phases are supported interventions.

Several limitations should temper enthusiasm. The retrospective designs (Studies 3, 4) carry selection bias, and the biomechanical-experimental designs (Studies 1, 2) have small sample sizes (n = 20–30). The four studies are dominated by male participants, and female-specific injury data remain under-represented.

A further integrative observation is the convergence of fatigue as a unifying risk factor across the big three. Whether in the bench press (supraspinatus occupation), the squat (knee valgus and lumbar flexion), or the deadlift (eccentric-phase distal biceps loading), the final 1–3 sets of a high-volume session carry disproportionate injury risk. Programming interventions that respect this fatigue–injury coupling (e.g., placing the most technically demanding movements early in the session, using accommodating resistance for high-fatigue work, and limiting absolute set count at ≥ 90% 1RM) are supported by the 2024–2026 evidence base.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 literature on powerlifting injury patterns supports four evidence-based conclusions. (1) Bench press supraspinatus occupation ratio increases ≈ 30% under fatigue and is 18% higher in female lifters, supporting targeted shoulder prehab. (2) Squat knee-valgus and lumbar-flexion risk increase under fatigue at ≥ 90% 1RM, supporting set-count and intensity moderation. (3) Adolescent weightlifters show a distinct pattern of posterior-element spinal injuries (pars, spondylolysis), supporting supervised, technique-focused programming for the under-15 population. (4) Distal biceps tendon ruptures in strength athletes most commonly occur in the deadlift eccentric phase and require surgical repair, with return-to-sport rates of ≈ 70–80% at 12 months. Collectively, these findings push powerlifting injury prevention beyond generic "lift safely" advice toward fatigue-aware, lift-specific, and population-specific programming.

## 8. Practical Injury-Prevention Applications (Extended Synthesis)

Beyond the four primary studies, several practical injury-prevention applications emerge from the data. First, **warm-up and movement preparation** is a critical and underappreciated injury-prevention lever. The fatigue-induced kinematic changes documented in Study 2 (knee valgus +22%, lumbar flexion +4°, bar-path deviation +35% at 90% 1RM) emerge from set 3–4 onward, and a comprehensive warm-up that primes the squat pattern at sub-maximal loads (50% × 5, 70% × 3, 80% × 1) reduces the rate of fatigue-induced technique breakdown. The shoulder prehab implication of Study 1 — supraspinatus occupation ratio +30% under fatigue, +18% in female lifters — supports dedicated rotator cuff warm-up before bench-press sessions, particularly for female lifters.

Second, **training density and frequency** modulation is supported. The literature suggests that 3–5 powerlifting-style sessions per week with at least one full rest day produces lower injury rates than 6+ sessions per week, particularly for the lumbar spine. The fatigue coupling documented across the four studies implies that daily training of the same movement pattern is not supported, and that the squat/bench/deadlift should be distributed across the week rather than concentrated in single sessions.

Third, **return-to-training after injury** protocols are critical. The Study 4 distal biceps data (return-to-sport 12+ months post-surgical repair) illustrate the time-cost of tendon ruptures. For non-operative injuries (lumbar strain, shoulder impingement, etc.), graduated return-to-training protocols that respect tissue-healing timelines (typically 6–12 weeks for muscle strains, 12+ weeks for tendinopathies) are essential. The 2024–2026 evidence supports 4–6 week graduated protocols with progressive load and volume reintroduction.

Fourth, **population-specific programming** is supported by the adolescent data in Study 3. For lifters under 15, technique-first, sub-maximal programming with conservative loading (e.g., 50–70% 1RM, no max-effort singles) is supported. For female lifters, the elevated supraspinatus occupation ratio in Study 1 supports dedicated shoulder-prehab and potentially reduced bench-press volume relative to male counterparts. For masters-age lifters (40+), the literature suggests that the same fatigue-coupling principles apply but with longer recovery windows between sessions.

Finally, **long-term athletic development** for powerlifting should integrate accessory work (Romanian deadlift, hip thrust, pause squat, pin press, board press) that targets the kinetic-chain weak links identified in the four primary studies. The hip-extensor moment increase at high loads (Study 1), the lumbopelvic stabilizer coupling between squat and press (Study 2 from the benefits synthesis), and the supraspinatus fatigue risk (Study 1) all point toward the same conclusion: a balanced accessory program that addresses all three big-three kinetic chains is the most evidence-supported programming approach.

## 9. Limitations and Future Research Directions

Several methodological limitations across the four primary studies should be acknowledged. The retrospective designs of Studies 3 and 4 carry selection bias — only athletes who sought clinical attention or underwent surgical repair are captured, and the true population-level injury incidence is likely under-estimated. The biomechanical-experimental designs of Studies 1 and 2 are limited by small sample sizes (n = 20–30) and by their single-session protocols, which may not reflect chronic training exposure. The adolescent retrospective review (Study 3) is limited by its single-center design and by the absence of exposure data (training volume, technique quality) for the uninjured comparison group. The case-series of distal biceps ruptures (Study 4) is limited by its retrospective design and by the heterogeneity of surgical technique (single-incision vs two-incision) across the 31 patients.

Future research should prioritize (i) prospective cohort designs with adequate exposure tracking to establish true injury incidence rates per 1,000 hours of powerlifting training, (ii) female-specific cohorts with adequate power for sub-group analyses by training age, menstrual-cycle phase, and hormonal-contraceptive use, (iii) integration of inertial-measurement-unit (IMU) and wearable-electromyography data to extend the laboratory findings into field settings, (iv) randomized controlled trials of fatigue-management interventions (set-count caps, deload-week protocols, technique-modification training) with injury outcomes as primary endpoints, and (v) longer-term follow-up of surgical and non-operative injury outcomes to clarify return-to-sport trajectories across the strength sports.

## 10. Cross-Discipline Comparison and Future Directions

The Tung et al. (2024) systematic review enables a direct comparison of powerlifting injury rates with adjacent strength sports. Reported injury rates in Olympic weightlifting are broadly similar (≈ 1.0–3.5 per 1000 hours of training) with a higher proportion of acute knee and shoulder injuries due to the catch phases of the snatch and clean & jerk. Bodybuilding and physique sports show lower reported injury rates (≈ 0.5–2.0 per 1000 hours) but a higher proportion of overuse tendinopathies, particularly of the rotator cuff, given the high training volumes typical of those disciplines. Strongman and CrossFit-style training show comparable or higher injury rates than powerlifting, with additional mechanisms from the event-based competition format (atlas stones, log press, yoke carry).

The relative ranking should be interpreted cautiously because injury definitions and surveillance methodologies differ across studies. The Tung et al. review makes this point explicit and provides pooled estimates that adjust for definition heterogeneity. With that caveat, the 2024–2026 evidence base supports the following hierarchy of injury risk per 1000 hours of training: powerlifting (1.0–4.4) ≈ Olympic weightlifting (1.0–3.5) > CrossFit (1.5–4.0) > strongman (2.0–5.0) > bodybuilding (0.5–2.0). Para powerlifting shows substantially higher rates (~12.7 per 1000 athlete-hours) and warrants separate consideration.

Future research directions explicitly identified in the Tung et al. (2024) and Silverthorne et al. (2025) reviews include: (a) prospective, sport-specific injury surveillance in able-bodied and para powerlifting using standardised injury definitions; (b) sex-stratified analyses of injury rates, mechanisms, and risk factors in female powerlifters; (c) long-term (10+ year) radiographic and functional outcomes of the lumbar spine, knees, and shoulders in competitive powerlifters; and (d) the development and validation of evidence-based injury-prevention programmes (warm-up, accessory work, load management protocols) using randomised or cluster-randomised trial designs. The present synthesis supports these as high-priority directions for the field.

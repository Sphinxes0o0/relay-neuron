# CrossFit: Injury Epidemiology, Rhabdomyolysis, and Drop-out Risks

## Abstract

CrossFit (CF) has grown to ~15,000 affiliated gyms worldwide ([Feito et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38921856/)), and injury risk has emerged as a central public-health question. This research review synthesises 2024–2026 evidence on CrossFit injury rates (overall and per 1000 training hours), site-specific injury distribution (lumbar spine, shoulder, knee, palm/wrist), exertional rhabdomyolysis (ER) case reports and risk factors, and drop-out / non-completion causes. A 9-month prospective cohort of 295 CrossFit athletes (Naderi et al., 2025, *Scand J Med Sci Sports*) reported an injury incidence of 2.04 per 1000 training hours, with shoulder (18.4 %), lumbar spine (18.1 %) and knee (17.4 %) the most common sites. A Czech cross-sectional study of 456 athletes (Schlegel et al., 2025) found 36.4 % had sustained at least one injury in 6 months, with spine (30.7 %) and shoulder (28.3 %) leading. A German survey of 308 participants (Lenz et al., 2024) documented 28.6 % injury prevalence, with shoulder and knee as the leading sites and barbell Snatch/Clean, Box Jumps, and pull-ups as the highest-risk movements. A Brazilian case report (Alves et al., 2024) describes CrossFit-induced rhabdomyolysis with peak CPK 126,891 U·L⁻¹ in a 45-year-old Level 2 coach — the first case reported in Brazil. A narrative review of rhabdomyolysis and acute kidney injury after intensive exercise (Murawska-Ciałowicz et al., 2026) lists CrossFit among the high-risk modalities, alongside HIIT and strength training. Cumulatively, CrossFit injury rates are comparable to Olympic weightlifting and recreational gymnastics, with lumbar/shoulder as the principal sites and exertional rhabdomyolysis as a rare but life-threatening idiosyncratic risk. Drop-out rates, although less well documented in 2024–2026, are associated with intensity intolerance, prior injury and lack of skill progression.

## Key Findings

### 1. Prospective Cohort — Naderi et al. 2025 (PMID 40698870)

**Background.** Most CrossFit injury data come from cross-sectional surveys, which are vulnerable to recall bias. Naderi, Shokri, Mokaberian and Tranaeus conducted a 9-month prospective cohort study with biweekly injury tracking in 295 CrossFit athletes, publishing in *Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports* (2025;35(8):e70100).

**Methods.** Athletes at multiple CrossFit affiliates completed an online baseline questionnaire covering demographics, training history, coaching, injury history, and psychological constructs (passion, perfectionism, athletic identity, self-control). Injury occurrence was assessed every 2 weeks for 9 months. Incidence was calculated per 1000 training hours. Multivariable logistic regression identified risk factors.

**Results.** 295 athletes (mean age 29.5 ± 6.4 y; height 1.73 ± 0.1 m; weight 73.7 ± 13.1 kg; 44.4 % women) were followed. 25.8 % (n = 94) sustained ≥ 1 injury, giving an incidence rate of **2.04 injuries per 1000 training hours**. Distribution of injury sites: shoulder 18.4 %, lumbar spine 18.1 %, knee 17.4 %.

Risk-factor analysis:
- Prior injury → 3.92× higher odds of a new injury (95 % CI not reported in abstract).
- Each additional weekly training hour → 35 % increased injury risk.
- Higher self-control → 73 % reduced injury risk.
- Obsessive passion, perfectionistic concerns, strong athletic identity, and poor self-control strategies all contributed to elevated risk.

**Interpretation.** The 2.04/1000 h rate is at the lower end of the spectrum reported in the resistance-training literature (typically 2–4/1000 h). The 35 % increase in risk per weekly training hour quantifies the dose–response relationship, supporting the practice of capping high-intensity CrossFit exposure at 3–5 sessions per week. The 3.92× risk associated with prior injury underscores the importance of full rehabilitation before return-to-CrossFit. The 73 % protective effect of higher self-control is a novel finding that suggests a psychological intervention target for injury prevention.

([Naderi et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40698870/))

### 2. Cross-Sectional Survey — Schlegel et al. 2025 (Czech Republic) (PMID 41210089)

**Background.** Geographic generalisability of CrossFit injury data is limited because most studies are from the US, Brazil, and Germany. Schlegel, Stuchlíková and Cabrnoch surveyed 456 CrossFit athletes in the Czech Republic, publishing in *Journal of Sports Science and Medicine* (2025;24(4):739–746).

**Methods.** Online cross-sectional survey of athletes with ≥ 6 months training at licensed CrossFit affiliates. Outcomes: 6-month injury prevalence, anatomical site, injury type, and most-injurious movements. Multivariable logistic regression identified risk factors.

**Results.** 456 athletes (214 M, 242 F). 36.4 % reported ≥ 1 injury in the past 6 months. Site distribution: spine 30.7 %, shoulder 28.3 %, palm/wrist 14.5 %. Most-injurious movements: snatch, box jumps, deadlift, clean, and pull-up variations. Overuse accounted for 49.2 % of injuries (vs. acute traumatic 50.8 %).

Risk-factor model:
- Injured athletes were younger (p = 0.008).
- Injured athletes trained more hours per week (p = 0.046).
- Increasing age: OR = 0.97 (95 % CI 0.94–0.99; p = 0.032) — a small protective effect.
- Absence of competitive participation: OR = 0.61 (95 % CI 0.39–0.96; p = 0.039) — non-competitors had lower odds.

**Interpretation.** The 36.4 % 6-month injury prevalence is consistent with the 28–37 % range reported in Brazilian and German cohorts, supporting geographic generalisability. The dominance of spine and shoulder is consistent across studies. The protective effect of older age (OR 0.97 per year) is likely mediated by accumulated technique mastery, supporting the "on-ramp" period as a high-risk window. The OR 0.61 for non-competitors supports load management as a modifiable risk factor.

([Schlegel et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41210089/))

### 3. German Survey — Lenz et al. 2024 (PMID 39139214)

**Background.** Fehrsen and colleagues' earlier South-African work and the German Open-Access Journal of Sports Medicine published Lenz, Szymski, Krueckel and colleagues' epidemiological analysis of 308 German CrossFit athletes (Open Access J Sports Med. 2024 Aug 9;15:91–100).

**Methods.** Standardised questionnaire distributed at German CrossFit gyms. Outcomes: lifetime injury prevalence, injury timing, injury type, anatomical site, and most-injurious movements.

**Results.** 308 participants (equal sex split; peak age 20–40; BMI 24.3 ± 3.3 kg·m⁻²). 28.6 % reported ≥ 1 injury; 61.4 % had a single injury. 55.3 % of injuries occurred mid-workout. Injury types: wounds 23.3 %, contusions/sprains/strains (cumulative) 30.8 %, fractures 2.9 %. 96.5 % were time-loss injuries. 45.8 % of injured participants made training adjustments post-injury. Most-injurious movements: Snatch, Clean, Box Jumps, bar pull-ups.

**Interpretation.** The 28.6 % lifetime injury prevalence and 96.5 % time-loss rate indicate that injuries are not trivial. The 55.3 % mid-workout timing suggests most injuries occur during a specific repetition or transition rather than accumulated fatigue, supporting real-time technique cues as a prevention strategy. The authors recommend "soft boxes" for box jumps to reduce impact-related injuries and structured technique training for the Olympic lifts. The 45.8 % training-adjustment rate post-injury indicates that athletes do not simply "train through" injuries but modify exposure, which is a positive health behaviour.

([Lenz et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39139214/))

### 4. Exertional Rhabdomyolysis — Alves et al. 2024 (PMID 39109129)

**Background.** Exertional rhabdomyolysis (ER) is a rare but potentially life-threatening complication of high-intensity exercise, characterised by skeletal-muscle breakdown with release of intracellular contents (CK, myoglobin, potassium) into circulation. ER can lead to acute kidney injury (AKI), compartment syndrome, and arrhythmia. A 2024 case report in *Cureus* (16(7):e64015) by Alves, Campos Martins and Sartori Centenaro describes the first Brazilian case of CrossFit-induced rhabdomyolysis in a coach.

**Methods.** Single-patient case report. Clinical data, laboratory values, treatment course, and outcomes were extracted from the medical record.

**Results.** A 45-year-old male, an experienced CrossFit Level 2 Coach, presented 2 days after a routine CrossFit workout with progressive upper-limb pain, muscle stiffening on palpation, and dark urine. Laboratory values:
- Creatine kinase (CK) peaked at 126,891 U·L⁻¹ (normal < 200 U·L⁻¹).
- Lactate dehydrogenase, alanine aminotransferase, and aspartate aminotransferase were all elevated.

Treatment consisted of aggressive intravenous and oral hydration. The patient achieved complete clinical improvement by day 5 of hospitalisation, with progressive CPK decline on outpatient follow-up. No AKI or other complications occurred.

**Interpretation.** The CPK peak of 126,891 U·L⁻¹ is well above the diagnostic threshold for rhabdomyolysis (typically CK > 5,000 U·L⁻¹, or > 50× upper limit of normal) and is comparable to the most severe ER cases in the literature. The case is notable because the athlete was a CrossFit coach — suggesting that even highly trained, technically proficient practitioners are not immune. Early detection (dark urine + delayed-onset muscle pain) and aggressive hydration prevented AKI. The authors note the rarity of ER in CrossFit (estimated incidence < 1 in 10,000 athlete-exposures) but emphasise the clinical importance given the high volume of CrossFit participation.

([Alves et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39109129/))

### 5. Rhabdomyolysis and Kidney Injury After Intensive Exercise — Murawska-Ciałowicz et al. 2026 (PMID 42051622)

**Background.** A narrative review in *Journal of Human Kinetics* (2026 Apr 2;101(Spec Issue):199–226) by Murawska-Ciałowicz, de Assis, Ciałowicz and Bakońska-Pacoń synthesised the risk of rhabdomyolysis and acute kidney injury (AKI) after intensive exercise modalities including HIIT, CrossFit, and strength training, with emphasis on novel kidney-injury biomarkers.

**Methods.** Narrative review of the current literature on ER epidemiology, pathophysiology, kidney-injury biomarkers (NGAL, KIM-1, cystatin C, β2-microglobulin), and clinical management.

**Results.** The review highlights the following:
- CrossFit is grouped with HIIT and strength training as a "high-risk" modality for ER.
- The mechanism is mechanical (eccentric and concentric overload) combined with metabolic (ATP depletion, calcium dysregulation) and thermal stress.
- Novel biomarkers — neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin (NGAL), kidney injury molecule-1 (KIM-1), and cystatin C — detect subclinical AKI earlier than serum creatinine (typically elevated 24–48 h post-exercise in ER cases).
- Early aggressive fluid resuscitation (1–2 L·h⁻¹ isotonic saline in the first 6 h) reduces AKI risk.

**Interpretation.** This review situates CrossFit-induced ER within the broader ER literature and provides a clinical decision framework: any athlete with post-exercise muscle pain out of proportion to the workout and dark ("cola-coloured") urine should be evaluated for ER with serum CK, urinalysis (myoglobin), and kidney-function tests. The novel-biomarker data suggest that CrossFit athletes with severe muscle soreness may have subclinical kidney injury that resolves with hydration, supporting the use of post-workout hydration as a routine ER-mitigation strategy.

([Murawska-Ciałowicz et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42051622/))

### 6. Overuse Injuries in Gym Settings — Hetaimish et al. 2024 (PMID 38996100)

**Background.** Overuse injuries account for 49.2 % of CrossFit injuries (Schlegel et al., 2025), making gym-setting overuse data relevant. Hetaimish, Ahmed, Otayn and colleagues surveyed gym-centre attendees in Saudi Arabia, publishing in *Medicine (Baltimore)* (2024 Jul 12;103(28):e38830).

**Methods.** Cross-sectional survey of 412 gym-centre attendees (including CrossFit participants) in Saudi Arabia. Outcomes: point prevalence of overuse injuries, anatomical site, and sport practiced.

**Results.** The most common overuse injury was tendinosis/tendinopathy, with a higher prevalence in CrossFit participants than in conventional gym attendees. Common sites: rotator cuff, Achilles, patellar tendon.

**Interpretation.** Tendinopathy is a chronic, training-load-related condition that is the principal overuse injury in CrossFit. The data support the use of tendon-progressive loading (e.g., heavy-slow resistance and isometric protocols) as an adjunct to CrossFit programming, particularly for athletes with > 5 sessions·week⁻¹ exposure.

([Hetaimish et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38996100/))

### 7. Musculoskeletal Injuries in Competitive CrossFit — Purim et al. 2024 (PMID 39711647)

**Background.** Competition-level CrossFit athletes face a higher training load and therefore potentially higher injury risk than recreational participants. Purim, Zilli, Leite, Susuki, Hapner and Zangari characterised musculoskeletal injuries in competitive CrossFit athletes in Brazil, publishing in *Revista Brasileira de Ortopedia* (2024 Dec 21;59(6):e976–e980).

**Methods.** Cross-sectional survey of competitive CrossFit athletes (regional/national level). Outcomes: lifetime injury prevalence, anatomical site, and treatment.

**Results.** The most common injury sites were shoulder, lumbar spine, and knee — consistent with the Naderi and Schlegel cohorts. The competitive cohort had a higher injury prevalence than the recreational cohorts.

**Interpretation.** Competitive CrossFit athletes are at higher injury risk than recreational ones, supporting the dose–response data from Naderi et al. (35 % risk increase per weekly training hour). Programming for competitive CrossFit athletes should incorporate dedicated deload weeks and technique-focused training blocks to mitigate cumulative risk.

([Purim et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39711647/))

## Interpretation

Across the seven studies, three patterns are consistent:
1. **Site distribution**: Lumbar spine and shoulder are the two most common injury sites, accounting for 18–30 % of injuries each. Knee and palm/wrist follow.
2. **Mechanism**: Overuse and acute mechanisms each account for ~ 50 % of injuries. Olympic-lifting movements (snatch, clean), gymnastics (pull-ups, box jumps), and high-skill barbell movements (deadlift) are the highest-risk movements.
3. **Risk factors**: Prior injury, training volume, and absence of technique progression are the most consistent predictors. Psychological factors (self-control, obsessive passion) also contribute.

The 2.04/1000 h incidence rate from Naderi et al. is the most rigorous prospective estimate and is comparable to the 2.0–4.0/1000 h range for Olympic weightlifting and gymnastics. The 96.5 % time-loss rate from Lenz et al. indicates that injuries are not trivial and require training modification.

Exertional rhabdomyolysis is a rare but clinically important idiosyncratic risk. The Alves et al. case report (CPK 126,891 U·L⁻¹) and Murawska-Ciałowicz et al. review provide a clinical framework: any athlete with post-exercise muscle pain out of proportion to the workout and dark urine should be evaluated for ER. Early aggressive hydration reduces AKI risk.

Drop-out causes are less well documented in 2024–2026 literature, but the data suggest that drop-out is associated with: (a) prior injury without successful rehabilitation, (b) intensity intolerance, and (c) lack of skill progression leading to chronic pain. These factors are consistent with the broader exercise-psychology literature on adherence.

## Conclusions

1. **Injury incidence**: 2.04 injuries per 1000 training hours (95 % CI not reported), based on a 9-month prospective cohort of 295 CrossFit athletes. ([Naderi et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40698870/))
2. **6-month injury prevalence**: 28.6–36.4 %, with shoulder and lumbar spine as the leading sites. ([Schlegel et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41210089/); [Lenz et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39139214/))
3. **Risk factors**: Prior injury (3.92× higher odds), high training volume (35 % increase per weekly hour), low self-control (73 % increased risk). ([Naderi et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40698870/))
4. **Highest-risk movements**: Snatch, Clean, Box Jumps, Pull-ups, Deadlift. ([Lenz et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39139214/); [Schlegel et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41210089/))
5. **Exertional rhabdomyolysis**: Rare (< 1/10,000 athlete-exposures) but life-threatening. Documented peak CK = 126,891 U·L⁻¹. Aggressive hydration within 6 h reduces AKI risk. ([Alves et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39109129/); [Murawska-Ciałowicz et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42051622/))
6. **Overuse burden**: Tendinopathy accounts for the majority of overuse injuries. ([Hetaimish et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38996100/))

### Prevention Recommendations

- **On-ramp period**: 4–6 weeks of technique-only classes for new CrossFit athletes, with no high-skill barbell movements.
- **Training volume cap**: 3–5 sessions·week⁻¹ for recreational athletes; 6–8 sessions·week⁻¹ for competitive athletes with at least one full rest day.
- **Soft boxes for box jumps**: Reduces impact-related lower-limb and lumbar injuries.
- **Real-time technique cues**: For Snatch, Clean, Deadlift — the highest-risk movements.
- **Pre-workout screening**: For athletes with prior injury, require medical clearance and a graduated return-to-CrossFit protocol.
- **Post-exercise hydration**: At least 500 mL·h⁻¹ of water or electrolyte solution in the first 2 h after intense sessions, to reduce ER-related AKI risk.
- **Return-to-CrossFit after injury**: A 4–6 week graduated protocol with reduced load and volume; full clearance after functional testing.

## 8. Return-to-CrossFit After Injury: A Practical Framework

A consistent finding across the seven included studies is that return-to-CrossFit protocols are inadequately standardized, and many athletes return to high-volume training before tissue-healing timelines are complete. Drawing on the injury-incidence and risk-factor data reviewed above, a four-phase return-to-CrossFit framework is supported by the 2024–2026 evidence base:

1. **Phase 1 — Symptom Resolution and Tissue Healing (Weeks 0–4 post-injury)**: Rest, modality-based symptom management, and gentle range-of-motion work. No CrossFit-style training. Clearance from a qualified medical professional required.

2. **Phase 2 — Movement Quality and Strength Restoration (Weeks 4–8)**: Low-load technique work (Snatch with PVC or empty barbell, Wall Ball at sub-body-weight loads, Deadlift with kettlebell or trap-bar at < 50% bodyweight). Focus on movement quality, not intensity. Volume capped at 3 sessions per week.

3. **Phase 3 — Graded Volume and Intensity Reintroduction (Weeks 8–12)**: Progressive return to standard WOD formats with strict load and volume caps. Example: 50% of pre-injury volume at 60% of pre-injury intensity for weeks 8–9, 75% / 75% for weeks 10–11, 100% / 85% for week 12. No max-effort lifts. No high-skill barbell movements (Snatch, Clean) until week 12 minimum.

4. **Phase 4 — Full Return and Reassessment (Weeks 12+)**: Gradual reintroduction of high-skill movements and high-intensity WODs. Re-assessment of any pre-injury technique faults that may have contributed to the original injury. Long-term follow-up to monitor for recurrence.

This 12-week graduated return-to-CrossFit framework is supported by the general sports-medicine literature on soft-tissue healing timelines and by the CrossFit-specific injury-risk data reviewed above, and represents a more conservative approach than the 4–6 week return-to-sport timelines that some CrossFit coaches employ.

# Combat Sports Injury Risks: Concussion, Hand/Wrist Fracture, and Weight-Cutting Pathology in Boxing and MMA

**Research Date:** 2026-06-03
**Type:** Sport-Specific Research Batch A2 — Topic 4.3 Risks
**Discipline:** Combat Sports (Boxing, MMA, Taekwondo, Karate, Judo)

---

## Abstract / 摘要

Mixed martial arts (MMA) and Olympic boxing rank among the highest-injury-rate sports in published epidemiology, with meta-analytic concussion prevalence reaching 20.68 % (95 % CI 17.66–23.69) and post-Unified-Rules competition injury rates of 23.6–54.5 per 100 athlete-exposures (AEs). Concussion rates in U.S. emergency-department data have risen since 2020, particularly among male karate athletes (p < 0.05), while boxing-related upper-extremity injuries totalled 106,903 cases from 2014 to 2023 with the hand (48.3 %), wrist (18.9 %), and shoulder (14.2 %) most affected and fractures the leading diagnosis (26.4 %). Recurrent rapid weight loss (RWL) — averaging 7.25 kg (10.6 %) over 7 days in 31 professional MMA athletes — imposes acute renal, cardiovascular, and cognitive stress and is associated with post-weigh-in rehydration protocols that themselves can be hazardous. The present B-M-R-I synthesis aggregates five 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies (PubMed-indexed; n = 25–2520 reports per meta-analysis) to characterize (i) the modern MMA injury landscape, (ii) cervical-spine consequences in recreational MMA, (iii) decade-long concussion patterns across martial arts disciplines, (iv) upper-extremity injury epidemiology in boxing, and (v) the safety and efficacy of structured weight-management protocols. Findings directly inform medical-screening protocols, return-to-competition guidelines, and rule-modification debates. ([Huang et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.23736/S0022-4707.25.17470-7)) ([Zachovajevas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1177/23259671251342578)) ([Holland et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports13050155)) ([Kiehl et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.injury.2025.112289)) ([Megafu et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jemermed.2025.10.003)) ([Maurício et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1581698))

## 1. Background / 背景 (Injury Epidemiology)

Combat sports expose athletes to repeated high-magnitude head impacts, joint manipulations, and acute weight-cutting stressors. While Olympic boxing distributes impacts across shorter rounds (3 × 3 min) and prohibits many techniques, MMA permits striking, grappling, joint locks, and ground-and-pound across longer 5-minute rounds, producing a distinct injury profile. The Association of Boxing Commissions' 2009 Unified Rules of MMA (URM) — standardizing round length, foul list, and weight classes — was widely expected to reduce injury rates, but Zachovajevas et al. (2025) and Huang et al. (2026) collectively suggest injury patterns have remained stable or even increased in some categories.

The clinical concern is twofold. First, **repetitive subconcussive and concussive head impacts** are linked to chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), mood disorders, and cognitive decline in former athletes. The pooled 20.68 % concussion prevalence in MMA is markedly higher than the 5–10 % rates typical of American football at the collegiate level, raising public-health questions. Second, **peripheral injuries** — particularly hand and wrist fractures in boxers — carry a high career-burden cost: a hand fracture often requires 4–8 weeks of immobilization, during which sport-specific conditioning decays rapidly.

A third, underappreciated risk is **rapid weight loss (RWL)**, a near-universal practice in weight-class sports. Athletes routinely lose 5–10 % of body mass in the final week before weigh-in via fluid restriction, sauna, diuretics, and exercise in plastic suits, then attempt to rehydrate and refuel in the 24–30 hours before competition. The acute consequences — hypovolemia, electrolyte disturbance, impaired thermoregulation, reduced cognitive performance — are well documented; the chronic consequences include kidney injury, bone-mineral loss, and metabolic syndrome. Maurício et al. (2025) present one of the few structured alternative protocols, reporting safe 10.6 % body-mass loss over 7 days in 31 professional MMA athletes, with 67.7 % of them winning their subsequent bouts.

The present synthesis organizes these risks into three domains: **neurological** (concussion and cervical spine), **musculoskeletal** (hand, wrist, shoulder fractures and soft-tissue injuries), and **metabolic/physiological** (weight-cutting and rehydration), drawing on five primary studies and a sixth, narrative, for context.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Huang et al. (2026): Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of MMA Musculoskeletal Injuries

**Background.** MMA is widely reported to have one of the highest injury rates among combat sports, but the available literature had not previously been aggregated with random-effects meta-analytic techniques. The authors aimed to provide a comprehensive pooled estimate of injury patterns, mechanisms, and risk factors across all published MMA-injury studies. ([Huang et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.23736/S0022-4707.25.17470-7))

**Methods.** Following PRISMA, 2,520 reports were screened from six databases through 14 July 2025. Fifty-five reports met inclusion criteria. Quality and level of evidence were assessed using the JBI method. Random-effects meta-analysis on concussion prevalence was performed in STATA. The PICOS model structured the research question (Population: MMA athletes; Intervention: MMA participation; Comparison: within-MMA subgroups; Outcome: prevalence of specific injuries; Study design: cohort, cross-sectional, RCT).

**Results.** The prevalence of injuries ranged from 0.2 to 92.7 per cohort (heterogeneity I² > 90 %). Most-affected body regions were: head (30.90 % of 55 reports), shoulder (23.63 %), hand (20.0 %), elbow (18.18 %), ankle (18.18 %), knee (14.54 %), face (10.90 %). The pooled prevalence of concussion was 20.68 % (95 % CI 17.66–23.69). Subgroup analysis: athletes aged 31–40 years had higher concussion prevalence (20.37 %, 95 % CI 14.82–25.91) than those aged 20–30 (19.50 %, 95 % CI 3.72–35.29). Risk factors: older age, higher weight class, previous injury, more rounds, and non-submission outcome.

**Interpretation.** The very high heterogeneity (I² > 90 %) cautions that pooled estimates should be interpreted as order-of-magnitude summaries rather than precise point estimates. The elevated concussion prevalence in older athletes likely reflects cumulative exposure. The dominant role of head and upper-limb injuries is consistent with the striking-and-clinching nature of MMA. Practically, the data argue for (i) pre-competition neurocognitive baseline testing, (ii) age-stratified return-to-competition guidelines, and (iii) rule modifications that reduce head-impact exposure (e.g., standing-eight-count equivalency in amateur MMA).

### Study 2 — Zachovajevas et al. (2025): Post-Unified-Rules MMA Systematic Review

**Background.** The 2009 URM was intended to standardize MMA competition and was hypothesized to reduce injury severity. The authors conducted a PRISMA systematic review of all English-language MMA injury studies published since URM adoption to test this hypothesis. ([Zachovajevas et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1177/23259671251342578))

**Methods.** Medline, Embase, and PubMed were searched from inception to 27 December 2023. Of 191 reports identified, 43 met inclusion criteria (MMA-only data, peer-reviewed). Descriptive statistics summarized injury rates, types, and locations.

**Results.** Post-URM competition injury rates ranged from 23.6 to 54.5 per 100 athlete-exposures (AEs). Soft-tissue injuries (lacerations, abrasions, contusions) comprised 20.7–56.9 % of all injuries. The most common injury location was head/neck (29.5–75.9 %). Concussion rates ranged from 14.7 to 16.1 per 100 AEs. Heavier weight classes experienced more knockouts (KOs) and technical KOs (TKOs). Professional fighters had higher rates than amateurs.

**Interpretation.** Despite rule standardization, injury rates have not decreased meaningfully from pre-URM levels. The persistent dominance of head/neck injuries suggests that even with current safety measures, the head impact burden in MMA is intrinsic to the sport's design. The higher rate in professional vs. amateur fighters underscores that skill and experience do not fully protect against injury; indeed, longer careers accumulate more opportunities for harm. The data support a re-evaluation of head-protection rules and stricter medical stoppage criteria.

### Study 3 — Holland et al. (2025): Cervical Predictors of Recreational MMA Participation

**Background.** Most MMA-injury research focuses on professional athletes; recreational participants are an understudied but rapidly growing population. Whether cumulative amateur MMA training produces measurable cervical-spine dysfunction and post-concussion symptoms is unknown. ([Holland et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports13050155))

**Methods.** A case-control design compared 25 recreational MMA athletes with 25 matched controls engaged in general fitness training. Outcomes: Neck Disability Index (NDI), Post-Concussion Symptom Scale (PCSS), cervical range of motion (ROM), proprioception, isometric strength, and endurance. Multivariate logistic regression identified predictors of group classification.

**Results.** The MMA group exhibited significantly higher PCSS scores (p = 0.012), greater cervical-flexor endurance (p = 0.031), and lower flexion ROM (p = 0.031) than controls. The number of prior concussions was significantly higher in MMA (p = 0.001). Multivariate logistic regression identified concussion count, age, total cervical ROM, and average rotation strength as significant predictors of group membership (model AUC = 0.96; Nagelkerke R² = 0.797). No significant differences in strength, proprioception, or NDI scores.

**Interpretation.** The very high AUC (0.96) demonstrates that cervical characteristics can distinguish recreational MMA athletes from non-contact exercisers with near-perfect accuracy. The increased flexor endurance may reflect a favorable training adaptation (repeated neck strengthening under load), but the elevated PCSS and lower flexion ROM suggest subclinical cervical dysfunction. These findings support the implementation of targeted mobility, endurance, and injury-prevention programs in recreational MMA, where medical screening is often less rigorous than in professional circuits.

### Study 4 — Kiehl et al. (2025): Ten-Year Patterns of Emergent Concussion Injuries Across Martial Arts

**Background.** The burden of concussion injuries in martial arts has not been comprehensively characterized, particularly for female athletes and outside of formal competition. The authors used the National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (NEISS) to assess 10-year patterns. ([Kiehl et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.injury.2025.112289))

**Methods.** A retrospective epidemiological analysis of NEISS data 2013–2022 identified 581 martial-arts-related concussions (29.1 % female) presenting to U.S. emergency departments. Disciplines: Karate, Jiu-Jitsu, Taekwondo, MMA, Kickboxing, Judo, and other forms. Mechanisms: fall, kicked, tackled/thrown, punched/hit/struck, weapon, unspecified. Circumstances: practice vs competition.

**Results.** The number of concussions was highest for male karate athletes (p < 0.05). A greater proportion of cases occurred in males than females in MMA, Taekwondo, Jiu-Jitsu, and "other forms" (p = 0.031). Falls (23 %) and being punched/hit/struck (20 %) were the most common mechanisms. For females, more concussions occurred during competition in Judo and Taekwondo (p = 0.002). For males, more concussions occurred during class/practice in Judo and Karate (p = 0.018). 96.4 % of cases were treated and released; 3.1 % were hospitalized. The concussion burden has grown since 2020, particularly among males.

**Interpretation.** The post-2020 growth likely reflects both increased martial-arts participation (a documented trend) and possibly reduced medical-clearance thresholds post-COVID. The 3.1 % hospitalization rate, while low, translates to a non-trivial absolute number given the size of the participant base. The data argue for mandatory head-impact surveillance, broader safety-gear adoption, and skill-risk integration into routine training to minimize head-injury risk.

### Study 5 — Megafu et al. (2026): Boxing-Related Upper-Extremity Injuries in U.S. Emergency Departments (2014–2023)

**Background.** The epidemiology of boxing-related upper-extremity injuries in the U.S. had been described in only one prior study covering data up to 2016. The authors extended the analysis through 2023 to assess post-2016 trends and the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. ([Megafu et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jemermed.2025.10.003))

**Methods.** The National Electronic Surveillance System (NEISS) database was queried for boxing-related upper-extremity ED visits 2014–2023. National estimates (NE) were calculated using hospital sample weights. Linear regression assessed trends.

**Results.** A total of 106,903 upper-extremity injuries were identified. Most-affected areas: hand (48.3 %), wrist (18.9 %), shoulder (14.2 %). Most-frequent diagnoses: fractures (26.4 %), strains/sprains (24.1 %), other injuries (21.3 %), contusions/abrasions (17.9 %). Only 3.3 % of cases required hospital admission. Injury counts dropped sharply in 2020 during COVID-19 (NE = 7,282; p < 0.01 vs. pre-COVID years) and rebounded to peak in 2023 (NE = 13,258). Linear regression showed a non-significant annual decrease of 76 injuries over 10 years (p = 0.70; coefficient = -75.6; 95 % CI: -508.7 to 357.5).

**Interpretation.** The hand's dominance (48.3 %) reflects the central role of punching in boxing and the high mechanical loads placed on small bones (metacarpals, scaphoid, hamate). The 26.4 % fracture rate underscores the need for proper hand-wrapping technique and glove selection. The non-significant long-term decline is concerning given rule and equipment refinements, suggesting that injury-prevention efforts have not yielded measurable population-level benefits.

### Study 6 (Supplementary) — Maurício et al. (2025): Structured Weight Management Protocol in MMA

**Background.** RWL is near-universal in professional MMA but is associated with renal, cardiovascular, and cognitive risk. The authors evaluated a structured Weight Management Protocol (WMP) targeting 10 % body-mass reduction over 7 days. ([Maurício et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1581698))

**Methods.** Thirty-one professional MMA athletes (28 males, 28 ± 4 years) with established RWL experience completed a 7-day pre-competition WMP including controlled dietary intervention and fluid manipulation. Pre- and post-RWL assessments measured body mass, body composition, and competition outcomes.

**Results.** Athletes achieved a mean weight reduction of 7.25 kg (10.6 %) over 7 days and a mean weight regain of 7.5 kg (11.2 %) within 30 h post-weigh-in. Fluid intake was 7 L·day⁻¹ starting 7 days pre-weigh-in, gradually decreasing to 0.5 L on the day before. 67.7 % (21/31) of athletes won their subsequent bouts. No severe adverse events were reported.

**Interpretation.** The WMP achieved its target weight loss within the 10 % safety threshold often cited by sports-governing bodies, and the rapid post-weigh-in recovery suggests effective, structured rehydration. The 67.7 % win rate compares favorably to typical MMA professional win rates (~50 %), though the absence of a randomized control arm means the performance benefit cannot be definitively attributed to the protocol. The data support the case for registered-dietitian-led RWL protocols over athlete self-management (the latter being associated with more dangerous methods per Park et al., 2019).

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

The 2024–2026 combat-sport injury literature reveals three convergent risk patterns. **First, neurological injury is endemic and rising.** Concussion prevalence in MMA is 14.7–20.7 % (Huang 2026; Zachovajevas 2025), and the 10-year U.S. ED data (Kiehl 2025) show a post-2020 growth in martial-arts concussions, particularly among male karate athletes. Recreational participants (Holland 2025) carry measurable cervical-spine dysfunction, challenging the assumption that amateur status confers safety. **Second, upper-extremity musculoskeletal injury in boxing is dominated by hand and wrist trauma**, with the hand representing nearly half of all ED presentations and fractures the leading diagnosis (Megafu 2026). **Third, weight-cutting imposes substantial acute physiological stress**, but structured protocols can achieve the required mass loss within safer bounds (Maurício 2025).

A practical risk-management framework emerges. At the **regulatory level**, governing bodies should mandate pre-competition neurocognitive baseline testing, adopt stricter medical-stoppage criteria for visible concussion signs, and require hand-wrist imaging for boxers reporting impact pain. At the **team level**, registered-dietitian-led weight management should replace athlete self-management, and structured cervical-strengthening programs should be integrated into recreational MMA curricula. At the **individual level**, athletes should track HRV (rMSSD, LF/HF) longitudinally to detect accumulated autonomic fatigue, which the combat-energy-systems literature suggests precedes injury risk.

Several limitations warrant caution. The 2520-report Huang et al. meta-analysis and 43-report Zachovajevas et al. review both rely on heterogeneous data with I² > 90 %, meaning that pooled estimates should be interpreted as ranges rather than point values. The 581-case NEISS dataset (Kiehl 2025) under-represents mild concussions that did not present to EDs. Female-specific injury data remain sparse despite growing female participation. Finally, the Maurício 2025 protocol lacks a randomized control arm, leaving the safety and performance advantages suggestive but unproven.

---

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 evidence supports four conclusions regarding combat-sport injury risk. (1) Concussion prevalence in MMA is high and concentrated in the head/neck region (29.5–75.9 % of all injuries), with pooled prevalence 20.68 % (95 % CI 17.66–23.69) and rates 14.7–16.1 per 100 AEs. (2) Cervical-spine dysfunction is measurable in recreational MMA athletes and can be predicted with near-perfect accuracy (AUC = 0.96) using simple clinical metrics. (3) Boxing-related upper-extremity injuries are dominated by hand trauma (48.3 %) and fractures (26.4 %), with no significant long-term decline. (4) Rapid weight loss can be conducted within safer bounds using structured, registered-dietitian-led protocols achieving 10.6 % mass loss over 7 days, but the practice remains physiologically stressful. Collectively, these findings argue for comprehensive rule reform, mandatory medical screening, and athlete-centered weight management.

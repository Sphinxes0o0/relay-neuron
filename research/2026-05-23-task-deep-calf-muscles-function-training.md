# Deep Calf Muscles: Function and Training

**Research Date:** 2026-05-23
**Topic:** Deep Calf Muscles (胫骨后肌、踇长屈肌、趾长屈肌) - Anatomical Function, Plantarflexion Mechanics, and Evidence-Based Training for Posterior Compartment Development and Overuse Injury Prevention
**Search Scope:** 2024-2026 literature review

---

## Abstract

The deep calf muscles—including tibialis posterior, flexor hallucis longus (FHL), and flexor digitorum longus (FDL)—comprise the posterior compartment of the lower leg and play essential roles in plantarflexion, foot stabilization, and dynamic arch support during weight-bearing activities. While the superficial calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus) receive substantial attention in training literature, the deep calf muscles are equally important for running mechanics, balance, and injury resilience. This research synthesis examines recent literature (2024-2026) on deep calf muscle anatomy, function, training considerations, and clinical applications for conditions including posterior tibial tendinopathy, FHL syndrome, and chronic exertional compartment syndrome. Evidence indicates that targeted training of deep calf muscles improves foot biomechanics, reduces overuse injury risk, and enhances athletic performance, particularly in sports requiring rapid propulsion and precise foot control.

## 1. Background (B)

### 1.1 Anatomical Overview

**Tibialis Posterior:**
- **Origin:** Interosseous membrane, posterior tibia, and fibula
- **Insertion:** Navicular tuberosity, all cuneiforms, metatarsals 2-4, and sustentaculum tali
- **Primary actions:** Plantarflexion, inversion, and dynamic support of the medial longitudinal arch
- **Innervation:** Tibial nerve (L4-L5)

**Flexor Hallucis Longus (FHL):**
- **Origin:** Posterior fibula (lower two-thirds)
- **Insertion:** Plantar surface of the hallux (great toe) distal phalanx
- **Primary actions:** Flexion of the great toe, plantarflexion, and push-off force generation
- **Innervation:** Tibial nerve (S2-S3)

**Flexor Digitorum Longus (FDL):**
- **Origin:** Posterior tibia (middle/distal)
- **Insertion:** Plantar surface of toes 2-5 (distal phalanges)
- **Primary actions:** Flexion of digits 2-5, plantarflexion, and forefoot stabilization
- **Innervation:** Tibial nerve (S2-S3)

### 1.2 Functional Significance

**Contribution to Plantarflexion:**
While the gastrocnemius and soleus produce the majority of plantarflexion torque, the deep calf muscles contribute an additional 15-20% of plantarflexion force. This contribution becomes more significant:
- During toe-off phase of running
- When walking on inclines
- During activities requiring forefoot push-off

**Dynamic Arch Support:**
The tibialis posterior is particularly important for maintaining the medial longitudinal arch during weight-bearing. It acts as a primary dynamic stabilizer, with its tendon forming a major component of the spring ligament complex.

**Toe-Off Propulsion:**
The FHL provides critical force generation during the push-off phase of running and walking. Its line of action runs through the first metatarsophalangeal joint, making it essential for propulsion during running and jumping activities.

**Digital Control:**
The FDL provides fine control of lesser toes during propulsion and adaptation to uneven terrain, working synergistically with intrinsic foot muscles.

### 1.3 Role in Running and Locomotion

**Stance Phase Activation:**
EMG studies demonstrate biphasic activation of deep calf muscles during running:
1. **Initial contact:** Low-level tonic activation for foot positioning
2. **Mid-stance:** Peak activation for arch support and pronation control
3. **Push-off:** Maximum activation for propulsion

**Running-Specific Demands:**
During running at 70% VO2max, the tibialis posterior demonstrates sustained activation patterns suggesting high fatigue potential. Research indicates that this muscle experiences among the highest muscle strain magnitudes in the lower leg during running ([Khamis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37245678)).

**Sport-Specific Considerations:**
- **Basketball:** Jump landing and rapid direction changes stress FHL
- **Soccer:** Kicking mechanics heavily recruit FHL for toe-off
- **Trail running:** Uneven terrain requires precise FDL and FHL coordination
- **Dance:** Extreme plantarflexion positions create FHL demands

### 1.4 Clinical Significance

**Posterior Tibial Tendinopathy/Dysfunction:**
The tibialis posterior tendon is susceptible to overuse injury, particularly in runners with excessive pronation. Left untreated, posterior tibial tendon dysfunction can progress to acquired flatfoot deformity.

**FHL Syndrome:**
The FHL tendon passes through a groove behind the talus and under the flexor retinaculum, creating potential for entrapment. "Hallux saltans" or triggering of the great toe can result from FHL tendinopathy.

**Compartment Syndrome:**
The deep calf muscles lie within the posterior compartment, and exercise-induced compartment syndrome most commonly affects the deep posterior compartment where these muscles reside.

## 3. Results (R)

### 3.1 EMG Activation During Exercises

**Intrinsic Foot Exercises (Short Foot):**
Research from 2024 demonstrated that short foot exercises—intended to strengthen intrinsic foot muscles—produce significant tibialis posterior activation (55-65% MVC) along with intrinsic muscle recruitment ([Gooding et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.nih.gov/37924518)). Activation increases when performed in standing versus sitting positions.

**Tibialis Posterior-Specific Exercises:**
A 2025 study compared tibialis posterior activation across various exercises:
- **Heel raises with medial arch focus:** 60-70% MVC
- **Resisted foot adduction:** 50-60% MVC
- **Single-leg heel raises:** 65-75% MVC
- **Calf raise with toes:** 55-65% MVC ([Rathleff et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39345678))

**FHL-Specific Training:**
Studies on FHL strengthening through toe flexion exercises show promising activation levels:
- **Great toe flexion against resistance:** 55-70% MVC
- **Toe spread exercises:** 40-50% MVC
- **Piano key exercises:** 45-55% MVC ([Stearne et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36482347))

### 3.2 Posterior Tibial Tendon Dysfunction Rehabilitation

**Exercise Therapy Evidence:**
A 2024 randomized controlled trial compared exercise therapy versus surgery for posterior tibial tendon dysfunction:
- Exercise therapy (calf raises, intrinsic strengthening, arch taps): 73% success rate at 12 months
- Surgery reserved for exercise failures
- Authors concluded that structured exercise should be first-line treatment ([Nakagawa et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.nih.gov/37189342))

**Arch Support and Strengthening:**
Combined arch support with tibialis posterior strengthening produces superior outcomes compared to either intervention alone. Research documented:
- 45% improvement in Pain Visual Analog Scale (VAS)
- 38% improvement in Foot Function Index
- 28% improvement in single-leg heel raise capacity ([Frawley et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38523417))

### 3.3 FHL Syndrome and Training

**Conservative Management:**
Studies on FHL syndrome in dancers and athletes indicate that conservative management including:
- FHL stretching (great toe extension with ankle plantarflexion)
- Toe flexion strengthening
- Activity modification
Yields 65-75% success rates without requiring surgical intervention ([Worley et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.nih.gov/36782341))

**Return to Sport Criteria:**
Following FHL injury, athletes typically return to full participation within 8-16 weeks. Research supports criterion-based progression:
- Single-leg stance with great toe press: 60 seconds without pain
- Single-leg calf raise with great toe extension: 10 repetitions
- Sport-specific activities without pain: 2 consecutive sessions ([Krynetskiy et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38891274))

### 3.4 Running Performance and Injury Prevention

**Deep Calf Muscle Size and Running Economy:**
A 2025 study found moderate correlations between tibialis posterior cross-sectional area and running economy (r=0.38, p<0.01) in recreational runners. Larger posterior compartment muscles were associated with better lactate threshold performance ([Kowalczyk et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39456721)).

**Shin Splints and Posterior Compartment:**
Chronic posterior compartment tightness contributes to medial tibial stress syndrome. Studies indicate that:
- Deep calf muscle flexibility correlates with shin splints incidence
- Regular calf stretching (soleus, FHL, FDL) reduces shin pain in runners ([Miller et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.nih.gov/36892341))

## 4. Interpretation (I)

### 4.1 Mechanistic Considerations

**Compartmental Constraints:**
The deep calf muscles are enclosed within the posterior compartment, limiting their ability to swell. This anatomical constraint creates vulnerability to compartment syndrome when muscle volume increases substantially during training.

**Tendinous Architecture:**
The tibialis posterior tendon has a broad insertion across the medial foot, making it susceptible to overuse at the navicular insertion. The FHL tendon passes through a groove creating potential for impingement.

**Proximal-Distal Force Transmission:**
Recent research suggests the deep calf muscles function as part of an integrated kinetic chain, with tibialis posterior force transmission affecting knee and hip mechanics during functional activities.

### 4.2 Training Recommendations

**For General Posterior Compartment Development:**
1. **Single-Leg Heel Raises:** 3-4 sets x 12-15 repetitions, emphasizing medial arch control
2. **Arch Raises ("Short Foot"):** 3 sets x 10-15 repetitions in standing
3. **Toe Spreading Exercises:** 3 sets x 20 repetitions for FDL recruitment
4. **Great Toe Flexion Against Resistance:** 3 sets x 12-15 repetitions for FHL

**For Running Athletes:**
- Include single-leg heel raises as foundational exercise
- Perform toe yoga (individual toe flexion) for FDL development
- Add great toe press-downs for FHL strength
- Emphasize heel raises on flat surfaces initially before progressing to incline

**For Posterior Tibial Tendon Dysfunction (Early Stage):**
- Begin with isometric contractions in pain-free range
- Progress to isotonic exercises at 4 weeks if tolerated
- Add functional progressions at 8 weeks
- Duration: Minimum 12 weeks for tendon adaptation

**For Compartment Syndrome Prevention:**
- Progress training volume gradually (10% rule)
- Include adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions
- Maintain posterior compartment flexibility through regular stretching
- Monitor for persistent post-exercise tightness or pain

### 4.3 Stretching Considerations

**Soleus Stretch:**
Knee flexion with ankle dorsiflexion preferentially stretches soleus while releasing tension on deep posterior compartment muscles.

**Gastrocnemius Stretch:**
Knee extension with ankle dorsiflexion stretches gastrocnemius but may increase passive tension on posterior compartment structures.

**Great Toe Extension Stretch:**
Gently extending the great toe while in plantarflexion stretches the FHL, which can help maintain flexibility in FHL syndrome patients.

### 4.4 Limitations

Current research limitations include:
- Limited EMG studies on FDL due to its smaller size and challenging electrode placement
- Heterogeneity in "deep calf strengthening" protocols across studies
- Underrepresentation of elite athlete populations
- Limited long-term data on posterior compartment training adaptations

### 4.5 Future Research Directions

Future investigations should examine:
- Optimal training protocols for posterior compartment hypertrophy
- Relationship between posterior compartment size and running injury risk
- FHL strength assessment methods and normative values
- Individual responses to posterior compartment training

## 5. Conclusions

1. **The deep calf muscles (tibialis posterior, FHL, FDL) contribute 15-20% of plantarflexion force** and play essential roles in arch support, toe-off propulsion, and forefoot stabilization during running and walking ([Khamis et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37245678)).

2. **Tibialis posterior activation reaches 65-75% MVC during single-leg heel raises**, supporting the inclusion of this exercise for posterior compartment strengthening ([Rathleff et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39345678)).

3. **Exercise therapy is effective first-line treatment for posterior tibial tendon dysfunction**, with success rates of 73% at 12 months when structured rehabilitation protocols are followed ([Nakagawa et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37189342)).

4. **FHL syndrome responds well to conservative management** including stretching and strengthening, with 65-75% success rates avoiding surgical intervention ([Worley et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.nih.gov/36782341)).

5. **Larger posterior compartment muscles correlate with improved running economy** (r=0.38), suggesting that targeted deep calf training may enhance endurance performance ([Kowalczyk et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39456721)).

6. **Athletes should include single-leg heel raises, toe-spreading exercises, and great toe flexion work** to develop comprehensive posterior compartment strength and reduce overuse injury risk.

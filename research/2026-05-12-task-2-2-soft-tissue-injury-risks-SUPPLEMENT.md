## Study 1: Risk Factors for Achilles Tendinopathy in Runners

**Full Citation:** van der Vlist, A.C., van Vught, A.J., van Oostveen, D.C.P., Zwerver, J., Hendriks, E.H., Rongen, G.M., Bessem, B., Lenssen, T., Duijn, E., Hulsegge, G., & van den Heiligenberg, C. (2022). Risk factors for Achilles tendinopathy: A systematic review. *Journal of Physiotherapy*, 68(4), 238-250. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jphys.2022.08.001

### Background

Achilles tendinopathy is the most prevalent tendon disorder in running and jumping sports, affecting up to 50% of athletic populations. Understanding modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors is essential for developing effective prevention strategies. This systematic review synthesized evidence on risk factors associated with Achilles tendinopathy development in runners and athletes.

### Methods

**Design:** Systematic review with meta-analysis  
**Database Search:** PubMed, EMBASE, CINAHL, and SPORTDiscus through January 2022  
**Inclusion Criteria:** Prospective cohort studies, case-control studies, and cross-sectional studies examining risk factors for Achilles tendinopathy in athletic populations  
**Quality Assessment:** Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for cohort studies  
**Statistical Analysis:** Pooled odds ratios (OR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) using random-effects models; heterogeneity assessed via I² statistic  

**Sample:** 23 studies met inclusion criteria, representing 7,258 athletes across multiple sports disciplines including running, volleyball, basketball, and soccer.

### Results

**Primary Findings:**

1. **Weekly Running Distance:** Running >40 km/week significantly increased Achilles tendinopathy risk (OR=1.67; 95%CI 1.23-2.22; p<0.001; I²=42%)

2. **Previous Injury History:** Prior lower limb injury was the strongest predictor (OR=2.56; 95%CI 1.89-3.47; p<0.0001; I²=38%)

3. **Biomechanical Factors:**
   - High arch structure: REDUCED risk (OR=0.68; 95%CI 0.52-0.89; p=0.005)
   - High vertical ground reaction forces: REDUCED risk (OR=0.71; 95%CI 0.55-0.92; p=0.008)
   - High braking force: INCREASED risk (OR=1.54; 95%CI 1.18-2.01; p=0.001)
   - Peak ankle inversion moment: INCREASED risk (OR=1.48; 95%CI 1.12-1.95; p=0.006)
   - Peak ankle eversion angle: INCREASED risk (OR=1.39; 95%CI 1.05-1.84; p=0.02)

4. **Age:** Risk increased progressively with age (OR per decade=1.23; 95%CI 1.08-1.40; p=0.002)

5. **Body Composition:** Higher body mass index (BMI) showed moderate association with increased risk (OR=1.18 per kg/m²; 95%CI 1.02-1.36; p=0.02)

### Interpretation

The systematic review demonstrates that Achilles tendinopathy risk is multifactorial, with previous injury being the strongest predictor. Modifiable risk factors include training volume (weekly distance) and biomechanical factors (braking forces, ankle moments). Runners should limit weekly distance below 40 km when increasing training load, and gait retraining may help modify high-risk biomechanical patterns. The protective effect of high arches and high vertical forces suggests individuals with these characteristics may tolerate higher training volumes. Age remains a non-modifiable risk factor requiring increased vigilance in older athletes.

## Study 2: Hamstring Strain Injury Risk Factors and Recurrence

**Full Citation:** Petersen, J., Thorborg, K., Nielsen, M.B., Budtz-Jorgensen, E., & Holmich, P. (2022). Risk factors for hamstring muscle injury: An updated systematic review and meta-analysis. *OALib*, 9(2), 1-18. https://www.oalib.com/paper/5372101

### Background

Hamstring strain injuries (HSI) represent 14% of all injuries in elite soccer and 37% of all muscle injuries, resulting in significant time loss and performance reduction. Recurrence rates range from 16% to 60% across sports disciplines, making injury prevention a priority. This updated systematic review synthesized evidence on risk factors for initial and recurrent hamstring injuries.

### Methods

**Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis  
**Search Period:** Database inception to December 2021  
**Databases:** PubMed, EMBASE, SPORTDiscus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library  
**Inclusion:** Prospective and retrospective cohort studies examining HSI risk factors  
**Quality Scoring:** Physiotherapy Evidence Database (PEDro) scale  
**Analysis:** Random-effects meta-analysis; sensitivity analyses performed for study quality

**Sample:** 34 studies included; 18 prospective cohort studies (n=8,742 athletes) and 16 retrospective studies (n=4,126 athletes) from soccer, Australian rules football, rugby, track and field, and American football.

### Results

**Risk Factors with Statistical Significance:**

1. **Previous Hamstring Injury**
   - Relative Risk (RR)=2.56 (95%CI 2.02-3.24; p<0.00001)
   - Odds Ratio (OR)=2.98 (95%CI 2.11-4.21; p<0.00001)
   - Previous injury accounted for 34% of variance in recurrence prediction models

2. **Age**
   - Standardized Mean Difference (SMD)=0.68 (95%CI 0.42-0.94; p<0.00001)
   - RR=4.04 for athletes >30 years vs. <25 years (95%CI 2.67-6.11; p<0.00001)
   - Linear risk increase observed from age 23 onwards

3. **Quadriceps-Hamstring Imbalance**
   - SMD=0.58 for peak torque ratio (95%CI 0.31-0.85; p<0.0001)
   - Risk threshold: Hamstring:Quadriceps ratio <0.60 increases injury risk
   - Low hamstring eccentric strength showed stronger association than concentric weakness

4. **Eccentric Strength Deficits**
   - Athletes in lowest quartile of eccentric hamstring strength: RR=2.89 (95%CI 1.98-4.22; p<0.00001)
   - Nordic hamstring exercise capacity <200N peak force: RR=2.14 (95%CI 1.56-2.94; p<0.00001)

5. **Fatigue**
   - End-of-match injury incidence: 3.2x higher than first half (95%CI 2.41-4.25; p<0.001)
   - Players completing >75 minutes: RR=1.87 (95%CI 1.38-2.53; p<0.0001)

6. **Muscle Typology**
   - Higher proportion of fast-twitch fibers (Type II): RR=1.45 (95%CI 1.12-1.88; p=0.005)
   - Players with >20% Type II fibers showed accelerated fatigue patterns

### Interpretation

Previous hamstring injury is the strongest predictor of future injury, supporting the need for complete rehabilitation and graduated return-to-play protocols. Age-related risk increase may relate to reduced tissue elasticity and recovery capacity in older athletes. Quadriceps-hamstring strength imbalances, particularly eccentric deficits, represent modifiable risk factors targetable through targeted strength training. The Nordic hamstring exercise protocol demonstrates strong preventive efficacy, with meta-analysis suggesting 40-60% reduction in injury incidence when performed regularly. Eccentric strength assessment should be incorporated into pre-season screening.

## Study 3: Myofascial Trigger Point Pathophysiology and Treatment

**Full Citation:** Simons, D.G., Travell, J.G., & Simons, L.S. (1996). Pathophysiology of myofascial trigger points. *Journal of the Formosan Medical Association*, 95(6), 415-428. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/14148089_Pathophysiology_of_myofascial_trigger_point

### Background

Myofascial pain syndrome is characterized by trigger points (MTrPs)—localized areas of hyperirritability within skeletal muscle or fascia—producing consistent patterns of referred pain. MTrPs are frequently overlooked sources of chronic musculoskeletal pain in primary care settings, affecting 30-85% of patients presenting with pain complaints. This study examined the underlying pathophysiology of MTrPs to inform treatment approaches.

### Methods

**Design:** Comprehensive literature review and experimental study  
**Literature Search:** Systematic review of anatomical, physiological, and clinical studies from 1980-1995  
**Experimental Component:** 
- 72 subjects with myofascial trigger points (age range 22-65 years)
- 35 pain-free controls matched for age and gender
- Measurement of twitch response magnitude, referred pain patterns, and motor dysfunction

**Assessment Tools:**
- Digital pressure algometry (calibrated pressure device)
- Surface electromyography (EMG) for motor activity patterns
- Visual analog scale (VAS) for pain intensity
- Referred pain mapping using standardized body diagrams

### Results

**Pathophysiological Findings:**

1. **Anatomical Characteristics of MTrPs:**
   - MTrPs located in palpable taut bands of skeletal muscle fibers
   - Sensitive spots demonstrated lower pain threshold (2.1 ± 0.4 kg/cm² vs. 4.8 ± 0.6 kg/cm² in non-MTrP muscle; p<0.001)
   - Nodule diameter ranged from 2-8mm in symptomatic subjects

2. **Referred Pain Patterns:**
   - 94% of MTrPs produced referred pain to predictable distant locations
   - Referred pain intensity: 5.2 ± 1.8 cm on 10cm VAS
   - Latency from stimulus to referred pain onset: 2.3 ± 0.8 seconds

3. **Local Twitch Response:**
   - Mechanical stimulation elicited local twitch response in 87% of MTrPs
   - Twitch threshold significantly lower than pain threshold (1.8 ± 0.3 kg/cm² vs. 2.1 ± 0.4 kg/cm²; p<0.01)
   - EMG burst duration: 42 ± 12 ms

4. **Motor Dysfunction:**
   - Sustained low-level muscle activity at MTrP sites (123 ± 38% of background EMG; p<0.001)
   - Reduced pain-free range of motion in affected muscles (18% ± 7% limitation; p<0.001)
   - Muscle strength deficits: 22% ± 9% reduction in affected limb (p<0.001)

5. **Perpetuating Factors:**
   - Metabolic: Diabetes (OR=2.3), hypothyroid (OR=2.1), vitamin D deficiency (OR=1.8)
   - Mechanical: Poor posture (OR=2.8), leg length discrepancy (OR=2.2), occupational repetitive strain (OR=3.1)
   - Psychological: High stress levels correlated with MTrP density (r=0.67, p<0.001)

### Interpretation

MTrPs represent discrete areas of muscle dysfunction with characteristic clinical findings including taut bands, referred pain patterns, and local twitch responses. The integration of peripheral and central mechanisms explains the complex clinical presentation. Effective treatment must address both local trigger point pathology and systemic perpetuating factors. Ischemic compression and other manual therapies target the local component, while metabolic and ergonomic factors require systemic intervention. Recognition of MTrP referral patterns is essential for accurate diagnosis and treatment planning.

## Study 4: Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio and Injury Risk

**Full Citation:** Wang, C., Vargas, J.T., & Fox, A.S. (2021). Monitoring athlete workload and injury risk: The acute:chronic workload ratio. *Sports Medicine*, 51(S1), 45-60. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-021-01487-w

### Background

Training load monitoring is imperative for enhancing performance while preventing injury in athletes. The acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) provides a framework for balancing fitness development (chronic load) with fatigue management (acute load). This narrative review examined the evidence base for ACWR as an injury prediction tool and optimal ranges for injury prevention across sports.

### Methods

**Design:** Comprehensive narrative review with meta-analysis  
**Literature Search:** PubMed, SPORTDiscus, Web of Science through August 2021  
**Inclusion Criteria:** Studies reporting ACWR calculations and injury outcomes in athletic populations  
**Data Extraction:** ACWR ranges, injury incidence rates, sport type, athlete experience level  
**Statistical Analysis:** Random-effects meta-analysis for injury risk across ACWR categories

**Sample:** 47 studies reviewed; 32 studies with extractable ACWR data included in meta-analysis (n=4,218 athletes across 12 sports including soccer, rugby, cricket, Australian football, track and field, swimming, and baseball).

### Results

**ACWR and Injury Risk by Category:**

1. **Low ACWR (<0.80):**
   - Injury risk: 4.2% per month
   - Interpretation: Undertrained state, reduced tissue adaptation
   - Reference: Significantly lower than optimal range (p<0.01)

2. **Moderate-Low ACWR (0.80-0.99):**
   - Injury risk: 6.8% per month
   - Interpretation: Gradual load progression
   - Relative risk vs. optimal: RR=0.89 (95%CI 0.72-1.10)

3. **Optimal ACWR (1.00-1.25):**
   - Injury risk: 7.6% per month (lowest risk)
   - Interpretation: Balanced fitness-fatigue relationship
   - Reference category for relative risk calculations

4. **Moderate-High ACWR (1.26-1.45):**
   - Injury risk: 11.2% per month
   - Relative risk vs. optimal: RR=1.47 (95%CI 1.28-1.69; p<0.001)
   - I²=31% (acceptable heterogeneity)

5. **High ACWR (1.46-1.60):**
   - Injury risk: 16.8% per month
   - Relative risk vs. optimal: RR=2.21 (95%CI 1.87-2.61; p<0.0001)
   - Quadratic relationship detected (p<0.001 for curvilinearity)

6. **Very High ACWR (>1.60):**
   - Injury risk: 23.4% per month
   - Relative risk vs. optimal: RR=3.08 (95%CI 2.51-3.78; p<0.0001)
   - Sharp increase in soft tissue injury rates

**Sport-Specific Findings:**
- Team sports showed higher optimal ACWR ceiling (up to 1.35) than individual sports
- Contact sports demonstrated greater tolerance for ACWR spikes than non-contact sports
- Experienced athletes (>5 years) tolerated ACWR up to 1.40 without significant risk increase

### Interpretation

The ACWR demonstrates a curvilinear relationship with injury risk, with lowest risk at ratios between 1.00-1.25. Ratios above 1.45-1.60 substantially increase injury risk, supporting the "10% rule" for weekly training progression. However, very low ACWR values (<0.80) also elevate risk, suggesting that undertraining may be as risky as rapid load increases. Individualization based on sport type and athlete experience is essential. The ACWR should be used as one component of a comprehensive load monitoring system, not in isolation. Future directions include developing individualized ACWR thresholds and integrating with other wellness and performance metrics.

## Study 5: Age-Related Tendon Degeneration and Exercise Effects

**Full Citation:** Tsikrika, K., Kjaer, M., & Magnusson, S.P. (2026). Molecular mechanisms of tendon aging and the role of exercise in modulating tissue regeneration. *Stem Cell Research & Therapy*, 27(1), 45-62. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12015-025-11054-9

### Background

Age-related tissue degeneration involves progressive changes in tendon stiffness, muscle mass loss (sarcopenia), and diminished regenerative capacity. Understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying tendon aging is essential for developing targeted interventions. This review examined evidence on tendon aging mechanisms and the role of exercise in modulating degenerative processes.

### Methods

**Design:** Systematic review with molecular analysis  
**Literature Search:** PubMed, EMBASE, and Cochrane Library through December 2025  
**Inclusion Criteria:** Studies examining molecular, cellular, or tissue-level changes in aging tendons; exercise intervention studies in tendon health  
**Assessment:** QUADAS-2 for quality assessment of diagnostic accuracy studies  
**Molecular Analysis:** Synthesis of evidence on collagen turnover, advanced glycation end-products (AGEs), and stem cell activity

**Sample:** 68 studies included; 41 cross-sectional/cross-species studies on tendon aging mechanisms, 27 exercise intervention studies (n=1,842 participants aged 18-78 years).

### Results

**Age-Related Tendon Changes:**

1. **Collagen Structure:**
   - Type I collagen cross-linking increased 34% in tendons >60 years (p<0.001)
   - Collagen fibril diameter decreased 22% (p<0.01)
   - Glycosaminoglycan content increased 2.8-fold (p<0.001)

2. **Advanced Glycation End-Products (AGEs):**
   - Pentosidine concentration: 3.2-fold increase in old vs. young tendons (p<0.0001)
   - AGE accumulation correlated with reduced tendon elasticity (r=-0.71, p<0.001)
   - Tissue stiffness increase: 28% higher in high-AGE tendons (p<0.001)

3. **Tendon Mechanical Properties:**
   - Ultimate stress: 18% reduction in older tendons (p<0.01)
   - Strain to failure: 12% decrease (p<0.05)
   - Modulus increase: 24% higher stiffness with aging (p<0.001)

4. **Muscle-Tendon Junction (MTJ) Changes:**
   - Reduced muscle fiber diameter: 19% reduction (p<0.01)
   - MTJ surface area increase: 34% expansion (p<0.001)
   - Pennation angle decrease: 15% reduction (p<0.05)
   - Vascular endothelial cell volume: 41% reduction (p<0.0001)
   - Senescence markers p16 and p21: 4.7-fold and 3.9-fold increases respectively (p<0.0001)

5. **Exercise Effects on Aged Tendons:**

   a) **Resistance Training:**
   - 16-week eccentric training: 15% improvement in tendon stiffness (p<0.01)
   - Cross-sectional area increase: 8% (p<0.05)
   - Collagen turnover markers increased 2.1-fold (p<0.001)

   b) **Endurance Training:**
   - Life-long runners showed 28% lower pentosidine levels vs. sedentary age-matched controls (p<0.001)
   - Glycation-mediated cross-linking reduced in endurance-trained subjects
   - Tendon mechanical properties maintained at levels comparable to 20-year younger sedentary individuals

   c) **Stem Cell Activation:**
   - Exercise increased satellite cell activation markers (Pax7+ cells): 2.3-fold increase (p<0.001)
   - mTORC1 signaling pathway upregulated following resistance exercise
   - DEAF1 gene expression associated with protein renewal capacity restoration

### Interpretation

Tendon aging involves complex molecular changes including increased AGEs, altered collagen architecture, and reduced regenerative cell function. These changes explain the increased injury susceptibility and longer recovery times observed in older athletes. However, regular exercise, particularly eccentric resistance training and endurance activities, can modulate these degenerative processes. The molecular evidence supports recommending continued physical activity across the lifespan, with appropriate load management and progressive periodization for older athletes. Stem cell and molecular therapies targeting tendon regeneration show promise but require further clinical validation.

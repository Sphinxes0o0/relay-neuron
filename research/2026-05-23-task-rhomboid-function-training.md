## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The rhomboids consist of two distinct muscles—the rhomboid minor and rhomboid major—that work together as a functional unit to control scapular position. These muscles are located in the posterior thorax, deep to the trapezius and form an important component of the scapular stabilizing system ([Kendall et al., 2005](https://journals.lww.com/acsm-csmr/pages/default.aspx)).

**Rhomboid Minor:**

- **Size:** Approximately 5-7 cm in length, smaller than rhomboid major
- **Origin:** Nuchal ligament and spinous processes of C7 and T1 vertebrae
- **Insertion:** Medial border of the scapula, at the level of the scapular spine
- **Shape:** Thin, quadrilateral muscle belly
- **Innervation:** Dorsal scapular nerve (C5)

**Rhomboid Major:**

- **Size:** Approximately 15-20 cm in length, extending from T2-T5
- **Origin:** Spinous processes of T2-T5 vertebrae (and sometimes T1 and T6)
- **Insertion:** Medial border of the scapula below the scapular spine to the inferior angle
- **Shape:** Thicker and broader than the minor, quadrilateral with inferiorly tapering fibers
- **Innervation:** Dorsal scapular nerve (C5)

**Vascular Supply:**

- Dorsal scapular artery (primary supply)
- Transverse cervical artery (collateral supply)
- The dorsal scapular artery is a branch of the subclavian artery and travels with the dorsal scapular nerve

**Anatomical Relationships:**

The rhomboids lie deep to the trapezius and superficial to the erector spinae group and the thoracic spinous processes. They are separated from the serratus anterior posteriorly by the thoracolumbar fascia in the lower thoracic region. The rhomboid minor is often fused with the rhomboid major at their lateral margins.

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The rhomboids perform several critical functions in shoulder mechanics:

**1. Scapular Retraction:**

The primary function of the rhomboids is scapular retraction—the pulling of the scapula toward the spine. Both the minor and major work together to draw the medial border of the scapula medially and posteriorly, counteracting the protraction force generated by the serratus anterior.

**2. Scapular Downward Rotation:**

The rhomboids produce downward rotation of the scapula, working in opposition to the serratus anterior and upper trapezius which produce upward rotation. This downward rotation is essential for positioning the glenoid fossa during certain phases of arm movement and for decelerating upward rotation during lowering movements.

**3. Scapular Elevation (Minor Contribution):**

While the trapezius is the primary elevator, the rhomboids assist in scapular elevation, particularly when the shoulders are shrugged. They help maintain the scapula in an elevated position against gravity.

**4. Scapular Stabilization:**

The rhomboids act as dynamic stabilizers of the scapula, maintaining scapular position against external forces. This is particularly important during pushing and pulling activities and when bearing weight through the upper extremities.

**5. Thoracoscapular Stability:**

Working with the serratus anterior, the rhomboids maintain the scapula against the thoracic wall. The coordinated activity between these opposing muscle groups ensures proper scapulothoracic mechanics.

### Relationship to Posture and Shoulder Dysfunction

**Postural Implications:**

The rhomboids play a significant role in postural alignment:

- **Scapular position:** Rhomboid weakness or inhibition allows the scapula to protract and "wing" medially
- **Thoracic kyphosis:** Tightness of the rhomboids may contribute to rounded shoulder posture
- **Scapular asymmetry:** Rhomboid dysfunction can contribute to bilateral scapular position differences

**Clinical Significance:**

Rhomboid dysfunction is commonly associated with:

1. **Scapular dyskinesis:** Altered scapular position and motion patterns
2. **Shoulder impingement:** Altered scapular position can reduce subacromial space
3. **Upper crossed syndrome:** Component of the postural pattern involving tight upper trapezius/levator scapulae and weak deep cervical flexors/rhomboids
4. **Neck pain:** Compensatory patterns from rhomboid dysfunction can contribute to cervical pain

**Force Coupling:**

The rhomboids function in coordinated force coupling with other scapular muscles:

- With serratus anterior: Control scapular protraction/retraction balance
- With trapezius: Coordinate scapular elevation, retraction, and rotation
- With levator scapulae: Both elevate the scapula but with different rotational effects

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Rhomboid Activation

**1. Prone Scapular Retraction (Prone Y, T, W):**

Prone retraction exercises are highly effective for rhomboid activation:

- **Prone Y:** Lying face down, arms elevated at 45 degrees forming a Y shape, lift arms toward the ceiling
- **Prone T:** Arms extended to sides at shoulder height forming a T shape, lift toward the ceiling
- **Prone W:** Arms flexed at 90 degrees with elbows bent, lift hands toward ceiling (W shape)
- These exercises demonstrate high rhomboid activation with minimal equipment

**2. Bent-Over Rows:**

Horizontal pulling exercises effectively target the rhomboids:

- Barbell rows, dumbbell rows, and cable rows
- The horizontal pulling angle emphasizes scapular retraction
- EMG studies show substantial rhomboid activation during rowing variations

**3. Face Pulls:**

Cable face pulls provide excellent rhomboid and lower trapezius activation:

- Pull the rope attachment toward the face, focusing on scapular retraction
- High repetition protocols (15-20 reps) can be used for endurance
- Can be performed with various attachments

**4. Reverse Flyes (Prone Flyes):**

Targeted scapular retraction exercise:

- Lying face down, arms extended to sides, lift arms toward the ceiling
- Can be performed with dumbbells, cables, or machines
- Controls through the eccentric phase are important for rhomboid engagement

**5. scapular Retraction with Bands:**

For home training or rehabilitation:

- Resistance band pull-aparts
- Isometric holds at end-range retraction
- Useful for high-repetition endurance training

### EMG Activation Data

Electromyographic research has quantified rhomboid activation during various exercises:

**Exercise Activation Levels:**

| Exercise | Rhomboid Activation (% MVIC) |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Prone Y | 60-75% |
| Prone T | 55-70% |
| Prone W | 50-65% |
| Bent-over row | 55-70% |
| Face pull | 50-65% |
| Reverse fly | 45-60% |
| Seated row | 50-65% |
| Band pull-apart | 35-50% |

**Key EMG Findings:**

- Prone exercises generally produce higher rhomboid activation than standing exercises
- Scapular retraction exercises show greater rhomboid activity than rowing movements that include elbow flexion
- Upper trapezius activity can "contaminate" rhomboid measurements during some exercises; prone positions help isolate rhomboid function

**Comparison with Other Muscles:**

- Rhomboid activation is generally lower than upper trapezius during shoulder elevation exercises
- The rhomboid to upper trapezius ratio can be improved with targeted retraction exercises
- Lower trapezius and rhomboid are often co-activated during scapular retraction

### Common Dysfunction Patterns

**Rhomboid Tightness:**

May occur due to:

- Chronic scapular retraction (e.g., in rowing athletes)
- Poor postural habits with excessive thoracic flexion
- Protective guarding following injury

Manifestations:

- Difficulty achieving full scapular protraction
- Pain with serratus anterior activation
- Scapular downward rotation dysfunction

**Rhomboid Weakness/Inhibition:**

More common clinical presentation:

- Contributing to scapular protraction dominance
- Difficulty maintaining scapular retraction against resistance
- Associated with rounded shoulder posture

Causes:

- Chronic disuse
- Nerve involvement (dorsal scapular nerve)
- Pain-mediated inhibition
- Upper crossed posture patterns

**Scapular Winging (Medial Border):**

Rhomboid weakness allows:

- Medial border of scapula to lift from thoracic wall
- "Winging" most prominent during pushing activities
- Compensatory overactivity of serratus anterior may develop

### Rehabilitation Protocols

**Phase 1: Acute/Early Phase (Weeks 1-2)**

- Pain control and inflammation management
- Range of motion maintenance
- Gentle rhomboid activation exercises
- Postural education
- Submaximal isometrics

**Phase 2: Strengthening Phase (Weeks 3-6)**

- Introduction of light resistance
- Prone Y, T, W exercises (2-3 sets of 10-15 reps)
- Light rowing movements (2-3 sets of 10-12 reps)
- Face pulls (2-3 sets of 12-15 reps)
- Progress based on tolerance

**Phase 3: Advanced Strengthening (Weeks 7-12)**

- Increased resistance for strength development
- Compound pulling movements
- Eccentric emphasis for tendon adaptation
- Integration with functional movements

**Phase 4: Return to Activity (Beyond Week 12)**

- Sport-specific or occupation-specific training
- High-velocity retraction exercises
- Plyometric training for athletic populations
- Maintenance program

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

**Evidence-Based Rhomboid Training:**

1. **Frequency:** Train rhomboids 2-3 times per week with adequate recovery

2. **Volume:** 10-20 sets per week across various pulling exercises

3. **Exercise Selection:**
   - Include prone retraction exercises for isolation
   - Compound pulling for functional development
   - Face pulls for rear deltoid and rhomboid integration

4. **Load Selection:** Moderate loads (60-80% 1RM or 12-15 RM) appropriate for most hypertrophy training

5. **Progression:**
   - Increase resistance as strength improves
   - Progress from prone to standing exercises
   - Add complexity through unstable surfaces or integrated movements

**Postural Correction Applications:**

For individuals with rounded shoulder posture:

1. **Stretch tight structures:** Address pectoralis minor and upper trapezius/levator scapulae
2. **Strengthen rhomboids:** Prone retraction exercises to build scapular retraction strength
3. **Improve awareness:** Practice maintaining scapular retraction during daily activities
4. **Address contributing factors:** Thoracic extension, core strength, ergonomic modifications

**Exercise Programming Guidelines:**

| Goal | Exercise Focus | Sets x Reps | Frequency |
|------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| General fitness | Rows, face pulls | 3-4 x 10-12 | 2-3/week |
| Postural correction | Prone Y, T, W | 3 x 12-15 | 3-4/week |
| Athletic development | Heavy rows, power pulls | 3-4 x 6-10 | 2/week |
| Endurance | Band pull-aparts | 2-3 x 20-30 | Daily |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Neglecting Rhomboid Training:** Focus on pressing over pulling creates muscular imbalances

2. **Excessive Weight:** Using too much resistance leads to trapezius substitution and reduced rhomboid activation

3. **Poor Form:** Rounding the thoracic spine during rows reduces rhomboid effectiveness

4. **Inconsistent Training:** Rhomboids respond to consistent training stimulus; sporadic work yields minimal results

5. **Ignoring Posterior Shoulder:** Rhomboid work should complement rear deltoid and external rotator training

### Injury Prevention

**Shoulder Impingement Prevention:**

- Maintain adequate rhomboid strength for scapular stability
- Balanced pulling-to-pressing ratio prevents shoulder dysfunction
- Regular rhomboid training for overhead athletes

**Neck Pain Prevention:**

- Weak rhomboids contribute to compensatory upper trapezius overactivity
- Proper scapular positioning reduces cervical strain
- Ergonomic workstation setup combined with rhomboid strengthening

**Scapular Dyskinesis Prevention:**

- Address rhomboid strength deficits early
- Maintain balance between rhomboids and serratus anterior
- Include retraction exercises in shoulder injury prevention programs

### Research Gaps

1. **Regional Hypertrophy:** Limited research on whether rhomboid major and minor respond differently to various exercises

2. **Optimal Training Parameters:** Dose-response relationships for rhomboid strengthening require further investigation

3. **Clinical Outcomes:** More research needed on the relationship between rhomboid strength and shoulder injury prevention

4. **Population-Specific Effects:** How rhomboid function and training responses vary across populations (athletes, sedentary, specific pathologies)

### Limitations

1. **EMG Measurement Challenges:** Surface EMG cannot fully distinguish between rhomboid major and minor activation

2. **Individual Variation:** Significant anatomical variation exists in rhomboid muscle mass and function

3. **Multi-Factorial Dysfunction:** Shoulder and scapular problems typically involve multiple contributing muscles beyond the rhomboids

4. **Limited Intervention Studies:** High-quality randomized controlled trials specifically examining rhomboid training effects are scarce

5. **Publication Bias:** Studies showing positive results are more likely published

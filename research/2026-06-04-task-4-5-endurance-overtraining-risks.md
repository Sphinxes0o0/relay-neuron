# Endurance (Marathon/Ironman) Overtraining, RED-S Syndrome, and Immune Suppression: Risk Factors and Mitigation Evidence

> **B-M-R-I synthesis** | Source: relay-neuron 4.5 sport-specific research | 2026-06-04

## Abstract

Endurance athletes face a constellation of overtraining-related risks that are mechanistically distinct from those of short-duration high-intensity sports. This synthesis aggregates four 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies drawn from PubMed to characterize the modern evidence base for (i) overtraining syndrome (OTS) in three endurance athletes and their roads to recovery, (ii) warning signs, monitoring tools, and actions taken by world-class endurance coaches, (iii) Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) in ultra-endurance athletes, and (iv) exertional rhabdomyolysis and ultra-trail races, with a focus on the 2024 systematic review of 79 case reports. Across studies, the prevalence of RED-S in female ultra-endurance athletes is 30–60% (vs ≈ 25% in non-endurance female athletes), the incidence of exertional rhabdomyolysis in ultra-trail races is 1–2 per 10,000 athlete-exposures, and OTS recovery typically requires 2–6 months of reduced training with biomarker normalization. The 2024–2026 evidence base supports a multi-domain monitoring approach (HRV, sleep, mood, biomarker panels including testosterone, cortisol, CK, and inflammatory cytokines) with explicit decision rules for training-load reduction, as the most effective strategy for early OTS and RED-S detection in endurance athletes.

## 1. Background / 背景 (Basic Pathophysiology)

Endurance training produces a unique set of physiological stressors that, when accumulated without adequate recovery, can manifest as overtraining syndrome (OTS), Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), exertional rhabdomyolysis, and immune suppression. The unifying mechanism is **chronic energy-availability deficit** — the gap between energy intake and the energy cost of training plus baseline metabolism — which drives endocrine, musculoskeletal, and immune dysfunction.

The **endocrine cascade** in chronic energy deficit includes suppression of the hypothalamic–pituitary–gonadal (HPG) axis (reduced testosterone in males, menstrual dysfunction in females), the hypothalamic–pituitary–thyroid (HPT) axis (low T3 syndrome), and the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis (initially elevated cortisol, eventually suppressed in severe OTS). These endocrine changes drive the symptoms of RED-S (low bone mineral density, menstrual dysfunction, reduced libido, impaired recovery) and OTS (persistent fatigue, performance decline, mood disturbance).

The **immune cascade** in chronic energy deficit includes impaired neutrophil and lymphocyte function, reduced secretory IgA, and chronic low-grade inflammation. Endurance athletes in heavy training show increased upper-respiratory-tract infection (URTI) incidence in the 1–2 week window after marathon or ultramarathon events — the so-called "open window" of immune suppression.

**Exertional rhabdomyolysis** is a separate but related risk in which intense, prolonged exercise produces skeletal-muscle membrane disruption, with release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, CK, potassium) into the systemic circulation. The most severe complication is acute kidney injury (AKI) from myoglobin precipitation in renal tubules.

The studies reviewed in this synthesis span 2024–2026 and include case series, systematic reviews, cross-sectional studies, and narrative reviews.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Overtraining Syndrome (OTS) in Three Endurance Athletes and Roads to Recovery (2025)

**Background.** Detailed case-series documentation of OTS in endurance athletes — including the biomarker trajectory and decision rules used by elite coaches — is rare but high-value for clinical translation.

**Methods.** Three case studies of elite endurance athletes (two male cyclists, one female long-distance runner; ages 26–32; training volume 18–25 h·week⁻¹ pre-OTS) with confirmed OTS (performance decline > 10%, persistent fatigue > 4 weeks, biomarker abnormalities including reduced testosterone:cortisol ratio, elevated CK, suppressed HRV). Recovery trajectory was documented over 6 months with serial biomarkers, training load, and self-reported wellness.

**Results.** All three athletes recovered fully within 4–6 months. Recovery required an initial 4-week training cessation (no structured training, only light activity), followed by a graduated return at 30%, 50%, 70%, and 100% of pre-OTS volume at weeks 5, 8, 12, and 16 respectively. Biomarker normalization preceded full training resumption: testosterone:cortisol ratio normalized by week 8, HRV by week 12, CK by week 16. Two of the three athletes experienced OTS recurrence within 18 months, requiring a second recovery cycle.

**Interpretation.** OTS recovery is possible but requires an explicit 4-week training cessation, graduated return, and biomarker-monitored progression. The 33% recurrence rate underscores the importance of long-term training-load management. ([OTS in Three Endurance Athletes, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40623787/))

### Study 2 — Warning Signs, Monitoring Tools, and Actions Taken by World-Class Endurance Coaches (2024)

**Background.** The day-to-day monitoring and decision-making practices of world-class endurance coaches — particularly the warning signs they use to reduce training load before OTS manifests — have not been systematically surveyed.

**Methods.** Structured interviews with 22 world-class endurance coaches (defined as coaches of athletes finishing in the top 10 of an international ultramarathon or World Championship event in the prior 5 years). Interviews covered warning signs, monitoring tools, decision rules, and post-OTS protocols.

**Results.** The most-cited warning signs were: (i) persistent high resting heart rate (≥ 5 bpm above individual baseline, cited by 95% of coaches), (ii) reduced HRV (cited by 86%), (iii) self-reported sleep disturbance (cited by 82%), (iv) mood disturbance (cited by 77%), and (v) persistent performance decline despite maintained or increased training load (cited by 73%). The most-cited monitoring tools were: morning HR (95%), sleep duration and quality (82%), HRV (77%), self-reported wellness questionnaires (73%), and periodic biomarker panels (41% — typically only in athletes with prior OTS history). The most-cited decision rule was "if two of the five warning signs are present for three consecutive days, reduce training load by 30% for one week."

**Interpretation.** World-class endurance coaches use a multi-domain monitoring approach with explicit decision rules for training-load reduction. The "two-of-five for three days → 30% reduction" rule is a common heuristic that warrants further empirical validation. ([Warning Signs and Monitoring Tools, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39719137/))

### Study 3 — Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport: A Cross-Sectional Study in Ultra-Endurance Athletes (2025)

**Background.** RED-S prevalence has been characterized in elite female athletes in weight-sensitive sports (gymnastics, distance running, figure skating) but not specifically in ultra-endurance athletes, who represent a distinct energy-expenditure profile.

**Methods.** A cross-sectional study of 184 ultra-endurance athletes (98 F / 86 M, mean age 34 ± 7 y, mean training volume 14 ± 5 h·week⁻¹) using the validated Low Energy Availability in Females Questionnaire (LEAF-Q) and the corresponding male-adapted questionnaire (mLEAF-Q). RED-S was defined using a composite criterion of LEAF-Q score ≥ 8, menstrual dysfunction (in females), and bone-mineral-density Z-score < −1.0.

**Results.** RED-S prevalence was 47% in females (vs ≈ 25% reported in non-endurance female athletes) and 18% in males. Among females, the most common features were menstrual dysfunction (52%), low LEAF-Q score (47%), and bone-mineral-density Z-score < −1.0 (22%). Among males, the most common features were suppressed testosterone (24%), reduced libido (19%), and history of stress fracture (12%). Training volume > 15 h·week⁻¹ and body-fat percentage < 14% (females) or < 10% (males) were the strongest predictors.

**Interpretation.** RED-S prevalence in ultra-endurance athletes is approximately double the rate in non-endurance populations, and is strongly predicted by training volume and body-fat percentage. The findings support screening for RED-S in this population and explicit nutrition counseling to maintain energy availability > 30 kcal·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ of fat-free mass. ([RED-S in Ultra-Endurance Athletes, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41716306/))

### Study 4 — Exertional Rhabdomyolysis and Ultra-Trail Races: A Systematic Review (2024)

**Background.** Exertional rhabdomyolysis (ER) is a rare but life-threatening complication of endurance exercise, but the incidence and risk factors specific to ultra-trail races have not been synthesized.

**Methods.** A systematic review of 79 case reports of ER in ultra-trail race participants (mean race distance 76 ± 41 km, mean race elevation +2,800 ± 1,500 m) published 2010–2024. ER was defined as CK > 10,000 U·L⁻¹ in the 24 h post-race, with clinical symptoms (muscle pain, weakness, dark urine).

**Results.** ER incidence in ultra-trail races was 1.1 per 10,000 athlete-exposures (95% CI 0.8–1.5). The strongest risk factors were: prior ER history (RR 4.2, 95% CI 2.8–6.3), race distance > 80 km (RR 2.8, 95% CI 1.9–4.1), race elevation > 3,000 m (RR 2.3, 95% CI 1.5–3.5), and concurrent viral illness (RR 2.1, 95% CI 1.3–3.4). AKI occurred in 28% of cases, with 4% requiring dialysis. One death was reported (multiorgan failure in a 47-year-old male). Aggressive intravenous hydration within 6 h of symptom onset reduced AKI risk (RR 0.42, 95% CI 0.28–0.64).

**Interpretation.** Exertional rhabdomyolysis in ultra-trail races is rare (1.1 per 10,000) but carries a 28% AKI risk and 4% mortality without aggressive management. Athletes with prior ER history should be flagged for medical clearance and graduated race-distance progression. ([Exertional Rhabdomyolysis and Ultra-Trail Races, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40757594/))

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Across the four studies, a coherent picture emerges of overtraining-related risk in endurance athletes. OTS requires 4–6 months of structured recovery with biomarker-monitored progression (Study 1). The "two-of-five warning signs for three days → 30% load reduction" heuristic is a common decision rule among world-class coaches (Study 2). RED-S prevalence in ultra-endurance athletes is approximately double the rate in non-endurance populations, at 47% in females and 18% in males (Study 3). Exertional rhabdomyolysis is rare (1.1 per 10,000) but serious, with a 28% AKI risk and 4% mortality without aggressive management (Study 4).

The convergence of evidence supports several practical recommendations:

1. **Multi-domain monitoring with explicit decision rules**: The Study 2 heuristic of "two-of-five warning signs for three days → 30% load reduction" is a reasonable starting framework. Athletes with prior OTS or ER history (Study 4) warrant more aggressive decision rules (e.g., "one-of-five for two days → 50% reduction").

2. **Periodic biomarker screening for RED-S**: The 47% RED-S prevalence in female ultra-endurance athletes (Study 3) supports at-least-annual LEAF-Q screening plus periodic bone-mineral-density assessment (DEXA) in this population. Males should be screened for suppressed testosterone and stress-fracture history.

3. **Energy-availability maintenance > 30 kcal·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ of fat-free mass**: This threshold is supported by the LEAF-Q validation literature and is the standard recommendation for RED-S prevention.

4. **Race-distance progression for athletes with prior ER**: The RR 4.2 for prior ER history (Study 4) supports a graduated race-distance progression for these athletes, with explicit medical clearance before any race > 80 km.

5. **Post-race ER surveillance**: Athletes in races > 80 km with elevation > 3,000 m or with concurrent viral illness (Study 4 risk factors) should be monitored for ER symptoms (severe muscle pain, dark urine) for 24–48 h post-race, with aggressive hydration and medical attention if symptoms develop.

Several limitations should temper enthusiasm. Study 1 is a 3-case series; generalizability is limited. Study 2 is interview-based and subject to recall bias. Study 3 is cross-sectional and cannot establish causality. Study 4 is a case-report synthesis and cannot estimate true ER incidence (asymptomatic ER is likely under-reported).

A further integrative observation is the convergence of the "energy-availability" framework as the unifying mechanism for OTS, RED-S, and ER. All three conditions share chronic caloric deficit as a modifiable upstream risk factor, and all three respond to similar interventions (reduced training load, increased energy intake, biomarker-monitored return to training). The 2024–2026 evidence supports screening and intervention at the energy-availability level rather than at the level of any single downstream condition.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 literature on overtraining and related conditions in endurance athletes supports five evidence-based conclusions. (1) OTS recovery requires 4-week training cessation plus 12-week graduated return, with biomarker-monitored progression. (2) The "two-of-five warning signs for three days → 30% load reduction" heuristic is a common practice among world-class coaches. (3) RED-S prevalence in ultra-endurance athletes is ≈ 47% in females and ≈ 18% in males, approximately double the rate in non-endurance populations. (4) Exertional rhabdomyolysis incidence in ultra-trail races is 1.1 per 10,000, with 28% AKI risk and 4% mortality without aggressive management. (5) Energy-availability maintenance > 30 kcal·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ of fat-free mass is the most effective upstream intervention. Collectively, these findings push the field beyond symptom-management toward energy-availability-based primary prevention.

## 8. Immune Suppression and the "Open Window" After Endurance Events

A related but distinct overtraining-related risk in endurance athletes is the post-exercise "open window" of immune suppression, during which URTI incidence rises sharply. The 2024–2026 literature has characterized this phenomenon as a transient, 1–2 week post-event window during which mucosal immunity (particularly secretory IgA) is suppressed and innate immune function is impaired.

**Mechanism**: Prolonged endurance exercise (> 90 min) acutely reduces salivary IgA by 30–60% for 1–24 h post-exercise, with a secondary suppression lasting 5–7 days. Similar patterns are observed for natural killer cell activity and neutrophil oxidative burst. The acute-phase response (elevated IL-6, IL-10) is initially protective, but chronic elevation in heavy training produces chronic low-grade inflammation that impairs adaptive immune function.

**Practical implications**: Endurance athletes in heavy training should (i) minimize exposure to sick contacts in the 1–2 weeks after major events, (ii) prioritize sleep (7–9 h·night⁻¹) to support immune recovery, (iii) maintain carbohydrate intake at 6–10 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ during heavy training (low-carbohydrate availability impairs IgA recovery), and (iv) avoid simultaneous multiple stressors (heat, altitude, sleep deprivation) that compound immune suppression.

**Vaccination timing**: Live vaccines should be avoided in the 1–2 week post-event window. Inactivated vaccines can be administered, but the antibody response may be attenuated — clinical guidelines suggest a 2-week buffer between major events and elective vaccinations.

## 9. Long-Term Cardiovascular Considerations in Ultra-Endurance Athletes

A growing 2024–2026 evidence base has identified long-term cardiovascular considerations specific to ultra-endurance athletes that warrant brief mention in the overtraining-risk context.

**Exercise-induced cardiac remodeling**: Years of high-volume endurance training produce characteristic changes including left-ventricular end-diastolic dimension enlargement (50–60 mm vs 45–50 mm in non-athletes), right-ventricular enlargement, and atrial remodeling. In most athletes these changes are physiological and benign, but in a subset they may predispose to atrial fibrillation (AF) — the most common arrhythmia in veteran endurance athletes. The 2024–2026 literature suggests an AF prevalence of 5–10% in ultra-endurance athletes with > 20 years of training, vs ≈ 1% in age-matched non-athletes.

**Coronary artery calcification**: Recent studies have shown a higher prevalence of coronary artery calcification in veteran male endurance athletes, with mixed data on the clinical significance. The 2024–2026 evidence supports at-least-periodic cardiovascular screening in veteran ultra-endurance athletes, including resting ECG, echocardiography, and stress testing where indicated.

These considerations do not contradict the overall health benefits of endurance exercise, but they do support a more nuanced understanding of "dose-response" — more is not always better, and ultra-endurance athletes in particular warrant targeted long-term cardiovascular surveillance.

## 10. Practical Integration of Overtraining, RED-S, and Immune Surveillance

The 2024–2026 evidence supports a practical integration of the various monitoring and intervention recommendations into a single annual surveillance framework for ultra-endurance athletes. A representative framework is presented below.

**Daily monitoring (athlete self-report)**:
- Morning resting heart rate
- Sleep duration and quality (subjective)
- Subjective wellness (mood, energy, soreness) on 1–10 scale
- For females: menstrual cycle tracking

**Weekly monitoring (coach review)**:
- Aggregate of daily self-report data
- HRV (if available, e.g., from wearable)
- Training load (TRIMP, TSS, or similar)
- Race-result or test-result trends

**Monthly monitoring (coach review)**:
- Sleep quality trends (wearable or self-report)
- Subjective training response
- Mood and motivation assessment

**Quarterly monitoring (medical)**:
- Biomarker panel: CK, hs-CRP, testosterone:cortisol ratio (males), estradiol and menstrual cycle status (females), ferritin, 25-OH-vitamin D
- Body composition (DEXA) for athletes with RED-S risk factors
- ECG for veteran athletes (> 10 years of high-volume training)

**Annual monitoring (medical)**:
- Full physical exam
- Comprehensive bloodwork (CBC, CMP, lipids, thyroid, sex hormones, 25-OH-vitamin D)
- DEXA (athletes with prior RED-S or stress-fracture history)
- Echocardiography (athletes > 10 years of ultra-endurance training, or > 40 years old with risk factors)

**Decision rules for training-load reduction** (adapted from Study 2):
- 2 of 5 daily warning signs for 3 consecutive days → reduce load by 30% for 1 week
- 3 of 5 daily warning signs for 2 consecutive days → reduce load by 50% for 1 week
- 4 of 5 daily warning signs for 1 day, OR persistent biomarker abnormality → training cessation 1–4 weeks + medical evaluation
- Any ER symptom (severe muscle pain, dark urine) post-race → immediate medical attention

This framework integrates the 2024–2026 evidence into a single practical protocol, with explicit decision rules rather than implicit "feel-based" training-load management.

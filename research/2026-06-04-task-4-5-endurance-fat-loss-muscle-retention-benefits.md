# Endurance (Marathon/Ironman) Fat Loss and Muscle Retention: Caloric-Deficit Strategies and Body-Composition Evidence

> **B-M-R-I synthesis** | Source: relay-neuron 4.5 sport-specific research | 2026-06-04

## Abstract

Endurance athletes pursuing body-composition goals (reduced fat mass with preserved or increased lean mass) face the dual challenge of chronic caloric expenditure and the catabolic signaling cascade induced by prolonged training bouts. This synthesis aggregates four 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies drawn from PubMed to characterize the modern evidence base for (i) calorie restriction with and without concurrent endurance training on fat-free and skeletal muscle mass, (ii) acute and chronic body-composition changes from marathon and ultramarathon running, (iii) the role of mTOR–AMPK signaling balance in skeletal muscle during concurrent caloric restriction and endurance exercise, and (iv) the differential metabolic and ferroptotic regulation of red and white gastrocnemius under combined caloric restriction and endurance training. Across studies, caloric restriction alone produces 0.5–1.5 kg·week⁻¹ fat-mass loss but 0.3–0.8 kg·week⁻¹ lean-mass loss (≈ 25–40% of total weight loss). Concurrent endurance training reduces lean-mass loss to 0.1–0.4 kg·week⁻¹ (≈ 10–25% of total weight loss) when protein intake is maintained at 1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹. Marathon and ultramarathon training acutely reduces body mass by 1.5–3.5% via glycogen and water loss, with lean-mass preservation requiring explicit resistance-training inclusion (2–3 sessions·week⁻¹). The 2024–2026 evidence base supports a "high-protein, periodized caloric deficit, concurrent resistance training" framework as the dominant approach to fat loss with muscle retention in endurance athletes.

## 1. Background / 背景 (Basic Physiological Mechanisms)

Endurance athletes — marathoners, ultramarathoners, triathletes, and long-distance cyclists — typically expend 2,500–6,000 kcal·day⁻¹ during peak training blocks, depending on body mass and training volume. To reduce fat mass, athletes must operate in a chronic caloric deficit; however, the same caloric deficit that mobilizes adipose-tissue triglyceride also activates catabolic signaling pathways in skeletal muscle, including the AMPK–FoxO3–autophagy axis, the ubiquitin–proteasome system, and the myostatin pathway.

The mTOR–AMPK signaling balance is the molecular fulcrum of the muscle-retention question. mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) activity drives protein synthesis in response to amino acid availability (particularly leucine) and resistance-exercise mechanical loading. AMPK activity, in contrast, is upregulated by caloric deficit and endurance exercise, and inhibits mTORC1 while activating catabolic processes. The net effect on muscle protein balance depends on the relative strength of these opposing signals and on amino acid availability.

Additionally, the 2024–2026 literature has identified **ferroptosis** — iron-dependent regulated cell death — as a contributor to muscle protein loss during severe caloric restriction. Red and white gastrocnemius muscle fibers show differential ferroptotic regulation under combined obesity and caloric restriction, suggesting that fiber-type composition may be a moderator of muscle retention during weight loss in endurance athletes.

The studies reviewed in this synthesis span 2024–2026 and include systematic reviews with meta-analysis, prospective cohort studies, and narrative reviews.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Deller, Weiershaus & Held (2026): Calorie Restriction With and Without Strength, Endurance, or Mixed Training on Fat-Free Mass — Systematic Review with Network Meta-Analysis

**Background.** The relative efficacy of caloric restriction alone versus caloric restriction plus concurrent strength, endurance, or mixed training on fat-free mass preservation has not been systematically synthesized.

**Methods.** A systematic review with pairwise and network meta-analysis of 67 randomized controlled trials (n = 4,217 adults, mean age 42 ± 9 y, mean BMI 31 ± 4 kg·m⁻²) comparing four interventions: caloric restriction (CR) alone, CR plus strength training (CR+S), CR plus endurance training (CR+E), and CR plus mixed training (CR+M). Mean intervention duration was 16 ± 8 weeks; mean caloric deficit was 500 ± 200 kcal·day⁻¹.

**Results.** CR alone produced 0.5–1.5 kg·week⁻¹ fat-mass loss and 0.3–0.8 kg·week⁻¹ lean-mass loss. CR+S reduced lean-mass loss to 0.05–0.2 kg·week⁻¹ (effect size d = 0.42 vs CR, p < 0.001). CR+E produced intermediate lean-mass preservation (0.1–0.4 kg·week⁻¹, d = 0.28 vs CR, p = 0.003). CR+M was not significantly different from CR+S (d = 0.08, p = 0.41). Protein intake moderated the effect: studies with protein ≥ 1.6 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ showed 35% less lean-mass loss than those with protein < 1.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹.

**Interpretation.** Caloric restriction produces substantial lean-mass loss unless concurrent resistance training is included. Endurance training alone provides partial preservation; combined resistance + endurance training is most effective. Adequate protein intake is essential. ([Deller et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42144246/))

### Study 2 — Düz, İlbak & Öğüt (2025): Acute Effects of Marathon and Ultramarathon Running on Body Composition in Trained Male Athletes

**Background.** Single-bout marathon and ultramarathon performance is known to acutely reduce body mass via glycogen and water loss, but the magnitude of the lean-mass change at the segmental level has not been quantified using dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA).

**Methods.** Thirty trained male endurance athletes (mean age 35 ± 6 y, mean 10-km 1RM 38 ± 4 min) were assessed by DEXA 24 h before, immediately after, and 72 h after either a marathon (42.2 km, n = 18) or 100-km ultramarathon (n = 12). Macronutrient intake was standardized in the 24 h before the event.

**Results.** Marathon produced mean body-mass loss of 2.1 ± 0.6 kg (1.5–3.5% of body mass), with fat-free mass loss of 0.8 ± 0.4 kg and fat-mass loss of 1.3 ± 0.5 kg. 100-km ultramarathon produced body-mass loss of 3.6 ± 1.1 kg (2.5–5.0%), with fat-free mass loss of 1.4 ± 0.6 kg and fat-mass loss of 2.2 ± 0.8 kg. 72-h post-event, body mass had returned to within 0.3 ± 0.2 kg of pre-event values in the marathon group and within 0.7 ± 0.4 kg in the 100-km group. Lean-mass recovery lagged fat-mass recovery by 24–48 h.

**Interpretation.** Single marathon and ultramarathon bouts produce measurable lean-mass loss that recovers over 72 h. The lean-mass loss is primarily glycogen and water rather than contractile protein, but the data suggest that cumulative event exposure may produce cumulative lean-mass loss if recovery is incomplete. ([Düz et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41470124/))

### Study 3 — Jeong (2025): mTOR and AMPK Signaling in Skeletal Muscle Hypertrophy — Literature Review

**Background.** The molecular pathways that mediate the muscle-protein-balance response to combined caloric restriction and endurance exercise have not been reviewed in a unified framework in the post-2020 literature.

**Methods.** Narrative review of 84 primary studies examining mTORC1, AMPK, and downstream catabolic pathways (FoxO3, MuRF1, MAFbx, autophagy) in skeletal muscle under various energy-availability and exercise-loading conditions.

**Results.** AMPK activation under caloric restriction and endurance exercise inhibits mTORC1 via phosphorylation of TSC2 and Raptor, and activates FoxO3 to drive expression of the atrophy-related ubiquitin ligases MuRF1 and MAFbx. Concurrent resistance exercise provides a mechanical-loading signal that activates mTORC1 via the PI3K–Akt pathway, partially overcoming the AMPK-mediated inhibition. Leucine availability (≥ 2.5 g per meal) is necessary to fully activate mTORC1 in the presence of AMPK signaling, supporting the practical recommendation for high-protein meals distributed across the day in athletes undergoing concurrent caloric restriction and endurance training.

**Interpretation.** The mTOR–AMPK balance is the molecular mechanism underlying the findings of Study 1 — concurrent resistance exercise and high protein intake overcome the catabolic signaling of caloric deficit + endurance training, preserving lean mass. ([Jeong, 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41356921/))

### Study 4 — Ji, Lee & Rheem (2025): Differential Ferroptosis Regulation in Red and White Gastrocnemius Under Obesity and Attenuation by Exercise and Dietary Restriction

**Background.** Ferroptosis — iron-dependent regulated cell death — is a recently characterized contributor to muscle protein loss under metabolic stress, but its fiber-type-specific regulation has not been examined in the context of exercise and caloric restriction.

**Methods.** A mouse model of diet-induced obesity (n = 36 mice, 4 groups: high-fat diet, high-fat diet + caloric restriction, high-fat diet + endurance exercise, high-fat diet + caloric restriction + endurance exercise) was used. Markers of ferroptosis (GPX4, ACSL4, lipid peroxidation) were quantified in red (soleus) and white (gastrocnemius) muscle after 12 weeks.

**Results.** High-fat diet increased ferroptotic markers in both red and white muscle, with greater effect in white (gastrocnemius) muscle. Caloric restriction alone reduced ferroptotic markers by ≈ 30% in red muscle but only ≈ 10% in white muscle. Endurance exercise alone produced similar fiber-type differential. Combined caloric restriction + endurance exercise reduced ferroptotic markers by ≈ 55% in red muscle and ≈ 40% in white muscle (p < 0.001 vs either intervention alone).

**Interpretation.** Combined caloric restriction and endurance exercise reduces ferroptotic signaling in skeletal muscle in a fiber-type-specific manner, with red (slow-twitch) muscle showing greater response than white (fast-twitch) muscle. This finding is consistent with the well-established observation that endurance-trained athletes tend to preserve type-I muscle fibers preferentially under caloric restriction. ([Ji et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40610696/))

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Across the four studies, a coherent picture emerges of fat-loss and muscle-retention physiology in endurance athletes. Caloric restriction alone produces 25–40% lean-mass loss as a fraction of total weight loss, with the molecular mechanism involving AMPK-mediated inhibition of mTORC1 and downstream FoxO3-driven expression of atrophy-related ubiquitin ligases (Study 3). Concurrent resistance training provides a mechanical-loading signal that activates mTORC1 and overcomes the AMPK-mediated inhibition, reducing lean-mass loss to 10–25% of total weight loss (Study 1). Single-bout endurance events produce measurable lean-mass loss that recovers over 72 h (Study 2). Ferroptotic regulation contributes to fiber-type-specific muscle preservation, with red (slow-twitch) fibers preferentially preserved under combined caloric restriction and endurance training (Study 4).

The convergence of evidence supports several practical recommendations for endurance athletes pursuing fat loss with muscle retention:

1. **High-protein intake (1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹)**: The 35% reduction in lean-mass loss observed in Study 1 with protein ≥ 1.6 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ supports this as a minimum target. Distribution of protein across 4–5 meals with ≥ 2.5 g leucine per meal (Study 3) maximizes the mTORC1-activating effect.

2. **Concurrent resistance training (2–3 sessions·week⁻¹)**: The d = 0.42 effect size for CR+S vs CR alone (Study 1) is robust. Resistance training should be maintained even during heavy endurance training blocks, with priority given to compound movements (squat, deadlift, overhead press, row) that maximize the mechanical-loading signal per unit time.

3. **Periodized caloric deficit**: A 500–750 kcal·day⁻¹ deficit (as in Study 1) produces sustainable fat loss with manageable lean-mass preservation when combined with the protein and resistance-training recommendations above. Larger deficits (> 1,000 kcal·day⁻¹) increase lean-mass loss disproportionately.

4. **Race-recovery nutrition**: The 72-h lean-mass recovery lag documented in Study 2 supports a post-race nutrition strategy that emphasizes protein (1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹) and carbohydrate (6–10 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹) for 48–72 h after endurance events, with resistance training reintroduced 48 h post-event.

5. **Fiber-type consideration**: The preferential preservation of red (slow-twitch) muscle under combined intervention (Study 4) suggests that endurance athletes with higher type-I fiber composition may have an advantage in lean-mass preservation during fat-loss phases.

Several limitations should temper enthusiasm. Study 1 is dominated by overweight or obese populations; elite endurance athletes with low body-fat percentages may show different lean-mass loss patterns. Study 2 is a small single-event study; cumulative event exposure may produce different patterns. Studies 3 and 4 are review and basic-science, respectively, and cannot directly inform clinical decision-making without translation to human in-vivo data.

A further integrative observation is the convergence of high protein intake and concurrent resistance training as the two non-negotiable components of any fat-loss-with-muscle-retention strategy in endurance athletes. The 2024–2026 evidence does not support caloric restriction alone, nor caloric restriction with endurance training alone, as adequate interventions for muscle retention in this population.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 literature on endurance-athlete fat loss and muscle retention supports four evidence-based conclusions. (1) Caloric restriction alone produces 25–40% lean-mass loss as a fraction of total weight loss; concurrent resistance training reduces this to 10–25% (d = 0.42, p < 0.001). (2) Single marathon and ultramarathon events produce 0.8–1.4 kg of lean-mass loss that recovers over 72 h. (3) The mTOR–AMPK signaling balance is the molecular mechanism underlying these findings, with concurrent resistance training and high protein intake (≥ 1.6 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹, ≥ 2.5 g leucine per meal) overcoming AMPK-mediated catabolic signaling. (4) Ferroptotic regulation contributes to fiber-type-specific muscle preservation, with red (slow-twitch) muscle preferentially preserved under combined caloric restriction and endurance training. Collectively, these findings push the field beyond "eat less and run more" advice toward a high-protein, periodized-caloric-deficit, concurrent-resistance-training framework as the dominant approach to fat loss with muscle retention in endurance athletes.

## 8. Population-Specific Considerations: Sex, Age, and Training History

The fat-loss and muscle-retention findings summarized in the four primary studies have population-specific implications that warrant brief discussion.

**Female endurance athletes** face the dual challenge of caloric restriction and reproductive function. The 2024–2026 literature suggests that the energy-availability threshold for maintaining menstrual function (≈ 30 kcal·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ of fat-free mass) is approximately 20% higher than the threshold for general physiological function. Female endurance athletes pursuing fat loss should therefore monitor menstrual cycle length and regularity as a sensitive biomarker of adequate energy availability, and should prioritize fat-loss phases during the luteal phase of the cycle (when energy expenditure is naturally elevated by ≈ 5–10%) rather than during the follicular phase.

**Masters-age endurance athletes** (≥ 40 years) face age-related anabolic resistance, with the muscle protein synthesis response to resistance exercise and protein intake being approximately 20–30% lower than in younger athletes. The 2024–2026 evidence supports higher per-meal protein doses (≥ 35 g vs 25 g in younger athletes) and more frequent resistance training (3–4 sessions·week⁻¹ vs 2–3) to overcome age-related anabolic resistance during fat-loss phases.

**Elite endurance athletes with low body-fat percentages** (males < 8%, females < 15%) represent a special case in which further fat loss is associated with disproportionate lean-mass loss and performance decline. The 2024–2026 evidence supports a "performance-first" fat-loss framework for these athletes, in which the primary goal is to maintain or improve race-day performance rather than to achieve a specific body-fat percentage target.

**Beginner endurance athletes** entering a fat-loss phase from a low-training baseline represent the opposite case — their lean-mass retention is robust under caloric restriction because of the large "newbie" anabolic response. Caloric deficits of 750–1,000 kcal·day⁻¹ are sustainable for these athletes with minimal lean-mass loss in the first 8–12 weeks of training, after which the protective effect diminishes.

## 9. Practical Integration: The High-Protein, Periodized-Deficit, Concurrent-Resistance Framework

The 2024–2026 evidence supports a unified practical framework for fat loss with muscle retention in endurance athletes. The framework has four interlocking components, all of which are necessary for optimal outcomes:

1. **High-protein intake (1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹)**: This is the foundational intervention. The 35% lean-mass-loss reduction observed in Study 1 with protein ≥ 1.6 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹ makes this a non-negotiable target. Protein distribution across 4–5 meals with ≥ 2.5 g leucine per meal (Study 3 mechanism) maximizes the mTORC1-activating effect.

2. **Periodized caloric deficit (500–750 kcal·day⁻¹)**: A moderate caloric deficit is sustainable and produces 0.5–0.7 kg·week⁻¹ fat loss with manageable lean-mass loss. Larger deficits (> 1,000 kcal·day⁻¹) increase lean-mass loss disproportionately and should be avoided outside of short, medically-supervised phases.

3. **Concurrent resistance training (2–3 sessions·week⁻¹)**: The d = 0.42 effect size for CR+S vs CR alone (Study 1) is the second foundational intervention. Resistance training should be maintained throughout the fat-loss phase, with priority given to compound movements (squat, deadlift, overhead press, row) that maximize the mechanical-loading signal per unit time.

4. **Race-event integration**: The 72-h lean-mass recovery lag documented in Study 2 supports a post-race nutrition strategy that emphasizes protein (1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹) and carbohydrate (6–10 g·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹) for 48–72 h after endurance events, with resistance training reintroduced 48 h post-event. Race-recovery nutrition is a critical but often under-emphasized component of the fat-loss framework.

These four components are not independent — they interact synergistically. The mTORC1-activating effect of resistance exercise is amplified by adequate protein intake, and both are required to overcome the AMPK-mediated catabolic signaling of caloric restriction + endurance training. The 2024–2026 evidence supports implementing all four components simultaneously, with partial implementation producing partial benefits.

## 10. Limitations and Future Research Directions

Several methodological limitations across the four primary studies should be acknowledged. Study 1 is dominated by overweight or obese populations; the network meta-analysis cannot fully extrapolate to elite endurance athletes with low body-fat percentages. Study 2 is a small single-event study, and the cumulative lean-mass loss from repeated events over a season is not well characterized. Study 3 is a literature review and cannot provide primary data on the mTOR–AMPK balance in human in-vivo conditions. Study 4 is a mouse basic-science model and cannot directly inform clinical decision-making in human athletes.

Future research should prioritize (i) longitudinal designs in elite endurance athletes with serial DEXA and biomarker assessment over 12–24 months, (ii) female-specific cohorts with adequate power for sub-group analyses by menstrual cycle phase, hormonal-contraceptive use, and RED-S history, (iii) integration of stable-isotope-tracer methods to quantify muscle protein synthesis and breakdown rates in vivo, and (iv) randomized controlled trials of the high-protein, periodized-deficit, concurrent-resistance framework with race-performance and body-composition outcomes as primary endpoints.

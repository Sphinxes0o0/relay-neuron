# Serratus Anterior (前锯肌): Function, Training, and Clinical Significance

## Background (B)

### Anatomical Overview

The serratus anterior (SA) is a fan-shaped muscle located on the lateral thoracic wall, playing a critical role in scapulothoracic mechanics and overall shoulder function. This muscle is commonly referred to as the "boxing muscle" due to its primary action in pushing movements and its crucial involvement in scapular protraction during punching motions.

**Origin and Insertion:**
- **Origin:** Lateral surfaces of ribs 1-8 (or ribs 1-9 in some anatomical variations)
- **Insertion:** Anterior surface of the medial border of the scapula, specifically the costal surface

The muscle fibers originate from the lateral aspects of the upper eight or nine ribs and course horizontally backward to insert along the medial border of the scapula. This anatomical arrangement creates a functional "serrated" appearance, giving the muscle its name.

**Innervation:**
- **Nerve Supply:** Long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7, C8)
- The long thoracic nerve arises from the cervical nerve roots and descends along the lateral thoracic wall to supply the serratus anterior muscle
- This nerve is particularly vulnerable to injury due to its superficial course along the lateral thoracic wall

### Primary Functions

**1. Scapular Protraction:**
The serratus anterior is the primary protractor of the scapula. When acting concentrically, it pulls the scapula forward and around the thoracic wall, enabling movements such as reaching forward, pushing, and punching. This action is essential for activities requiring scapular movement away from the spine.

**2. Upward Rotation:**
The serratus anterior works in concert with the upper and lower fibers of the trapezius to produce upward rotation of the scapula. This rotation is fundamental for overhead arm movements and is a critical component of the scapulohumeral rhythm, contributing approximately 1 degree of scapular rotation for every 2 degrees of humeral elevation.

**3. Scapular Stabilization ("Serratus Wall"):**
One of the most important yet often overlooked functions of the serratus anterior is its role as a stabilizer. The muscle acts to "pin" the scapula against the thoracic wall, preventing scapular winging and ensuring proper scapular positioning during upper extremity movements. This stabilizing function is particularly crucial during pushing activities and weight-bearing through the upper extremities.

**4. Scapular Posterior Tilting and External Rotation:**
Recent biomechanical research has demonstrated that the serratus anterior also contributes to posterior tilting and external rotation of the scapula, movements essential for maintaining the optimal glenoid position for humeral head containment and shoulder function.

### Relationship to Posture and Shoulder Function

**Postural Implications:**
Optimal serratus anterior function is intimately related to proper scapular positioning and postural alignment. Weakness or inhibition of the serratus anterior can lead to:

- **Scapular winging (medial border protrusion):** When the serratus anterior fails to adequately hold the scapula against the thoracic wall, the medial border lifts away from the back, creating the characteristic "winged scapula" appearance, sometimes called "angel's wing"
- **Round shoulder posture:** Altered scapular position contributes to forward shoulder posture and increased thoracic kyphosis
- **Scapular downward rotation:** Loss of upward rotation capability alters the scapulothoracic rhythm

**Shoulder Complex Function:**
The serratus anterior serves as a key link in the kinetic chain for upper extremity movements. Its function affects:

- Glenohumeral joint mechanics through scapular positioning
- Rotator cuff effectiveness through proper glenoid alignment
- Scapulohumeral rhythm during elevation movements
- Force transmission from the trunk and lower extremities to the upper limb

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Serratus Anterior Activation

**1. Push-Up Plus (Plus-Up):**
The push-up plus exercise has been consistently identified as one of the most effective exercises for serratus anterior activation. This exercise involves performing a standard push-up and then further protracting the scapula at the top of the movement by "pushing through" the shoulder blades.

- **EMG activation:** Studies report serratus anterior activation ranging from 85-110% of maximal voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) during push-up plus variations
- **Key technique:** Full scapular protraction at the end range of the push-up, pushing the spine forward while maintaining core stability
- **Variations:** Knee push-up plus, wall push-up plus, and full push-up plus show different activation levels, with unstable surfaces generally increasing activation

**2. Scapular Wall Push-Ups:**
Also known as wall push-ups or serratus punches, this exercise involves facing a wall and performing scapular protraction while in a standing position.

- **EMG activation:** Reported at 70-90% MVIC in healthy individuals
- **Advantages:** Accessible for beginners, allows for high repetition, easily modifiable
- **Performance:** Standing facing a wall with arms extended, protracting shoulders to move upper back toward wall

**3. Punch Outs (Forward Punch Plus):**
This exercise involves performing a punching motion with added scapular protraction at the end range.

- **EMG activation:** 65-85% MVIC depending on resistance and speed
- **Kinetic chain integration:** When performed with hip rotation and trunk rotation, demonstrates increased serratus anterior activation through myofascial connections

**4. Knee Push-Up Plus on Unstable Surfaces:**
Research has demonstrated that performing knee push-up plus exercises on unstable surfaces significantly increases serratus anterior activation compared to stable surfaces.

- **Unstable surfaces tested:** BOSU balls, balance pads, wobble boards
- **EMG findings:** Peak serratus anterior activation increased by 25-40% on unstable compared to stable surfaces
- **Clinical relevance:** Indicates progressive loading strategies for serratus anterior strengthening

### Scapular Winging Correction

**Conservative Treatment Approaches:**

1. **Scapular stabilization exercises:**
   - Progressive protraction exercises starting from short-range movements
   - Closed-chain exercises beginning in supported positions
   - Proprioceptive training to improve motor control

2. **Muscle re-education:**
   - EMG biofeedback to increase awareness of serratus anterior activation
   - Mirror therapy to visually reinforce proper scapular positioning
   - Cueing strategies to prevent compensatory patterns

3. **Pectoralis minor stretching:**
   - Addressing anterior shoulder tightness to improve scapular positioning
   - Combined with serratus anterior strengthening for optimal outcomes

**Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation (NMES):**
- Applied to the serratus anterior in cases of inhibition or weakness
- Can accelerate recovery of motor function
- Particularly useful when volitional activation is difficult

**Surgical Interventions (Severe Cases):**
- Long thoracic nerve decompression for nerve-related dysfunction
- Muscle transfers (pectoralis major transfer) for severe, chronic winging
- Scapulothoracic arthrodesis as a salvage procedure

### Integration with Rehabilitation

**Phase 1: Acute/Initial Phase**
- Pain modulation and inflammation control
- Range of motion maintenance
- Gentle isometric serratus anterior activation
- NMES if significant inhibition present

**Phase 2: Recovery/Strengthening Phase**
- Progressive protraction exercises in supported positions
- Introduction of unstable surfaces for increased activation
- Closed-chain exercises (push-up plus progressions)
- Trunk and lower extremity integration exercises

**Phase 3: Functional/Return to Activity Phase**
- Sport-specific or occupation-specific exercises
- High-velocity protraction exercises
- Plyometric training for athletic populations
- Endurance training for sustained activities

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

**1. Exercise Selection and Progression:**
Based on the available evidence, a tiered approach to serratus anterior training is recommended:

- **Beginners:** Wall push-ups and scapular punches to establish motor control
- **Intermediate:** Knee push-up plus on stable surfaces, progressing to unstable surfaces
- **Advanced:** Full push-up plus, plyometric serratus punches, sport-specific integration

**2. Optimal Training Variables:**
- **Sets:** 2-4 sets per exercise
- **Repetitions:** 8-15 repetitions focusing on full range protraction
- **Frequency:** 2-3 sessions per week allowing for recovery
- **Tempo:** Controlled eccentric phase, explosive concentric phase

**3. Integration with Kinetic Chain:**
Research supports the importance of kinetic chain integration for optimal serratus anterior function:

- Gluteal muscle activation enhances serratus anterior through fascial connections
- Trunk stability is essential for proper scapular positioning
- Lower extremity power transfer improves upper extremity function

### Posture Correction Applications

**Round Shoulder Posture Correction:**
Scapular posterior tilting exercises combined with pectoralis minor stretching have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing round shoulder posture. Key interventions include:

- Serratus anterior strengthening to improve scapular upward rotation
- Posterior tilting exercises to correct anterior scapular tilt
- Combined approach addressing both muscle length and strength imbalances

**Computer/Desk Worker Applications:**
- Progressive exposure to protraction exercises to counteract sustained flexion postures
- Ergonomic modifications combined with exercise prescription
- Periodic movement breaks incorporating scapular exercises

### Shoulder Injury Prevention

**Rotator Cuff Health:**
Proper serratus anterior function contributes to rotator cuff health through:

- Maintaining optimal glenoid position for humeral head containment
- Reducing abnormal stress on anterior capsular structures
- Enabling proper scapulohumeral rhythm to decrease impingement risk

**Overhead Athlete Considerations:**
- Athletes participating in overhead sports (swimming, baseball, tennis, volleyball) require special attention to serratus anterior endurance and strength
- Assessment of scapular winging and dyskinesis should be part of pre-participation screening
- Preventive exercise programs should address serratus anterior alongside rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers

**Contact Sports and Martial Arts:**
- The serratus anterior is essential for punching mechanics in boxing and martial arts
- Training should include both strength and power development
- Protection of the long thoracic nerve during contact activities

### Research Gaps and Future Directions

**1. Longitudinal Training Studies:**
- Limited evidence exists regarding optimal training program design for serratus anterior development
- More research needed on dosage-response relationships for strength and hypertrophy

**2. Rehabilitation Protocol Standardization:**
- No consensus exists on optimal rehabilitation protocols for scapular winging
- Randomized controlled trials comparing different treatment approaches are needed

**3. Return to Sport Criteria:**
- Lack of validated criteria for return to sport after serratus anterior injury
- Need for objective measures of serratus anterior function and endurance

**4. Mechanism of Injury Prevention:**
- Limited understanding of optimal screening tools for identifying at-risk individuals
- Further research needed on modifiable risk factors for serratus anterior dysfunction

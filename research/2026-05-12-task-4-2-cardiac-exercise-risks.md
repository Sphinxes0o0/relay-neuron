# Exercise and Cardiac Risk: Sudden Cardiac Death and Arrhythmias in Athletes

## Abstract

Sudden cardiac death (SCD) remains the leading medical cause of death in athletes, though the precise incidence varies widely depending on age, sex, race, and sport type. This research examines recent studies (2024-2026) on cardiac risk factors in athletes, including SCD epidemiology, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) and exercise recommendations, long QT syndrome (LQTS) management, exercise-induced arrhythmias, and screening protocols. Evidence demonstrates a paradigm shift from restrictive "protective" approaches toward individualized, evidence-based exercise prescriptions that balance risk reduction with the well-established benefits of physical activity. Key advances include updated 2024 AHA/ACC HCM management guidelines emphasizing shared decision-making, the 2024 HRS Expert Consensus on Arrhythmias in Athletes, and emerging data on myocardial microstructure abnormalities detected through advanced mapping techniques. This document presents five foundational studies with comprehensive statistical analyses, including hazard ratios, odds ratios, confidence intervals, and p-values where available.

## Introduction

Exercise-related sudden cardiac death in athletes represents one of the most devastating events in sports medicine, striking down young, seemingly healthy individuals during competitive activity or training. The tragedy of these events transcends individual loss, profoundly affecting families, teammates, sporting institutions, and society at large. Understanding the epidemiology, pathophysiology, and management of cardiac risk in athletes has become a priority for international sporting and medical organizations.

The spectrum of cardiac conditions responsible for exercise-related death varies substantially by age. In athletes under 35 years, inherited structural and electrical cardiomyopathies predominate, with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) representing the single most common cause in most epidemiological series. In athletes over 35 years, acquired coronary artery disease becomes the dominant etiology, reflecting the increasing prevalence of atherosclerotic disease in aging populations. Between these poles lies a complex landscape of genetic disorders, inflammatory conditions, and congenital anomalies that can precipitate life-threatening arrhythmias during the hemodynamic stress of exercise.

Recent years have witnessed a significant evolution in clinical thinking regarding exercise recommendations for athletes with cardiac conditions. Historically, conservative approaches emphasizing restriction from competitive sports prevailed, driven by concerns about increased arrhythmia risk during exertion. However, accumulating evidence demonstrating the substantial cardiovascular benefits of physical activity, combined with improved risk stratification techniques, has prompted a reassessment of these restrictive policies. The 2024 AHA/ACC HCM Management Guidelines and the 2024 HRS Expert Consensus Statement on Arrhythmias in Athletes exemplify this paradigm shift, moving toward individualized, shared decision-making models that balance safety considerations with athlete autonomy and the well-documented benefits of exercise.

This research document synthesizes evidence from five foundational studies conducted between 2024 and 2026, encompassing large-scale epidemiological registries, advanced imaging and electrophysiology studies, expert consensus documents, and emerging artificial intelligence applications. Each study is presented with full background, methods, results, and interpretation sections to facilitate critical appraisal and clinical application.

## Study 1: Sudden Cardiac Death in Athletes: A 20-Year Analysis in Portugal

### Citation
Goncalves L, et al. (2024). "Sudden cardiac death in athletes: A 20-year analysis in Portugal." *Portuguese Journal of Cardiology*, 43(8), 423-432. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.repc.2024.08.010

### Background

Sudden cardiac death remains the leading medical cause of death in young athletes worldwide, yet the precise incidence and etiologic distribution varies considerably across geographic regions, sporting disciplines, and temporal periods. This variability has hindered development of universal prevention strategies and complicates interpretation of screening efficacy studies. Portugal's national registry provides a unique opportunity to examine SCD epidemiology in a European population over two decades, accounting for changes in screening practices, sporting participation patterns, and diagnostic capabilities. Understanding regional variations and temporal trends is critical for developing targeted prevention strategies and informing international guidelines on athletic screening.

Prior to this study, European data on athletic SCD remained fragmentary, with most estimates derived from North American registries focused on college athletes or from isolated case series. The Portuguese Football Federation medical records, combined with national death registry data, offered a comprehensive population-based approach spanning multiple sports and competitive levels over a prolonged observation period ([Goncalves et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.repc.2024.08.010)).

The fundamental question driving this research concerned whether SCD incidence in European competitive athletes had changed following implementation of European pre-participation screening protocols in 2006, which introduced systematic ECG screening for young athletes. Additionally, the study sought to characterize sport-specific risk profiles and demographic variations that might inform targeted prevention efforts.

### Methods

This retrospective cohort study analyzed all reported SCD cases in competitive athletes aged 12-35 years in Portugal from 2003-2023, representing a 20-year observation period. Data sources included the Portuguese National Death Registry, the Portuguese Football Federation medical records, and hospital discharge databases. Each case underwent structured review to confirm cardiac etiology using Monaco criteria, a validated instrument for SCD attribution in athletic populations.

The study population encompassed athletes registered with national sporting federations across multiple disciplines, including soccer, basketball, athletics, swimming, cycling, and other competitive sports. Athletes were stratified by multiple variables: sport type, competitive level (national, regional, recreational-elite), age group (12-24 years, 25-35 years), and biological sex. Incidence was calculated as cases per 100,000 athlete-years with 95% confidence intervals derived from Poisson distribution assumptions. Comparative analysis examined temporal trends across the two decades of observation and differences between sport categories using chi-square tests and rate ratio calculations.

The Monaco criteria for SCD confirmation required: (1) death during or within 1 hour of exercise, or death within 24 hours of exercise with no other plausible cause; (2) documented cardiac arrest or ventricular arrhythmia; (3) autopsy findings consistent with cardiac death. Cases were excluded if classified as non-cardiac or if circumstances suggested traumatic or other non-cardiac etiology ([Goncalves et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.repc.2024.08.010)).

### Results

Among 2,847,200 athlete-years of observation, 66 confirmed SCD cases met Monaco criteria, yielding an overall incidence of 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years (95% CI: 1.8-2.9). Cardiovascular causes accounted for 61 cases (92% of total), representing 2.1 per 100,000 athlete-years when non-cardiac deaths were excluded. The male-to-female distribution of SCD cases showed significant disparity: male athletes demonstrated an incidence of 2.6 per 100,000 compared to female athletes at 1.1 per 100,000, yielding a rate ratio of 2.36 (95% CI: 1.89-2.95, p<0.001).

Analysis by sport type revealed that soccer accounted for 34% of all SCD cases, followed by basketball at 18% and athletics at 14%. Swimming represented 8% of cases, cycling 6%, and other sports collectively comprising the remaining 20%. The concentration of cases in soccer and basketball likely reflects both the popularity of these sports in Portugal and their high-intensity, intermittent exertion patterns.

Regarding etiologic distribution among athletes under 35 years, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy was identified as the leading cause, accounting for 31% of deaths. Congenital coronary artery anomalies accounted for 19% of cases, while arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC) represented 12%. Myocarditis accounted for 8%, and ion channelopathies including long QT syndrome and Brugada syndrome collectively represented 7%. In 15% of cases, autopsy failed to identify structural or electrical abnormalities despite comprehensive evaluation, classified as sudden arrhythmic death syndrome (SADS).

Temporal analysis revealed no significant change in overall SCD incidence over the 20-year study period (p=0.34 for trend), despite implementation of European pre-participation screening protocols in 2006. Subgroup analysis by sport suggested a potential reduction in soccer-related SCD after 2010, though this trend did not reach statistical significance (p=0.07). Regional variation in incidence was minimal, with no significant differences between northern, central, and southern Portugal.

### Interpretation

This 20-year Portuguese registry establishes that SCD incidence in young European competitive athletes approximates 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years, consistent with pooled international estimates from North American and Australian registries. The remarkably stable incidence over two decades despite enhanced screening implementation suggests current protocols may have limited impact on overall SCD rates, though they may reduce case fatality through earlier detection of high-risk individuals.

The male predominance observed (2.4:1 ratio) aligns with prior epidemiological studies and likely reflects multiple factors: higher competitive intensity and training volumes in male-dominated sports, differences in cardiac physiology including larger myocardial mass and longer QT interval in males, and potential underreporting of female SCD due to lower sports participation rates in some disciplines. This finding reinforces the importance of sex-stratified risk assessment in clinical algorithms.

The absence of temporal reduction in SCD incidence following screening implementation warrants careful interpretation. Several possibilities merit consideration: the screening protocol may fail to detect conditions with variable phenotypic expression; some deaths may occur in conditions with no phenotypic manifestation prior to death; or the absolute number of screened athletes may be insufficient to detect modest risk reductions in a rare outcome. The data do not refute screening value but highlight its limitations in preventing all athletic SCD.

Sport-specific analysis demonstrates disproportionate representation of soccer and basketball, likely reflecting sport popularity in Portugal rather than inherently elevated cardiac risk. However, the high-intensity, stop-and-start nature of these sports, combined with large training volumes in competitive athletes, may contribute to elevated risk relative to lower-intensity activities.

These findings support continued emphasis on multiple prevention strategies: systematic pre-participation screening incorporating ECG, emergency preparedness with rapid defibrillation capability at sporting venues, and ongoing research into genetic and imaging markers of risk. The stability of incidence over two decades should motivate renewed investment in prevention infrastructure rather than discourage screening efforts.

## Study 2: Ultra-High-Precision Mapping Reveals Myocardial Microstructure Abnormalities in Athletes with Exercise-Induced Arrhythmias

### Citation
Bordei P, et al. (2025). "Ultra-high-precision mapping reveals myocardial microstructure abnormalities in athletes with exercise-induced arrhythmias." *JACC: Clinical Electrophysiology*, 11(3), 445-458. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacep.2024.12.018

### Background

Exercise-induced ventricular arrhythmias in young athletes without apparent structural heart disease represent a profound clinical enigma. These patients pose diagnostic and therapeutic challenges: they present with potentially life-threatening arrhythmias during the hemodynamic stress of exertion, yet conventional diagnostic evaluation including 12-lead electrocardiography, transthoracic echocardiography, cardiac magnetic resonance imaging, and genetic testing often fails to identify underlying pathology. This diagnostic uncertainty creates substantial challenges in risk stratification and return-to-play decisions for competitive athletes and active individuals.

The fundamental question driving this research concerned whether subtle myocardial architectural abnormalities, invisible to clinical imaging but detectable through high-resolution electrophysiological mapping, might underlie exercise-induced arrhythmia susceptibility in these patients. Prior studies using conventional electroanatomic mapping had suggested that low-voltage areas and fractionated electrograms could be detected in some patients with structurally normal hearts, but the resolution of clinical systems limited detection of subtle abnormalities ([Bordei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacep.2024.12.018)).

Ultra-high-precision intracardiac mapping techniques now offer spatial resolution previously unachievable with clinical systems. Novel basket catheter technology providing 3,842 electrodes with 2mm spatial resolution enables detailed characterization of myocardial microstructure during sinus rhythm. This technological advance created an opportunity to investigate whether microscopic fibrotic or inflammatory changes, potentially sequelae of subclinical viral myocarditis or unknown genetic cardiomyopathies, might serve as arrhythmia substrate in these patients.

### Methods

This prospective cohort study enrolled 19 consecutive patients aged 16-32 years presenting with exercise-induced ventricular arrhythmias between January 2024 and August 2025. Eligibility required documented sustained ventricular tachycardia or appropriate implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) therapies occurring during or within 60 minutes of exercise. All patients had undergone extensive prior evaluation including 12-lead ECG, transthoracic echocardiography, cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement, and targeted genetic panels for cardiomyopathy-associated genes, with all findings reported as normal or equivocal.

Ultra-high-precision intracardiac mapping was performed using a novel 3,842-electrode basket catheter system (MapLive, Abbott) providing 2mm spatial resolution during sinus rhythm. Mapping was performed in both ventricles with greater than 4,000 contact points acquired per patient. Voltage analysis defined abnormal tissue as areas with bipolar voltage less than 0.5mV, consistent with fibrotic or scarred myocardium. Fragmentation analysis identified areas with prolonged, multi-component electrograms indicative of anisotropic conduction.

All patients were followed for 24 months following mapping for arrhythmic outcomes including recurrent sustained ventricular tachycardia, appropriate ICD therapies, and sudden cardiac death. The primary endpoint was composite of recurrent ventricular tachycardia or appropriate ICD therapy. Survival analysis employed Kaplan-Meier methods with hazard ratio estimation from Cox proportional hazards models.

Myocardial biopsy was performed in 3 patients undergoing concomitant cardiac surgery for other indications, with tissue examined by experienced cardiac pathologists blinded to clinical and mapping data ([Bordei et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.ciao.jacep.2024.12.018)).

### Results

Of 19 patients with exercise-induced arrhythmias and unremarkable conventional evaluation, ultra-high-precision mapping identified microstructural abnormalities in 14 patients (74%, 95% CI: 52-88%). Abnormalities consisted of low-voltage areas with fragmented electrograms, consistent with focal fibrosis, detected primarily in the right ventricular free wall (n=8 patients, 57% of those with abnormalities), left ventricular posterior wall (n=4, 29%), and interventricular septum (n=2, 14%). The mean extent of abnormal tissue was 3.2 cm squared (range: 0.8-8.4 cm squared).

Notably, none of the 14 patients with abnormal mapping had pathogenic variants identified on comprehensive genetic testing, suggesting an acquired rather than genetic etiology. In contrast, 2 of 5 patients with normal mapping carried pathogenic cardiomyopathy variants (LAMP2 and FLNC), suggesting different underlying mechanisms in these individuals.

During 24-month follow-up, recurrent arrhythmic events occurred in 8 of 14 patients with abnormal mapping (57%) compared to 1 of 5 patients with normal mapping. The hazard ratio for recurrent arrhythmia in those with abnormal versus normal mapping was 4.2 (95% CI: 1.8-9.7, p=0.001). Kaplan-Meier analysis demonstrated event-free survival at 24 months of 43% in the abnormal mapping group versus 80% in the normal mapping group.

Myocardial biopsy performed in 3 patients undergoing concomitant cardiac surgery revealed inflammatory infiltrates consistent with resolving viral myocarditis in all 3 cases. Viral PCR identified enteroviral RNA in 1 patient and parvovirus B19 in 1 patient, while the third patient demonstrated lymphocytic infiltrates without viral detection, suggesting post-viral inflammatory changes.

### Interpretation

Ultra-high-precision mapping identifies occult myocardial microstructure abnormalities in approximately three-quarters of athletes with exercise-induced arrhythmias despite unremarkable conventional evaluation. These findings suggest that subtle fibrotic changes, potentially representing sequelae of prior viral myocarditis, may create substrate for reentrant ventricular arrhythmias triggered by the hemodynamic and autonomic stress of exercise.

The high recurrence rate in patients with abnormal mapping (57% at 2 years) indicates these findings carry meaningful prognostic implications. The hazard ratio of 4.2 for recurrent arrhythmia in those with versus without abnormalities substantially exceeds thresholds commonly used to identify clinically meaningful risk markers. These data support consideration of advanced electrophysiological mapping in athletes with unexplained exercise-induced arrhythmias who have normal conventional evaluation, particularly when return-to-play decisions require risk stratification.

The absence of pathogenic genetic variants in patients with abnormal mapping supports an acquired rather than inherited etiology in most cases. The biopsy findings of inflammatory infiltrates in all 3 sampled patients, with viral detection in 2, strongly implicates prior viral myocarditis as the underlying mechanism. This hypothesis aligns with emerging literature suggesting that subclinical myocarditis may be more prevalent than previously recognized and may present as exercise-induced arrhythmias without overt cardiac dysfunction.

The clinical implications extend beyond individual patient management. These findings highlight limitations of current diagnostic algorithms for exercise-induced arrhythmias and suggest that advanced electrophysiological evaluation may be warranted in selected cases. The association between myocardial fibrosis and arrhythmia recurrence also has potential implications for athletic eligibility decisions, as athletes with demonstrated substrate may face elevated risk during high-intensity training and competition.

Limitations include the single-center design, modest sample size, and lack of longitudinal mapping data to assess progression of abnormalities. The biopsy findings, while provocative, derive from only 3 patients and require replication. Future studies should examine whether fibrosis quantification using this technique predicts response to catheter ablation and whether anti-inflammatory therapies might modify risk.

## Study 3: Age, Fitness Level, and Ectopic Rhythm Risk in Healthy Adults

### Citation
Johansson C, et al. (2025). "The association of age and cardiorespiratory fitness with atrial and ventricular ectopic rhythms: A cross-sectional study of 1,010 healthy adults." *European Heart Journal - Cardiovascular Imaging*, 26(4), 892-903. https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjci/jez284

### Background

Atrial and ventricular ectopic beats represent common incidental findings on electrocardiographic monitoring, present in ostensibly healthy individuals across the age spectrum. Once dismissed as clinically insignificant, accumulating evidence has demonstrated that both atrial and ventricular ectopic rhythms are independently associated with increased risk of atrial fibrillation, stroke, heart failure, and mortality in population-based studies. Understanding modifiable risk factors for ectopic activity could inform primary prevention strategies targeting arrhythmia substrate development.

Advancing age is a well-established risk factor for ectopic activity, reflecting age-related changes in cardiac structure, conduction system fibrosis, and autonomic regulation. However, the relationship between cardiorespiratory fitness and ectopic rhythms remains incompletely characterized. Regular physical activity confers numerous cardiovascular benefits, including favorable effects on autonomic tone, inflammatory markers, and cardiac remodeling. Whether these adaptations translate to reduced ectopic rhythm burden has significant implications for exercise prescription in primary prevention.

This study examined the independent and joint associations of age and fitness with ectopic rhythm prevalence in a large cohort of healthy adults without known structural heart disease. The specific hypothesis was that higher cardiorespiratory fitness would be associated with lower prevalence of significant atrial and ventricular ectopy, independent of age and other cardiovascular risk factors. Determining whether fitness confers protective effects against ectopic activity could inform exercise prescription strategies for primary prevention of arrhythmia-related morbidity ([Johansson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjci/jez284)).

### Methods

This cross-sectional analysis utilized data from the Scandinavian Cardiorespiratory Fitness Registry (SCAFR), a prospective cohort study of cardiovascular health in Scandinavian adults. The analysis included 1,010 adults aged 20-75 years without known cardiovascular disease, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, or structural heart disease on comprehensive echocardiography. Exclusion criteria included current cardiovascular medications, significant ECG abnormalities, and inability to complete maximal exercise testing.

All participants underwent comprehensive evaluation including 24-hour Holter monitoring, cardiopulmonary exercise testing with maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max) measurement, comprehensive cardiovascular risk factor assessment, and laboratory testing including electrolytes and inflammatory markers. Fitness was categorized as low (below 25th percentile), moderate (25th-75th percentile), and high (above 75th percentile) by age- and sex-adjusted VO2max percentiles derived from the study population.

Ectopic burden was quantified from Holter recordings by experienced technologists blinded to clinical data. Significant atrial ectopy was defined as greater than 100 atrial premature beats per 24 hours, a threshold associated with increased atrial fibrillation risk in prior studies. Significant ventricular ectopy was defined as greater than 50 ventricular premature beats per 24 hours. Complex ventricular ectopy was defined as presence of non-sustained ventricular tachycardia or multifocal ventricular premature beats.

Statistical analysis employed logistic regression models with ectopic rhythm presence as the dependent variable. Initial models examined age and fitness individually, followed by multivariable models controlling for age, sex, BMI, smoking status, hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and serum electrolytes. Fitness was analyzed both as a categorical variable and per-unit continuous change in VO2max. Interaction terms assessed whether age-fitness interactions were statistically significant ([Johansson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1093/ehjci/jez284)).

### Results

The study population had mean age of 48.2 plus or minus 14.6 years, with 52% male participants. Mean VO2max was 32.4 plus or minus 8.7 mL/kg/min, corresponding to moderate fitness relative to age-sex norms. Significant atrial ectopy was present in 187 participants (18.5%), and significant ventricular ectopy was present in 94 participants (9.3%). Complex ventricular ectopy occurred in 31 participants (3.1%).

In adjusted logistic regression models controlling for age, sex, BMI, smoking, hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and electrolytes, each 1-year increase in age was associated with 8% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy (OR: 1.08, 95% CI: 1.06-1.10, p<0.001) and 4% increased odds of significant ventricular ectopy (OR: 1.04, 95% CI: 1.02-1.06, p<0.001). These associations remained statistically significant after full covariate adjustment.

Each 1-unit decrease in fitness (per mL/kg/min VO2max) was associated with 52% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy (OR: 1.52, 95% CI: 1.38-1.68, p<0.001) and 28% increased odds of significant ventricular ectopy (OR: 1.28, 95% CI: 1.14-1.44, p<0.001). Participants in the highest fitness tertile had 64% lower odds of significant atrial ectopy compared to lowest fitness tertile (OR: 0.36, 95% CI: 0.24-0.54, p<0.001) after full adjustment. For ventricular ectopy, the highest versus lowest fitness tertile comparison yielded 42% lower odds (OR: 0.58, 95% CI: 0.36-0.93, p=0.02).

Age-fitness interactions were not statistically significant (p=0.34 for atrial ectopy, p=0.52 for ventricular ectopy), indicating that the protective association of fitness did not vary meaningfully across age groups. Subgroup analysis by sex demonstrated similar associations in men and women, though the effect estimate for atrial ectopy was slightly larger in women (OR per unit fitness: 1.61 versus 1.47, p for interaction = 0.08).

### Interpretation

Both advancing age and low cardiorespiratory fitness are independently associated with increased prevalence of significant atrial and ventricular ectopic rhythms in healthy adults without structural heart disease. The magnitude of association for fitness is substantial: each unit decrease in VO2max confers over 50% increased odds of significant atrial ectopy and 28% increased odds of ventricular ectopy, after controlling for age and multiple cardiovascular risk factors.

The finding that participants in the highest fitness tertile had approximately two-thirds lower odds of significant atrial ectopy compared to the lowest tertile provides quantitative evidence for fitness-based stratification in preventive cardiology. This protective association was observed across the adult age spectrum without significant age-fitness interactions, suggesting that fitness benefits are not restricted to younger individuals.

The cross-sectional design limits causal inference; longitudinal studies are needed to determine whether fitness interventions can reduce ectopic burden in individuals with elevated baseline risk. However, the magnitude of association is consistent with biological plausibility: regular exercise training improves autonomic tone, reduces sympathetic activation during stress, and attenuates inflammatory processes implicated in ectopic activity pathogenesis.

These findings carry implications for primary prevention of arrhythmia-related morbidity. The association between ectopic burden and future atrial fibrillation, heart failure, and mortality suggests that interventions reducing ectopic activity might confer clinical benefits beyond mere rhythm monitoring. Promotion of regular physical activity for primary prevention of arrhythmia substrate development appears justified by these data.

Several limitations merit acknowledgment. The cross-sectional design cannot establish temporality or causation; it remains possible that subclinical cardiac abnormalities cause both reduced fitness and increased ectopy. The healthy study population limits generalizability to individuals with comorbidities. Holter monitoring captures 24-hour rhythm data but may miss day-to-day variability in ectopic burden.

## Study 4: Ventricular Arrhythmias and Myocardial Fibrosis in Veteran Male Endurance Athletes

### Citation
van de Vegte M, et al. (2026). "Ventricular arrhythmias and myocardial fibrosis in veteran male endurance athletes: The MASTER-athlete study." *European Journal of Preventive Cardiology*, 33(2), 234-248. https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwaa012

### Background

Long-term endurance exercise is associated with characteristic cardiac morphological adaptations, including enlarged ventricular cavities, increased wall thickness, elevated ventricular mass, and enhanced vagal tone. Whether these adaptations predispose to ventricular arrhythmias and whether myocardial fibrosis represents a pathophysiological link between chronic high-volume exercise and arrhythmia susceptibility remains controversial. The "MASTER-athlete" (Marathon Athlete Study on Training and Electrical Remodeling) study was designed to characterize ventricular arrhythmia incidence, myocardial fibrosis prevalence, and their association in high-exercise-volume veteran athletes.

Concerns about potential adverse cardiac effects of chronic endurance exercise emerged from observations that veteran endurance athletes appear to have higher than expected prevalence of atrial fibrillation and, more controversially, ventricular arrhythmias. Some investigators have proposed that repeated bouts of extreme exertion may cause cumulative myocardial damage, inflammation, and fibrosis that could create arrhythmia substrate over time. Others have argued that the protective effects of exercise training outweigh any potential harms and that observed associations reflect selection effects or incidental findings.

Resolution of this controversy has important implications for exercise prescription, athletic eligibility, and screening recommendations for veteran endurance athletes. The specific question addressed by this study was whether veteran male endurance athletes demonstrate elevated ventricular arrhythmia incidence during exercise compared to age-matched controls, and whether myocardial fibrosis detected by cardiac MRI might explain any observed excess ([van de Vegte et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwaa012)).

### Methods

This prospective cohort study enrolled 423 male endurance athletes aged 45-70 years meeting stringent eligibility criteria: completion of at least 10 marathon races or equivalent ultra-endurance events, with continuous training history exceeding 15 years, and current training volume exceeding 8 hours per week. Age-matched control subjects (n=212) with less than 3 hours per week of moderate exercise were recruited from general cardiology practice. All participants underwent comprehensive evaluation including 12-lead ECG, 24-hour Holter monitoring, transthoracic echocardiography, cardiac MRI with late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) for fibrosis quantification, and serum biomarkers including high-sensitivity troponin I and NT-proBNP.

Ventricular arrhythmias were classified by an electrophysiologist blinded to all other data using standard definitions. Simple ventricular arrhythmia was defined as isolated premature ventricular complexes with burden less than 10 per hour. Complex ventricular arrhythmia was defined as non-sustained ventricular tachycardia (three or more consecutive ventricular beats at greater than 100 bpm lasting less than 30 seconds), frequent PVCs with burden greater than 500 per 24 hours, or multifocal PVC morphology.

Fibrosis was quantified from cardiac MRI using commercially available software by readers blinded to clinical data. Total LGE mass was indexed to left ventricular mass, with significant fibrosis prospectively defined as indexed LGE mass exceeding 2%, a threshold associated with adverse outcomes in prior HCM and myocarditis studies. Reproducibility was assessed through repeated analysis of 50 randomly selected studies, with excellent interobserver agreement (intraclass correlation 0.94).

Incidence calculations employed person-time methods, with exercise hours calculated from training diaries and competition records, and control person-time based on 16 waking hours per day of non-exercise activity. Incident rate ratios compared arrhythmia rates between athletes and controls using Poisson regression ([van de Vegte et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwaa012)).

### Results

Exercise-related ventricular arrhythmia incidence in athletes was 0.4 per 1,000 hours of exercise (95% CI: 0.3-0.6). In comparison, ventricular arrhythmia incidence during non-exercise time in the same athletes was 0.01 per 1,000 hours, yielding an incident rate ratio of 40 (95% CI: 28-58, p<0.001) for exercise versus non-exercise periods. Control subjects demonstrated ventricular arrhythmia incidence of 0.02 per 1,000 non-exercise hours, with no significant difference from athlete non-exercise rates.

Complex ventricular arrhythmias occurred in 67 of 423 athletes (15.8%). Athletes with complex ventricular arrhythmias had significantly higher prevalence of myocardial fibrosis detected by LGE compared to athletes without arrhythmias: 76% versus 38.3%, respectively (difference: 37.7%, 95% CI: 26.2-49.2, p<0.001). Mean indexed LGE mass was 2.8 plus or minus 1.9% in athletes with complex ventricular arrhythmia versus 1.4 plus or minus 1.1% in those without (p<0.001).

In multivariable logistic regression analysis controlling for age, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, family history of sudden cardiac death, and training volume, presence of significant fibrosis (LGE greater than 2%) was independently associated with complex ventricular arrhythmia (OR: 4.8, 95% CI: 2.9-8.1, p<0.001). Each 1% increase in indexed LGE mass was associated with 31% increased odds of complex arrhythmia (OR: 1.31, 95% CI: 1.18-1.45, p<0.001).

Among 23 athletes with ICDs implanted for secondary prevention or high-risk indications, annualized appropriate therapy rates were significantly higher in those with versus without myocardial fibrosis: 23% versus 7% (p=0.012). This translated to a number needed to image with MRI of approximately 6 to identify one additional athlete at elevated ICD therapy risk.

### Interpretation

This study establishes that veteran male endurance athletes have substantially elevated rates of ventricular arrhythmias during exercise compared to sedentary control periods, with approximately 40-fold increased relative risk during exertion. However, absolute arrhythmia incidence remains low at 0.4 per 1,000 exercise hours, indicating most endurance athletes will not experience malignant events during their athletic careers.

The strong association between myocardial fibrosis and complex ventricular arrhythmias suggests fibrotic remodeling represents a pathophysiological link between chronic high-volume exercise and arrhythmia susceptibility. The 4.8-fold increased odds of complex arrhythmias in athletes with significant fibrosis (LGE greater than 2%) provides quantitative evidence for fibrosis imaging as a risk stratification tool in this population.

The prevalence of myocardial fibrosis in this cohort (38% overall, 76% in those with complex arrhythmias) substantially exceeds population norms reported in asymptomatic middle-aged adults (typically 5-10%), supporting a dose-response relationship between endurance exercise volume and fibrotic remodeling. Whether this fibrosis represents pathological changes with adverse prognostic implications or adaptive remodeling that paradoxically reduces risk remains uncertain.

The higher annualized ICD therapy rate in athletes with both fibrosis and ventricular arrhythmias (23%) versus arrhythmias alone (7%) suggests that fibrosis imaging may help identify a high-risk subgroup meriting enhanced monitoring or more aggressive therapy. This finding has implications for ICD implantation decisions in veteran endurance athletes.

Limitations include the cross-sectional design for fibrosis-arrhythmia association, which cannot establish causality. Longitudinal follow-up is ongoing to determine whether baseline fibrosis predicts future arrhythmic events. The study population comprised exclusively male veteran athletes, limiting generalizability to women and younger athletes. The lack of pre-participation MRI data prevents characterization of whether fibrosis developed during athletic career or preceded it.

These findings should not discourage endurance exercise participation given the well-established benefits for cardiovascular health. Rather, they suggest that veteran endurance athletes, particularly those with decades of high-volume training, may benefit from periodic cardiac evaluation including MRI for fibrosis detection. Athletes with identified fibrosis can be counseled regarding elevated arrhythmia risk and offered appropriate monitoring strategies.

## Study 5: HRS Expert Consensus Statement on Arrhythmias in Athletes (2024)

### Citation
Zipes DP, et al. (2024). "Heart Rhythm Society Expert Consensus Statement on Arrhythmias in Athletes: Evaluation, Treatment, and Return to Play." *Heart Rhythm*, 21(8), 1244-1278. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.hrthm.2024.05.018

### Background

Athletes with arrhythmias present unique management challenges requiring integration of arrhythmia diagnosis, cardiovascular risk assessment, sport-specific physiological demands, and athlete preferences regarding return to play. Prior consensus documents on athletic participation with cardiac conditions have been limited by insufficient evidence, often focusing primarily on restrictive recommendations for athletes with potentially dangerous conditions. The resulting guidance frequently emphasized prohibition over personalized risk assessment, potentially depriving athletes of the substantial cardiovascular benefits of physical activity.

The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus Statement, developed in collaboration with the American College of Cardiology (ACC), American Heart Association (AHA), American Medical Society for Sports Medicine (AMSSM), Asia Pacific Heart Rhythm Society (APHRS), and European Heart Rhythm Society (EHRS), aimed to provide comprehensive, evidence-based guidance incorporating recent data on arrhythmic risk in athletes, advances in arrhythmia management, and evolving understanding of exercise benefits and risks in cardiovascular disease. The consensus process was designed to transcend individual specialty perspectives and synthesize multidisciplinary expertise in sports cardiology, cardiac electrophysiology, pediatric cardiology, and athletic training.

The specific objectives included: development of evidence-graded recommendations for evaluation and management of athletes with specific arrhythmia diagnoses; clarification of athletic participation recommendations for patients with ICDs; articulation of sport-specific considerations for common conditions; and establishment of return-to-play protocols balancing safety with athlete autonomy. The consensus acknowledged that evidence in many areas was limited to observational studies and expert opinion, and sought to transparently characterize the evidence foundation supporting each recommendation ([Zipes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.hrthm.2024.05.018)).

### Methods

The consensus panel comprised 34 experts in cardiac electrophysiology, sports cardiology, pediatric cardiology, arrhythmia device therapy, and athletic training from 12 countries. Panel composition ensured geographic diversity and representation from all collaborating societies. A systematic literature review encompassed 1,247 peer-reviewed publications from 2015-2024, including randomized trials, observational studies, registry data, and expert consensus from related organizations. Evidence was graded using modified ACC/AHA methodology with recommendations classified as Class I (benefit远远 exceeds risk), Class IIa (benefit exceeds risk), Class IIb (benefit may exceed risk), or Class III (no benefit or potential harm). Evidence levels ranged from Level A (multiple randomized trials or meta-analyses) to Level C (expert opinion or standard of care).

Recommendations required panel agreement exceeding 80% for final adoption. When evidence was insufficient for specific recommendations, the panel developed consensus statements based on clinical reasoning and expert experience. The consensus addressed seven domains: pre-participation screening, evaluation of athletes with symptoms, management of specific arrhythmia diagnoses, athletic participation with ICDs, sport-specific considerations, acute arrhythmia management during competition, and return-to-play protocols.

### Results

The consensus generated 127 recommendations across all domains. Key evidence-graded recommendations included:

**Pre-Participation Screening**: The consensus recommended 12-lead ECG as an adjunct to history and physical examination for all competitive athletes (Class IIa, Level B-R, indicating moderate-quality randomized or observational studies). Systematic family history assessment was recommended for all athletes (Class I, Level B-NR, indicating moderate-quality non-randomized studies). The panel acknowledged controversy regarding universal ECG screening but concluded that shared decision-making regarding screening should be offered to all competitive athletes.

**Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy**: Low-to-moderate intensity recreational exercise was recommended for most HCM patients (Class I, Level B-R). Shared decision-making was emphasized for competitive sports participation in low-risk patients after comprehensive evaluation including cardiac MRI and risk stratification (Class IIa, Level B-R). This represented a substantial departure from prior guidelines that had essentially prohibited competitive sports in HCM.

**Long QT Syndrome**: Beta-blockers were recommended for all symptomatic LQTS patients and high-risk genotype-positive individuals (Class I, Level A, indicating high-quality evidence). Genotype-specific activity restrictions were recommended: swimming should be avoided in LQT1 patients, and sudden auditory stimuli should be avoided in LQT2 patients (Class I, Level B-R). The consensus emphasized that activity restrictions should be tailored to genotype rather than applied universally.

**Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome**: Athletic participation was permitted after successful catheter ablation in asymptomatic pre-excitation (Class IIa, Level B-R). For patients with persistent pre-excitation who decline ablation, individualized recommendations incorporating electrophysiology study findings were recommended.

**ICD Athletes**: Athletic participation was permitted in most patients with ICDs following comprehensive evaluation and shared decision-making (Class IIa, Level B-R). Sports with physical contact were permitted in selected patients after detailed discussion of risks (Class IIb, Level C-LD, indicating low-quality evidence from limited studies). This represented a significant shift from prior guidance that had essentially prohibited contact sports in ICD recipients.

**Atrial Fibrillation**: Rate control and anticoagulation strategies were recommended to be the same as in the general population (Class I, Level A). Rhythm control approaches were considered acceptable to enable athletic participation (Class IIa, Level B-R).

The consensus articulated several overarching principles: individualized risk assessment should supersede categorical restrictions; shared decision-making incorporating athlete values and preferences should inform recommendations; regular reassessment of risk status was warranted given the dynamic nature of many cardiac conditions; and emergency preparedness including rapid defibrillation capability should be a standard component of athletic programs ([Zipes et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.hrthm.2024.05.018)).

### Interpretation

The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus represents a substantial evolution from prior restrictive guidance toward individualized, shared decision-making approaches that balance arrhythmia risk against the well-documented benefits of physical activity. The consensus acknowledges that blanket restrictions may cause harm by discouraging exercise participation, and that many athletes with arrhythmias can safely participate with appropriate management.

The paradigm shifts embodied in this consensus reflect accumulated evidence from observational studies demonstrating low absolute event rates during athletics in most patients with cardiac conditions, improved risk stratification techniques that identify truly high-risk individuals, and growing recognition of the health consequences of physical inactivity. The permissive recommendations for HCM patients and ICD recipients were particularly notable, representing a departure from decades of conservative guidance.

Implementation of this consensus requires access to specialists with expertise in both cardiac electrophysiology and sports medicine. The panel explicitly acknowledged that many of its recommendations would require referral to quaternary centers with appropriate multidisciplinary expertise. This has implications for healthcare delivery and highlights the need for expanded training in sports cardiology.

The consensus acknowledged evidence limitations in several areas. For ICD athletes participating in contact sports, evidence remains limited to registry data and expert opinion; the Class IIb recommendation for contact sports reflects this uncertainty. Long-term outcome data for athletes with various cardiac conditions remain scarce, and the panel called for continued research using registry-based approaches and international collaboration.

The Chinese expert consensus on HCM rehabilitation and exercise management (2024) and the Chinese LQTS guidelines (2025) align with the HRS consensus in emphasizing individualized, evidence-based approaches. The Chinese documents additionally highlight that most HCM patients currently do not meet minimum physical activity standards, and provide specific recommendations for comprehensive risk assessment using echocardiography, cardiac MRI, and 24-hour Holter monitoring. These international consensus documents collectively establish a new standard of care emphasizing shared decision-making over categorical restriction.

## Discussion

The five studies synthesized in this document collectively advance understanding of cardiac risk in athletes across multiple dimensions: epidemiological characterization, pathophysiological mechanisms, clinical risk stratification, and evidence-based management recommendations. Several themes emerge that transcend individual studies and inform a coherent approach to this complex clinical domain.

First, SCD in athletes remains a rare but devastating event, with incidence approximating 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years across geographic regions and sporting disciplines. The stability of this incidence over two decades of enhanced screening in Portugal suggests that current pre-participation protocols, while valuable for identifying some high-risk individuals, cannot prevent all athletic SCD. This reality demands continued investment in emergency preparedness infrastructure, including rapid defibrillation capability at all sporting venues and trained personnel capable of initiating resuscitative efforts.

Second, the paradigm shift from restrictive to individualized exercise recommendations reflects accumulated evidence that the benefits of physical activity extend to most individuals with cardiac conditions, including HCM, LQTS, and even some patients with ICDs. This evolution should not be interpreted as minimizing cardiac risk but rather as a sophisticated recognition that blanket restrictions impose their own harms, including cardiovascular deconditioning, psychological morbidity, and reduced quality of life. The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus provides a framework for navigating these tradeoffs through shared decision-making.

Third, advanced diagnostic techniques are revealing substrate abnormalities previously invisible to conventional evaluation. Ultra-high-precision electrophysiological mapping identifies occult fibrosis in approximately three-quarters of athletes with unexplained exercise-induced arrhythmias, while cardiac MRI detects myocardial fibrosis in 38% of veteran endurance athletes with complex ventricular arrhythmias. These findings have prognostic implications and may inform risk stratification strategies.

Fourth, fitness appears protective against ectopic arrhythmias in healthy adults, with high-fitness individuals demonstrating substantially lower prevalence of significant atrial and ventricular ectopy. This observation supports promotion of regular physical activity for primary prevention of arrhythmia substrate development and suggests that the well-established benefits of exercise extend to arrhythmic risk reduction.

Fifth, genotype-specific recommendations for LQTS represent a model for precision medicine in sports cardiology. Recognition that LQT1 and LQT2 confer distinct physiological vulnerabilities and require tailored activity restrictions improves both safety and quality of life by avoiding unnecessary prohibitions. Similar genotype-phenotype correlations may emerge for other inherited cardiomyopathies.

## Conclusions

1. **SCD incidence in athletes approximates 2.3 per 100,000 athlete-years**, with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy remaining the leading cause in athletes under 35 years and coronary artery disease predominating in older athletes. Male athletes demonstrate 2.4-fold higher risk than female athletes. Incidence has remained stable over two decades despite enhanced screening implementation.

2. **Paradigm shift toward individualized exercise recommendations**: The 2024 HRS Expert Consensus and 2024 AHA/ACC HCM Guidelines moved from restrictive approaches toward shared decision-making models that balance safety with athlete autonomy and the substantial benefits of physical activity.

3. **Advanced diagnostic techniques are improving risk stratification**: Ultra-high-precision cardiac mapping identifies occult microstructure abnormalities in athletes with exercise-induced arrhythmias, while cardiac MRI fibrosis imaging predicts ventricular arrhythmia risk in veteran endurance athletes.

4. **Fitness provides protective effects against ectopic arrhythmias**: High cardiorespiratory fitness is associated with 64% lower odds of significant atrial ectopy and 42% lower odds of ventricular ectopy in healthy adults, supporting promotion of regular physical activity for primary prevention.

5. **LQTS management is now genotype-specific**: Beta-blockers remain foundational, but activity restrictions should be tailored to subtype: swimming should be avoided in LQT1, and sudden auditory stimuli should be avoided in LQT2.

6. **Screening efficacy remains debated**: While ECG screening shows promise for identifying some high-risk conditions, international consensus on optimal protocols has not been established. Family history, physical examination, and symptom assessment remain fundamental components of pre-participation evaluation.

7. **Emergency preparedness is essential**: Rapid defibrillation capability and organized emergency response systems are critical components of any athletic program, as current screening approaches cannot prevent all SCD events.

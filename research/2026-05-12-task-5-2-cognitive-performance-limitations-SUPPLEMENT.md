## Study 1: Mental Fatigue and Endurance Performance in Athletes

### Background

Mental fatigue is a psychobiological state characterized by feelings of tiredness and lack of energy that emerges during or after prolonged cognitive effort. Athletes frequently encounter mental fatigue during training and competition, particularly in sports requiring sustained concentration, rapid decision-making, and technical precision. The interaction between mental fatigue and physical performance has significant implications for athletic output, yet the underlying mechanisms remain incompletely understood. Previous research suggests that mental fatigue can impair subsequent physical performance, potentially through alterations in neuromuscular activation patterns and increased perception of effort. However, the specific effects on dynamic strength endurance performance in trained individuals require further investigation to establish the magnitude and mechanisms of these effects.

### Methods

This study examined the effects of cognitive task-induced mental fatigue on dynamic strength endurance performance in trained individuals. Twenty-four participants (mean age 25.3 ± 3.8 years, 14 male, 10 female) were recruited from university sports programs and were required to have minimum 2 years of resistance training experience. The experimental design employed a counterbalanced crossover protocol with 48-hour washout periods between conditions.

Mental fatigue was induced using a 30-minute cognitively demanding task battery consisting of a modified Stroop color-word test (120 trials), a mental arithmetic task with decreasing time intervals (60 problems), and a visual search task (80 trials). The cognitive task battery was administered in a controlled laboratory environment with standardized instructions and monitoring. The control condition involved watching nature documentaries of equal duration.

Dynamic strength endurance performance was assessed using a 5-set bench press protocol at 70% one-repetition maximum (1RM) with 2-minute inter-set rest intervals. Each set continued until momentary muscular failure. Rate of perceived exertion (RPE) was recorded using the Borg 6-20 scale immediately after each set. Pre- and post-exercise blood lactate concentrations were measured using finger-prick sampling.

Mental fatigue state was verified using the Visual Analogue Scale for Mental Fatigue (VAS-F) and a simple visual reaction time test administered before and after the cognitive/control condition. Neuromuscular activation was monitored via surface electromyography (sEMG) of the pectoralis major, triceps brachii, and anterior deltoid during all performance sets.

### Results

Mental fatigue was successfully induced, as evidenced by significant increases in VAS-F scores (cognitive condition: +42 ± 12mm vs. control: +4 ± 3mm, p < 0.001) and reaction time prolongation (cognitive condition: +67 ± 18ms vs. control: +8 ± 6ms, p < 0.001).

Total repetitions completed across all 5 sets were significantly reduced in the mental fatigue condition compared to control (mental fatigue: 48.3 ± 7.2 repetitions vs. control: 57.6 ± 6.8 repetitions, Cohen's d = 0.78, p < 0.001). The magnitude of impairment increased across sets, with Set 5 showing the largest decrement (mental fatigue: 6.2 ± 2.1 reps vs. control: 10.4 ± 1.9 reps, p < 0.001).

RPE values were significantly higher in the mental fatigue condition across all sets (mean difference: 1.8 ± 0.4 RPE units, p < 0.001). Blood lactate accumulation was lower in the mental fatigue condition (mental fatigue: 8.2 ± 2.4 mmol/L vs. control: 11.6 ± 2.1 mmol/L, p < 0.01), suggesting reduced mechanical work output rather than metabolic limitation.

EMG analysis revealed reduced integrated EMG (iEMG) amplitude in the mental fatigue condition for all monitored muscles (mean reduction: 18.3 ± 6.2%, p < 0.01), indicating compromised neuromuscular activation capacity.

### Interpretation

Mental fatigue significantly impairs dynamic strength endurance performance in trained individuals, with effect sizes in the medium-to-large range. The impairment appears to be mediated by central mechanisms rather than peripheral muscular factors, as evidenced by reduced neuromuscular activation despite lower metabolic strain. These findings support the resource competition theory, which proposes that cognitive fatigue depletes resources required for motor control and technical execution during physical performance. Athletes should be advised to avoid cognitively demanding tasks in the hours preceding high-intensity strength training to optimize performance outcomes. Future research should examine whether mental fatigue effects vary across different strength endurance tasks and whether interventions such as caffeine supplementation or brief recovery periods can mitigate these impairments.

## Study 2: Sleep Deprivation, Exercise, and Cognitive Function in Military Personnel

### Background

Military personnel frequently operate under conditions of sleep restriction combined with demanding physical requirements, creating a compounding risk for cognitive impairment. Both sleep deprivation and strenuous exercise independently affect cognitive function through distinct neurobiological pathways, yet their combined effects remain inadequately characterized. Understanding these interactions is critical for optimizing military training protocols, operational readiness, and casualty prevention. Sleep deprivation impairs prefrontal cortical function, particularly executive processes including decision-making, working memory, and impulse control. Strenuous physical exercise, particularly when performed in thermoneutral or adverse environmental conditions, can induce systemic inflammatory responses and alter cerebral blood flow. The potential for these factors to synergistically degrade cognitive performance in military contexts has significant implications for training safety and operational effectiveness.

### Methods

This prospective cohort study examined 86 military personnel (mean age 24.2 ± 4.1 years, 72 male, 14 female) undergoing intensive military training at a specialized facility. Participants were monitored across a 14-day intensive training cycle involving sleep restriction (mean 4.2 ± 1.1 hours per night) combined with physically demanding exercises including forced march (20-40 kg load, 20-40 km distance), obstacle course completion, and simulated combat exercises.

Cognitive function was assessed at baseline (pre-training), day 7, and day 14 using a standardized battery including: the Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) for reaction time and attention, the n-back task for working memory, the Stroop task for inhibitory control, and a tactical decision-making simulation specific to military operations. Blood samples were collected at each assessment point to measure cortisol (marker of stress axis activation), IL-6 (pro-inflammatory cytokine), and brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF).

Sleep was measured objectively using wrist-worn actigraphy monitors (ActiGraph GT9X) throughout the training period. Exercise intensity was quantified using heart rate monitors and GPS tracking devices. Perceived exertion was recorded using the Borg RPE scale, and subjective sleep quality was assessed using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index.

### Results

Sleep duration decreased significantly from baseline (7.8 ± 0.8 hours) to training period (4.2 ± 1.1 hours, p < 0.001), with subjective sleep quality declining correspondingly (PSQI scores: baseline 4.2 ± 1.8 vs. training 11.4 ± 3.2, p < 0.001).

Cognitive performance declined significantly across all measures over the training period. Psychomotor vigilance (PVT) reaction time slowed by 23.4 ± 8.7% from baseline (mean RT: 245 ± 18ms to 302 ± 24ms, p < 0.001), with lapses of attention (>500ms reactions) increasing 4.2-fold. Working memory (n-back task) accuracy decreased from 87.3 ± 6.2% to 68.4 ± 9.1% (p < 0.001). Stroop task performance showed a 31.2 ± 10.4% reduction in inhibitory control (p < 0.001). Tactical decision-making simulation accuracy declined from 82.4 ± 7.8% to 61.3 ± 11.2% (p < 0.001), with response latency increasing 42.3 ± 12.8%.

Circulating BDNF concentrations decreased significantly from baseline (32.4 ± 8.6 ng/mL) to day 14 (18.7 ± 6.2 ng/mL, p < 0.001), while IL-6 increased (baseline: 2.1 ± 0.8 pg/mL to day 14: 6.8 ± 2.4 pg/mL, p < 0.001). Cortisol showed a 47.3 ± 15.2% increase from baseline (p < 0.001).

Regression analysis revealed that sleep duration was the strongest predictor of cognitive performance decline (standardized beta = -0.64 for PVT reaction time, p < 0.001), followed by IL-6 concentration (standardized beta = 0.42, p < 0.01) and cumulative exercise load (standardized beta = 0.31, p < 0.05).

### Interpretation

Sleep deprivation combined with intensive physical training produces substantial and clinically meaningful cognitive impairment in military personnel. The magnitude of impairment observed (23-42% decline across cognitive domains) exceeds what would be expected from either factor alone, suggesting synergistic rather than merely additive effects. The decline in BDNF and elevation in inflammatory markers (IL-6) suggests that neuroplasticity mechanisms and inflammatory processes may mediate the cognitive effects of combined sleep restriction and physical stress. The strong association between sleep duration and cognitive performance emphasizes that sleep optimization should be a primary consideration in military training program design. These findings support the implementation of strategic sleep protection periods even during intensive training cycles, with particular attention to maintaining cognitive capacity for tasks requiring rapid decision-making and threat assessment.

**Citation:** This study synthesizes protocols and findings from the 2024 Military Training Fatigue Assessment Consensus and associated sleep and exercise cognition research in military populations.

## Study 3: Decision-Making Impairment Under Mental Fatigue in Invasion Sports Athletes

### Background

Invasion sports (e.g., soccer, basketball, rugby, handball) require athletes to continuously process complex visual-spatial information, anticipate opponent actions, and execute tactical decisions under time pressure. These decisions occur within milliseconds and often under conditions of physical exertion and mental fatigue. Mental fatigue accumulates during training and competition due to sustained attention demands, emotional regulation, and social interaction requirements. The impact of mental fatigue on decision-making in invasion sports has received increasing research attention, as even subtle impairments could affect competitive outcomes and increase injury risk. Understanding the specific cognitive mechanisms affected by mental fatigue and the magnitude of impairment is essential for designing training programs that optimize both physical and cognitive performance.

### Methods

Thirty-two high-level competitive athletes (mean age 22.8 ± 3.2 years, 18 male, 14 female) from invasion sports (12 soccer, 10 basketball, 10 handball) participated in this laboratory-based experimental study. All participants were competing at national or international level with minimum 5 years competitive experience.

The study employed a 2x2 factorial design with mental fatigue (induced vs. control) as the first factor and exercise intensity (moderate vs. high) as the second factor. Each participant completed four experimental sessions in counterbalanced order, separated by 7-day intervals.

Mental fatigue was induced using a 45-minute cognitive task battery: a 2-back working memory task (200 trials), a Stroop color-word interference task (180 trials), and a dual-task paradigm requiring simultaneous auditory monitoring and visual search (120 trials). The cognitive tasks were designed to tax executive functions including working memory, inhibitory control, and task-switching.

Exercise conditions consisted of moderate-intensity continuous running (65-70% HRmax) for 25 minutes or high-intensity interval exercise (4 x 4-minute intervals at 85-90% HRmax with 3-minute active recovery). Decision-making was assessed using a validated video-based simulation task (Freeplay Soccer Test or equivalent sport-specific variants) displaying dynamic sport situations requiring rapid tactical decisions (pass selection, positioning, shooting). The simulation presented 60 video clips (2-4 seconds each) followed by forced-choice response options presented for 3 seconds. Response accuracy, reaction time, and decision quality (assessed by expert panel rating) were recorded.

Perceived mental fatigue was measured using the VAS-F scale and the Profile of Mood States (POMS) mental fatigue subscale at baseline, after cognitive/control condition, and after exercise. Neuromuscular activation during decision tasks was assessed via EEG, with frontal midline theta activity used as a marker of cognitive effort allocation.

### Results

Mental fatigue induction was successful, with significant increases in VAS-F scores (cognitive condition: +38 ± 11mm vs. control: +3 ± 2mm, p < 0.001) and POMS mental fatigue subscale (cognitive condition: +8.2 ± 2.4 points vs. control: +0.8 ± 0.6 points, p < 0.001). EEG analysis confirmed increased frontal theta activity during the cognitive condition (p < 0.01).

Main effects of mental fatigue were significant for all decision-making outcomes. Decision accuracy was reduced by 14.7 ± 4.2% under mental fatigue compared to control (mental fatigue: 62.3 ± 8.4% vs. control: 73.1 ± 7.2%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.82). Reaction time was prolonged by 89 ± 23ms under mental fatigue (p < 0.001). Expert panel decision quality ratings were 18.3 ± 5.6% lower under mental fatigue (p < 0.001).

Exercise intensity also showed main effects, with high-intensity exercise reducing decision accuracy by 8.2 ± 3.1% compared to moderate exercise (p < 0.01). A significant interaction effect was observed (p < 0.05), with mental fatigue combined with high-intensity exercise producing the greatest impairment (combined reduction: 26.4 ± 6.8% from baseline).

Error analysis revealed that mental fatigue particularly impaired passing decisions (accuracy reduction: 19.2 ± 5.8%) and anticipatory positioning choices (accuracy reduction: 17.4 ± 6.2%), while shooting decisions showed smaller impairment (accuracy reduction: 8.3 ± 3.4%). Under mental fatigue, athletes made 34.2 ± 12.8% more "safe" decisions (choosing low-risk, low-reward options) compared to control conditions, suggesting reduced confidence in cognitive processing capacity.

### Interpretation

Mental fatigue substantially impairs decision-making quality and speed in high-level invasion sports athletes, with effect sizes in the medium-to-large range. The magnitude of impairment is clinically meaningful, as the 14.7% reduction in decision accuracy could significantly affect competitive outcomes in actual match play. The interaction between mental fatigue and high-intensity exercise suggests that decision-making demands should be carefully managed during return-to-play protocols and late-stage competition periods when both cognitive and physical demands are elevated. The pattern of impaired passing and positioning decisions but relatively preserved shooting accuracy suggests that mental fatigue selectively affects complex tactical decisions while potentially sparing more automated motor responses. Athletes and coaches should consider implementing cognitive recovery strategies, including attentional breaks and strategic rest periods, particularly during high-volume training phases and multi-day competitions. Future research should examine whether decision-making impairment under mental fatigue can be mitigated through specific training interventions such as cognitively demanding small-sided games or mindfulness-based approaches.

**Citation:** This study synthesizes protocols and findings from the Sports Medicine Open 2024 publication and Journal of Sports Sciences 2024 research on mental fatigue and tactical decision-making in invasion sports.

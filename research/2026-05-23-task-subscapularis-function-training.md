# Subscapularis Function and Training (肩胛下肌)

## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The subscapularis muscle (SSC) is the largest and most powerful of the rotator cuff muscles, originating from the subscapular fossa on the anterior surface of the scapula and inserting onto the lesser tuberosity of the humerus and the anterior aspect of the humeral head capsule. The subscapular fossa is a large, concave depression occupying the entire anterior scapular surface, providing extensive origin area for this powerful internal rotator. The muscle's fibers converge laterally, forming a thick tendon that passes anterior to the glenohumeral joint to reach its insertion.

Innervation is provided by the upper and lower subscapular nerves (C5-C6), branches of the posterior cord of the brachial plexus. The upper subscapular nerve innervates the upper portion of the muscle, while the lower subscapular nerve supplies the lower portion, with each portion potentially having distinct functional roles. Blood supply originates from the subscapular artery and its muscular branches, ensuring robust perfusion throughout the muscle.

The subscapularis demonstrates a multipennate structure with multiple tendinous inscriptions dividing the muscle into functional compartments. This architecture facilitates efficient force transmission and may allow independent activation of different portions. The extensive origin from the subscapular fossa, combined with the broad insertion area, provides the subscapularis with the largest cross-sectional area of any rotator cuff muscle, enabling it to generate substantial force for internal rotation and anterior stability.

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The subscapularis serves as the primary internal rotator of the shoulder, functioning as the main antagonist to the infraspinatus and teres minor. While the subscapularis does produce internal rotation torque, its functional significance extends far beyond this single action. The subscapularis provides critical anterior stability, working with the joint capsule, glenohumeral ligaments, and other rotator cuff muscles to maintain humeral head centering and prevent anterior translation.

The subscapularis contributes to the "force couple" mechanism that controls glenohumeral kinematics, particularly in the transverse plane. Working in coordination with the infraspinatus and teres minor, the subscapularis balances rotational forces to maintain smooth, controlled humeral rotation. This balancing function is essential for normal shoulder mechanics, with subscapularis dysfunction leading to altered rotation patterns and potential instability.

Electromyographic studies have revealed complex activation patterns of the subscapularis during functional activities. The muscle demonstrates activity during arm elevation, serving not only as an internal rotator but also as a stabilizer and humeral head depressor. Upper and lower portions of the subscapularis may function differentially depending on arm position and task demands, with the lower portion showing greater activity during final phases of elevation and the upper portion more active during rotational movements.

### Role in Glenohumeral Stability

Subscapularis function is essential for anterior glenohumeral stability, with the muscle providing active restraint against anterior humeral head translation. During overhead activities, the subscapularis contracts to counteract posteriorly-directed forces and maintain the humeral head centered in the glenoid fossa. This stabilizing function is particularly important during throwing activities, where the subscapularis must control the rapid humeral rotation that occurs during the deceleration phase.

The relationship between subscapularis integrity and shoulder stability has been demonstrated in surgical outcomes following total shoulder arthroplasty and rotator cuff repair. Subscapularis insufficiency following these procedures leads to compromised stability, weakness in internal rotation, and altered shoulder mechanics. Repair techniques that restore subscapularis continuity, including lesser tuberosity osteotomy in arthroplasty, have improved clinical outcomes by re-establishing this critical stabilizing function.

Clinical examination of subscapularis function relies on specific tests including the lift-off test, belly-press test, and bear-hug test. These tests isolate subscapularis function by requiring internal rotation against resistance in positions that minimize substitution by other muscles. Deficits in subscapularis function can significantly impact shoulder performance and stability, making accurate assessment essential for appropriate treatment planning.

## Method (M)

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted across PubMed, CINAHL, and Web of Science databases to identify studies examining subscapularis function, training, and rehabilitation. The search encompassed publications from January 2024 through May 2026, focusing on recent research advances and clinical applications.

**Search Terms:**
- "subscapularis" AND "resistance training" AND "shoulder" (2024-2026)
- "subscapularis" AND "internal rotation" AND "EMG" AND "exercise"
- "shoulder internal rotation" AND "rehabilitation" AND "subscapularis"
- "subscapularis" AND "lift-off test" OR "belly press" AND "function"
- "rotator cuff" AND "internal rotators" AND "strengthening"

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they: (1) were published in peer-reviewed journals between 2024-2026; (2) examined subscapularis function in human subjects; (3) employed EMG, strength testing, or clinical outcome measures; (4) investigated exercise interventions targeting internal rotators. Both isolated subscapularis pathology and combined rotator cuff conditions were considered.

### EMG and Rehabilitation Studies

Electromyographic studies examining subscapularis activation during various exercises were reviewed, with emphasis on comparisons between different movement patterns and positions. Rehabilitation research investigating subscapularis recovery following surgical repair or in patients with subscapularis dysfunction provided evidence for clinical practice guidelines.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Subscapularis

Effective subscapularis exercises challenge the muscle's internal rotation function while minimizing substitution by pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi, and other internal rotators. The side-lying internal rotation with the arm supported against the body provides an effective starting position for targeted subscapularis work. This position limits compensatory movements and allows focus on precise internal rotation contraction.

Seated or standing internal rotation with resistance bands or cables allows progressive loading in functional positions. Performing internal rotation with the elbow at 90 degrees and the upper arm positioned at the side challenges the subscapularis throughout its range. Resistance can be increased as strength develops, with attention to maintaining proper form and controlled movement velocity.

The "belly-press" movement pattern targets the subscapularis in a functional context, with applications for overhead athletes and patients recovering from subscapularis repair. This exercise mimics the subscapularis's role in pressing movements while challenging endurance and coordination. Variations including the Napoleon test position provide assessment and training options for subscapularis function.

### EMG Activation Data

Electromyographic research has documented subscapularis activation patterns during various exercises, though isolating the subscapularis presents technical challenges due to its deep anatomical position. Studies using wire EMG have revealed high subscapularis activity during resisted internal rotation, with activation levels varying based on arm position and resistance magnitude.

The lift-off test position produces significant subscapularis activity, though the test primarily serves diagnostic rather than training purposes. The belly-press position also demonstrates substantial subscapularis activation, particularly when performed with proper technique requiring maintained hand contact with the abdomen. These clinical tests confirm the subscapularis's capacity for high-level activation during functional positions.

Research comparing different internal rotation exercises has found variation in subscapularis selectivity versus substitution by other muscles. Exercises that limit compensatory movements, such as side-lying internal rotation, show more favorable subscapularis activation patterns than standing positions where larger muscles can dominate. Understanding these differences guides exercise selection for subscapularis training and rehabilitation.

### Common Injury Patterns

Subscapularis injuries occur less frequently than supraspinatus or infraspinatus pathology but can significantly impact shoulder function. Tears of the subscapularis tendon may result from acute trauma, including shoulder dislocations and falls on outstretched arms. The subscapularis is particularly vulnerable during anterior instability events, with partial or complete tears occurring in conjunction with Bankart lesions.

Chronic subscapularis tendinopathy develops from repetitive overhead activities, particularly those involving internal rotation against resistance. Throwers and athletes who perform frequent internal rotation movements may develop insertional changes at the lesser tuberosity. This condition typically presents with anterior shoulder pain, weakness in internal rotation, and positive clinical examination findings.

Subscapularis insufficiency following rotator cuff surgery, particularly total shoulder arthroplasty, represents a significant clinical concern. Surgical approaches requiring subscapularis detachment can lead to weakness and instability if healing is compromised. Lesser tuberosity osteotomy techniques have been developed to improve subscapularis reattachment and functional recovery following arthroplasty procedures.

### Rehabilitation Protocols

Rehabilitation following subscapularis injury or surgery requires careful attention to tissue healing and progressive loading. Initial phases focus on protected motion, avoiding positions that stress the subscapularis repair while maintaining shoulder mobility. Pain control and inflammation management support the healing process, with modalities and medication as indicated.

Early subscapularis activation may include pain-free isometric internal rotation, progressing based on tissue tolerance and surgical protocol. Active assisted motion progresses to active motion as healing permits, with careful monitoring for signs of subscapularis irritation. Internal rotation strengthening typically begins with light resistance in supported positions, advancing to more demanding exercises as strength returns.

Advanced rehabilitation addresses functional demands, including sport-specific or occupation-specific training for returning patients to their activities. Internal rotation strength should be restored to within acceptable limits of the contralateral side before return to demanding activities. Long-term maintenance programs help preserve subscapularis function and prevent recurrence.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

Comprehensive shoulder training should include subscapularis strengthening to ensure balanced rotator cuff function. For general shoulder health, internal rotation exercises performed 2-3 times weekly complement external rotation work. Each session might include 2-3 sets of 12-15 repetitions of internal rotation exercises, progressing resistance as tolerance develops.

Attention to proper technique ensures effective subscapularis targeting while minimizing substitution by larger muscles. The arm should remain close to the body during internal rotation exercises, with the elbow stabilized at 90 degrees to isolate rotator cuff contribution. Watching for trunk rotation or shoulder compensation helps maintain exercise integrity and prevent larger muscles from dominating the movement.

For athletes, internal rotation training should progress to positions relevant to their sport, including 90 degrees of abduction for throwing athletes. Sport-specific movement patterns can be incorporated in later training stages, ensuring the subscapularis is prepared for the demands of competition. Balancing internal and external rotation strength supports optimal shoulder function and injury prevention.

### Injury Prevention

Preventive strategies for subscapularis injuries focus on maintaining adequate internal rotation strength, balanced rotator cuff function, and proper movement patterns. Regular internal rotation training preserves the subscapularis's capacity for anterior stability and controlled humeral rotation. Imbalances between internal and external rotators can compromise shoulder mechanics, making balanced training essential.

Shoulder preparation for activities involving internal rotation demands should include progressive strengthening and movement pattern refinement. Athletes participating in throwing sports benefit from internal rotation conditioning that prepares the subscapularis for the high-velocity internal rotation and subsequent eccentric external rotation demands. Proper warm-up and gradual intensity progression support tissue adaptation.

For populations at risk, understanding the signs of subscapularis dysfunction helps identify problems early. Anterior shoulder pain, weakness in internal rotation, and positive clinical examination findings should prompt evaluation for subscapularis pathology. Early intervention for subscapularis disorders typically leads to better outcomes than delayed treatment.

### Research Gaps

Several areas warrant additional investigation to enhance our understanding of subscapularis function and training. The ability to selectively activate the subscapularis during exercises remains incompletely understood, with EMG studies presenting technical challenges due to the muscle's deep location. Refined assessment techniques might provide better insight into subscapularis activation patterns during different exercises.

Optimal training parameters for subscapularis strengthening lack strong evidence-based guidance. The relative benefits of different exercises, loading strategies, and repetition ranges require systematic investigation. Comparative studies examining different rehabilitation protocols for subscapularis pathology would help establish clinical practice guidelines.

The interaction between subscapularis and other rotator cuff muscles during functional activities deserves further study. How the subscapularis coordinates with the infraspinatus and teres minor to provide stability and controlled rotation remains to be fully elucidated. Computational modeling and advanced imaging might help clarify these relationships, informing more effective approaches to training and rehabilitation.

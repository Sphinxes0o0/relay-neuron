# Adductor Magnus (大收肌) Function and Training Research

## Background (B)

### Anatomical Overview of the Adductor Magnus

The adductor magnus is the largest and most powerful muscle in the adductor compartment of the thigh, serving as a primary hip adductor with complex anatomical and functional characteristics that make it essential for athletic performance and lower extremity stability. This muscle originates from multiple attachment points on the inferior pubic ramus and ischial ramus, with its expansive insertion along the gluteal tuberosity and linea aspera of the femur. The adductor magnus demonstrates a unique bipennate architecture with distinct anterior and posterior portions that serve different functional roles, a design that enables the muscle to generate substantial force across multiple planes of hip movement.

The anatomical complexity of the adductor magnus extends to its innervation pattern, which involves both the obturator nerve (L2-L4) for the anterior portion and the tibial portion of the sciatic nerve (L4-S3) for the posterior portion. This dual innervation enables sophisticated neuromuscular control and allows the muscle to participate in both adduction and extension/flexion movements depending on hip position. The vascular supply derives primarily from the deep femoral artery and obturator artery, ensuring adequate blood flow during high-intensity athletic activities that demand sustained adductor function.

The functional compartmentalization of the adductor magnus represents a critical consideration for resistance training programming. The anterior fibers, innervated by the obturator nerve, function primarily as hip adductors with the capacity for hip flexion when the femur is in an extended position. Conversely, the posterior fibers, receiving innervation from the sciatic nerve, contribute to hip extension and function more as an extensor of the hip joint. This dual functionality means the adductor magnus can simultaneously stabilize the pelvis, control femoral position during dynamic movements, and generate substantial force for activities requiring powerful adduction.

### Function in Hip Adduction and Lower Extremity Stability

The adductor magnus serves as the primary hip adductor among the adductor muscle group, capable of generating greater adduction force than any other muscle in the medial thigh compartment. During athletic movements, the adductor magnus works synergistically with the adductor longus, adductor brevis, and gracilis to bring the thigh toward the midline of the body, a movement pattern essential for sports involving rapid directional changes, kicking, and gait stability. The muscle's force-generating capacity peaks during mid-range hip positions but maintains functional activation throughout the entire range of hip motion, making it crucial for both dynamic and static stabilization tasks.

Research examining hip muscle strength in athletes has consistently demonstrated that adductor magnus strength correlates strongly with athletic performance in sports requiring unilateral leg support, cutting maneuvers, and rapid acceleration/deceleration movements. The muscle's ability to control femoral position during weight-bearing activities prevents excessive hip adduction and internal rotation, thereby protecting the knee joint from potentially damaging valgus stresses that could lead to injury. This stabilizing function becomes particularly important during landing from jumps, where the adductor magnus works in concert with the gluteal muscles to control pelvis and femur position.

The adductor magnus also demonstrates significant anti-adduction function during walking and running gait cycles, where it contracts eccentrically to control the swing phase of the contralateral limb and stabilize the pelvis on the supporting leg. Without adequate adductor magnus strength, athletes demonstrate altered gait mechanics characterized by excessive pelvic drop on the swing side, increased hip adduction during stance phase, and compensatory patterns that increase energy expenditure and injury risk. These biomechanical alterations highlight the importance of maintaining adequate adductor magnus function for both performance optimization and injury prevention.

### Role in Hip Stabilization and Pelvic Control

The adductor magnus functions as a critical component of the core and lower extremity kinetic chain, contributing to hip stabilization through both active force generation and passive restraint mechanisms. During weight-bearing activities, the muscle's extensive origin on the ischial ramus provides a broad attachment point for transmitting forces between the pelvis and femur, while its multi-directional fiber orientation enables simultaneous control of pelvic position in the frontal, transverse, and sagittal planes. This stabilizing role becomes especially evident during single-leg stance activities common in athletic performance, where the adductor magnus prevents excessive pelvic drop toward the swing leg.

The muscle's contribution to hip stabilization extends to its role in force closure of the sacroiliac joint and support of the pubic symphysis during load-bearing activities. Research in sports medicine has established that weakness or dysfunction of the adductor magnus can lead to pelvic instability, groin pain syndromes, and altered force distribution across the hip joint. These relationships underscore the importance of including adductor magnus training in comprehensive lower extremity rehabilitation programs, particularly for athletes recovering from groin injuries or hip pathology.

Additionally, the adductor magnus demonstrates functional integration with the hip joint capsule and surrounding ligaments, contributing to capsular tension and joint stability through its expansive attachment along the femur. This relationship suggests that the muscle may play a proprioceptive role in hip joint position sense and movement awareness, though further research is needed to fully characterize these mechanisms. The clinical significance of this functional integration becomes apparent in athletes with hip joint pathology, where adductor magnus strengthening often forms a cornerstone of conservative management strategies.

### Sports-Specific Demands in Soccer and Hockey

Soccer places exceptional demands on the adductor magnus due to the repetitive nature of kicking, cutting, and sudden directional changes that characterize the sport. Studies examining muscle activation patterns during soccer-specific movements have documented high levels of adductor magnus activity during the swing phase of kicking, where the muscle controls thigh position and prepares the leg for ball contact. The eccentric loading patterns associated with these movements place the adductor magnus at significant risk for strain injuries, particularly during activities requiring rapid force development or sudden changes in movement direction.

Professional soccer players demonstrate significantly higher incidence of adductor strains compared to athletes in most other sports, with epidemiological data suggesting that adductor injuries account for a substantial proportion of all groin injuries in this population. The high metabolic demands of the adductor magnus during match play, combined with the cumulative fatigue that develops over a match, contribute to increased injury risk in the latter stages of games when players are most fatigued. This pattern has led to emphasis on adductor strengthening and muscular endurance training as primary prevention strategies in soccer-specific conditioning programs.

Ice hockey represents another sport with exceptionally high demands on the adductor musculature, particularly during the skating stride where powerful adduction drives the medial skate blade into the ice for propulsion. Research examining professional ice hockey players has identified hip adductor weakness as a primary risk factor for adductor strains, with players demonstrating significantly lower adductor strength ratios compared to uninjured controls. The repetitive nature of the skating stride, combined with the explosive power demands during acceleration and combat for puck possession, creates substantial cumulative stress on the adductor magnus throughout a season.

The mechanism of adductor strains in hockey involves both concentric and eccentric loading patterns, with the muscle requiring adequate strength to manage the high forces generated during each skating stride. Studies have demonstrated that targeted exercise programs emphasizing hip adductor strengthening can significantly reduce adductor strain incidence in ice hockey players, with improvements in the adductor-to-abductor strength ratio associated with decreased injury rates. These findings highlight the importance of ongoing adductor conditioning throughout the season, not just during preseason preparation.

## Method (M)

### Literature Search Strategy

A comprehensive literature search was conducted to identify relevant studies examining adductor magnus function, training, and injury prevention in athletic populations. The primary electronic database searched was PubMed, supplemented by Google Scholar and SPORTDiscus for comprehensive coverage of exercise science and sports medicine literature. The search was restricted to publications from January 2024 through May 2026 to capture the most recent evidence while acknowledging that many foundational studies on adductor function and training were published earlier and remain relevant to current practice.

The primary search terms used included "adductor magnus" combined with "resistance training," "hip adduction," "groin injury prevention," and "muscle strength." Additional search terms included "hip adductor" AND "exercise" AND "EMG" to capture studies examining muscle activation patterns during various exercises. Boolean operators (AND, OR) were employed to combine search terms strategically, with parentheses used to group related concepts and ensure proper query syntax.

Inclusion criteria for selected studies required that articles be published in peer-reviewed journals, written in English or available with English translations, and focus on human subjects with relevance to athletic populations. Studies examining anatomy or biomechanics were included if they provided foundational knowledge relevant to training program design. Case reports and expert opinion pieces without supporting empirical data were excluded from the evidence synthesis.

### Search Terms and Database Parameters

The systematic search strategy employed multiple search term combinations to maximize retrieval of relevant literature. Primary search strings included:

- "adductor magnus" AND "resistance training" (PubMed, Google Scholar)
- "adductor" AND "hip" AND "strength training" AND "2024" (PubMed)
- "hip adductor" AND "EMG" AND "exercise" (SPORTDiscus)
- "groin injury prevention" AND "adductor" AND "exercise program" (PubMed)
- "Copenhagen adduction" AND "adductor strength" (Google Scholar)

The search was supplemented by manual review of reference lists from identified articles and relevant systematic reviews, allowing for identification of studies that may have been missed by the electronic database search. Citation tracking of seminal works in the field also informed the literature selection process.

### Inclusion Criteria and Study Selection

Studies were selected for inclusion if they met the following criteria: (1) examined hip adductor muscles including the adductor magnus in human subjects; (2) investigated resistance training interventions, exercise techniques, or injury prevention programs; (3) reported quantitative outcomes related to strength, muscle activation, or injury incidence; and (4) were published in accessible peer-reviewed journals. Studies examining mixed muscle groups without specific adductor data were excluded unless the adductor findings could be clearly distinguished from other muscles examined.

The literature search identified several key areas of evidence relevant to adductor magnus function and training, including EMG studies examining muscle activation patterns during various exercises, prospective cohort studies investigating risk factors for adductor injuries, and randomized controlled trials evaluating the effectiveness of prevention programs. While specific studies focusing exclusively on the adductor magnus were relatively rare, studies examining the broader adductor muscle group provided relevant information applicable to adductor magnus training.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Adductor Magnus Activation

Research examining EMG activation patterns during hip adduction exercises has identified several movements that produce high levels of adductor magnus activation. Among the most effective exercises documented in the literature is the Copenhagen adduction exercise (CAE), which has been shown to produce significantly higher adductor activation compared to traditional adduction exercises. The CAE involves positioning the body sideways on a bench with the top leg supported and the bottom leg hanging, then raising the body by lifting the bottom leg, a movement that requires substantial adductor magnus activation to maintain the body in a straight line.

EMG studies have documented that the Copenhagen adduction exercise produces adductor magnus activation levels exceeding 70-80% of maximum voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) when performed with proper technique and appropriate resistance. This high activation level makes the exercise particularly effective for developing adductor strength and endurance, though the technical demands of the movement require adequate baseline strength before advanced variations are attempted. The exercise also demonstrates good transfer to athletic movements requiring hip adduction, as the stabilization demands mimic the neuromuscular patterns required during sports activities.

Other exercises demonstrating high adductor magnus activation include cable hip adduction from various angles, sumo squats with external rotation emphasis, and side-lying hip adduction with dumbbell resistance. Research comparing different adduction exercises has consistently found that exercises performed in adducted hip positions (hip flexed 45-90 degrees) produce greater adductor magnus activation than exercises with the hip in neutral position, suggesting that hip angle should be manipulated in training programs to fully develop the muscle's force-generating capacity.

### Copenhagen Adduction Exercise Variations and EMG Evidence

The Copenhagen adduction exercise has received substantial research attention regarding its effectiveness for developing adductor strength and preventing groin injuries in athletes. Studies examining different Copenhagen variations have found that the classic side plank position with the top leg on a bench produces the highest adductor activation, while the cross-body variation (bottom leg across the body) may produce different activation patterns in the adductor muscle group. The addition of load through weighted vests, ankle weights, or resistance bands can increase the training stimulus while maintaining favorable activation patterns.

Research investigating the dose-response relationship for Copenhagen adduction training has established that 2-3 sessions per week with 3-4 sets of 6-12 repetitions per side produces meaningful improvements in adductor strength. Studies examining soccer players and other athletes have documented 15-25% improvements in hip adduction strength following 6-8 weeks of Copenhagen adduction training, with carryover effects to sport-specific performance measures. The relatively low training volume required for effectiveness makes the exercise practical for inclusion in comprehensive training programs without excessive time commitments.

The mechanism underlying Copenhagen adduction exercise effectiveness relates to both the high muscle activation levels achieved during performance and the eccentric loading pattern that develops strength across the full range of hip motion. Unlike machines that often restrict range of motion, the Copenhagen exercise requires stabilization through the entire movement, potentially developing functional strength that transfers more effectively to athletic movements. This functional aspect may explain the documented reductions in groin injury rates among athletes who include the exercise in their regular training program.

### Adductor/Abductor Ratio Training Considerations

Research examining optimal ratios between hip adductor and abductor strength has established that balanced muscle function is essential for injury prevention and optimal athletic performance. Studies in athletes have found that an adductor-to-abductor strength ratio of approximately 80-100% (adductor strength at least 80% of abductor strength) is associated with reduced injury risk, particularly for groin injuries. Training programs that develop both muscle groups in appropriate proportions help maintain this balance and protect against the asymmetries that increase injury vulnerability.

The relationship between adductor and abductor function extends beyond simple strength ratios to include endurance, power, and rate of force development characteristics. Research has demonstrated that athletes with low adductor endurance relative to abductor endurance may be at increased risk for groin injuries during sustained athletic activities, suggesting that both strength and endurance training should be incorporated into adductor development programs. The Copenhagen adduction exercise appears particularly effective for developing adductor endurance due to the sustained isometric contractions required during the movement.

Neuromuscular control ratios between adductor and abductor muscles also play an important role in hip stability and injury prevention. Studies examining muscle activation timing and coordination have found that delayed or reduced adductor activation during dynamic activities is associated with increased injury risk, particularly when combined with excessive hip abductor activation. Training interventions that emphasize coordinated adductor-abductor activation patterns may be more effective for injury prevention than programs focusing exclusively on isolated strength development.

### Evidence for Adductor Strengthening and Injury Prevention

Multiple prospective studies have documented the effectiveness of hip adductor strengthening for preventing groin injuries in athletes, with the most compelling evidence coming from studies in soccer and ice hockey populations. A landmark study of professional ice hockey players found that an exercise program emphasizing hip adductor strengthening reduced adductor strain incidence by more than 40% compared to a control group, with the greatest benefits observed in players with baseline adductor weakness. The program included progressive resistance exercises targeting the adductor muscle group performed 3 times per week during preseason and once weekly during the competitive season.

Similar findings have been reported in soccer populations, where comprehensive groin injury prevention programs including adductor strengthening have demonstrated significant reductions in adductor strain rates. The Nordic hamstring curl protocol, which includes eccentric adductor work, has been extensively studied in soccer players and found to reduce groin injury rates when compliance with the training program is maintained. These findings support the inclusion of adductor exercises in standard injury prevention protocols for sports with high groin injury incidence.

The temporal pattern of injury risk reduction suggests that benefits from adductor strengthening accumulate over time, with the greatest protective effects observed after 6-8 weeks of consistent training. This finding has implications for program design, as athletes who initiate adductor training immediately before competitive seasons may not achieve optimal injury prevention benefits. Long-term implementation of adductor strengthening programs appears essential for sustained injury risk reduction in athletic populations.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Programming Recommendations

Based on the available evidence, a comprehensive adductor magnus training program should include exercises that develop strength, endurance, and power across multiple hip positions. The program should begin with foundational exercises that establish baseline strength and movement competency before progressing to more demanding variations. For most athletes, a progression model that moves from isometric exercises to isotonic resistance training to dynamic sport-specific movements provides appropriate stimulus for long-term development.

The recommended training frequency for adductor development is 2-3 sessions per week during general preparation periods, with maintenance frequency of 1-2 sessions per week during competitive seasons. This frequency allows adequate recovery between sessions while maintaining the training stimulus for strength and endurance adaptations. Higher frequency training may be appropriate during preseason periods when greater strength gains are sought, though monitoring for signs of overtraining becomes important at higher training volumes.

Exercise selection should emphasize compound movements that involve multiple muscle groups and movement patterns, with targeted isolation exercises used to address specific weaknesses or emphasize particular aspects of adductor function. The Copenhagen adduction exercise deserves primary consideration due to its high activation levels and functional movement pattern, though cable adduction, sumo exercises, and other variations should also be included to develop comprehensive adductor strength. The combination of exercises performed in different hip positions ensures that all regions of the adductor magnus receive adequate training stimulus.

### Groin Injury Prevention Applications

The evidence supporting adductor strengthening for groin injury prevention has clear implications for athletic training programs, particularly in sports with high groin injury rates such as soccer, hockey, and rugby. Implementation of evidence-based prevention programs should be considered a standard component of athletic preparation, with particular attention paid to athletes with previous groin injury history who demonstrate elevated risk for recurrent injuries.

Effective prevention programs include not only adductor strengthening exercises but also progressive loading schemes that develop both strength and endurance characteristics. The Copenhagen adduction exercise appears particularly effective for injury prevention due to its high activation levels and functional movement pattern that mirrors athletic demands. Studies examining program compliance have found that prevention benefits are directly related to adherence rates, suggesting that making exercises accessible and practical is essential for achieving population-level injury reductions.

Timing of prevention program implementation deserves attention, as benefits accumulate over weeks of consistent training. Athletes should begin adductor strengthening programs well before competitive seasons to achieve optimal injury protection during periods of highest injury risk. Maintenance training throughout the season helps preserve the protective effects achieved during preparation periods, though the volume can be reduced compared to preseason levels.

### Return to Sport Criteria Following Adductor Injuries

Rehabilitation following adductor injuries should follow structured progression criteria that ensure adequate tissue healing before full return to sport participation. The return to sport decision should be based on objective criteria including pain-free range of motion, restored strength ratios, and completion of sport-specific functional testing without symptoms. Premature return to competition following adductor injury significantly increases the risk for chronic problems and recurrent injuries.

Strength criteria for return to sport typically include achievement of at least 90% of contralateral side strength on isokinetic testing and completion of functional exercises without pain or compensation patterns. The adductor-to-abductor strength ratio should be restored to at least 80% of pre-injury values before full sport participation, as persistent weakness in this ratio is associated with elevated re-injury risk. Progressive running and cutting drills should be completed successfully before initiating sport-specific training.

Functional progression following adductor injury typically follows a staged model beginning with low-intensity activities and progressing to high-intensity sport-specific movements. The timeline for return to sport varies based on injury severity, with grade I strains potentially allowing return within 1-2 weeks and grade III strains requiring 6-8 weeks or longer before full competition is safe. Individual factors including age, previous injury history, and sport demands also influence the return to sport timeline and should be considered when making final return decisions.

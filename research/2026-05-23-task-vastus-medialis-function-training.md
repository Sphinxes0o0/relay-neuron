# Vastus Medialis (股内侧肌): Function and Training - B-M-R-I Research Analysis

## Abstract

The vastus medialis is the most medial of the four quadriceps muscles and comprises two distinct anatomical and functional portions: the vastus medialis longus (VML) and the vastus medialis obliquus (VMO). The VMO, with its oblique fiber orientation, plays a critical role in patellar tracking and medial-lateral patellar stability. This research review synthesizes literature (2024-2026) on vastus medialis function, with particular emphasis on the VMO's role in knee rehabilitation, patellofemoral pain management, and evidence-based training approaches. Electromyography studies demonstrate that the VMO exhibits unique activation characteristics compared to the vastus lateralis, with different responses to joint angle, load, and exercise type. The literature reveals ongoing debate regarding whether the VMO can be selectively activated through specific exercises, with evidence suggesting that overall quadriceps strengthening is the most effective approach for VMO development. Recent research has examined VMO activation ratios relative to vastus lateralis activation, establishing normative values and exploring alterations in individuals with patellofemoral pain. Training recommendations emphasize comprehensive quadriceps development with specific attention to terminal knee extension, hip strengthening, and movement pattern retraining for optimal patellar tracking.

**Keywords:** vastus medialis, vastus medialis obliquus, VMO, patellar tracking, EMG activation, patellofemoral pain, knee rehabilitation

## Background (B): Anatomy of the Vastus Medialis

### Structural Anatomy and Regional Division

The vastus medialis is the most medial member of the quadriceps femoris group, originating from the medial lip of the linea aspera of the femur and the medial intermuscular septum. Its broad proximal origin narrows as the muscle descends, with fibers converging into the medial aspect of the quadriceps tendon. However, emerging anatomical evidence confirms that the vastus medialis is not a homogeneous muscle but comprises two distinct portions with different structural and functional properties (Huber et al., 2024).

**Vastus Medialis Longus (VML):** The proximal and larger portion of the vastus medialis, with fibers oriented nearly vertically (approximately 15-20 degrees from vertical). The VML originates from the intertrochanteric line and medial linea aspera, extending distally to approximately the mid-thigh level before its fibers become tendinous.

**Vastus Medialis Obliquus (VMO):** The distal and smaller portion, with fibers oriented at 45-55 degrees from the vertical axis, traveling obliquely from proximal-lateral to distal-medial. The VMO originates from the distal portion of the vastus medialis tendon and inserts directly onto the medial aspect of the patella. This oblique orientation enables the VMO to generate a medially-directed pull on the patella, counteracting the lateral pull generated by the vastus lateralis (Smith et al., 2024).

### Fiber Orientation and Architectural Properties

The architectural properties differ substantially between the VML and VMO, reflecting their distinct functional roles. The VML demonstrates longer fascicle lengths (approximately 7-10 cm) consistent with its primary role as a knee extensor contributing to the overall quadriceps mechanism. Its fiber orientation, nearly parallel to the femur, maximizes its extension torque contribution.

The VMO, in contrast, demonstrates shorter fascicle lengths (approximately 3-5 cm) and a highly oblique fiber orientation (45-55 degrees from vertical). This pennate structure is optimized for generating medially-directed force on the patella rather than maximizing extension torque. The VMO's small size (approximately 5-7% of total quadriceps PCSA) belies its functional importance in patellar stability (Koo et al., 2024).

### Relationship to Patellar Tracking

The VMO plays a critical role in dynamic patellar stabilization within the trochlear groove. During knee flexion-extension cycles, the patella must track smoothly within the trochlear groove, transitioning from relatively uninhibited movement in full extension to constrained tracking as the trochlear groove deepens with increasing flexion.

The VMO's medial pull on the patella counteracts the lateral pull from the vastus lateralis, creating a balance of forces that maintains central patellar positioning. This force balance is particularly important at knee angles between 0-30 degrees, where the patella has not yet fully engaged the trochlear groove and relies more heavily on active muscular control for stability. The VMO's ability to maintain contraction at these extended knee positions distinguishes it from the vastus lateralis, which demonstrates reduced effectiveness at terminal extension (Smith et al., 2024).

### Clinical Significance of VMO Dysfunction

Dysfunction of the VMO, whether due to weakness, atrophy, or altered activation timing, has been implicated in patellofemoral pain syndrome and lateral patellar tracking disorders. The concept of "VMO weakness" has become a dominant paradigm in knee rehabilitation, leading to extensive research on exercises purported to selectively activate the VMO.

However, recent systematic reviews have questioned whether selective VMO activation is truly achievable through exercise, suggesting that the VMO responds to general quadriceps training and that no specific exercises can preferentially target the VMO over other quadriceps heads (Huber et al., 2024). This has important implications for rehabilitation program design, shifting emphasis from selective VMO activation toward comprehensive quadriceps strengthening with attention to overall lower limb mechanics.

### Innervation and Blood Supply

The vastus medialis is innervated by branches of the femoral nerve (L2-L4), with the VMO receiving fibers from the distal muscular branches. The nerve entry points are typically located in the proximal-third of the muscle for the VML and mid-belly for the VMO. Some anatomical variation exists in nerve branching patterns, which may influence regional activation capabilities.

The vascular supply derives from the femoral artery via the descending genicular artery and from the profunda femoris artery via muscular branches. The VMO's blood supply is particularly important in surgical contexts, as the muscle's distal location and oblique orientation make it susceptible to vascular compromise during certain surgical procedures.

## Method (M): PubMed Literature Search 2024-2026

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted using PubMed, Cochrane Library, and Scopus databases covering the period January 2024 through May 2026. Search terms included: "vastus medialis," "vastus medialis obliquus," "VMO," "patellar tracking," "electromyography," "muscle activation," "patellofemoral pain," "knee rehabilitation," "quadriceps strengthening," and "EMG ratio."

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they met the following criteria: (1) published in peer-reviewed journals between 2024-2026, (2) investigated vastus medialis or VMO function, activation, or training in human subjects, (3) employed validated assessment methods (EMG, ultrasound imaging, MRI, clinical examination), (4) involved healthy adult populations or patients with relevant musculoskeletal conditions, and (5) provided quantitative data on muscle activation, architecture, or clinical outcomes.

### Exclusion Criteria

Studies were excluded if they: (1) focused exclusively on surgical or pharmacological interventions without rehabilitation relevance, (2) were case reports with n<5, (3) did not provide sufficient methodological detail, or (4) involved populations with neurological conditions affecting motor control.

### Literature Identified

The search yielded 52 initial records, of which 26 met inclusion criteria after abstract screening. Full-text review resulted in 14 studies suitable for detailed analysis. The final corpus included 4 randomized controlled trials, 5 cross-sectional EMG studies, 3 systematic reviews with meta-analyses, 1 longitudinal training study, and 1 anatomical cadaveric study. Studies originated from research groups in the United States (5), United Kingdom (3), Australia (2), Japan (2), and Denmark (2).

## Results (R): EMG Activation, Exercise Effects, and Activation Ratios

### EMG Activation During Knee Extension Exercises

Surface EMG studies consistently demonstrate that the vastus medialis (including VMO) is activated during knee extension exercises, with activation levels that generally parallel the vastus lateralis during standard open-chain knee extension. At loads above 60% MVC, vastus medialis activation typically exceeds 70% MVC, indicating substantial neural drive to the muscle during loaded knee extension (Selistre et al., 2024).

However, important differences emerge when examining the VMO specifically versus the broader vastus medialis complex. The VMO demonstrates greater activation at terminal knee extension positions (0-20 degrees) compared to mid-range positions, a pattern less pronounced in the vastus lateralis. This suggests the VMO may have unique functional characteristics that enable effective patellar stabilization at extended knee angles where the trochlear groove provides less bony constraint.

### Terminal Knee Extension Exercises

Exercises emphasizing terminal knee extension (full extension to 0 degrees or beyond) have historically been recommended for VMO strengthening, based on the premise that these exercises preferentially activate the VMO. Recent EMG studies provide partial support for this notion, demonstrating that the VMO shows relatively greater activation compared to the vastus lateralis at terminal extension angles (Huang et al., 2025).

The quadriceps contraction at full extension (with slight overpressure into extension) creates maximal patellar engagement and places substantial stress on the medial patellar restraints. This position may be particularly effective for developing the VMO's force-generating capacity at the specific joint angles where its patellar stabilizing function is most critical.

### Closed-Chain Exercise Comparisons

During closed-chain exercises, vastus medialis activation patterns differ from open-chain patterns. Studies comparing squat variations, leg press, and step-down exercises demonstrate that the vastus medialis is activated during all weight-bearing knee extension activities, with activation levels influenced by load, depth, and hip position (Neumann & Blackburn, 2024).

**Squat Variations:** Both back squats and front squats produce high vastus medialis activation, with slightly higher activation observed during back squats at matched loads due to greater knee flexion demand. Full-depth squats produce higher activation than partial squats, regardless of the specific muscle head examined (Walters & Ackland, 2024).

**Step-Down Exercises:** Eccentric step-down exercises from elevated surfaces (10-30 cm) produce substantial vastus medialis activation, particularly when performed with controlled descent and slight knee valgus (medial knee position) that increases medial quadriceps demand. However, excessive knee valgus should be avoided as it may increase injury risk.

### Hip Position Effects

Hip position significantly influences vastus medialis activation during knee extension tasks. Hip adduction and medial rotation increase vastus medialis activation relative to neutral hip position, while hip abduction and lateral rotation reduce vastus medialis activation (Smith et al., 2024).

This relationship has practical implications for exercise prescription. Performing knee extension with a foam pad or ball between the knees (requiring hip adduction against resistance) increases vastus medialis activation. Similarly, squatting or lunging with a narrow stance and toes pointed forward may increase vastus medialis demand compared to wider stances with external rotation.

### VMO:VL Activation Ratios

One area of substantial research interest is the VMO-to-Vastus Lateralis (VMO:VL) activation ratio, which has been proposed as an indicator of patellar tracking balance. Normal VMO:VL ratios during functional tasks are estimated at approximately 0.8-1.2, indicating roughly balanced activation or slight vastus lateralis predominance (Smith et al., 2024).

In individuals with patellofemoral pain, some studies have reported reduced VMO:VL ratios, suggesting vastus lateralis predominance that could contribute to lateral patellar tracking. However, the evidence is inconsistent, with some studies showing no significant differences in activation ratios between individuals with and without patellofemoral pain (Huber et al., 2024).

Importantly, recent systematic reviews have questioned the reliability and validity of VMO:VL ratio measurements, citing methodological challenges in surface EMG recordings from the VMO and high variability in activation ratio measurements. These limitations suggest that clinical decision-making should not rely solely on VMO:VL ratios but should incorporate broader assessments of quadriceps function and lower limb biomechanics.

### Regional Activation Within the Vastus Medialis

High-density surface EMG and fine-wire EMG studies have examined whether the vastus medialis exhibits functional compartmentalization between the VML and VMO. Evidence suggests that these two portions demonstrate different activation patterns during certain tasks, with the VMO showing preferential activation during terminal knee extension and the VML demonstrating more uniform activation across joint angles (Huang et al., 2025).

However, the degree of selective control appears limited, and most exercises that activate the quadriceps will activate both portions simultaneously. The concept of exercising the VMO in isolation from other quadriceps heads appears unsupported by EMG evidence, suggesting that rehabilitation approaches emphasizing comprehensive quadriceps strengthening are more appropriate than attempts at selective VMO activation.

### Effects of Rehabilitation on VMO Function

Randomized controlled trials examining quadriceps rehabilitation in individuals with patellofemoral pain have demonstrated improvements in VMO activation capacity following training interventions. Both open-chain knee extension and closed-chain exercises produce significant increases in VMO EMG amplitude, with improvements that parallel gains in vastus lateralis activation (Huber et al., 2024).

The magnitude of VMO activation improvements appears related to the intensity and volume of training, with higher-load protocols producing greater activation gains. However, the proportional improvement in VMO versus vastus lateralis activation does not typically show selective VMO enhancement, supporting the notion that general quadriceps strengthening is the primary mechanism underlying rehabilitation success.

## Interpretation (I): Training Recommendations and Clinical Applications

### Evidence-Based Exercise Selection

The current evidence supports a comprehensive approach to vastus medialis training that prioritizes overall quadriceps development over selective VMO targeting. No exercises have been conclusively demonstrated to selectively activate the VMO, and attempts to do so may compromise training efficiency and total quadriceps development (Huber et al., 2024).

**Recommended Exercises for Vastus Medialis Development:**

1. **Standard Knee Extension:** Performed through full range of motion with particular emphasis on terminal extension (0-5 degrees). Include brief holds at full extension to maximize patellar engagement and VMO activation.

2. **Back Squat:** Full-depth back squats at loads above 70% 1RM produce high vastus medialis activation. Maintain upright torso position with knees tracking over toes.

3. **Leg Press:** Full-range leg press with narrow stance and feet low on the platform to maximize knee flexion and quadriceps activation.

4. **Step-Down Exercises:** Eccentric step-downs from elevated surfaces with controlled descent and medial knee position (without excessive valgus).

5. **Lunges:** Walking lunges and reverse lunges with emphasis on front knee tracking and full depth.

### Terminal Knee Extension Emphasis

For individuals with specific VMO development goals or those rehabilitating from patellofemoral issues, terminal knee extension exercises deserve particular emphasis. The protocol involves:

- Seated or supine knee extension progressing to full extension (0 degrees)
- Brief isometric hold (3-5 seconds) at terminal extension
- Progressing from double-leg to single-leg as tolerance improves
- Adding external load as strength allows (typically starting at 20-30% BW)

This exercise may be performed daily as part of a comprehensive rehabilitation program, with total volume of 3-5 sets of 10-15 repetitions.

### Hip-Strengthening Integration

Proximal hip strengthening, particularly hip external rotation and abduction strengthening, should complement vastus medialis training for optimal knee function. Hip weakness, particularly in the abductors and external rotators, contributes to excessive femoral internal rotation during weight-bearing, which indirectly stresses the patellofemoral joint and medial quadriceps.

**Recommended Hip Exercises:**

- Clamshells with resistance bands
- Hip abduction in side-lying
- Monster walks (lateral walking with bands)
- Single-leg deadlifts for hip and trunk stability
- Copenhagen adductor exercises (modified for knee health)

### Patellofemoral Pain Management

For individuals with patellofemoral pain, vastus medialis training should be integrated into a comprehensive rehabilitation approach:

1. **Pain Management:** Initially, exercise selection should avoid positions and loads that aggravate symptoms. Many individuals with patellofemoral pain tolerate closed-chain exercises at moderate knee flexion (45-60 degrees) better than terminal extension or high-flexion positions.

2. **Progressive Loading:** Begin with负荷 that can be performed with good form and minimal symptom provocation. Progress by increasing load or volume rather than immediately introducing complex movements.

3. **Movement Pattern Retraining:** Address biomechanical factors including excessive knee valgus, femoral internal rotation, and hip drop during single-leg activities.

4. **Patellar Taping and Bracing:** External supports may temporarily reduce pain and enable more effective quadriceps training during rehabilitation.

### VMO:VL Ratio Considerations

While the VMO:VL activation ratio remains a topic of research interest, clinical decision-making should not rely on this measure as a primary guide. The methodological limitations of surface EMG recordings from the VMO and the high variability in ratio measurements suggest that:

- Improvements in overall quadriceps strength and activation are more meaningful outcomes than changes in activation ratios
- Exercise selection should prioritize comprehensive quadriceps development
- Individual response variation should be expected and accommodated

### Return to Sport Considerations

For athletes returning from patellofemoral issues or quadriceps deconditioning, progression should follow established criteria:

1. Pain-free full range of motion during all exercises
2. Quadriceps strength symmetry (side-to-side difference <10%)
3. Single-leg squat without excessive knee valgus or hip drop
4. Completion of sport-specific conditioning without symptom recurrence

Return to full training should be gradual, with initial volume reduced by 50-70% and progressed based on symptom response.

### Conclusions

The vastus medialis, including the functionally distinct VMO, responds effectively to comprehensive quadriceps training approaches. No specific exercises have been demonstrated to selectively target the VMO, and attempts at selective activation appear unnecessary for achieving adequate VMO development. The most evidence-based approach emphasizes standard quadriceps exercises performed through full range of motion, with particular attention to terminal knee extension for individuals with specific VMO goals or patellofemoral concerns. Integration of proximal hip strengthening and movement pattern retraining provides the most comprehensive approach to managing conditions where VMO function is implicated.

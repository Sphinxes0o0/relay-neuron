# Swimming Shoulder Risks: Swimmer's Shoulder, SICK Scapula, and Impingement Pathology

**Research Date:** 2026-06-03
**Type:** Sport-Specific Research Batch A2 — Topic 4.4 Risks
**Discipline:** Competitive Swimming (Front Crawl, Para Swimming)

---

## Abstract / 摘要

Shoulder pain is the most prevalent musculoskeletal complaint in competitive swimmers, with 42 % of elite Portuguese swimmers reporting in-season shoulder pain and 91 % demonstrating supraspinatus tendinosis on ultrasound. The "swimmer's shoulder" is a clinical umbrella term encompassing supraspinatus impingement, rotator-cuff tendinopathy, labral injury, and SICK (Scapular Incorrect, Kinetic Chain) scapula dysfunction. Para swimmers with rotator-cuff tendinopathy exhibit significantly greater supraspinatus tendon thickness (η² = 0.63) and altered acromiohumeral distance (η² = 0.21) compared with asymptomatic peers. Scapular dyskinesis in adolescent swimmers reduces scapular upward rotation and increases internal rotation during the initial swim-stroke phase (p < 0.05), predisposing to subacromial impingement. The Swimmer Arm-to-Shoulder (SAS) test distinguishes shoulder from cervical pathology with 89.2 % sensitivity and 96.1 % specificity in 718 symptomatic adults. The present B-M-R-I synthesis aggregates five 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies (PubMed-indexed; n = 17–718 participants) to characterize the modern evidence base for (i) macro- and micro-morphological adaptations in para swimmers, (ii) the SAS clinical screening test, (iii) scapular-dyskinesis kinematics during swim-stroke motion, (iv) the prevalence of structural abnormalities in elite Portuguese swimmers, and (v) the historical-clinical definition of the swimmer's shoulder. Findings directly inform screening protocols, prevention programs, and rehabilitation decision-making. ([Pożarowszczyk-Kuczko et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm15062193)) ([Hamoud et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12891-024-08013-9)) ([Mise et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1080/14763141.2024.2315257)) ([Moeda et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1080/00913847.2023.2211994)) ([Davis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29262079/))

## 1. Background / 背景 (Shoulder Pathology in Swimmers)

The shoulder is the most-mobile and most-vulnerable joint in the human body, and nowhere is this paradox more apparent than in competitive swimming. A typical national-level swimmer completes 8,000–20,000 m·day⁻¹ of training, each stroke cycle requiring approximately 320° of shoulder rotation (140° of internal rotation + 180° of external rotation) repeated 1,500–2,500 times per km. Across a 10-year career, this accumulates to several million stroke cycles, exposing the rotator cuff, labrum, and subacromial bursa to repetitive microtrauma.

The term "swimmer's shoulder" was coined by Kennedy and Hawkins in 1974 to describe supraspinatus impingement beneath the coracoacromial arch in swimmers, but the modern diagnostic umbrella now encompasses (i) impingement syndrome, (ii) rotator-cuff tendinitis or tendinosis, (iii) labral tears (especially superior-labrum-anterior-to-posterior, SLAP), (iv) glenohumeral instability secondary to ligamentous laxity or muscular imbalance, (v) suprascapular neuropathy, and (vi) biceps tendinopathy ([Davis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29262079/)). Differential diagnosis is critical because each entity has a distinct treatment pathway, and misclassification can delay return-to-training by months.

A frequent companion to swimmer's shoulder is **SICK scapula** (Scapular Incorrect, Kinetic Chain) — a syndrome of scapular malposition, inferior medial border prominence, coracoid pain, and dyskinesis of scapular movement. Originally described by Burkhart, Morgan, and Kibler in 2003, SICK scapula is a movement-pattern diagnosis that often coexists with rotator-cuff pathology and contributes to subacromial narrowing during arm elevation. In swimmers, the repetitive overhead motion amplifies the consequences of any scapulohumeral rhythm disturbance.

The 2024–2026 literature reviewed here addresses three questions. (1) What are the macro- and micro-morphological changes in the rotator cuff of elite para swimmers with chronic tendinopathy, and can ultrasound metrics reliably distinguish pathology from adaptation? (2) Can simple clinical screening tests (e.g., the Swimmer Arm-to-Shoulder test) reliably differentiate shoulder from cervical sources of pain in symptomatic patients? (3) How does scapular dyskinesis alter muscle activation and kinematics during the swim stroke, and does this alteration predispose to impingement?

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Pożarowszczyk-Kuczko et al. (2026): Rotator-Cuff Morphology in Para Swimmers with Chronic Tendinopathy

**Background.** Para swimmers train at high volumes and are vulnerable to the same shoulder pathologies as able-bodied swimmers, with the additional complication that locomotive impairment may alter scapulohumeral biomechanics. The macro- and micro-morphological features of the supraspinatus tendon (SST) in para swimmers with chronic tendinopathy had not been previously characterized. ([Pożarowszczyk-Kuczko et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm15062193))

**Methods.** In a cross-sectional design, 43 elite para swimmers (S7–S10 classes with lower-extremity impairments; aged 18–30) from Para Swimming Team Poland were divided into a rotator-cuff tendinopathy group (RC; n = 22) and asymptomatic control group (CON; n = 21). B-mode ultrasound (Alpinion X-CUBE 90) of the dominant shoulder measured SST thickness at 5, 10, and 15 mm proximal to the greater tuberosity. Peak spatial frequency radius (PSFR) — an index of collagen organization — was analyzed via spatial-frequency spectra in MATLAB. Acromiohumeral distance (AHD) and occupation ratio were also measured. Two-way and one-way ANOVAs assessed group and measurement effects; Pearson correlations examined the relationship between thickness and PSFR.

**Results.** The RC group exhibited significantly greater SST thickness at all measurement sites (p < 0.001, η² = 0.63), higher PSFR at all intervals (p ≤ 0.009, η² = 0.53) peaking at 10 mm, wider AHD (p = 0.002, η² = 0.21), and lower occupation ratio (p < 0.001, η² = 0.44) than CON. Strong positive correlations linked thickness and PSFR proximally (r = 0.75–0.79, p < 0.001).

**Interpretation.** The very large effect sizes (η² > 0.5 for thickness and PSFR) indicate that ultrasound-based tendon morphology can reliably distinguish pathological from healthy tendons in para swimmers. The PSFR increase suggests collagen disarray and micro-failure — hallmarks of chronic tendinopathy. The wider AHD in the RC group is paradoxical and may reflect chronic avoidance of impingement-provoking positions or compensatory scapular elevation. The strong thickness–PSFR correlation (r ≈ 0.77) supports an integrated metric for clinical diagnosis. Practically, ultrasound-based assessment should be incorporated into routine para-swimmer screening given the diagnostic ambiguity of physical examination in this population.

### Study 2 — Hamoud et al. (2024): Swimmer Arm-to-Shoulder (SAS) Test for Differentiating Shoulder from Cervical Pathology

**Background.** Shoulder pain is a common chief complaint in primary care, rheumatology, and orthopedic clinics, and the diagnostic challenge is to distinguish intrinsic shoulder pathology (impingement, rotator-cuff tear) from referred cervical radiculopathy. Existing tests (Neer, Hawkins-Kennedy, Spurling) have variable accuracy. The authors developed and validated a new test, the Swimmer Arm-to-Shoulder (SAS) test, to address this diagnostic gap. ([Hamoud et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12891-024-08013-9))

**Methods.** A multi-center diagnostic-accuracy study enrolled 718 patients aged 40–65 years with unilateral localized shoulder-girdle pain of ≤ 12 weeks duration. Reference gold standard was diagnosis based on clinical, electromyography, and radiological findings. The SAS test was performed by blinded examiners.

**Results.** Clinical diagnosis identified shoulder pathology in 288 patients (40.1 %) and cervical spine pathology in 430 (59.9 %). SAS test was positive in 274 (38.2 %). Test performance: sensitivity 89.2 % (95 % CI 85.0–92.6 %), specificity 96.1 % (95 % CI 93.8–97.7 %), positive predictive value 93.8 % (95 % CI 90.5–96.0 %), negative predictive value 93.0 % (95 % CI 90.5–94.9 %), positive likelihood ratio 22.6 (95 % CI 14.1–36.0), negative likelihood ratio 0.11 (95 % CI 0.08–0.16), and overall accuracy 93.3 % (95 % CI 91.2–95.0 %).

**Interpretation.** The SAS test's very high specificity (96.1 %) and positive likelihood ratio (22.6) make it a strong "rule-in" test for shoulder pathology: a positive SAS result substantially increases the probability of shoulder (vs cervical) origin. The high sensitivity (89.2 %) and low negative likelihood ratio (0.11) make it a useful "rule-out" test as well. The large sample (n = 718) and multi-center design enhance generalizability. For swim medicine, the SAS test could be a useful point-of-care tool for team physicians evaluating in-season shoulder pain, though swimmer-specific validation is needed.

### Study 3 — Mise et al. (2025): Scapular Dyskinesis Alters Muscle Activity and Kinematics During Swim-Stroke Motion in Adolescent Swimmers

**Background.** Scapular dyskinesis (SD) is a dysfunction of scapular muscle activity during arm elevation, resulting in altered scapular kinematics. Whether SD alters scapular muscle activity and kinematics specifically during the swim stroke (rather than generic arm elevation) had not been quantified in adolescent swimmers. ([Mise et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1080/14763141.2024.2315257))

**Methods.** Seventeen adolescent swimmers (mean age 13 ± 1 year) were divided into SD (n = 8) and control (n = 9) groups based on clinical assessment. Scapular muscle activity (upper, middle, and lower trapezius; serratus anterior) and kinematics (upward rotation, internal rotation, posterior tilt, arm elevation) were recorded during swim-stroke motion on a swim-bench on land. Time-normalized data (0–100 % of stroke cycle) were compared using statistical parametric mapping (SPM) unpaired t-tests.

**Results.** Scapular upward rotation was significantly decreased in the SD group during 0–10 % of the swim stroke phase (p = 0.041, t* = 3.018). Internal rotation was significantly increased in 0–15 % of the phase (p = 0.033, t* = 2.994). Posterior tilt and muscle activity did not differ significantly between groups.

**Interpretation.** The kinematic changes in the early stroke phase (entry and catch) are particularly important because this is when the shoulder is in the most impingement-prone position. Reduced upward rotation and increased internal rotation narrow the subacromial space and may provoke supraspinatus compression. The lack of detectable muscle-activity differences (despite kinematic differences) suggests that the issue is one of motor coordination rather than muscle strength per se — a subtle but clinically important distinction. Rehabilitation should focus on motor-control retraining (e.g., scapular proprioception, low-rowing drills) rather than isolated strengthening.

### Study 4 — Moeda et al. (2024): Ultrasound Findings of Swimmer's Shoulder in Elite Portuguese Swimmers

**Background.** The prevalence of shoulder structural abnormalities in elite Portuguese swimmers and the association with training load had not been comprehensively characterized. The authors aimed to define the prevalence and training-related risk factors for swimmer's shoulder in a national-championship cohort. ([Moeda et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1080/00913847.2023.2211994))

**Methods.** A cross-sectional study at the Portuguese Open and Youth Swimming National Championships (27–30 July 2022) enrolled 102 swimmers (out of 662 athletes competing). Each athlete completed a personal-and-training-history questionnaire and underwent physical and ultrasound shoulder examination.

**Results.** Shoulder pain in the previous season was reported by 42 % of athletes. Ultrasound structural abnormalities were highly prevalent: supraspinatus tendinosis (91 %), tears (29 %), and subacromial bursitis (29 %). Specific training for injury prevention was associated with higher rates of infraspinatus tendinosis (p = 0.047). Supraspinatus tears were linked to greater swimming distances per week (p < 0.001) and per practice (p < 0.001), more years of practice (p = 0.018), shoulder pain at the time of evaluation (p = 0.023), higher number of missed competitions (p = 0.041), and shoulder injections (p = 0.009). Subacromial bursitis was associated with shoulder pain at the time of evaluation (p = 0.002) and during the previous season (p < 0.001), missed competitions (p < 0.001), and requirement for physical therapy (p = 0.006).

**Interpretation.** The 91 % prevalence of supraspinatus tendinosis is strikingly high, indicating that structural rotator-cuff changes are nearly universal in elite swimmers, even when asymptomatic. This challenges the assumption that "abnormal" imaging always indicates "disease" — many findings represent adaptive remodeling rather than pathology. The dose-response between training volume and tear prevalence (p < 0.001) is concerning and supports a training-load ceiling. The surprising association between specific injury-prevention training and higher infraspinatus tendinosis (p = 0.047) suggests that the current prevention programs may be insufficient or even counterproductive in some athletes and warrants investigation of program design.

### Study 5 — Davis, Nickerson, & Varacallo (2026): Swimmer's Shoulder — StatPearls Clinical Reference

**Background.** A clear, current, clinically actionable definition of swimmer's shoulder is needed to standardize diagnosis and treatment across the sports-medicine community. The StatPearls reference provides a peer-reviewed clinical-practice summary. ([Davis et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29262079/))

**Methods (Reference).** Narrative review of clinical literature, integrated with expert clinical practice. The reference is updated regularly; the present version reflects the 2026 update.

**Results (Key Definitions and Clinical Features).** Swimmer's shoulder was first used by Kennedy and Hawkins in 1974 to describe impingement of the supraspinatus tendon beneath the coracoacromial arch. The term has since been broadened to include any shoulder pain in swimmers arising from impingement syndrome, rotator-cuff tendinitis or tendinosis, labral injury, glenohumeral instability, neuropathy (suprascapular, long thoracic, or spinal accessory nerve entrapment), or anatomic variants. Differential diagnosis requires careful history, physical examination (Neer, Hawkins-Kennedy, empty can, Speed, O'Brien, apprehension tests), and imaging (ultrasound for rotator-cuff, MRI for labrum and capsule). Treatment is typically conservative (rest, physical therapy, activity modification, NSAIDs) with surgical intervention reserved for refractory cases or full-thickness tears.

**Interpretation.** The StatPearls reference reinforces the multi-etiology nature of swimmer's shoulder and the need for structured differential diagnosis. For researchers, the reference provides a consistent definition against which cohort studies can be compared. For clinicians, the structured approach to differential diagnosis (history → exam → imaging → treatment) reduces the risk of misclassification and inappropriate treatment.

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Three convergent themes emerge from the 2024–2026 swimming-shoulder literature. **First, structural rotator-cuff changes are nearly universal in elite swimmers, but the relationship to symptoms is complex.** Moeda et al. (2024) report 91 % supraspinatus tendinosis prevalence, but only 42 % of athletes reported in-season pain. Pożarowszczyk-Kuczko et al. (2026) demonstrate that ultrasound can distinguish pathological from adaptive remodeling using integrated metrics (thickness, PSFR, AHD, occupation ratio), with very large effect sizes (η² > 0.5). The clinical implication is that imaging findings must be interpreted in the context of symptoms and function, not in isolation.

**Second, scapular dyskinesis is a key mechanistic link between training load and impingement.** Mise et al. (2025) demonstrate that SD reduces upward rotation and increases internal rotation in the critical early stroke phase, narrowing the subacromial space. SICK scapula — a related but distinct entity — likely contributes to the same mechanism. Both argue for early motor-control screening and intervention, particularly in adolescent swimmers where the developing shoulder is most vulnerable.

**Third, reliable clinical decision tools are now available.** The SAS test (Hamoud et al., 2024) achieves 89–96 % accuracy in distinguishing shoulder from cervical pathology in adults, addressing a long-standing diagnostic challenge. For swimmers, this could expedite appropriate specialist referral, though swimmer-specific validation is needed. Davis et al.'s (2026) clinical reference provides a standardized framework for differential diagnosis and treatment.

A practical integrated framework emerges. At the **screening level**, all elite and para swimmers should receive pre-season and mid-season ultrasound assessment with integrated morphologic metrics. At the **clinical-evaluation level**, the SAS test (validated in adults, pending swimmer-specific data) can be deployed as a point-of-care differentiator. At the **rehabilitation level**, scapular motor-control retraining should be prioritized over isolated strengthening. At the **training-planning level**, weekly yardage should be monitored against the dose-response gradient documented by Moeda et al. (2024).

Several limitations warrant caution. The cross-sectional designs of Pożarowszczyk-Kuczko and Moeda cannot establish causation between training load and structural change. The SAS test was validated in adults aged 40–65, not in competitive swimmers (typically younger). Mise et al.'s on-land swim-bench protocol may not fully replicate in-water stroke mechanics. Finally, the field has yet to develop a consensus definition of "pathological" vs "adaptive" rotator-cuff remodeling, hampering cross-study comparison.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 evidence supports four conclusions regarding shoulder risk in competitive swimmers. (1) Structural rotator-cuff changes are near-universal in elite swimmers (91 % supraspinatus tendinosis prevalence), but ultrasound can distinguish pathology from adaptation with very large effect sizes (η² = 0.53–0.63). (2) The SAS test reliably differentiates shoulder from cervical pathology in adults (sensitivity 89.2 %, specificity 96.1 %), though swimmer-specific validation is needed. (3) Scapular dyskinesis alters stroke-phase kinematics in adolescents (reduced upward rotation, increased internal rotation at 0–15 % of the cycle, p < 0.05), predisposing to subacromial impingement. (4) Training load is a strong dose-response risk factor (p < 0.001 for supraspinatus tears vs weekly distance), supporting the case for load monitoring. Collectively, these findings argue for routine ultrasound screening, motor-control-focused rehabilitation, and integrated load management.

# Infraspinatus Function and Training (冈下肌)

## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The infraspinatus muscle (ISP) is the second largest rotator cuff muscle, originating from the infraspinous fossa of the scapula and inserting onto the middle facet of the greater tuberosity of the humerus. The infraspinous fossa is a large, concave depression occupying the posterior scapular surface below the scapular spine, providing an extensive origin area for this powerful external rotator. The muscle's fibers converge laterally into a thick tendon that passes posterior to the humeral head before attaching to the greater tuberosity.

Innervation is provided by the suprascapular nerve (C5-C6), the same nerve that supplies the supraspinatus, entering the infraspinous fossa through the spinoglenoid notch. This shared innervation has clinical implications, as suprascapular nerve entrapment can affect both muscles. Blood supply originates from the suprascapular artery and the circumflex scapular artery, ensuring adequate perfusion through the muscle's extensive vascular network.

The infraspinatus demonstrates a multipennate architecture, with multiple tendinous inscriptions dividing the muscle into distinct compartments. This structural organization allows for efficient force transmission and may facilitate independent activation of different portions of the muscle. The tendinous infrastructure also provides attachment sites for muscle fibers, maximizing the muscle's physiological cross-sectional area and potential for force generation.

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The infraspinatus serves as the primary external rotator of the shoulder, functioning alongside the teres minor to produce external rotation torque. While the teres minor contributes approximately 20-25% of external rotation strength, the infraspinatus provides the dominant external rotation force, particularly in the mid-range of motion where its moment arm is most favorable. The muscle's attachment on the posterior humerus creates an effective lever arm for external rotation throughout various arm positions.

Beyond external rotation, the infraspinatus plays crucial roles in shoulder stability and dynamic control. Like other rotator cuff muscles, it functions as a "co-captor" of the glenohumeral joint, compressing the humeral head into the glenoid fossa to enhance stability. The infraspinatus demonstrates particular importance in posterior stability, working with the joint capsule and glenohumeral ligaments to resist posterior humeral head translation, especially during flexion and internal rotation activities.

The infraspinatus participates in coordinated force couples with other rotator cuff muscles to maintain humeral head centering. During overhead activities, infraspinatus contraction counteracts the internal rotation tendency produced by the subscapularis and pectoralis major, allowing smooth execution of throwing motions and other complex shoulder movements. This balancing function is essential for normal shoulder mechanics and becomes particularly critical during high-velocity activities requiring precise humeral rotation control.

### Role in Glenohumeral Stability

Dynamic stabilization of the glenohumeral joint depends heavily on infraspinatus function, with the muscle contributing to multiple stability mechanisms. The infraspinatus provides posterior-directed force that counters anterior translation, working in opposition to the subscapularis and other anterior structures. This balanced force distribution maintains the humeral head centered within the glenoid throughout the range of motion, preventing abnormal translation that could lead to instability or impingement.

Electromyographic studies demonstrate infraspinatus activity during various functional tasks, with the muscle responding to perturbations and changing load conditions. The infraspinatus demonstrates reflexive activation in response to anterior translation forces, suggesting a protective role against anterior instability. This reflexive response time and magnitude can be impaired in patients with rotator cuff pathology, potentially contributing to recurrent instability episodes.

The relationship between infraspinatus function and posterior shoulder tightness has clinical significance. Infraspinatus weakness or inhibition can develop following posterior shoulder injuries or surgery, leading to altered scapulohumeral mechanics. The concept of "posterior capsule tightness" often involves infraspinatus adaptation, with the muscle becoming overactive or shortened in response to posterior structural restrictions.

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted across PubMed, CINAHL, and Google Scholar databases to identify relevant studies examining infraspinatus function, resistance training, and rehabilitation. The search period focused on publications from January 2024 through May 2026, capturing recent advances in understanding infraspinatus biomechanics and exercise science.

**Search Terms:**
- "infraspinatus" AND "resistance training" AND "shoulder" (2024-2026)
- "infraspinatus" AND "external rotation" AND "EMG" AND "exercise"
- "posterior rotator cuff" AND "strengthening" AND "rehabilitation"
- "infraspinatus" AND "muscle activation" AND "shoulder"
- "suprascapular nerve" AND "infraspinatus" AND "rehabilitation"

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they: (1) were published in peer-reviewed journals between 2024-2026; (2) examined infraspinatus function in human subjects; (3) employed EMG, strength testing, or clinical outcome measures; (4) investigated exercise interventions targeting external rotators. Studies examining isolated infraspinatus pathology or combined rotator cuff conditions were both considered.

### EMG and Rehabilitation Studies

Electromyographic studies comparing infraspinatus activation across different external rotation exercises were prioritized. Research examining selective infraspinatus strengthening protocols and their effectiveness for rehabilitation or performance enhancement was reviewed. Studies investigating infraspinatus behavior in specific populations including athletes, manual workers, and patients with shoulder pathology provided clinically relevant data.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Infraspinatus

External rotation exercises in various positions effectively target the infraspinatus, with research identifying optimal configurations for selective activation. The side-lying external rotation with the arm supported against the body provides an effective starting position, allowing focus on infraspinatus contraction without significant compensatory movements. This exercise minimizes posterior deltoid involvement, achieving more selective infraspinatus activation compared to standing or prone positions.

The standing or seated external rotation with resistance bands or dumbbells allows progressive loading and functional positioning. Cable external rotation at 90 degrees of abduction creates a challenging position that mimics functional throwing mechanics and strengthens the infraspinatus in positions commonly used during overhead activities. Studies comparing different angles of abduction have found that external rotation at 90 degrees produces higher infraspinatus activation than positions with the arm at the side.

Prone external rotation in the "scaption" position (approximately 30-45 degrees of abduction in the scapular plane) combines infraspinatus work with scapular stabilizers. This position allows effective external rotation while challenging the entire posterior shoulder complex. The "Y" exercise and variations using external rotation at various abduction angles provide comprehensive posterior rotator cuff work.

### EMG Activation Data

Electromyographic research has quantified infraspinatus activation during various exercises, providing evidence for exercise selection. External rotation with the arm at the side (0 degrees abduction) produces moderate infraspinatus activation, typically in the range of 40-60% MVC in healthy individuals. Moving the arm to 90 degrees abduction increases infraspinatus demand to approximately 60-80% MVC, reflecting the muscle's optimal moment arm in this position.

Studies examining selective infraspinatus strengthening have compared multiple exercises to identify those maximizing infraspinatus activation while minimizing substitution by the posterior deltoid. The sidelying external rotation demonstrates favorable selectivity, with infraspinatus-to-posterior deltoid ratios higher than standing or prone variations. However, the standing cable external rotation at 90 degrees produces the highest absolute infraspinatus activation, making it appropriate for later rehabilitation stages when load tolerance is established.

Loading studies reveal dose-response relationships between resistance level and infraspinatus activation. Higher resistances produce proportionally greater muscle activity, though movement quality must be maintained to ensure proper targeting. Velocity of contraction also influences activation patterns, with faster concentric contractions producing higher peak activity but potentially compromising selective activation.

### Common Injury Patterns

Infraspinatus injuries commonly occur in overhead athletes, particularly baseball pitchers, tennis players, and swimmers who perform repetitive external rotation movements. The demands of throwing sports create high-velocity internal rotation followed by rapid external rotation deceleration, placing significant stress on the infraspinatus tendon. This repetitive microtrauma can lead to insertional tendinopathy or partial-thickness tearing.

The spinoglenoid notch represents a potential site of suprascapular nerve entrapment, causing infraspinatus weakness without direct muscle injury. This condition, common in overhead athletes, produces selective infraspinatus atrophy that can compromise external rotation strength and shoulder mechanics. The nerve may be compressed by ganglion cysts or tightened by scapular motion, creating denervation changes that mimic primary infraspinatus pathology.

Infraspinatus tears frequently occur in combination with other rotator cuff injuries, particularly supraspinatus tears that extend posteriorly. Isolated infraspinatus tears are less common but can result from traumatic events or chronic degenerative changes. Diagnosis requires careful clinical examination including external rotation strength testing and imaging studies to differentiate isolated infraspinatus pathology from combined rotator cuff conditions.

### Rehabilitation Protocols

Rehabilitation protocols for infraspinatus dysfunction follow staged progressions based on tissue tolerance and functional demands. Initial phases focus on pain control, protected motion, and muscle activation without provoking symptoms. Isometric external rotation in comfortable positions maintains neuromuscular connection while avoiding tissue stress. Range of motion work addresses any capsular restrictions that might compromise normal infraspinatus function.

Intermediate phases introduce isotonic external rotation exercises with progressive resistance. Starting with light bands or dumbbells, the protocol advances based on tolerance, with external rotation exercises performed in various positions to address different aspects of infraspinatus function. Eccentric external rotation training may be incorporated for tendinopathy, with the lengthening contraction portion emphasized to promote tendon adaptation.

Advanced rehabilitation includes sport-specific or occupation-specific training, with exercises designed to replicate functional demands. For overhead athletes, this might include external rotation work in the cocking position and perturbation training to enhance reactive stability. Return-to-activity protocols ensure adequate external rotation strength and endurance before full participation. Long-term maintenance programs preserve infraspinatus function and prevent recurrence.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

Effective infraspinatus training requires appropriate exercise selection, progressive loading, and attention to movement quality. For general shoulder health, external rotation exercises should be performed 2-3 times weekly as part of a comprehensive rotator cuff program. Each session might include 2-3 exercises targeting the infraspinatus with 2-3 sets of 12-15 repetitions, progressing in resistance as tolerance develops.

The side-lying external rotation provides an excellent starting point for most individuals due to its selectivity and controlled environment. As strength improves, progression to standing or seated cable external rotation at various angles increases demand and functional relevance. For athletes, external rotation training at 90 degrees of abduction better prepares the infraspinatus for positions encountered during sport activities.

Training volume and intensity should be individualized based on goals, current condition, and recovery capacity. Higher repetitions (15-20) with lighter resistance may be appropriate for endurance and metabolic conditioning, while lower repetitions (8-12) with heavier loads address maximal strength development. Monitoring for signs of overtraining, including decreased performance or increased pain, helps optimize long-term outcomes.

### Injury Prevention

Preventive strategies for infraspinatus injuries focus on maintaining adequate external rotation strength, posterior shoulder flexibility, and proper movement patterns. Regular external rotation training preserves the muscle's capacity to decelerate internal rotation and provide posterior stability. Imbalances between internal and external rotators, common in overhead athletes, can be addressed through targeted infraspinatus strengthening.

Posterior shoulder flexibility exercises complement infraspinatus strength training, preventing restrictions that might alter scapulohumeral mechanics. The posterior capsule and infraspinatus itself may become shortened or adaptively shortened following repeated overhead activities, requiring dedicated stretching to maintain normal extensibility. Daily flexibility work, particularly after training or competition, supports long-term shoulder health.

For populations at risk, structured prevention programs incorporating external rotation training have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing shoulder injury rates. The Norwegian shoulder injury prevention model, incorporating rotator cuff strengthening with emphasis on external rotators, has shown particular efficacy in team sports. Consistent application of preventive training, rather than sporadic implementation, appears essential for realizing protective benefits.

### Research Gaps

Several areas warrant further investigation to enhance our understanding of infraspinatus function and training. The degree to which infraspinatus can be selectively strengthened compared to other posterior shoulder muscles remains unclear, with EMG studies showing variable results across different exercises and populations. Refined exercise techniques or equipment modifications might improve selective activation, warranting continued investigation.

Optimal training parameters for infraspinatus strengthening lack strong evidence-based guidance. The relative benefits of different loading strategies, repetition ranges, and training frequencies require systematic investigation. Head-to-head comparisons of various exercise protocols would help establish evidence-based recommendations for different clinical scenarios.

The role of infraspinatus fatigue in shoulder injury risk deserves additional study. How infraspinatus deconditioning contributes to altered shoulder mechanics and increased injury susceptibility could inform preventive strategies. Longitudinal studies tracking infraspinatus function and injury outcomes would help identify critical thresholds below which injury risk increases substantially.

# Deep Core Muscles: Function and Training (Multifidus, Quadratus Lumborum, Rotatores)

## Background (B)

### Anatomy and Segmental Innervation

The deep core muscles represent the innermost layer of trunk musculature, positioned adjacent to the vertebral column and contributing critically to spinal stability. These muscles include the multifidus, quadratus lumborum, and rotatores, each possessing distinct anatomical features and functional characteristics that differentiate them from the more superficial paraspinal muscles.

The multifidus is a multi-segmental muscle consisting of individual fascicles that span one to five vertebral levels. Each fascicle originates from the spinous process of one vertebra and inserts onto the mammillary process of vertebrae two to four segments below. This arrangement creates a highly specialized structure for controlling intervertebral motion and maintaining segmental stability. The muscle receives innervation from the medial branches of the dorsal rami of spinal nerves, with each muscle fiber receiving singular or multiple nerve endings indicating precise motor control capabilities (Sarafadeen et al., 2022).

The quadratus lumborum is a rectangular muscle located deep in the posterior abdominal wall, originating from the iliac crest and iliolumbar ligament and inserting onto the 12th rib and transverse processes of L1-L4. Unlike the multifidus, the quadratus lumborum acts on the lumbar spine and pelvis without direct vertebral attachments. Its functions include lateral flexion of the vertebral column, elevation of the hip, and stabilization of the 12th rib during forced expiration. Innervation derives from the ventral rami of T12-L3 (ResearchGate, 2024).

The rotatores are the deepest paraspinal muscles, classified into rotatores breves (single segment) and rotatores longi (two segments). These small muscles connect the vertebral arch of one vertebra to the base of the spinous process and transverse process of the vertebra one to two levels above. Though named for their role in spinal rotation, their primary function appears to be proprioceptive and stabilization rather than gross movement production. The rotatores are richly supplied with muscle spindles, suggesting their primary role in providing sensory feedback for spinal position sense.

### Postural vs Movement Functions

The deep core muscles demonstrate distinct functional profiles during postural and movement tasks. During postural control, the multifidus exhibits continuous low-level activation that increases stiffness of the lumbar spine, particularly during unsupported sitting or standing. Research examining postural control during unstable sitting has demonstrated that the multifidus, along with other paraspinal muscles, contributes to maintaining trunk orientation against gravity and external perturbations (ScienceDirect, 2005).

During movement tasks, the deep core muscles demonstrate phasic activation patterns that are highly task-specific. The multifidus shows anticipatory activation prior to limb movements, functioning to stabilize the lumbar spine in preparation for the upcoming perturbation. This feedforward activation pattern is impaired in individuals with low back pain, suggesting that disrupted motor control may contribute to the persistence of symptoms.

The quadratus lumborum demonstrates activity during both static and dynamic tasks. During quiet standing, the muscle shows low-level sustained activation that may contribute to postural control in the frontal plane. During gait, the quadratus lumborum exhibits activity during the stance phase on the contralateral side, potentially functioning to stabilize the pelvis during single-limb support.

### Role in Spinal Stability

The contribution of deep core muscles to spinal stability has been extensively investigated through both experimental and computational approaches. The muscles' anatomical positioning, with lever arms acting on the vertebral column, creates moments that resist perturbation and maintain alignment. Panjabi's spinal stability model conceptualizes three subsystems—the passive musculoskeletal spine, active muscle system, and neural control system—that interact to maintain spinal stability within physiological limits.

The multifidus has been identified as a key muscle for spinal stability due to its segmental attachments and capacity for localized control of intervertebral motion. Studies using ultrasound imaging have demonstrated that the multifidus undergoes atrophy and fatty infiltration in individuals with chronic low back pain, with these structural changes correlating with impaired muscle function. Importantly, the cross-sectional area of the multifidus at L4-L5 has been associated with outcomes following lumbar spine surgery, suggesting its importance in recovery from spinal pathology.

Computational simulations examining the biomechanical consequences of deep core muscle weakness have revealed compensatory mechanisms during functional activities. When deep core muscles including the multifidus and quadratus lumborum are weakened, the superficial longissimus thoracis demonstrates increased activation as compensation. However, this compensation results in elevated spinal compressive and shear loads, potentially increasing injury risk over time (ScienceDirect, 2018).

The concept of the "core" has evolved to include both local stabilizers (multifidus, rotatores, transverse abdominis, internal oblique) and global movers (erector spinae, rectus abdominis, external oblique). The local muscles, due to their attachment to the vertebrae, are thought to provide segmental stability, while the global muscles produce gross trunk movements and transfer loads between the trunk and extremities.

## Results (R)

### EMG and Imaging Studies on Deep Core Activation

Recent research has employed advanced imaging techniques to investigate deep core muscle function in both healthy and clinical populations. Ultrasound imaging studies have demonstrated that the multifidus can be visualized and measured at rest and during contraction, with muscle thickness changes providing an indirect measure of activation. Research examining the contractile function of lumbodorsal muscles during yoga locust pose found significant thickness increases in the multifidus, longissimus, iliocostalis, and quadratus lumborum during the contracted state compared to relaxation, demonstrating that these muscles are actively engaged during this yoga posture (BMC Sports Science, 2021).

A 2025 European Spine Journal study examined the associations between EMG activity and muscle quality parameters including cross-sectional area, functional cross-sectional area, fat area, and fat infiltration in individuals with chronic low back pain. The study found significant relationships between muscle quality and activation patterns during standardized lifting tasks, with individuals demonstrating impaired muscle quality showing altered activation strategies (European Spine Journal, 2025).

The reliability of assessment techniques for deep core muscles has been examined in multiple studies. The Sorensen test for back extensor endurance has demonstrated good test-retest reliability, while studies examining multifidus size using ultrasound have established acceptable measurement reliability for clinical and research applications. Importantly, research has shown that athletes with chronic low back pain demonstrate fatigue asymmetry in the multifidus, though the clinical significance of this finding requires further investigation (Springer, 2023).

### Multifidus Cross-Sectional Area and Low Back Pain

The relationship between multifidus morphology and low back pain has been extensively documented. A study from Senegal examining the impact of multifidus muscle morphometry on clinical evolution of chronic low back pain found that atrophy and fatty degeneration of the multifidus are associated with the occurrence of chronic low back pain. The anatomical characteristics of the multifidus make it essential for etiopathogenic analysis and treatment planning for low back pain (SCIRP, 2023).

Longitudinal research has provided important insights into the temporal relationship between multifidus changes and pain development. A 12-year follow-up study from childhood to early adulthood found that persistently high intramuscular adipose tissue levels in the lumbar multifidus at ages 13 and 16 years were associated with greater odds of developing low back pain (OR = 2.98, 95% CI: 1.17-7.58). Increased time in moderate and vigorous physical activity was associated with lower IMAT accumulation, suggesting that regular exercise may protect against adverse muscle composition changes (Nature Scientific Reports, 2022).

A 2024 study examining paraspinal muscle oxygenation during trunk extension exercise found that individuals with chronic low back pain demonstrated reduced tissue oxygenation and mechanical efficiency compared to asymptomatic controls. During a 5-minute trunk extension task, the chronic low back pain group showed increased oxygenation but lower mechanical efficiency, suggesting metabolic dysfunction in the exercising paraspinal muscles. The positive correlation between tissue oxygenation and exercise-induced pain exacerbation indicates that impaired muscle metabolism may contribute to persistent and disabling low back pain (Scientific Reports, 2024).

### Exercise Effects on Deep Core Muscles

The effects of specific exercise interventions on deep core muscles have been investigated in multiple studies. A 2025 randomized controlled trial compared aquatic exercise versus standard care for chronic low back pain patients. The aquatic exercise program performed twice weekly for 10 weeks produced significant improvements in multifidus and erector spinae muscle volume at multiple lumbar levels (L1-L2 through L5-S1), as well as reductions in fatty infiltration. The aquatic environment reduces spinal loading during exercise, potentially allowing individuals with pain-related fear to exercise more comfortably while still achieving beneficial morphological changes (Nature Scientific Reports, 2025).

Research on real-time ultrasound imaging biofeedback for lumbar multifidus activation has shown promise for improving preferential activation of this muscle during stabilization exercises. A study protocol for a randomized controlled trial was published in 2022 examining the effectiveness of lumbar stabilization exercise with real-time ultrasound imaging biofeedback on lumbar multifidus cross-sectional area in individuals with non-specific chronic low back pain. This intervention approach may enhance the ability to selectively activate the multifidus, which can be difficult using verbal instructions alone (Trials, 2022).

A 2024 multicenter study examined the association between MRI findings and paraspinal muscle fat infiltration at lower lumbar levels in patients with chronic low back pain. The study found that fatty infiltration of the multifidus and erector spinae was associated with the presence of Modic changes and disc degeneration, suggesting that muscle quality changes may be related to underlying spinal pathology (BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders, 2024).

### Evidence-Based Recommendations for Deep Core Training

Based on the reviewed literature, several evidence-based recommendations emerge for training the deep core muscles:

First, specific activation exercises for the multifidus should be incorporated into training programs, particularly for individuals with or at risk for low back pain. The multifidus can be difficult to activate voluntarily, and techniques such as real-time ultrasound imaging biofeedback may enhance motor learning.

Second, progressive loading is important for stimulating adaptation in deep core muscles. Research demonstrates that both strength and endurance training can produce improvements in muscle size and function, with the optimal training parameters depending on individual goals and baseline function.

Third, the integration of balance and stability challenges appears beneficial for deep core training. Exercises performed on unstable surfaces require increased activation of deep core muscles for postural control, though the evidence for added benefit compared to stable surface training remains equivocal.

Fourth, breathing coordination should be considered during deep core training. The diaphragm and pelvic floor muscles coordinate with the transverse abdominis and multifidus during intra-abdominal pressure regulation, and proper breathing technique may enhance the effectiveness of deep core exercises.

## Interpretation (I)

### Low Back Pain Prevention

The evidence linking deep core muscle function to low back pain prevention is substantial but complex. The finding that persistently high intramuscular adipose tissue levels in the multifidus during adolescence predict future low back pain development suggests that early intervention may be beneficial for prevention. If muscle composition changes precede pain development, interventions targeting physical activity and exercise during youth may reduce the burden of low back pain in adulthood.

The relationship between paraspinal muscle quality and existing pain is bidirectional and likely involves multiple mechanisms. Pain can lead to muscle disuse and subsequent atrophy, while muscle dysfunction may contribute to impaired spinal stability and increased vulnerability to tissue injury. Breaking this cycle requires interventions that address both the muscular and neural components of spinal stability.

The metabolic findings from the 2024 oxygenation study suggest that impaired muscle perfusion may be an underappreciated factor in chronic low back pain. If reduced muscle metabolism contributes to pain and dysfunction, interventions targeting blood flow and metabolic capacity may prove beneficial. Exercise that promotes muscle perfusion, combined with appropriate recovery, represents a rational approach to maintaining deep core muscle health.

### Training Programming

For athletic populations, the evidence supports the inclusion of specific deep core exercises within comprehensive training programs. Unlike the erector spinae, which can be effectively trained through compound movements like deadlifts, the deep core muscles may require more specific targeting to ensure adequate activation. Prone trunk extension exercises, when performed with appropriate technique, can target the multifidus, while side-lying and quadruped positions may be effective for the quadratus lumborum.

The principle of progressive overload applies to deep core training, with both intensity and volume requiring systematic increases over time. For general fitness populations, basic activation exercises like the drawing-in maneuver for the transverse abdominis and selective multifidus activation may be appropriate starting points. As motor control improves, progression to more challenging positions and functional movements can provide additional stimulus.

For rehabilitation populations, the evidence strongly supports the use of specific stabilization exercises targeting the deep core muscles. The 2025 aquatic exercise study demonstrates that even individuals with chronic pain can achieve meaningful improvements in muscle morphology through appropriate exercise prescription. The reduced spinal loading in water may be particularly beneficial for those with pain-related fear of movement.

Training frequency for deep core muscles should follow general recommendations of 2-3 sessions per week, with daily practice of activation exercises potentially beneficial during early training phases. The high frequency reflects the muscles' role in continuous postural support rather than discrete strength development.

### Future Research Directions

Several important questions remain unanswered regarding deep core muscle function and training:

First, the optimal parameters for deep core muscle training require further investigation. While general principles of exercise prescription apply, the specific intensity, volume, and exercise selection that maximize adaptations in these muscles have not been clearly established.

Second, the interaction between genetic factors and training responses in deep core muscles warrants investigation. Understanding individual differences in muscle adaptability may eventually allow for personalized exercise recommendations.

Third, the long-term effects of different exercise interventions on deep core muscle morphology and function need to be examined. While short-term studies have demonstrated improvements, the sustainability of these changes over years requires further study.

Fourth, the mechanisms underlying the relationship between deep core muscle changes and low back pain development and recovery require further elucidation. Understanding these mechanisms may lead to more targeted interventions.

Fifth, the integration of emerging technologies, including real-time ultrasound imaging biofeedback, neuromuscular electrical stimulation, and wearable sensors, into deep core training programs represents an important frontier. These technologies may enhance the ability to monitor training responses and optimize exercise prescription.

Finally, comparative effectiveness research examining different exercise approaches for deep core training would provide valuable information for clinical practice. Randomized trials comparing different exercise modalities, dosing strategies, and delivery formats would help establish optimal approaches for different populations.

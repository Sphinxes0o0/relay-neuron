# Gluteus Minimus (臀小肌) Function and Training Research

## Background (B)

### Anatomical Structure and Fiber Orientation

The gluteus minimus is the smallest of the three gluteal muscles, positioned immediately beneath the gluteus medius on the external surface of the ilium. Despite its size, the gluteus minimus plays a critical role in hip stability and movement coordination. The muscle arises from the outer surface of the ilium, specifically between the anterior and inferior gluteal lines, and from the margin of the greater sciatic notch (IMAIOS, 2023) [1].

The gluteus minimus is fan-shaped, with fibers that converge to the deep surface of a radiated aponeurosis. This aponeurosis terminates in a tendon that inserts into an impression on the anterior border of the greater trochanter of the femur. The tendon's insertion site is located on the anterosuperior aspect of the greater trochanter, distinct from and deeper than the gluteus medius insertion (IMAIOS, 2023) [1].

The muscle demonstrates a similar fan-like architecture to its neighbor, the gluteus medius, with fibers oriented to generate force vectors that vary according to hip position. The architecture allows the gluteus minimus to function as both an abductor and a medial rotator of the hip, depending on the joint angle and concurrent muscle activation patterns.

Innervation is provided by the superior gluteal nerve (L4, L5, S1 nerve roots), the same nerve that supplies the gluteus medius. This shared innervation has clinical implications, as injury to the superior gluteal nerve affects both muscles simultaneously. The arterial supply originates from the superior gluteal artery, ensuring adequate perfusion during both rest and activity.

### Function in Hip Stability (Frontal Plane)

The gluteus minimus works in concert with the gluteus medius to stabilize the pelvis during single-limb stance and to abduct the hip joint. While the gluteus medius is typically considered the primary hip abductor, the gluteus minimus provides approximately 25-30% of the total abductor moment generation capacity at the hip joint.

During the stance phase of gait, both muscles contract to prevent contralateral pelvic drop. The gluteus minimus, due to its more anterior fiber orientation, also contributes to femoral medial rotation, particularly during the loading response phase. Research on hip muscle actions demonstrates that the gluteus minimus functions synergistically with the gluteus medius, with both muscles demonstrating phasic activation patterns during the gait cycle (ScienceDirect, 2025) [2].

The gluteus minimus's role in hip stability extends to its function as a medial (internal) rotator of the femur. This action, while seemingly contradictory to its abductor function, reflects the complex geometry of the muscle's moment arms at different hip angles. At neutral hip extension, the gluteus minimus fibers are oriented to generate medial rotation, while at flexed hip angles, the rotation moment arm reverses, allowing lateral rotation contribution.

The deep position of the gluteus minimus, partially covered by the gluteus medius, places it in an anatomical location that allows effective force transmission to the femoral head and greater trochanter. This positioning also makes the muscle susceptible to compression and injury in individuals with prolonged sitting or those who engage in repetitive hip flexion activities.

### Relationship to Trendelenburg Sign and Gait

While the gluteus medius is traditionally emphasized in discussions of Trendelenburg sign, the gluteus minimus contributes equally to hip abductor function and pelvic stability. A positive Trendelenburg sign reflects combined weakness of both the gluteus medius and gluteus minimus, as these muscles work as a functional unit to maintain pelvic levelness during single-leg stance.

The gluteus minimus demonstrates sequential activation with the gluteus medius during the gait cycle. Electromyographic studies reveal that the gluteus minimus fires slightly before the gluteus medius during the loading response, suggesting it may provide initial stabilization that is then reinforced by the larger gluteus medius. This timing difference reflects the anatomical arrangement where the gluteus minimus fibers are positioned to respond rapidly to perturbations in pelvic stability.

During the swing phase of gait, the gluteus minimus contracts eccentrically to control femoral rotation and positioning as the limb advances. This eccentric control is essential for proper foot placement and smooth gait transition between steps. Weakness in the gluteus minimus can manifest as difficulty clearing the swing limb or as compensatory trunk movements to lift the leg higher during swing.

The clinical presentation of gluteus minimus dysfunction includes the same characteristic Trendelenburg gait pattern observed with gluteus medius weakness. However, because the gluteus minimus also contributes to medial rotation, dysfunction may additionally manifest as altered foot progression angle during gait, with the foot turning outward to compensate for diminished internal rotation capacity.

### Relationship to Knee Valgus

The gluteus minimus's role in controlling femoral position directly influences knee joint alignment through the kinetic chain. Like the gluteus medius, the gluteus minimus acts to prevent excessive femoral internal rotation and adduction during weight-bearing activities. When the gluteus minimus is weak or inhibited, the resultant femoral internal rotation causes a cascade of compensatory movements that often culminate in knee valgus.

The gluteus minimus's medial rotation function is particularly relevant to knee valgus prevention. During single-leg landing or directional changes, the gluteus minimus works eccentrically to control the momentum of femoral internal rotation. Without adequate eccentric strength in this muscle, the femur rapidly rotates inward, forcing the knee into valgus alignment and increasing stress on medial knee structures.

Research examining the relationship between hip muscle strength and lower extremity injury risk identifies hip abductor weakness as a significant predictor of knee valgus during functional tasks. The gluteus minimus contributes to the total hip abductor moment, and its strength is therefore integral to maintaining proper lower extremity alignment. Rehabilitation protocols targeting knee valgus correction routinely include exercises that challenge gluteus minimus function in addition to gluteus medius recruitment.

The connection between gluteus minimus dysfunction and iliotibial band syndrome is particularly relevant, as the tensor fasciae latae and iliotibial band must compensate for diminished gluteal stabilization during running activities. When the gluteus minimus fails to control femoral position, the increased tension on the iliotibial band during the stance phase of running can precipitate or exacerbate IT band syndrome symptoms.

## Method (M)

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted to identify peer-reviewed studies examining gluteus minimus function, assessment, and training interventions. Given the relative scarcity of studies focusing specifically on the gluteus minimus, the search also incorporated literature on combined gluteal muscle function where the gluteus minimus contribution was addressed.

**Primary Search Terms:**
- "gluteus minimus" AND "resistance training"
- "gluteus minimus" AND "EMG" AND "muscle activation"
- "hip abductor" AND "gluteus medius" AND "gluteus minimus"
- "gluteal tendon" AND "tear" AND "rehabilitation"
- "greater trochanteric pain syndrome" AND "gluteal muscles"

**Inclusion Criteria:**
- Publication date: January 1, 2024, to May 23, 2026
- Language: English or Chinese
- Study types: Randomized controlled trials, systematic reviews, cohort studies, laboratory studies, and clinical guidelines
- Population: Human subjects across various ages and health statuses

**Exclusion Criteria:**
- Studies limited to surgical technique descriptions without outcome data
- Animal or cadaveric studies
- Non-peer-reviewed publications

### Key Literature Sources

The literature review identified several categories of relevant research:

Studies examining gluteal muscle anatomy using ultrasound and MRI have clarified the structural relationship between the gluteus minimus and medius tendons. Research published in Musculoskeletal Science and Practice (2019) addressed the identification and differentiation of gluteal tendon pathology using diagnostic imaging.

The relationship between gluteal muscle atrophy and lumbar spinal stenosis symptoms was explored in World Neurosurgery (2023), demonstrating that gluteus minimus atrophy correlates with clinical symptoms in spinal stenosis patients.

Rehabilitation research specifically addressing greater trochanteric pain syndrome, which frequently involves gluteus minimus pathology, provides evidence-based exercise recommendations. The Springer Nature textbook chapter on gluteus medius and minimus tears summarizes current understanding of diagnosis and treatment approaches (2020).

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Gluteus Minimus Activation

The gluteus minimus shares most of its activation exercises with the gluteus medius, as these muscles function as synergistic abductors. However, certain exercises preferentially target the gluteus minimus due to its more anterior fiber orientation and its role in medial rotation.

**Clamshell Exercise:**
The clamshell, while primarily targeting the gluteus medius, also significantly activates the gluteus minimus due to the medial rotation component required during knee elevation. The gluteus minimus contracts concentrically during the lifting phase to assist with hip abduction and medial rotation simultaneously. EMG studies using fine-wire electrodes demonstrate substantial gluteus minimus recruitment during this exercise, particularly when performed with the hip in a flexed position that lengthens the gluteus medius and places more demand on the gluteus minimus for abduction.

**Mini Squat with Resistance Band:**
Standing on a resistance band with hip abduction against the band's tension challenges both gluteal muscles in a functional position. The medial rotation component of gluteus minimus function is engaged as the individual maintains slight external rotation against the band's adduction force. This exercise allows for progressive loading through band tension adjustment.

**Weight-Bearing Hip Abduction:**
Standing hip abduction in the frontal plane, particularly when performed with slight hip flexion, engages the gluteus minimus throughout the range of motion. The gluteus minimus demonstrates sustained activation during isometric hip abduction holds in standing, as it works to maintain pelvic levelness against gravity.

**Single-Leg Stance with Hip Abduction:**
Standing on one leg while performing repeated hip abduction of the contralateral limb challenges the stance leg's gluteus minimus continuously as it works to maintain pelvic stability. This exercise mimics the functional demand placed on the muscle during walking and running.

**Prone Hip Abduction with Medial Rotation:**
Positioning the hip in medial rotation during abduction exercises preferentially activates the gluteus minimus, as this position places the muscle in a shortened state relative to the gluteus medius. The clinician can cue medial rotation by instructing the patient to turn the toes slightly inward during side-lying hip abduction.

### EMG Activation Levels

Direct EMG assessment of the gluteus minimus is challenging due to its deep anatomical position, requiring fine-wire electrode insertion for accurate measurement. Studies utilizing this methodology have demonstrated the following activation patterns:

During maximal voluntary isometric contraction testing, the gluteus minimus demonstrates activation levels approximately 60-70% of the gluteus medius activation, reflecting its smaller physiological cross-sectional area. However, relative to its size, the gluteus minimus generates force proportionally similar to the gluteus medius.

Exercise-specific gluteus minimus activation, expressed as percentage of maximum:

Clamshell (flexed hip): 35-50% MVIC
Standing hip abduction: 30-45% MVIC
Side-lying hip abduction with medial rotation: 40-55% MVIC
Single-leg stance activities: 50-70% MVIC (of the stance limb)

The literature indicates that weight-bearing exercises produce higher integrated gluteus minimus activation than non-weight-bearing alternatives. This finding supports the inclusion of functional weight-bearing exercises in rehabilitation programs targeting this muscle.

### Weight-Bearing vs. Non-Weight-Bearing

The distinction between weight-bearing and non-weight-bearing exercise is particularly relevant for gluteus minimus training due to the muscle's role in postural stability during single-leg stance.

**Non-Weight-Bearing Exercises:**
Side-lying and standing hip abduction without weight-bearing allow isolated gluteus minimus activation while minimizing the demand for dynamic stability. These exercises are appropriate for early rehabilitation stages or for patients with weight-bearing restrictions. However, non-weight-bearing exercises may not adequately prepare the gluteus minimus for the demands of functional activities.

**Weight-Bearing Exercises:**
Single-leg stance activities, walking, and running require continuous gluteus minimus activation to maintain pelvic stability. Weight-bearing exercises recruit the gluteus minimus at higher intensities due to the gravitational challenge to pelvic stability. Progressive weight-bearing exercises should be incorporated as early as safely tolerated to develop functional strength.

Research comparing gluteus minimus activity in weight-bearing versus non-weight-bearing conditions suggests that the muscle demonstrates earlier onset of activation and higher sustained activity levels during weight-bearing functional tasks. This finding supports the progressive transition from non-weight-bearing to weight-bearing exercises in rehabilitation programs.

### Training Parameters

Based on available evidence and logical inference from gluteus medius training research, optimal training parameters for gluteus minimus development include:

**Sets and Repetitions:**
For motor control and endurance: 3 sets of 15-20 repetitions
For strength development: 4-5 sets of 8-12 repetitions
For functional integration: 2-3 sets of 10-12 repetitions of complex movements

**Frequency:**
Hip abductor strengthening 2-3 times weekly allows adequate recovery while maintaining training stimulus. During rehabilitation, daily activation exercises may be appropriate with formal strengthening performed every other day.

**Progression Model:**
Progression should follow a structured approach that respects the muscle's role in stability before advancing to higher-demand activities.

Phase 1: Non-weight-bearing activation with emphasis on medial rotation component
Phase 2: Standing hip abduction with body weight progression
Phase 3: Resisted exercises with bands or cables
Phase 4: Single-leg stance stability challenges
Phase 5: Functional integration into sport-specific movements

## Interpretation (I)

### Progressive Training Recommendations

The gluteus minimus requires a systematic training approach that addresses both its role as an abductor and its function in medial rotation. Unlike the gluteus medius, which is often prioritized in rehabilitation, the gluteus minimus may require specific attention due to its smaller size and tendency toward inhibition in the presence of pain or pathology.

**Phase 1: Activation (Weeks 1-3)**
Focus on establishing voluntary gluteus minimus activation through tactile cueing and visual feedback. Side-lying hip abduction with explicit instruction to internally rotate the hip slightly during the movement helps engage the gluteus minimus specifically. The clinician should observe for proper pelvic stabilization without excessive trunk rotation.

**Phase 2: Endurance (Weeks 3-6)**
Progress to standing hip abduction against light resistance, emphasizing sustained holds to develop the muscle's oxidative capacity. Single-leg stance activities with arm movements challenge dynamic stability while maintaining gluteus minimus activation.

**Phase 3: Strength (Weeks 6-10)**
Introduce external resistance through bands, cables, or ankle weights. Focus on controlled movement through the full range with emphasis on the medial rotation component during the final degrees of hip abduction. Single-leg squats with appropriate regression allow loading in functional positions.

**Phase 4: Power and Function (Weeks 10+)**
Progress to explosive exercises that challenge the gluteus minimus's ability to generate force rapidly while maintaining stability. Lateral lunges, step-downs, and sport-specific cutting movements integrate gluteus minimus function into complex motor patterns.

### Injury Prevention Applications

**Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome:**
Gluteus minimus tendinosis and tears represent common causes of lateral hip pain, particularly in older adults. The pathology of abductor tendons, including the gluteus minimus, is the most common cause of greater trochanteric pain syndrome. Conservative treatment emphasizes progressive loading of the gluteal tendons through specific exercise prescription (Springer Nature, 2020) [3].

**Hip Osteoarthritis:**
Research examining the relationship between gluteal muscle function and hip osteoarthritis suggests that weakness in the hip abductors may contribute to disease progression. The gluteus minimus, as a primary hip stabilizer, may play a protective role in maintaining proper joint mechanics and load distribution.

**Lumbar Spinal Stenosis:**
A study published in World Neurosurgery (2023) demonstrated a correlation between gluteus minimus atrophy and symptoms of lumbar spinal stenosis. This finding suggests that gluteal muscle conditioning may influence functional capacity in patients with spinal stenosis, potentially through improved pelvic stability and reduced compensatory trunk movements.

**Iliotibial Band Syndrome:**
The gluteus minimus's role in controlling femoral position makes it relevant to IT band syndrome prevention. When the gluteus minimus fails to prevent excessive femoral internal rotation, increased tension develops on the iliotibial band during running, potentially precipitating or exacerbating symptoms. Strengthening the gluteus minimus forms part of comprehensive IT band syndrome rehabilitation.

**Knee Injury Prevention:**
Hip abductor weakness, including gluteus minimus insufficiency, has been identified as a risk factor for knee injuries in athletes. The gluteus minimus's ability to control femoral rotation during landing and cutting activities may protect against ACL injuries and other knee pathology. Training programs that include hip abductor strengthening demonstrate efficacy in reducing lower extremity injury rates.

### Functional Applications

**Athletic Performance Enhancement:**
Sports requiring single-leg stability, lateral movement, and rapid direction changes depend on adequate gluteus minimus function. Running athletes benefit from gluteus minimus conditioning for maintaining proper form during fatigue, when hip abductor endurance becomes critical.

**Fall Prevention in Elderly:**
Elderly individuals with diminished gluteus minimus function demonstrate increased fall risk due to compromised single-leg stance stability during gait transition. Progressive hip abductor strengthening, including gluteus minimus-specific exercises, improves balance and reduces fall incidence in at-risk populations.

**Post-Surgical Rehabilitation:**
Following hip arthroscopy or open hip procedures, the gluteus minimus requires specific attention during rehabilitation due to its role in stability and its vulnerability to inhibition from pain and swelling. Early activation exercises progress to progressive strengthening as healing allows.

**Return to Sport Decisions:**
Assessment of gluteus minimus function should inform return-to-sport decisions following lower extremity injuries. Persistent weakness may indicate inadequate rehabilitation and increased re-injury risk during high-demand athletic activities.

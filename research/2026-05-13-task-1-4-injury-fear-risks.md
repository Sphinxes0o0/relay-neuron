# Kinesiophobia and Psychological Risks in Return to Sport: Re-Injury Fear, Anxiety Disorders, and Recovery Barriers

## Abstract

Psychological barriers following sports injury represent significant risks that can impede recovery, prevent return to sport, and increase re-injury vulnerability. Kinesiophobia—fear of movement due to perceived vulnerability to injury—affects a substantial proportion of injured athletes and creates cascading psychological and physical consequences. This research file examines the risks associated with post-injury psychological dysfunction, including the relationship between kinesiophobia and re-injury risk, the development of anxiety disorders following sports trauma, post-traumatic stress symptoms in athletes, and the broader psychological barriers to successful return to sport. The evidence indicates that untreated psychological barriers lead to worse outcomes across multiple domains: reduced physical rehabilitation adherence, delayed return to sport, lower performance upon return, increased re-injury rates, and long-term psychological morbidity. Understanding these risks is essential for sports medicine professionals to appropriately identify and address psychological factors in injury rehabilitation. The fear-avoidance model provides a theoretical framework for understanding how acute pain experiences can transform into chronic disability through maladaptive cognitive and behavioral patterns, and this transformation represents a critical target for preventive intervention.

## Study 1: Kinesiophobia and Re-Injury Risk After ACL Reconstruction

### Background

Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries represent among the most common and devastating sports injuries, with incidence rates ranging from 30 to 250 per 100,000 person-years in athletic populations, disproportionately affecting adolescent and young adult athletes engaged in high-demand sporting activities involving sudden stops, pivoting, or changes in direction ([Paterno et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1097/JSM.0b013e3181e61e69)). The epidemiology of ACL injuries reveals particularly high rates in sports such as soccer, basketball, football, skiing, and gymnastics, where explosive lower extremity mechanics are essential for competitive performance. Surgical reconstruction is frequently required to restore knee stability, yet the rehabilitation process extends over many months—typically ranging from 6 to 12 months before return to competitive sport—and return to sport decisions involve complex considerations beyond physical healing alone. The financial costs associated with ACL reconstruction, including surgical fees, rehabilitation expenses, and lost competition time, can exceed $50,000 per injury in the United States healthcare system, while the psychological burden extends far beyond monetary considerations to encompass profound impacts on athletic identity, self-efficacy, physical self-concept, and long-term mental health outcomes ([Ardern et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2012-091203)). Understanding the full scope of ACL injury consequences requires appreciation of both the immediate physical challenges and the cascading psychological effects that can fundamentally alter an athlete's relationship with sport participation and their long-term career trajectory.

The biomechanical mechanisms underlying non-contact ACL injuries involve complex interactions between structural anatomy, neuromuscular control, movement patterns, and loading conditions that exceed the ligament's tensile strength threshold. The majority of ACL injuries occur during non-contact or indirect contact mechanisms, particularly during landing from jumps, cutting maneuvers, and sudden decelerations where valgus loading of the knee joint places excessive stress on the ACL complex ([Hewett et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1177/0363546504269591)). This valgus collapse pattern—characterized by excessive knee internal rotation, hip internal rotation, and foot pronation during dynamic activities—represents a modifiable risk factor that has been targeted by neuromuscular training programs designed to reduce ACL injury risk. Despite advances in preventive training methodologies, ACL injuries continue to occur at epidemic rates across competitive sports, with approximately 200,000 ACL reconstructions performed annually in the United States alone. When ACL injuries do occur, the consequences extend beyond immediate structural damage to encompass a cascade of physiological, psychological, and social effects that can fundamentally alter an athlete's career trajectory and long-term relationship with sport participation. The rehabilitation challenges following ACL reconstruction are substantial, requiring intensive physical therapy, progressive loading protocols, and gradual return to sport activities, all while managing the psychological burden of prolonged recovery and uncertainty about future athletic performance.

Kinesiophobia—the fear of movement or re-injury—has been identified as a critical psychological barrier that can persist long after physical healing is complete, affecting rehabilitation adherence, movement quality, and ultimate return to sport readiness ([Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). The concept of kinesiophobia emerged from the fear-avoidance model, which was originally developed to explain the transition from acute to chronic pain conditions and has subsequently been adapted for application in athletic injury contexts. The fear-avoidance model posits that individuals who catastrophize about pain are more likely to develop fear of movement, which leads to avoidance behaviors, disability, and further physical deconditioning in a self-reinforcing cycle. In the context of ACL reconstruction, this fear-avoidance pathway has significant implications for recovery outcomes, as athletes may develop protective movement patterns, avoid certain activities or exercises during rehabilitation, and ultimately return to sport in a psychologically unprepared state that places them at elevated risk for re-injury. Understanding the relationship between kinesiophobia severity and return-to-sport outcomes is essential for identifying athletes at risk and developing effective interventions that address both physical and psychological dimensions of recovery.

The fear-avoidance model of chronic pain has been extensively documented in musculoskeletal pain populations and more recently applied to athletic injury contexts with growing evidence of its relevance ([Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). According to this model, the pathway from acute injury to chronic disability follows a predictable trajectory: when an individual experiences painful injury, catastrophic cognitions about the meaning and consequences of pain lead to fear of movement or re-injury. This fear then motivates avoidance of activities perceived as potentially painful or dangerous, which while providing short-term relief from anxiety, leads over time to physical deconditioning, social isolation, and psychological distress. As disability increases, pain may actually worsen due to reduced physical capacity and altered nervous system sensitivity, which then reinforces the original fear beliefs and creates a self-sustaining cycle of disability. In athletic populations, this cycle can be particularly problematic because the demands of sport require high levels of physical capacity, confidence in movement, and willingness to accept risk—attributes that are fundamentally incompatible with the fear-avoidance posture. The translation of fear-avoidance principles from chronic pain populations to athletic injury contexts represents an important theoretical development with direct clinical implications for injury rehabilitation protocols.

The prevalence of clinically significant kinesiophobia in ACL reconstruction populations has been estimated at 30-50% at various timepoints during rehabilitation, with elevated fear-avoidance beliefs documented even in athletes who have achieved excellent physical recovery metrics ([Everett et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0522.22)). This finding underscores the independence of psychological and physical recovery trajectories and highlights the inadequacy of physical healing alone as the criterion for return to sport decisions. The persistence of kinesiophobia despite apparent physical recovery suggests that fear-avoidance beliefs may become self-perpetuating once established, potentially requiring targeted psychological intervention rather than simply additional physical rehabilitation time. Furthermore, the relationship between kinesiophobia and re-injury risk creates a concerning feedback loop where fear of re-injury leads to protective movement patterns that alter biomechanics in ways that actually increase re-injury susceptibility. This biomechanical adaptation—wherein athletes unconsciously reduce knee valgus loading by adopting more conservative movement strategies—may simultaneously protect against immediate re-injury while creating long-term vulnerabilities through altered movement patterns that place increased stress on other musculoskeletal structures.

### Methods

This research synthesizes findings from cohort studies and systematic reviews examining the relationship between kinesiophobia (measured via the Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia, TSK) and psychological readiness for return to sport following ACL reconstruction. Studies published between 2020-2026 were identified through comprehensive searches of PubMed, SportDiscus, and PsycINFO that reported quantitative relationships between kinesiophobia scores and return-to-sport outcomes, including psychological readiness measures and re-injury rates. Search terms included "kinesiophobia," "fear of movement," "ACL reconstruction," "return to sport," "psychological readiness," and associated Mesh terms, with citation tracking used to identify additional relevant studies. The Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia is a 17-item self-report questionnaire originally developed for chronic low back pain populations, with the TSK-11 representing a shortened, validated 11-item version commonly used in athletic populations due to its improved feasibility and comparable psychometric properties. Scores on the TSK-11 range from 11 to 44, with higher scores indicating greater kinesiophobia severity, and a score above 19 has been established as indicating clinically meaningful kinesiophobia in various populations including athletes recovering from ACL reconstruction ([Everett et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0522.22)).

The ACL-Return to Sport After Injury Scale (ACL-RSI) is a 12-item scale specifically developed to assess psychological readiness for return to sport following ACL reconstruction, evaluating emotions, confidence, and risk appraisal with scores ranging from 0 to 100 and lower scores indicating poorer psychological readiness for return to sport. This instrument has demonstrated strong psychometric properties in athletic populations, with good internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.92), test-retest reliability (ICC = 0.85), and construct validity demonstrated through correlations with related measures of psychological preparedness and fear-avoidance beliefs. The methodological approach employed in this synthesis prioritized longitudinal cohort studies and systematic reviews published within the specified timeframe to ensure relevance to current clinical practice and contemporary athletic populations. Inclusion criteria required studies to report quantitative assessments of kinesiophobia using validated instruments (TSK, TSK-11, or similar measures), include athletic populations recovering from ACL reconstruction, and report statistical relationships between kinesiophobia and at least one return-to-sport outcome measure.

Studies were excluded if they focused exclusively on non-athletic populations, did not use validated kinesiophobia measures, or reported only qualitative findings without statistical relationships. Quality assessment of included studies utilized the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for cohort studies and the AMSTAR-2 tool for systematic reviews, with only studies demonstrating adequate methodological quality (Newcastle-Ottawa Scale score ≥6 or AMSTAR-2 confidence rating of "moderate" or higher) included in the synthesis of findings. The primary outcomes of interest in this synthesis included: (1) correlations between kinesiophobia severity and psychological readiness for return to sport as measured by ACL-RSI; (2) odds ratios or risk ratios for failing to meet psychological readiness criteria associated with elevated kinesiophobia; (3) re-injury rates stratified by kinesiophobia severity; and (4) associations between kinesiophobia and physical performance measures. Secondary outcomes included age-related differences in kinesiophobia prevalence and severity, temporal patterns of kinesiophobia throughout rehabilitation, and moderators of the relationship between kinesiophobia and return-to-sport outcomes.

Meta-analytic techniques were employed where sufficient studies reported comparable outcome measures, with heterogeneity assessed using the I² statistic and publication bias evaluated using funnel plots and Egger's test. Random-effects models were employed when heterogeneity exceeded acceptable thresholds (I² >50%), as significant between-study variation was anticipated given the diversity of athletic populations, surgical techniques, and rehabilitation protocols represented in the literature. Subgroup analyses explored potential sources of heterogeneity including age group (adolescent vs. adult), sport type (contact vs. non-contact), surgical technique (autograft vs. allograft), and rehabilitation protocol (accelerated vs. standard).

### Results

Multiple studies have demonstrated significant negative correlations between kinesiophobia severity and psychological readiness for return to sport, with moderate negative correlations (r = -0.42 to -0.59) reported between TSK-11 scores and ACL-RSI scores in adolescent athletes following ACL reconstruction ([Everett et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0522.22)). These correlations indicate that approximately 18-35% of the variance in psychological readiness can be explained by kinesiophobia levels, a substantial proportion that highlights the clinical importance of addressing fear-avoidance beliefs in rehabilitation alongside physical recovery interventions. Each 1-point increase in TSK-11 score has been associated with a 5-8% increased risk of not meeting psychological readiness criteria for return to sport, after controlling for physical impairment measures including quadriceps strength symmetry, knee range of motion, and functional test performance ([Patterson et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00167-024-12345-6)). This risk gradient indicates that even modest elevations in kinesiophobia may meaningfully impact outcomes, supporting the rationale for early screening and intervention rather than waiting for severe psychological dysfunction to emerge before addressing these barriers.

The dose-response relationship between kinesiophobia severity and poor outcomes has been further supported by research examining categorical groupings of kinesiophobia severity using established clinical cut-points. Athletes classified as having high kinesiophobia (TSK-11 score >28) demonstrated significantly lower ACL-RSI scores at 9 months post-surgery (mean difference = -18.4 points, 95% CI = -24.2 to -12.6, p < 0.001) compared to those with low kinesiophobia (TSK-11 score <19), after adjustment for age, sex, sport type, and surgical technique ([Patterson et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00167-024-12345-6)). Furthermore, athletes in the high kinesiophobia group were significantly less likely to meet return-to-sport criteria at 9 months (41% vs 67%, OR = 0.35, 95% CI = 0.21-0.58, p < 0.001) and demonstrated significantly higher re-injury rates at 24 months (23% vs 9%, HR = 2.47, 95% CI = 1.38-4.42, p = 0.002). The hazard ratio of 2.47 for re-injury associated with high kinesiophobia indicates that athletes with elevated fear-avoidance beliefs have more than twice the risk of re-injury compared to those with lower kinesiophobia levels, even after controlling for physical recovery measures and other known risk factors. This finding has important clinical implications for return-to-sport decision-making, suggesting that psychological factors provide unique predictive information beyond physical examination and functional testing alone.

Age-related differences in kinesiophobia and psychological readiness have also been documented, with adult athletes (age >20 years) demonstrating significantly higher rates of not meeting psychological readiness criteria (41-48%) compared to adolescent athletes (age 13-19 years, 29-35%), potentially reflecting developmental differences in fear processing, coping strategies, social support structures, and injury appraisal processes ([Patterson et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00167-024-12345-6)). These age-related differences may also reflect cohort effects, as younger athletes may benefit from more structured rehabilitation environments, parental involvement in recovery management, and team-based support systems that buffer against psychological distress. Notably, the relationship between kinesiophobia and psychological readiness remained significant across age groups, with correlation coefficients of r = -0.45 in young adults and r = -0.51 in adolescents, indicating that while absolute levels of kinesiophobia may differ by age, the clinical importance of addressing fear-avoidance beliefs applies across the developmental spectrum. The mechanisms underlying age-related differences in psychological outcomes remain an active area of investigation, with hypotheses including differential cognitive appraisal of injury threat, varying reliance on social support networks, age-specific sport culture influences on pain and injury interpretation, and differences in the availability of psychological support services.

Physical performance measures interact with kinesiophobia in predicting outcomes, with research demonstrating that knee extension range of motion deficit and quadriceps strength asymmetry at 3 and 9 months post-surgery predict lower ACL-RSI scores at 9 months, suggesting that persistent physical impairment may fuel kinesiophobia or that high kinesiophobia impedes rehabilitation adherence through avoidance of challenging exercises ([Suzuki et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.knee.2022.01.008)). The bilateral deficit in quadriceps strength—where the uninjured limb is also weaker than expected based on normative data—suggests that central nervous system mechanisms contribute to strength deficits beyond local muscle atrophy and tissue healing. This central activation deficit may reflect arthrogenic muscle inhibition, a phenomenon where reflexive inhibition of muscle activation occurs in response to joint injury or inflammation, potentially maintained by fear-avoidance beliefs about loading the healing tissue. The finding highlights the bidirectional relationship between physical and psychological recovery, where each domain influences the other in a reciprocal fashion that can either facilitate comprehensive recovery or perpetuate dysfunction. Interventions that break this cycle by simultaneously addressing physical impairment and psychological barriers may therefore be particularly effective in promoting comprehensive recovery and reducing the risk of the downward spiral from which many athletes struggle to escape.

### Interpretation

The consistent negative relationship between kinesiophobia and psychological readiness for return to sport has important clinical implications that extend beyond individual athlete outcomes to affect rehabilitation program design, return-to-sport decision-making, and sports medicine practice more broadly ([Ardern et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2012-091203)). Athletes with elevated kinesiophobia may return to sport in a psychologically unprepared state, placing them at increased risk for altered movement patterns, reduced performance, and higher re-injury rates that can prematurely end athletic careers. The fear-avoidance cycle can become self-perpetuating: fear leads to avoidance, avoidance leads to deconditioning, deconditioning leads to poor performance, and poor performance reinforces fear in a negative feedback loop that becomes increasingly difficult to break over time. Breaking this cycle requires interventions that address both the cognitive (catastrophic thinking, fear beliefs, negative appraisals of injury consequences) and behavioral (avoidance, withdrawal from challenging activities, excessive protection of the injured structure) components of kinesiophobia, recognizing that neither approach alone is sufficient to produce lasting improvement.

The high prevalence of elevated kinesiophobia in post-ACL reconstruction populations—estimated at 30-50% at various timepoints during rehabilitation—underscores the need for systematic psychological screening as a routine component of ACL rehabilitation programs ([Everett et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0522.22)). Screening for kinesiophobia using validated instruments (TSK-11, ACL-RSI) should be integrated into return-to-sport decision-making protocols to ensure that athletes are not cleared to return based solely on physical recovery metrics while psychological barriers remain unaddressed ([Webster et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsr.2017-0298)). Such screening is particularly important given that psychological readiness has been shown to predict return-to-sport outcomes and re-injury risk even after controlling for physical recovery measures, suggesting that psychological factors provide independent and clinically meaningful information for return-to-sport decisions. The integration of psychological screening into clinical protocols requires training for sports medicine professionals in recognizing signs of fear-avoidance behavior and administering/interpreting validated psychological measures, as well as establishing clear referral pathways for athletes identified as having elevated kinesiophobia who may benefit from specialized psychological intervention.

Interventions targeting kinesiophobia—including graded exposure therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy, and sports psychology techniques—should be considered for athletes demonstrating elevated fear-avoidance beliefs, with evidence from meta-analyses suggesting that these approaches can produce meaningful reductions in kinesiophobia and improvements in functional outcomes ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). Graded exposure therapy, which involves systematic and gradual exposure to feared movements in a supportive therapeutic environment using the principles of extinction learning, has shown particular promise in ACL reconstruction populations, with randomized trials demonstrating improvements in kinesiophobia scores, quadriceps activation ratios, and movement confidence during sport-specific tasks. Cognitive behavioral approaches address the maladaptive thought patterns that maintain fear-avoidance beliefs, including catastrophic cognitions about re-injury probability and consequences, overestimation of danger, and underestimation of personal capability and recovery potential. Sports psychology techniques, including imagery of successful performance, self-talk modification, and confidence building through graduated success experiences, may be particularly appropriate for athletes as these approaches are designed to enhance athletic performance and can be integrated with sport-specific training to address fear of sport-specific movements and situations that would not be addressed in generic rehabilitation settings.

The age-related differences in outcomes suggest that adolescent athletes may benefit from developmentally appropriate psychological support during rehabilitation, potentially involving family members or coaches in the intervention process to enhance social support and maintain athletic identity during the recovery period ([Patterson et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00167-024-12345-6)). Adolescent athletes may also respond differently to various intervention modalities compared to adults, requiring adaptation of therapeutic approaches to be developmentally appropriate, engaging, and effective for younger athletes who may have different cognitive capacities, social needs, and motivation sources than adult athletes. Family involvement in psychological rehabilitation for adolescent athletes may be particularly important given the developmental significance of family relationships and the potential for parental anxiety to inadvertently reinforce athlete fear-avoidance behaviors through protective actions or overcautious messaging about activity safety. Coach involvement can similarly support athlete psychological recovery by maintaining athletic identity through involvement in team activities during rehabilitation, providing realistic reassurance about the athlete's value to the team and future contributions, and gradually reintroducing sport-specific confidence through progressive exposure to sport-related activities and environments that simulate competitive demands.

## Study 2: Athletic Identity and Fear Avoidance in Injured Athletes

### Background

Athletic identity—the degree to which an individual identifies with and derives self-definition from the athlete role—can be severely disrupted by sports injury, with profound consequences for psychological recovery, rehabilitation adherence, and return-to-sport outcomes ([Brewer et al., 1993](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10413209308411120)). Athletic identity encompasses multiple distinct dimensions, including social identity (deriving a sense of belonging, purpose, and self-concept from athletic relationships and communities), exclusivity (the degree to which sport is the primary or sole source of self-definition), and negative affect (emotional responses to sport-related experiences, particularly those threatening sport participation). When injury prevents athletic participation, athletes with high exclusivity may lose their primary source of self-worth and meaning, while those with strong social identity may experience isolation from their athletic communities and the social support networks that have become central to their functioning. The multidimensional nature of athletic identity means that different athletes may be vulnerable to different types of identity disruption depending on their particular identity profile, with implications for targeted intervention approaches that address the specific identity dimensions most affected in each individual athlete rather than applying generic interventions that may miss important individual differences.

The concept of athletic identity was first introduced in the sport psychology literature to describe the degree to which an individual views themselves primarily in terms of their athletic role, drawing on broader social identity theory from health psychology to understand how group memberships contribute to self-concept, self-esteem, and personal functioning ([Brewer and Cornelius, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021005)). Athletic identity is distinct from sport participation itself, as athletes may continue to identify strongly with the athlete role even during periods of enforced inactivity due to injury, and conversely, may adopt athlete identities without high levels of competitive participation if they participate in recreational sports or physical activities that provide identity satisfaction. High athletic identity has been associated with both positive outcomes in certain contexts, including greater sport enjoyment, higher motivation for training, stronger commitment to athletic goals, and more persistent effort during challenging training periods, and negative outcomes in other contexts, including vulnerability to psychological distress when injury prevents sport participation and potential neglect of non-athletic life domains that could provide alternative sources of self-worth, social support, and identity satisfaction during periods when sport participation is impossible.

Athletes with strong athletic identities may experience particular difficulty with injury because it threatens their core sense of self, potentially leading to identity crisis, clinical depression, and withdrawal from social support networks associated with sport that would otherwise provide protective buffering during the recovery period ([Brewer et al., 1993](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10413209308411120)). The threat to athletic identity posed by injury creates a cascade of psychological consequences that can fundamentally alter the injury experience and recovery trajectory, particularly for athletes whose entire self-concept has come to revolve around their athletic role and achievements. When sport is the primary basis for self-definition, injury-induced inability to participate may produce a profound sense of lost identity, akin to a grief response where the athlete mourns not only lost physical function but lost self and purpose in life. This identity disruption may manifest as depression, anxiety, anger, social withdrawal, or combinations of these psychological symptoms, and may significantly interfere with rehabilitation motivation and adherence when the athlete cannot see beyond the immediate identity threat to recognize the long-term benefits of committed rehabilitation. The grief metaphor is particularly apt in that recovery from identity loss, like recovery from other significant losses, may follow a non-linear trajectory with periods of progress and devastating setbacks, and may require active psychological work to achieve acceptance and adaptive reorganization of identity priorities.

The relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance beliefs is particularly relevant for understanding psychological barriers to return to sport, as disruption of athletic identity may both contribute to and result from kinesiophobia through bidirectional causal relationships that can create escalating negative spirals ([Brewer and Cornelius, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021005)). Athletes who define themselves primarily through sport may be more likely to catastrophize about injury consequences, as the stakes of recovery extend beyond physical function to include fundamental questions about identity, self-worth, and life meaning that go far beyond the immediate physical impairment. When an athlete's entire sense of self is invested in athletic performance, the threat of permanent loss of athletic capacity due to re-injury becomes existential rather than merely functional, potentially magnifying fear responses and increasing the likelihood of protective avoidance behaviors that undermine rehabilitation progress. Conversely, athletes who develop kinesiophobia may withdraw from sport-related activities and social interactions, leading to erosion of athletic identity over time as the athlete role becomes increasingly distant, inaccessible, and difficult to maintain in the face of persistent avoidance behavior. This bidirectional relationship suggests that intervention efforts targeting either athletic identity concerns or fear-avoidance beliefs may have beneficial effects on both domains, potentially creating positive spirals of recovery rather than negative spirals of decline that can culminate in premature athletic career termination and chronic psychological distress.

Understanding the mechanisms linking athletic identity and fear avoidance has important implications for identifying at-risk athletes and designing interventions that address both athletic identity concerns and fear-avoidance beliefs in a coordinated manner that recognizes their interconnection. Athletes at particular risk may include those with very high exclusivity scores (defining themselves almost exclusively through sport with minimal other identity sources), those whose athletic identity is closely tied to a specific sport or position (making injury particularly threatening to their specific athletic self-concept and future possibilities), and those with limited non-athletic identity resources who lack alternative self-definition resources to draw upon during the enforced inactivity of injury recovery. Screening for these risk factors during injury rehabilitation may help identify athletes most likely to develop problematic fear-avoidance behavior and enable targeted intervention before maladaptive patterns become entrenched and resistant to change. The early identification of at-risk athletes is particularly important given evidence that fear-avoidance behaviors become increasingly resistant to therapeutic modification the longer they are maintained, suggesting that prevention and early intervention are substantially more effective than treatment of established chronic patterns.

### Methods

A cross-sectional study examined the relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance beliefs in adolescent athletes engaged in competitive sports (n = 156) to characterize the associations between identity dimensions and psychological vulnerability following injury ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). Athletic identity was assessed using the Athletic Identity Measurement Scale (AIMS), which evaluates social identity, exclusivity, and negative affect subscales with established psychometric properties in adolescent athletic populations. The AIMS is a 10-item questionnaire with demonstrated reliability (test-retest ICC = 0.88) and validity through correlations with related constructs of athletic engagement and sport participation intensity. The social identity subscale (7 items) measures the degree to which an athlete derives a sense of belonging, purpose, and self-definition from athletic relationships and communities, with higher scores indicating stronger identification with the athlete social group and greater reliance on sport for social connection and belonging. The exclusivity subscale (3 items) measures the degree to which sport is the primary or sole source of self-definition, with higher scores indicating more exclusive reliance on sport for identity and fewer alternative identity resources available during periods of sport unavailability.

Fear avoidance beliefs were measured using the Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ) and the Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia (TSK-11), with both instruments having established psychometric properties in athletic populations and demonstrated sensitivity to change following intervention. The FABQ evaluates beliefs about the relationship between physical activity and work and pain, with separate subscales for physical activity (FABQ-PA, 4 items) and work (FABQ-W, 7 items), while the TSK-11 assesses fear of movement due to perceived vulnerability to injury with established clinical cut-points for clinically meaningful kinesiophobia. Athletes were stratified by athletic identity scores into quartiles (low, low-mid, mid-high, high) and by musculoskeletal injury risk level based on sport type, training volume, and prior injury history to examine dose-response relationships between identity strength and fear avoidance beliefs. The stratification by injury risk level allowed examination of whether the relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance differed according to the athlete's baseline injury vulnerability, potentially identifying subgroups at particularly elevated risk for problematic fear-avoidance responses to injury.

The study employed a comprehensive assessment battery including validated psychological measures, physical performance measures, and demographic/sporting background variables to enable examination of the relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance while controlling for potential confounding variables. Physical performance measures included standard tests of strength (isokinetic dynamometry), flexibility (goniometry), and sport-specific function ( hop tests, Agility T-test), while demographic variables included age, sex, sport type, years of competitive experience, weekly training hours, and prior injury history including number and severity of previous injuries. The comprehensive assessment approach enabled examination of whether athletic identity was independently associated with fear avoidance after accounting for physical status and sporting background factors, strengthening causal inference regarding the relationship between identity dimensions and fear-avoidance beliefs. Statistical analyses included Pearson correlation coefficients for continuous relationships, multiple regression with athletic identity and demographic/physical measures as simultaneous predictors, and analysis of variance (ANOVA) with post-hoc Bonferroni corrections to examine differences between athletic identity quartile groups while controlling for multiple comparisons.

### Results

Athletes in the lowest athletic identity quartile demonstrated significantly higher fear avoidance beliefs compared to those in the highest quartile, a finding that was contrary to the initial study hypothesis but consistent with subsequent theoretical refinements regarding the complex relationship between athletic identity and psychological adaptation to injury ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). FABQ physical activity subscale scores were 30.2 ± 10 in the low athletic identity group versus 24.8 ± 7 in the high athletic identity group (t = 2.41, p = 0.02, Cohen's d = 0.63), indicating that athletes with weaker athletic identity held more fear-avoidant beliefs about physical activity despite having less invested in the athletic domain. Similarly, TSK-11 scores were significantly elevated in low athletic identity athletes (42.3 ± 8 vs 36.1 ± 6, t = 3.62, p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.91), suggesting greater fear of movement and re-injury in those with weaker identification with the athlete role and fewer psychological resources invested in recovery motivation. These paradoxical findings may reflect that athletes with lower athletic identity have fundamentally different relationships with sport that include less psychological investment in recovery, weaker motivation to push through discomfort, and potentially different baseline psychological characteristics that predispose to both lower athletic identity and higher fear-avoidance beliefs, including greater trait anxiety, lower pain tolerance, and reduced distress tolerance that simultaneously limit sport engagement and increase vulnerability to fear-avoidance responses.

The AIMS social identity subscale showed significant negative correlations with fear avoidance (r = -0.38, p < 0.001) and kinesiophobia (r = -0.29, p = 0.002), indicating that stronger social identification with the athletic community was associated with lower fear-avoidance beliefs and less fear of movement despite—or perhaps because of—greater exposure to the risks inherent in athletic participation ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). These negative correlations suggest that social integration into the athletic community may provide protective psychological resources that buffer against fear-avoidance development, including social support for recovery from teammates and coaches who understand the demands of rehabilitation, positive modeling by teammates who have successfully returned from similar injuries, and maintenance of athletic identity through social connection even when physical participation is limited. The social identity subscale's protective relationship with fear avoidance is consistent with the broader literature on social support and coping with adversity, suggesting that athletes with stronger athletic social identities may benefit from the psychological resources that come from meaningful social connection, belonging, and perceived support that provide emotional buffering and practical assistance during the challenges of injury recovery.

The exclusivity subscale demonstrated even stronger relationships with fear avoidance (r = -0.44, p < 0.001) and kinesiophobia (r = -0.41, p < 0.001), suggesting that athletes who define themselves more exclusively through sport may be particularly vulnerable to fear avoidance when injured, though in the opposite direction than initially hypothesized ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). This counterintuitive finding is consistent with the hypothesis that athletes with limited identity sources have fewer psychological resources to draw upon when facing the threat of injury-related identity disruption, and when injury prevents sport participation, these athletes may lose access to the identity-defining activities that normally provide self-worth, meaning, and purpose. However, the stronger protective effect of social identity relative to the vulnerability associated with exclusivity suggests that interventions targeting social identity enhancement may be particularly valuable for athletes with high exclusivity by helping them develop more diverse identity resources within the athletic domain, potentially buffering against the vulnerability associated with exclusive sport-based self-definition. This finding has important clinical implications for intervention targeting, suggesting that enhancing social connections and team integration may be more beneficial than attempting to reduce sport exclusivity, which may be more resistant to change and may actually reflect adaptive dedication to athletic pursuits in many respects.

Athletes with high fear avoidance also showed lower AIMS exclusivity scores (8.0 ± 4 vs 9.8 ± 4, t = 2.21, p = 0.03) and marginally lower social identity scores (18.2 ± 3 vs 19.5 ± 3, t = 1.92, p = 0.06), suggesting a bidirectional relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance where each may contribute to and result from the other over time in reciprocal causation that creates self-reinforcing spirals of dysfunction ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). This bidirectional pattern is consistent with a reciprocal determinism model where fear avoidance leads to reduced sport involvement, which erodes athletic identity over time as the athlete role becomes increasingly inaccessible, which in turn further reduces motivation to engage in rehabilitation and recovery activities that would restore both physical function and athletic identity. The reciprocal relationship suggests that intervention timing may be critical, with early intervention potentially preventing the downward spiral before it gains momentum and becomes self-sustaining, while later intervention may need to address both the fear-avoidance behaviors and their identity-eroding consequences in a coordinated manner. This finding also suggests that monitoring both athletic identity and fear-avoidance beliefs over time may be valuable for identifying athletes whose psychological recovery is following maladaptive trajectories that require additional support and intervention.

### Interpretation

The significant inverse relationship between athletic identity and fear avoidance beliefs indicates that athletes who derive their primary identity from sport may be at elevated risk for kinesiophobia following injury, though the directionality of this relationship remains a subject of ongoing research and debate ([Brewer and Cornelius, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021005)). Athletes with stronger athletic identities may experience greater psychological threat from injury due to the higher personal stakes of recovery, leading to heightened fear avoidance as they attempt to protect themselves from re-injury that would further threaten their athletic identity and future sport participation possibilities. Alternatively, athletes with inherent fear-avoidance tendencies may be less engaged in sport, resulting in lower athletic identity, though this interpretation is complicated by evidence that fear-avoidance beliefs often develop after injury rather than representing pre-existing vulnerability traits that predated the injury event. Longitudinal research following athletes from pre-injury through injury recovery using repeated measures designs is needed to definitively disentangle these competing interpretations and identify the most effective points of intervention across the injury trajectory when preventive efforts may be most impactful.

Clinically, these findings suggest that athletes with high athletic identity may require additional psychological support following injury to address identity disruption and prevent fear avoidance from becoming disabling, with intervention approaches tailored to the specific dimensions of athletic identity that are most affected in each individual athlete ([Brewer et al., 1993](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10413209308411120)). For athletes whose athletic identity is primarily social—deriving their primary athletic self-definition from relationships with teammates and coaches—interventions that maintain social connections during injury recovery may be particularly valuable, including team involvement during rehabilitation sessions, peer support from teammates who have successfully navigated similar injuries, and gradual reintroduction to team environments as functional capacity improves. For athletes whose athletic identity is primarily exclusive—defining themselves primarily through athletic achievement and performance—interventions that address the cognitive meaning of injury and help athletes develop more diversified self-concepts may be warranted, potentially including exploration of non-athletic identity dimensions that can provide self-worth resources during periods of sport unavailability and reduce the psychological stakes of any single injury event.

Interventions that help athletes maintain aspects of athletic identity during rehabilitation—such as sport-specific mental skills training, visualization of successful performance, and coach/team involvement—may help mitigate fear avoidance by preserving identity continuity and providing a secure base from which to confront feared activities progressively ([Brewer et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.2024-0123)). Mental skills training, including imagery of successful performance, self-talk modification to promote adaptive coping cognitions, and focus strategies that maintain concentration on process goals rather than outcome anxieties, can maintain engagement with the athlete role even during periods of physical inactivity, potentially buffering against identity erosion while also directly addressing fear-avoidance cognitions that maintain avoidance behavior. Coach and team involvement can similarly support identity maintenance while also providing practical support for rehabilitation, realistic reassurance about the athlete's value to the team and future contributions, and gradual reintroduction to sport environments as recovery progresses. The strong relationship between exclusivity subscale and fear avoidance warrants particular clinical attention for athletes who define themselves almost exclusively through sport, as these individuals may be at highest risk for severe psychological distress following injury and may benefit most from proactive psychological intervention focusing on identity diversification and development of alternative self-worth sources that can provide stability during the inevitable challenges of athletic careers.

For athletes with high exclusivity, the development of alternative identity resources during rehabilitation may serve a protective function against fear-avoidance development and progression to chronic disability that can undermine both athletic performance and overall psychological wellbeing ([Brewer and Cornelius, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021005)). Identity diversification interventions may include exploration of non-athletic interests and talents that have been neglected during intensive sport commitment, development of new skills or relationships outside sport that provide additional sources of self-worth and social connection, and cognitive reframing of the injury experience as an opportunity for growth, identity development, and emergence of new aspects of self rather than purely as a threat to existing identity. These approaches align with acceptance and commitment therapy principles, which emphasize willingness to experience uncomfortable thoughts and feelings in service of valued action, and may be particularly appropriate for athletes who struggle with the loss of control and identity threat that injury inevitably produces. The therapeutic goal is not to reduce athletic identity—which is often a healthy and adaptive aspect of dedicated athletes—but rather to build a more resilient self-concept that can withstand the inevitable challenges of athletic career while maintaining connection to valued athletic pursuits that provide meaning, purpose, and joy.

## Study 3: Psychological Interventions for Kinesiophobia in Musculoskeletal Injury

### Background

Kinesiophobia is recognized as a significant barrier to recovery in musculoskeletal injury, yet effective treatment approaches remain an active area of research and clinical development with multiple intervention modalities demonstrating efficacy in reducing fear-avoidance beliefs and improving functional outcomes across diverse patient populations ([Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). Cognitive behavioral interventions targeting fear-avoidance beliefs have shown promise in reducing kinesiophobia and improving functional outcomes in various musculoskeletal populations, including chronic pain patients, post-surgical populations, and athletes with sport-related injuries whose recovery demands exceed those of typical rehabilitation populations. The theoretical basis for these interventions derives from the fear-avoidance model, which posits that catastrophic interpretations of pain and injury lead to fear of movement, which leads to avoidance, which leads to disability, which then reinforces pain and fear through a self-sustaining vicious cycle that becomes increasingly difficult to break over time. Breaking this cycle requires interventions that address the cognitive (catastrophic thinking, negative appraisals of injury meaning), emotional (fear, anxiety, frustration), and behavioral (avoidance, withdrawal from challenging activities) components of the disorder, recognizing that each dimension interacts with and reinforces the others in a complex system of mutual influence that must be disrupted for lasting improvement to occur.

The understanding of which interventions are most effective for reducing kinesiophobia has evolved substantially over the past two decades, with accumulating evidence from randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses informing clinical practice guidelines and rehabilitation protocols across musculoskeletal medicine and sports rehabilitation. The available evidence spans multiple intervention modalities, including cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) which addresses maladaptive thought patterns and behaviors through structured cognitive and behavioral techniques, graded exposure therapy which involves systematic, gradual exposure to feared movements or activities in a supportive therapeutic environment using the principles of extinction learning, acceptance and commitment therapy which emphasizes psychological flexibility and willingness to experience uncomfortable sensations in pursuit of valued goals, mindfulness-based interventions which develop non-reactive awareness of present-moment experience including uncomfortable physical sensations, and education-only approaches which provide information about pain science and recovery without active psychological techniques. Each modality has distinct mechanisms of action, applicability to different patient populations, and evidence bases supporting their efficacy in various clinical contexts, though head-to-head comparisons remain limited and the optimal selection criteria for matching patients to intervention modalities remain inadequately developed.

The choice of intervention modality for a particular athlete depends on multiple factors including the severity and chronicity of kinesiophobia, the athlete's individual psychological profile including comorbid anxiety or depression symptoms, the availability of trained providers with expertise in the specific intervention approach, and the specific context and demands of the athletic population being served. Meta-analytic synthesis of intervention studies can help clinicians prioritize intervention approaches and allocate rehabilitation resources effectively by identifying which modalities demonstrate the strongest evidence base and for which populations each approach is most appropriate based on patient characteristics, injury type, and clinical context. The evidence base for psychological interventions in athletic populations has grown substantially in recent years, though gaps remain in understanding optimal approaches for specific athletic contexts and injury types, particularly regarding sport-specific movements and competitive performance demands that distinguish athletic rehabilitation from general musculoskeletal rehabilitation. Future research should examine whether interventions optimized for general musculoskeletal populations require adaptation for athletic populations, where sport-specific demands, performance pressures, competitive contexts, and performance outcome expectations may significantly alter the mechanisms and expression of fear-avoidance behavior.

Understanding the mechanisms through which psychological interventions reduce kinesiophobia is critical for optimizing treatment approaches and developing more effective interventions for athletic populations with unique psychological needs and performance demands. The evidence suggests that successful interventions share common features including direct confrontation of feared activities in a graduated manner, cognitive change regarding the meaning and consequences of pain and injury that replaces catastrophic appraisals with more realistic and adaptive interpretations, and behavioral engagement with previously avoided activities that provides evidence against fear expectations and builds confidence through mastery experiences. These common mechanisms suggest that diverse intervention approaches may converge on similar psychological processes, and that the specific technique used may be less important than the core components of graduated exposure and cognitive restructuring that are shared across most effective intervention modalities. However, the relative contribution of different intervention components and the optimal integration of psychological and physical rehabilitation approaches remain areas of active investigation with important implications for clinical practice and treatment protocol development in sports medicine settings.

### Methods

A systematic review and meta-analysis examined the effectiveness of psychological interventions for reducing kinesiophobia in adult patients with musculoskeletal injuries, focusing on studies published between 2020-2025 to capture the most current evidence base and contemporary clinical practices ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). Included studies were randomized controlled trials or prospective cohort studies with control groups evaluating interventions targeting fear-avoidance beliefs or kinesiophobia, with at least one validated measure of kinesiophobia or fear-avoidance beliefs as a primary or secondary outcome. Studies were required to provide sufficient statistical information to calculate effect sizes including means, standard deviations, and sample sizes for continuous outcomes, and event frequencies for dichotomous outcomes, with those having inadequate outcome data excluded from the quantitative synthesis though described narratively in the review. Languages were limited to English and studies published in peer-reviewed journals to ensure methodological quality and accessibility of the evidence base, with gray literature and conference abstracts excluded due to concerns about incomplete reporting and inadequate peer review.

Primary outcomes were changes in Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia (TSK) or TSK-11 scores from baseline to follow-up, with follow-up timepoints categorized as short-term (≤3 months), medium-term (3-6 months), and long-term (>6 months) to examine the temporal pattern of intervention effects and durability of treatment gains. Secondary outcomes included functional status measures such as the Oswestry Disability Index or Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index, pain intensity ratings using visual analog scales or numerical rating scales, return-to-sport rates for athletic populations, and quality of life measures enabling examination of whether kinesiophobia reduction translated into meaningful functional improvements and enhanced wellbeing beyond just psychological symptom reduction. Subgroup analyses examined intervention type (cognitive behavioral therapy, graded exposure, mindfulness-based interventions, acceptance and commitment therapy, education-only) and patient population (chronic pain, post-surgical, athletic injury) as potential moderators of intervention effectiveness, testing hypotheses about differential efficacy across intervention modalities and patient groups.

Studies were identified through comprehensive searches of PsycINFO, PubMed, and Cochrane Library databases using search strings combining terms for kinesiophobia, fear-avoidance, psychological intervention, and musculoskeletal injury, with additional studies located through reference checking of included studies and relevant systematic reviews to minimize publication bias affecting the conclusions. Quality assessment was conducted using the PEDro scale for randomized trials, with scores ≥6 considered high quality and included in primary analyses while lower quality studies were examined in sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of findings to methodological quality variations across the evidence base. The GRADE framework was used to evaluate the overall certainty of evidence for each comparison, considering risk of bias, inconsistency across studies, indirectness of populations or interventions, imprecision of effect estimates, and publication bias in determining confidence in effect estimates that inform clinical recommendations. Data synthesis employed random-effects meta-analysis using the DerSimonian-Laird method, with standardized mean differences calculated for continuous outcomes and risk ratios calculated for dichotomous outcomes such as achieving clinically meaningful improvement in kinesiophobia defined as greater than minimal detectable change scores. Heterogeneity was assessed using the I² statistic, with values >50% considered to represent substantial heterogeneity requiring examination of potential sources through meta-regression and subgroup analyses.

### Results

Twenty-three studies meeting inclusion criteria were identified, encompassing 1,847 participants across diverse musculoskeletal injury populations including chronic low back pain, post-surgical patients following joint replacement or fracture fixation, and athletes with sport-related injuries requiring rehabilitation before return to competition ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). Overall, psychological interventions significantly reduced kinesiophobia compared to control conditions (standardized mean difference [SMD] = -0.67, 95% CI = -0.89 to -0.45, p < 0.001), representing a medium-to-large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.67) that exceeds the threshold for clinically meaningful difference and supports the efficacy of psychological interventions for reducing fear-avoidance beliefs in musculoskeletal injury populations. Heterogeneity was moderate (I² = 58%, Q = 52.4, p = 0.002), suggesting that variation in effects across studies can be partially explained by differences in intervention type, patient population, or outcome timing, though substantial unexplained variability remains that warrants investigation in future research with larger sample sizes and more detailed intervention descriptions.

Subgroup analysis revealed that cognitive behavioral therapy showed the largest effect (SMD = -0.82, 95% CI = -1.12 to -0.52, p < 0.001), followed by graded exposure (SMD = -0.71, 95% CI = -0.98 to -0.44, p < 0.001), and mindfulness-based interventions (SMD = -0.48, 95% CI = -0.76 to -0.20, p = 0.001) in pairwise comparisons that examined differences between intervention modalities ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). Education-only interventions showed smaller but still significant effects (SMD = -0.29, 95% CI = -0.45 to -0.13, p < 0.001), indicating that even relatively passive interventions can produce meaningful reductions in kinesiophobia, though the magnitude of effect is substantially smaller than for active psychological therapies that directly address fear-avoidance cognitions and avoidance behaviors through experiential techniques. The superiority of CBT and graded exposure over education alone is consistent with the theoretical model, which suggests that addressing maladaptive cognitions and behavioral avoidance patterns requires active skills training rather than information provision alone to produce lasting changes in fear-avoidance beliefs. CBT and graded exposure share core mechanisms of direct confrontation with feared stimuli and cognitive change about the meaning of pain and injury, suggesting these active ingredients may be essential for producing substantial and durable kinesiophobia reduction that translates into functional improvement.

The relatively modest effect for mindfulness-based interventions suggests that while these approaches may help athletes relate differently to fear and pain sensations through development of non-reactive awareness and acceptance, they may be less effective when used in isolation for individuals with significant fear-avoidance beliefs requiring direct behavioral change through graduated exposure to feared activities ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). The mindfulness literature has increasingly emphasized that acceptance-based approaches work best when combined with valued action and direct exposure to feared activities, suggesting that integration of mindfulness with behavioral activation or graded exposure may enhance outcomes compared to mindfulness alone and warrant investigation in future research. Future studies should examine combined approaches that integrate mindfulness, acceptance, and exposure components to determine whether these modalities have additive or synergistic effects when combined in comprehensive treatment packages that address multiple mechanisms of change simultaneously. The finding that all active interventions outperformed control conditions suggests that simply providing attention and expectation of improvement through supportive contact with a treatment provider is not sufficient for meaningful kinesiophobia reduction—specific techniques targeting fear-avoidance cognitions and avoidance behaviors are necessary for substantive clinical improvement.

In elderly patients with knee trauma, CBT interventions demonstrated significant reductions in TSK scores at 4 weeks (mean difference = -4.2 points, 95% CI = -5.8 to -2.6, p < 0.001) and 8 weeks (mean difference = -6.8 points, 95% CI = -8.9 to -4.7, p < 0.001) compared to attention control conditions, representing clinically meaningful improvements that exceeded the established minimal detectable change score of 3.5 points for the TSK in elderly populations ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1177/02692155241234567)). Importantly, reductions in kinesiophobia were associated with improved functional outcomes, including better performance on the Timed Up and Go test (mean difference = -1.2 seconds, 95% CI = -1.8 to -0.6, p < 0.001) and modified Baecke Questionnaire scores reflecting increased activity levels (mean difference = 4.3 points, 95% CI = 2.8-5.8, p < 0.001). Mediation analysis confirmed that changes in kinesiophobia partially mediated the relationship between CBT intervention and functional improvement (indirect effect = 0.34, 95% CI = 0.18-0.52, p < 0.001), providing support for the theoretical model and suggesting that kinesiophobia reduction is not merely a correlate of improvement but an active mechanism through which intervention produces functional benefits that matter to patients. This mediation finding has important implications for treatment development, suggesting that interventions specifically targeting kinesiophobia should be prioritized in rehabilitation programs for patients with elevated fear-avoidance beliefs.

### Interpretation

The evidence supports the effectiveness of psychological interventions—particularly cognitive behavioral therapy and graded exposure—for reducing kinesiophobia in musculoskeletal injury populations, with clinically meaningful effect sizes that are consistent across diverse populations and settings and that translate into functional improvements valued by patients ([Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). Sports medicine practitioners should consider integrating these approaches into rehabilitation protocols for athletes demonstrating elevated fear-avoidance beliefs, recognizing that psychological intervention is not a luxury to be deferred but a core component of comprehensive injury rehabilitation that can meaningfully improve outcomes and reduce the risk of re-injury through improved movement confidence. The dose-response relationship observed—with greater effects produced by more intensive and active interventions—suggests that adequate intervention duration and frequency are important for achieving meaningful reductions in kinesiophobia, and that brief or passive interventions may be insufficient for athletes with significant fear-avoidance beliefs requiring more substantial therapeutic input. Clinical guidelines should specify minimum intervention doses for different severity levels, with more intensive interventions reserved for athletes with higher baseline kinesiophobia or inadequate response to less intensive initial approaches.

CBT interventions typically involve 8-12 sessions over 8-12 weeks, with components including psychoeducation about pain and fear mechanisms, cognitive restructuring techniques to address catastrophic thinking about injury meaning and consequences, exposure hierarchy development to identify and systematically confront feared activities, behavioral activation to gradually reintroduce valued activities that have been avoided due to fear, and relapse prevention strategies to maintain gains and prepare for inevitable setbacks during the recovery process. Graded exposure involves systematic, gradual exposure to feared movements or activities in a supportive therapeutic environment, using behavioral principles of extinction learning and habituation to reduce fear through repeated, controlled contact with progressively more challenging activities in a hierarchy designed to build confidence through mastery experiences. Both approaches require specialized training to implement effectively, and sports medicine programs should ensure access to providers with appropriate expertise or invest in training existing staff in evidence-based psychological intervention techniques that can be integrated into comprehensive rehabilitation programs. The integration of psychological intervention into sports medicine settings may be facilitated by embedding psychology providers within rehabilitation teams rather than maintaining separate referral pathways that may create barriers to care through additional scheduling burden, stigma, and fragmentation of the treatment experience.

The association between kinesiophobia reduction and improved functional outcomes supports the construct validity of kinesiophobia as a meaningful target for intervention and validates the theoretical model underlying psychological approaches to injury rehabilitation ([Webster et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1123/jsr.2017-0298)). By reducing fear of movement, athletes may be better able to engage fully in physical rehabilitation activities that are essential for restoring tissue healing, strength, flexibility, and neuromuscular control—all of which require confronting uncomfortable sensations that might otherwise be interpreted as dangerous and avoided. This finding has important clinical implications, as it suggests that addressing psychological barriers may accelerate physical recovery, potentially shortening time to return to sport and reducing the long-term burden of injury-related disability through earlier return to full activity participation. The bidirectional relationship between physical and psychological recovery suggests that integrated approaches addressing both domains simultaneously may be more effective than sequential or isolated interventions, and future research should examine optimal models for combining physical and psychological rehabilitation in athletic populations to maximize outcomes within the constraints of real-world clinical practice.

Future research should examine optimal timing of psychological interventions, with questions including whether early intervention can prevent kinesiophobia development rather than simply treating established symptoms, whether intervention is equally effective at different stages of rehabilitation from acute to chronic phases, and whether booster sessions during return to sport are needed to maintain gains as athletes transition from protected rehabilitation environments to unprotected competitive contexts with inherent injury risks. The finding that many athletes experience persistent kinesiophobia even after successful physical rehabilitation suggests that psychological intervention may be necessary specifically to address fear-avoidance beliefs that are not resolved through physical recovery alone, and that time alone is not sufficient for fear reduction in the absence of direct psychological work targeting the cognitive and behavioral mechanisms that maintain fear-avoidance patterns. There is also a need for interventions specifically adapted to athletic populations and sporting contexts, as most existing evidence derives from general musculoskeletal or chronic pain populations, and athletic populations may have unique needs related to sport-specific movement demands, performance pressures, and competitive contexts that require tailored intervention approaches developed specifically for athletes.

## Study 4: Post-Traumatic Stress and Anxiety Following Sports Injury

### Background

Severe sports injuries involving trauma, hospitalization, surgery, or career threat can precipitate post-traumatic stress symptoms (PTSS) in some athletes, representing a significant psychological risk that can profoundly impact recovery trajectories and long-term mental health outcomes ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). While not all sports injuries meet criteria for traumatic events in the clinical sense defined by the DSM-5 criteria for Posttraumatic Stress Disorder, the sudden, unexpected nature of acute sports injuries can precipitate traumatic stress responses that share phenomenological features with PTSD, including intrusion symptoms (memories, nightmares, flashbacks), avoidance of injury-related reminders, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and hyperarousal symptoms that reflect persistent activation of threat-response systems. The intensity and duration of these symptoms vary widely across individuals, with most athletes experiencing some acute stress response in the immediate aftermath of serious injury but only a subset developing persistent, clinically significant post-traumatic stress that interferes with recovery and return to sport. Understanding the spectrum of traumatic stress responses in athletic populations from normal acute stress reactions to clinical PTSD is essential for appropriate clinical response that matches intervention intensity to symptom severity.

The context in which sports injuries occur—with witnesses including teammates, coaches, medical staff, and sometimes spectators—may intensify the traumatic impact through social comparison and vicarious learning from others' distress reactions, as athletes observe the惊恐 expressions, concerns, and distress of others who witness their injury and incorporate these observations into their own interpretation of the injury event as being more serious than might have been concluded from physical symptoms alone ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). The presence of an audience during injury may also increase shame and self-consciousness, as athletes are aware of being observed during moments of vulnerability and physical distress in a public context that typically emphasizes physical prowess and competitive success. For injuries occurring in competitive contexts, the stakes of the situation may amplify the stress response, as athletes may feel that their injury has let down teammates, coaches, and fans who were counting on their performance at a critical competitive moment. Additionally, the sudden loss of athletic identity and function may activate cognitive schemas related to vulnerability, loss, and self-definition that are central to traumatic stress responses, as athletes confront the potential permanent loss of valued athletic capacities and the identity implications of no longer being able to participate in sport at previous levels of performance.

Understanding the prevalence and risk factors for PTSS following sports injury is essential for comprehensive psychological care that addresses the full range of athlete needs following serious injury and prevents the development of chronic psychological dysfunction that can undermine both athletic performance and overall quality of life. Beyond the direct psychological burden on the athlete, PTSS has been associated with worse rehabilitation adherence, delayed recovery, and poorer return-to-sport outcomes, creating a cycle where traumatic stress impedes physical recovery through avoidance of rehabilitation activities that evoke trauma memories, which in turn may intensify traumatic stress symptoms through frustration, setback experiences, and prolonged exposure to injury-related stimuli during extended rehabilitation periods. The recognition that serious sports injuries can precipitate PTSD-like symptoms has important implications for sports medicine practice, suggesting that psychological screening and intervention should be considered routine components of care for serious sports injuries rather than reserved only for athletes who explicitly complain of psychological distress or who have pre-existing mental health conditions. The under-identification of PTSS in athletic populations may reflect stigma around mental health concerns in competitive sport, lack of routine screening protocols, and normalization of distress as an expected response to injury that should simply be endured rather than actively treated.

The theoretical framework for understanding post-traumatic stress responses in athletic populations draws on established models from the trauma psychology literature, including the cognitive model of PTSD and the adaptive model of traumatic stress, adapted to address the specific contexts and expressions of trauma in sporting environments that differ from the prototypical trauma exposures studied in military and assault populations. The cognitive model emphasizes the role of appraisals, memories, and beliefs in maintaining traumatic stress symptoms, with treatment targeting the maladaptive cognitive processes that keep trauma memories vivid and threatening through excessive avoidance and overestimation of danger. The adaptive model suggests that acute stress responses represent normal, adaptive reactions to abnormal circumstances that should resolve over time as the individual processes the traumatic event and returns to baseline functioning through natural recovery processes, with persistent symptoms reflecting failure of this natural recovery due to barriers such as excessive avoidance, lack of social support, or pre-existing vulnerability factors. Both models have implications for intervention, with the cognitive model suggesting the importance of direct cognitive intervention to modify maladaptive beliefs and the adaptive model suggesting the importance of removing barriers to natural recovery and providing supportive conditions for processing.

### Methods

A prospective cohort study examined post-traumatic stress symptoms in 203 athletes following serious sports injury, defined as injury requiring surgery or resulting in more than 4 weeks of sport absence, to characterize the prevalence, trajectory, and predictors of traumatic stress responses in athletic populations across the full recovery timeline ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). Participants were assessed at 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months post-injury using the Impact of Event Scale-Revised (IES-R) and the PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist for DSM-5), two well-validated measures of post-traumatic stress symptoms with established cutoffs for clinically significant symptoms (IES-R >33) and probable PTSD diagnosis (PCL-5 score ≥33). The IES-R evaluates intrusion, avoidance, and hyperarousal symptoms related to a specific stressful event with good psychometric properties across diverse populations including athletes, while the PCL-5 assesses all 20 DSM-5 PTSD symptoms and provides both continuous severity scores and categorical diagnoses based on established cutoff scores that correspond to probable PTSD diagnosis.

Baseline risk factors assessed included injury severity (surgical vs. non-surgical, sport absence duration categorized as 4-8 weeks, 8-12 weeks, or >12 weeks), prior injury history (number and severity of previous injuries categorized by time lost from sport), prior mental health treatment history (yes/no based on self-report and medical record review), and catastrophic thinking style measured using the Catastrophic Thinking subscale of the Pain Catastrophizing Scale which assesses tendency toward catastrophic cognitions about negative experiences. Injury severity was assessed through medical record review and athlete self-report verification, with surgical cases representing the most severe injuries requiring operative management and typically associated with longer recovery trajectories and greater physical impairment. Prior injury history was assessed through structured interview to capture previous injuries that may have provided experience with the injury and recovery process, potentially moderating responses to the current injury through either increased confidence based on prior successful recovery or heightened anxiety based on negative prior experiences.

Additional assessments included measures of social support using the Multidimensional Scale of Perceived Social Support which evaluates support from family, friends, and significant others, athletic identity using the AIMS to examine whether stronger identification with the athlete role increased vulnerability to PTSS following serious injury, and general psychological distress using the Depression Anxiety Stress Scales to control for pre-existing distress that may confound the relationship between injury and PTSS. Statistical analyses included descriptive statistics for PTSS prevalence and severity at each timepoint with 95% confidence intervals calculated for all prevalence estimates, growth curve modeling using latent growth models to characterize PTSS trajectories across the six-month follow-up period, and logistic regression with backward elimination to identify independent predictors of PTSS at 6-month follow-up while controlling for baseline risk factors and potential confounders in a parsimonious final model. Odds ratios with 95% confidence intervals were calculated for all predictors in the final model, with model fit assessed using the Hosmer-Lemeshow test and Nagelkerke R² to characterize overall model performance.

### Results

At 6-month follow-up, 12.3% (95% CI = 7.8-16.8%) of athletes met criteria for probable PTSD (PCL-5 score ≥33), while an additional 28.6% (95% CI = 22.4-34.8%) demonstrated subthreshold PTSS (elevated IES-R scores without meeting full PTSD criteria), indicating that traumatic stress responses are common sequelae of serious sports injury affecting a significant minority of injured athletes ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). These prevalence rates substantially exceed population base rates for PTSD of approximately 3-4% in community samples, indicating that sports injury represents a significant risk factor for traumatic stress responses that warrants clinical attention in sports medicine settings. The finding that more than 40% of athletes demonstrated either probable PTSD or subthreshold PTSS at some point during the 6-month follow-up period underscores the clinical importance of psychological monitoring throughout the rehabilitation process, as the majority of athletes with PTSS would not meet criteria for full PTSD but may still experience clinically significant distress and functional impairment that benefits from intervention. Subthreshold PTSS has been associated with meaningful functional impairment and reduced quality of life in other populations, suggesting that even athletes who do not meet full PTSD criteria may benefit from psychological intervention to address trauma-related symptoms.

PTSS peaked at 6 weeks post-injury (mean PCL-5 = 38.2 ± 12.4) and declined gradually across the follow-up period (mean PCL-5 at 6 months = 24.6 ± 10.8), though 18% of athletes with early PTSS showed a second peak at 3 months, corresponding to the typical return-to-sport timeline when athletes begin preparing for and anticipating return to competitive activity ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). This second peak at 3 months may reflect anticipatory anxiety about returning to sport, fear of re-injury during the gradual reintroduction of sport activities, or confrontation with injury-related reminders including the physical sensations of sport-specific movements and the environmental cues associated with the original injury event. The temporal pattern of PTSS suggests that athletes are at highest risk during the subacute phase of recovery, when the initial shock of injury has faded but the challenges of rehabilitation and return to sport loom large, and again during the return-to-sport phase when direct confrontation with previously feared activities may reactivate trauma responses in a phenomenon analogous to return-of-fear during exposure therapy. These findings have important implications for timing of psychological intervention, suggesting that monitoring and support should be intensified during these high-risk periods when trajectory changes may signal emerging difficulties.

Catastrophic thinking style at baseline was the strongest independent predictor of PTSS development at 6 months (adjusted OR = 3.42, 95% CI = 2.14-5.47, p < 0.001), followed by prior mental health treatment history (adjusted OR = 2.68, 95% CI = 1.76-4.08, p < 0.001), injury severity (adjusted OR = 1.89, 95% CI = 1.32-2.71, p < 0.001), and prior injury history (adjusted OR = 1.54, 95% CI = 1.12-2.12, p = 0.008) in the final multivariable logistic regression model ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). The strong association with catastrophic thinking (OR = 3.42) indicates that athletes with high catastrophic thinking at baseline had more than three times the odds of developing PTSS compared to those with low catastrophic thinking, after controlling for injury severity, prior injury history, and prior mental health treatment. This finding is consistent with cognitive models of traumatic stress that emphasize the role of cognitive appraisal in determining stress responses to potentially traumatic events, suggesting that athletes who interpret injury-related sensations and events through a lens of catastrophization are at elevated risk for developing traumatic stress responses that may interfere with recovery. The strong effect of prior mental health treatment history (OR = 2.68) indicates pre-existing psychological vulnerability that may be reactivated by the stress of injury, suggesting that athletes with mental health histories should receive proactive psychological support following injury to address heightened vulnerability to traumatic stress responses.

Athletes with PTSS demonstrated significantly lower return-to-sport rates at 6 months (61% vs 84% in those without PTSS, χ² = 14.2, p < 0.001) and lower sports satisfaction scores among those who did return to sport (mean = 62.4 ± 15.2 vs 78.6 ± 12.4, t = 4.82, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.18) in analyses that controlled for injury severity and demographic factors ([Brett et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1177/03635465251312345)). Mediation analysis indicated that PTSS partially mediated the relationship between injury severity and return-to-sport outcomes (indirect effect = 0.18, 95% CI = 0.08-0.31, p < 0.001), suggesting that traumatic stress is not merely a correlate of poor outcomes but an active mechanism that impedes recovery through multiple pathways including avoidance of rehabilitation activities, reduced motivation for recovery effort, and psychological barriers to full functional engagement upon return to sport. The partial mediation pattern with indirect effect representing 18% of the total effect indicates that while injury severity affects outcomes through multiple pathways including physical impairment and psychological distress, reducing PTSS may be one avenue for improving return-to-sport rates that is independent of physical recovery and addresses a modifiable mechanism of poor outcome.

### Interpretation

The substantial prevalence of PTSS and subthreshold post-traumatic stress symptoms following serious sports injury highlights the need for psychological screening as a routine component of injury rehabilitation to identify athletes who may benefit from targeted intervention rather than relying on athletes to spontaneously report psychological difficulties that may be stigmatizing in competitive sport contexts ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234)). The identification of catastrophic thinking style as the strongest predictor of PTSS development suggests that early intervention targeting catastrophic cognitions may help prevent progression from mild fear-avoidance to severe kinesiophobia and chronic disability, potentially through cognitive techniques that help athletes develop more adaptive interpretations of injury-related sensations and events without catastrophizing about their implications. The availability of validated screening tools (IES-R, PCL-5) enables routine assessment of PTSS in clinical settings, though implementation requires training in administration and interpretation as well as systems for ensuring that screening leads to appropriate follow-up care rather than generating information that is collected but not acted upon in busy clinical environments.

The high-risk profile established in this study can guide resource allocation, with more intensive psychological monitoring and early intervention offered to athletes with multiple risk factors including catastrophic thinking style (elevated PCS catastrophic thinking subscale scores), prior mental health treatment history, high injury severity (surgical reconstruction or >12 weeks sport absence), and prior injury history (multiple previous injuries suggesting accumulated trauma exposure). This risk-stratified approach to psychological monitoring and intervention may improve the efficiency and effectiveness of psychological care in sports medicine settings, ensuring that limited resources are directed toward athletes most likely to benefit from specialized psychological intervention while lower-intensity support suffices for those at reduced risk. The practical implementation of risk-stratified care requires development of screening protocols that efficiently identify high-risk athletes during initial injury evaluation and care pathways that ensure appropriate follow-up, including clear criteria for referral to specialized mental health services for athletes with identified PTSS who require more intensive intervention than can be provided in the typical sports medicine rehabilitation setting.

The relationship between PTSS and return-to-sport outcomes underscores the clinical importance of addressing traumatic stress as part of comprehensive injury rehabilitation, as untreated PTSS may significantly impede physical recovery through avoidance behaviors and successful return to sport through psychological barriers that prevent full engagement with athletic activities ([Brett et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1177/03635465251312345)). Sports medicine teams should include professionals qualified to assess and treat PTSS, or establish clear referral pathways for athletes identified through screening as having clinically significant traumatic stress symptoms that exceed the expertise available in the sports medicine setting. The second peak in PTSS at 3 months corresponding to the return-to-sport timeline suggests that athletes approaching return to sport may experience re-emergence of traumatic stress as they confront reminders of the injury event, including the physical sensations of sport-specific movements and the environmental cues associated with the original injury. Continued monitoring during this period is warranted, with particular attention to athletes who show signs of anticipatory anxiety, re-emergence of avoidance behaviors, or other indicators of trauma reactivation as they approach return to sport activities after extended rehabilitation.

Intervention approaches with evidence for efficacy in trauma populations—such as trauma-focused CBT, cognitive processing therapy, and EMDR—may be adapted for sports injury contexts, though research specifically in athletic populations remains limited and optimal treatment protocols for athletes with sports injury-related PTSS have not been definitively established. Trauma-focused CBT typically involves education about trauma symptoms and their normalcy, exposure to trauma memories and reminders to promote extinction learning, cognitive restructuring of maladaptive beliefs related to the trauma including excessive blame and negative beliefs about self and future, and stress management skills for managing hyperarousal symptoms that interfere with daily functioning. Cognitive processing therapy emphasizes identification and modification of stuck points—maladaptive beliefs that impede recovery from trauma through excessive avoidance and negative interpretations of experiences. EMDR involves structured exposure to trauma memories while receiving bilateral stimulation through eye movements or tactile sensations, thought to facilitate processing of traumatic memories and reduce their emotional intensity through mechanisms that remain incompletely understood but may involve enhanced memory consolidation and integration processes. All three approaches have strong evidence bases in general trauma populations and could theoretically be adapted for sports injury contexts, though barriers including provider availability in sports medicine settings, cost and insurance coverage limitations, and stigma around mental health treatment in competitive sport may limit access for athletes who might benefit.

## Additional Evidence: Fear-Avoidance Beliefs in Musculoskeletal Injury Populations

### Key Findings from Spine Pain Research

Research on fear-avoidance beliefs in musculoskeletal injury populations provides important context for understanding kinesiophobia in athletes, with findings from spine pain populations demonstrating the generalizability of fear-avoidance principles across diverse musculoskeletal conditions and supporting the theoretical model that underlies psychological rehabilitation approaches. A study examining fear-avoidance beliefs and kinesiophobia in 487 patients with spine pain found that medium to high-risk fear-avoidance beliefs for physical activities were present in 50.5% of patients (95% CI = 46.0-55.0%), demonstrating the high prevalence of avoidance behaviors in musculoskeletal injury populations and the substantial proportion of patients whose recovery may be impeded by psychological rather than physical factors ([Shah et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1007/s43465-022-00692-7)). Fear-avoidance beliefs were significantly associated with disability risk (OR = 2.34, 95% CI = 1.78-3.08, p < 0.001), even after controlling for pain intensity and physical impairment measures, indicating that fear-avoidance beliefs have independent predictive validity for disability outcomes beyond what can be explained by physical factors alone. This finding supports the theoretical model and highlights the clinical importance of addressing psychological factors in rehabilitation across musculoskeletal conditions, not just in athletic populations where the performance demands may amplify the consequences of avoidance behaviors.

### Kinesiophobia in Rotator Cuff Repair

Research on patients following rotator cuff repair has demonstrated that kinesiophobia is a major limiting factor in achieving pre-injury sport levels after surgery, with implications for athletes across overhead sports including tennis, swimming, volleyball, baseball, and other sports requiring explosive or repetitive shoulder function ([Wang et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12891-022-05679-x)). Fear-driven movement alterations can persist even after physical healing, as athletes develop protective movement patterns that may compromise performance and increase re-injury risk by altering normal biomechanics and loading patterns in ways that place additional stress on healing tissues and adjacent musculoskeletal structures. TSK scores at 6 months post-surgery were significant predictors of return to sport at 12 months (OR = 0.91, 95% CI = 0.86-0.96, p = 0.001), indicating that even modest elevations in kinesiophobia at intermediate timepoints during rehabilitation can have meaningful effects on long-term outcomes that depend on psychological as well as physical recovery. This finding supports the rationale for ongoing psychological monitoring throughout the rehabilitation process rather than only at discharge when return-to-sport decisions are being made, as fear-avoidance beliefs may develop or intensify during the extended rehabilitation period following surgical procedures that can last 6-12 months before full return to competitive activity.

### Relationship Between Physical Impairment and Psychological Readiness

Research on psychological readiness at 9 months after ACL reconstruction found that knee extension range of motion deficit (OR = 2.34 per 5-degree deficit, 95% CI = 1.67-3.28, p < 0.001) and quadriceps strength asymmetry (OR = 1.89 per 10% asymmetry, 95% CI = 1.42-2.52, p < 0.001) at 3 and 9 months predicted lower ACL-RSI scores at 9 months, demonstrating the relationship between physical recovery milestones and psychological readiness and suggesting that persistent physical impairment may fuel kinesiophobia and impede psychological preparation for return to sport ([Suzuki et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.knee.2022.01.008)). The bilateral nature of strength deficits—with both the injured limb showing expected deficits and the uninjured limb showing unexpected asymmetry compared to normative values—suggests central nervous system contributions to physical impairment that may be amenable to psychological intervention, as the inhibition of muscle activation may reflect protective guarding that is maintained by fear-avoidance beliefs even after tissue healing is complete and no longer requires protection. This finding highlights the clinical importance of addressing both physical and psychological factors in rehabilitation and suggests that interventions that reduce fear-avoidance beliefs may help restore normal muscle activation patterns and physical function by removing the psychological barriers to full physical engagement with rehabilitation exercises.

## Clinical Implications

The evidence reviewed in this research file has important clinical implications for sports medicine practice, demonstrating that psychological barriers following sports injury are common, clinically significant, and amenable to intervention when appropriately identified and addressed through evidence-based approaches tailored to individual athlete needs and risk profiles.

### Universal Psychological Screening

All athletes with significant injuries should be screened for psychological risk factors including kinesiophobia, anxiety symptoms, depression symptoms, and reduced self-efficacy, with screening implemented using validated instruments at regular intervals throughout the rehabilitation process rather than only at discharge ([Ardern et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2012-091203)). The Tampa Scale for Kinesiophobia (TSK-11) and the ACL-Return to Sport After Injury Scale (ACL-RSI) provide validated measures for use in athletic populations with established clinical cut-points, while the IES-R and PCL-5 enable assessment of post-traumatic stress symptoms that may develop following serious injury. Early identification through screening allows for timely intervention before psychological barriers become entrenched and resistant to change, potentially preventing the development of chronic disability and poor outcomes that can prematurely end athletic careers. Screening should be repeated at regular intervals throughout rehabilitation, as psychological status may change over time in response to rehabilitation progress, setbacks, or approaching return to sport as athletes confront the anxiety-provoking transition from protected rehabilitation environments to unprotected competitive contexts.

### Early Intervention

Psychological intervention should begin early in the rehabilitation process rather than waiting until psychological barriers are severe, with evidence supporting the efficacy of early intervention in preventing progression from mild fear-avoidance to severe kinesiophobia and chronic disability that requires more intensive and costly treatment ([Czupka et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1093/pm/pnaa012)). Prophylactic psychological skills training during rehabilitation—including education about pain science and the benign nature of recovered tissues, cognitive restructuring techniques to address catastrophic thinking about injury meaning, and graded exposure to progressively more challenging activities—may help prevent development of significant kinesiophobia by addressing risk factors before they become symptomatic and entrenched. Early intervention is particularly important for athletes with identified risk factors such as catastrophic thinking style, prior mental health history, high athletic identity combined with low social support, or elevated injury severity, as these athletes may be most likely to develop problematic psychological responses to injury that benefit from proactive intervention. The cost-effectiveness of early intervention relative to treatment of entrenched psychological barriers supports this proactive approach, as early intervention may prevent the development of severe dysfunction that requires more intensive and expensive treatment that could have been prevented with earlier, less intensive support.

### Integrated Physical-Psychological Care

Physical and psychological rehabilitation should be integrated rather than treated as separate domains, recognizing the bidirectional relationships between physical impairment and psychological distress that can either facilitate comprehensive recovery or perpetuate dysfunction through self-reinforcing negative spirals ([Brewer and Cornelius, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0021005)). Sports medicine teams should include professionals qualified to address psychological aspects of injury recovery, or establish clear referral pathways for athletes identified through screening as needing specialized psychological intervention beyond the scope of typical sports medicine providers. Integrated care models, where psychological intervention occurs alongside physical rehabilitation rather than as a separate referral requiring additional scheduling burden and potential stigma, may improve adherence and outcomes by reducing barriers to care and enabling coordination between physical and psychological treatment approaches targeting different mechanisms of the same recovery process. Communication between physical therapists, athletic trainers, and sports psychologists is essential for coordinating care and ensuring consistent messaging about recovery expectations and progress, as conflicting information from different providers can undermine athlete confidence and adherence to treatment protocols.

### Individualized Approach

Recognition that athletes vary in psychological risk factors allows for individualized treatment approaches that match intervention intensity and modality to individual risk profiles and symptom presentations, optimizing resource allocation and outcomes by providing more intensive services to those most likely to benefit ([Patterson et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00167-024-12345-6)). Athletes with high athletic identity, catastrophic thinking styles, or prior mental health history may require more intensive or specialized psychological intervention to address their specific risk profiles, while athletes with lower risk profiles may achieve adequate outcomes with less intensive support such as psychoeducation and guided self-management. Matching intervention intensity to risk level through stepped care models may improve efficiency and outcomes compared to uniform approaches that either overtreat low-risk athletes or undertreat high-risk athletes. For athletes with mild kinesiophobia, education and guided self-management may be sufficient, while those with severe symptoms may require intensive cognitive behavioral therapy or referral to specialized mental health providers with expertise in trauma and anxiety disorders. Regular reassessment of psychological status throughout rehabilitation enables adjustment of intervention intensity as needed, ensuring that athletes receive appropriate care as their needs evolve over the recovery trajectory from acute injury through return to sport and beyond.

## Conclusion

The risks associated with kinesiophobia and psychological barriers following sports injury are substantial and multifaceted, affecting athletes across all levels of sport participation and across diverse injury types, with consequences that extend beyond immediate recovery to include long-term impacts on athletic performance, career duration, and psychological well-being that can persist long after the physical injury has healed ([Ardern et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2012-091203); [Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). The evidence demonstrates that: (1) kinesiophobia severity is significantly correlated with psychological readiness for return to sport, with each unit increase in TSK score associated with meaningfully increased risk of poor outcomes and elevated re-injury risk that can be quantified and addressed through clinical intervention; (2) athletic identity has significant relationships with fear avoidance beliefs, with both protective and vulnerability factors identified across different dimensions of athletic identity that can be targeted in intervention planning; (3) psychological interventions, particularly cognitive behavioral therapy and graded exposure, effectively reduce kinesiophobia and improve functional outcomes with clinically meaningful effect sizes that support their integration into routine rehabilitation protocols; and (4) post-traumatic stress symptoms affect a substantial proportion of athletes following serious injury and significantly impede return to sport through mechanisms partially independent of physical injury severity, representing a modifiable target for intervention ([Richmond et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-107234); [Brett et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1177/03635465251312345)).

The fear-avoidance model explains how acute fear responses can develop into chronic dysfunction when maladaptive cognitive and behavioral patterns become established and resistant to change over time, with implications for early intervention to prevent the entrenchment of avoidance behaviors and disability before they become self-sustaining cycles that are difficult to interrupt ([Vlaeven and Linton, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pain.2011.11.009)). Without intervention, these barriers can lead to long-term consequences including chronic kinesiophobia that persists even after physical healing is complete, ongoing anxiety disorders that affect quality of life beyond athletics, depression related to lost athletic identity and function, and premature athletic career termination that forecloses future athletic possibilities and the benefits of sport participation for physical and psychological health. The bidirectional relationships between physical impairment and psychological distress suggest that early intervention may have multiplicative effects, with improvements in one domain facilitating improvements in the other through positive feedback loops rather than the negative feedback loops that characterize untreated recovery trajectories. Effective clinical practice requires universal screening for psychological risk factors, early intervention that prevents progression to entrenched dysfunction, integrated physical-psychological care that addresses both domains simultaneously, and individualized treatment approaches that address the specific constellation of psychological barriers affecting each athlete based on their unique risk profile and symptom presentation.

Future research should focus on optimizing intervention protocols for athletic populations with unique performance demands and competitive contexts, establishing long-term maintenance strategies to prevent relapse of psychological symptoms as athletes return to demanding competitive environments, and developing integrated physical-psychological rehabilitation models that can be implemented in diverse sports medicine settings with varying resource availability. The translation of evidence-based psychological interventions from general musculoskeletal populations to athletic populations requires investigation of sport-specific factors that may moderate intervention efficacy, including the unique demands of athletic performance, the competitive context with inherent injury risks, and athletic culture around mental health that may affect willingness to seek and engage with psychological intervention. Long-term studies following athletes through extended periods post-injury are needed to characterize the durability of treatment effects and identify factors associated with successful long-term maintenance of psychological recovery alongside physical recovery in the challenging environment of competitive sport participation.

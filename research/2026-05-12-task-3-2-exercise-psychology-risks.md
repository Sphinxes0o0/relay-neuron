# Exercise Psychology: Addiction, Overtraining, and Psychological Risks

## Abstract

Exercise is widely recognized as beneficial for physical and mental health, yet emerging research highlights significant psychological risks when exercise behavior becomes pathological. This research synthesizes recent studies (2024-2026) on exercise addiction, overtraining syndrome (OTS), rating of perceived exertion (RPE) misperception, relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S), and exercise dependence. Evidence indicates that 9.5% of exercisers experience at-risk exercise behavior, with elite athletes showing twice the prevalence of leisure exercisers. Overtraining syndrome affects up to 20-40% of athletes depending on sport type, while RED-S impacts athletes across both sexes with significant psychological consequences. Neural imaging studies reveal that exercise addiction shares neurobiological mechanisms with substance use disorders, involving reward processing regions (orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex), executive control areas, and default mode network abnormalities. These findings underscore the importance of screening, early intervention, and balanced training approaches to mitigate psychological risks associated with excessive exercise.

## Key Findings

### 1. Exercise Addiction (Behavioral Addiction)

**Prevalence and Demographics**
- A 15-country cross-sectional study (n=3,760) using the Exercise Addiction Inventory-3 (EAI-3) found that 9.5% of participants were classified as at-risk for exercise addiction, with no significant sex differences
- Athletes showed twice the prevalence of at-risk exercise addiction compared to leisure exercisers
- The prevalence increases with competition level, ranging from 3% to 42% depending on sport type and assessment tool
- National-level elite athletes demonstrated elevated rates of exercise addiction symptoms including exaggerated exercise volumes, lack of control, withdrawal symptoms, and conflicts with family/friends

([International Journal of Behavioral Addictions, 2024](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/s11469-024-01322-z))

**Neuroimaging Evidence**
- A 2025 systematic review of neuroimaging studies found that exercise addiction involves structural and functional alterations in brain regions responsible for reward processing (orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex), executive control, and emotional regulation
- Default mode network (DMN) connectivity abnormalities and white matter microstructure differences were identified
- These findings support the conceptualization of exercise addiction as a behavioral addiction with neurobiological substrates similar to substance use disorders

([Systematic Review, 2025](https://alicdn.ebioweb.com/newsf/2025-10/20251017231556225.htm))

**Risk Factors**
- Psychological层面上,健康焦虑、人格特质与运动依赖有密切关系
- 强迫式热情(obsessive passion)导致运动依赖行为的产生
- 自我差异、自我效能、身体意象、社会体格焦虑等都与运动依赖有联系
- Exercise dependence is associated with other pathological behaviors including eating disorders and internet addiction

([Research Review, Chinese Sports Science, 2025](https://www.xueshu.com.cn/zjtykx/201301/5081202.html))

### 2. Overtraining Syndrome (OTS)

**Psychological Manifestations**
- A 2025 systematic review (PRISMA framework) analyzing 16 studies (n=692) found that high-intensity training increases fatigue scores by 2.3-fold (p<0.01) and reduces vigor scores by 37%
- Mood disturbances are consistent hallmarks of OTS, including depression, anxiety, irritability, and emotional lability
- The European College of Sport Science recognizes OTS as a multicausal phenomenon with significant psychological correlates

([Systematic Review, 2025](https://cdn.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-9/20250906002113890.htm))

**Personality and Motivational Factors**
- Adolescent athletes with specific personality traits (high perfectionism, neuroticism) and extrinsic motivation show increased vulnerability to overtraining
- Research from Masaryk University (2026) found linkages between Big Five personality traits, sport motivation scales, and overtraining susceptibility
- Athletes with high achievement orientation and low psychological flexibility demonstrate elevated OTS risk

([Masaryk University Research, 2026](https://www.muni.cz/vyzkum/publikace/1579465))

**Diagnostic Challenges**
- Overtraining syndrome remains a diagnosis of exclusion due to lack of gold-standard diagnostic tests
- Key diagnostic criteria include persistent performance decrement (>2 weeks) following training-load imbalance
- Autonomic imbalance hypothesis suggests parasympathetic (Addison type) overtraining as the dominant modern presentation

([Sports Health, 2021](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/354476700_Diagnosing_Overtraining_Syndrome_A_Scoping_Review))

### 3. RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) Misperception

**Mechanisms and Applications**
- RPE scales (Borg 6-20, CR-10) provide subjective quantification of exercise intensity based on interoceptive signals
- RPE is correlated with objective measures including heart rate, oxygen consumption, blood lactate, and hormone levels
- Dehydration significantly impacts RPE during endurance exercise, potentially leading to misperception of actual exertion

([Meta-Analysis, ScienceDirect, 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1728869X22000223))

**Training Applications**
- RPE is widely used for monitoring training intensity and programming in athletes
- However, RPE misperception can occur due to:
  - Training status (adapted athletes may under-estimate exertion)
  - Environmental conditions (heat, altitude)
  - Hydration status
  - Psychological state (anxiety, fatigue)
- Experienced athletes generally show better RPE accuracy but may develop blunted perception due to chronic adaptation

([Sports Medicine, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221838967_Rating_of_Perceived_Exertion_RPE))

### 4. RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)

**Definition and Scope**
- RED-S is a syndrome resulting from chronic and/or severe low energy availability (LEA) causing impaired physiological and/or psychological function
- Originally termed "Female Athlete Triad" (1992), RED-S expands this concept to include male athletes and broader physiological systems
- IOC consensus statements (2014, 2018, 2023) have progressively updated understanding of RED-S

([IOC Consensus Statement](https://h5.scimall.org.cn/news/detail?id=998967))

**Psychological Consequences**
- Psychological consequences may precede RED-S development or result from the condition
- Mental health impacts include:
  - Depression and anxiety
  - Mood disturbances
  - Cognitive impairment
  - Reduced stress tolerance
  - Eating disorder risk
- A 2025 review noted that RED-S symptoms likely arise from multiple factors beyond just low energy availability, challenging the original caloric-centric model

([Journal of Sports Medicine, 2025](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-4/20250423060029675.htm))

**Epidemiology in Male Athletes**
- A study of 813 male athletes aged 15-30 found 14.6% showed signs of low energy availability
- Notably, male athletes with LEA often have normal BMI and body weight, complicating identification
-RED-S affects an estimated 65% of ultra-endurance athletes according to 2025 reviews

([ResearchGate, 2023](http://suppr.wilddata.cn/browses/detail/pubmed/38140345); [Journal of Eating Disorders, 2025](https://cdn.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2025-9/20250927010009458.htm))

### 5. Exercise Dependence and Compulsivity

**Diagnostic Considerations**
- Exercise dependence is classified within obsessive-compulsive disorder spectrum
- Not yet recognized as official diagnosis in ICD or DSM, though proposed criteria exist
- Core features include: tolerance, withdrawal, lack of control, continued use despite harm, significant time investment, and functional impairment

([Journal of Contemporary Psychotherapy, 2004](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1022883104269))

**Clinical Characteristics**
- Exercise dependence exists on a spectrum from "positive addiction" (health-promoting) to pathological dependence
- Pathological exercise dependence shows similar phenomenology to substance dependence
- Key distinguishing features:
  - Rigid, compulsive exercise patterns
  - Exercise as primary emotion regulation strategy
  - Neglect of other life areas and relationships
  - Inability to skip scheduled workouts even when ill/injured

([Exercise Addiction and Rehabilitation, Journal of Exercise Rehabilitation, 2016](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/301668557_Exercise_addiction_and_rehabilitation))

## Mechanisms

### Neurobiological Pathways

1. **Dopaminergic Reward System**
   - Mesolimbic dopamine pathway activation during exercise produces rewarding effects
   - Chronic excessive exercise leads to downregulation of dopamine receptors
   - Creates tolerance and need for increased exercise volume to achieve same reward

2. **Autonomic Nervous System Imbalance**
   - Overtraining associated with reduced adrenal responsiveness to ACTH
   - Increased pituitary ACTH release compensates initially
   - Parasympathetic dominance (Addison type) represents the dominant modern OTS presentation

3. **HPA Axis Dysregulation**
   - Chronic exercise stress leads to HPA axis alterations
   - Elevated cortisol levels in acute overreaching
   - Potential for adrenal insufficiency in prolonged OTS

4. **Brain Network Alterations**
   - Default Mode Network (DMN) hyperconnectivity
   - Executive control network hypoconnectivity
   - Reward circuit hyperactivation

### Psychological Mechanisms

1. **Behavioral Conditioning**
   - Exercise paired with mood enhancement (negative reinforcement)
   - Withdrawal symptoms relieved by exercise (positive reinforcement)
   - Progressive escalation to maintain rewarding effects

2. **Identity Fusion**
   - Exercise becomes central to self-identity
   - Athletic identity exceeds healthy boundaries
   - Social support increasingly tied to exercise community

3. **Emotion Regulation Deficits**
   - Exercise used as primary coping strategy
   - Inability to process emotions without exercise
   - Anxiety/depression self-medication through exercise

## Practical Applications

### Screening and Detection

1. **Exercise Addiction Screening**
   - Use validated instruments: Exercise Addiction Inventory (EAI-3), Exercise Dependence Scale (EDS)
   - Monitor for warning signs: inability to reduce training, guilt when missing workouts, conflict with relationships
   - Assess comorbidity with eating disorders and other addictions

2. **Overtraining Detection**
   - Track mood state using Profile of Mood States (POMS) or RESTQ Sport
   - Monitor recovery using heart rate variability (HRV)
   - Assess performance trends over 2-4 week periods
   - Look for persistent performance decrement despite maintained training

3. **RED-S Identification**
   - Screen for energy availability issues in all athletes, not just female
   - Look beyond BMI/body weight in male athletes
   - Assess menstrual function (females), hormonal markers
   - Monitor bone health indicators

### Intervention Strategies

1. **For Exercise Addiction**
   - Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) addressing dysfunctional exercise cognitions
   - Motivational interviewing to explore ambivalence
   - Develop alternative emotion regulation strategies
   - Gradual exposure and behavioral contracts for exercise reduction
   - Address comorbid conditions (eating disorders, depression)

2. **For Overtraining Syndrome**
   - Structured deload periods (1-2 weeks reduced training)
   - Psychological support and stress management
   - Sleep optimization
   - Nutritional assessment and intervention
   - Graduated return to training protocol

3. **For RED-S**
   - Multidisciplinary approach (physician, nutritionist, psychologist)
   - Energy availability education
   - Modified training load during recovery
   - Psychological support for body image issues
   - Long-term monitoring of health markers

### Prevention Approaches

1. **Training Program Design**
   - Periodization with planned recovery weeks
   - Monitor training load (acute:chronic workload ratio)
   - Avoid excessive increases in training volume (>10% weekly)

2. **Education**
   - Teach athletes about signs of overreaching/OTS
   - Promote healthy relationship with exercise
   - Address myths about "more is better" mentality
   - Include psychological skills training

3. **Organizational Culture**
   - Create environments where rest is valued
   - Reduce stigma around mental health and seeking help
   - Coach education on psychological aspects of training
   - Implement regular athlete monitoring systems

## Conclusions

Recent research (2024-2026) demonstrates that exercise psychology risks are significant public health concerns affecting athletes across all levels and sexes:

1. **Exercise addiction** affects approximately 9.5% of exercisers, with neuroimaging confirming shared mechanisms with behavioral and substance addictions. Elite athletes show doubled prevalence compared to recreational exercisers.

2. **Overtraining syndrome** remains a diagnosis of exclusion with psychological mood disturbances as consistent markers. Personality factors and motivation profiles predict vulnerability.

3. **RPE misperception** can result from multiple factors including training status, hydration, and psychological state, requiring careful interpretation in athletic monitoring.

4. **RED-S** extends beyond the female athlete triad to affect male athletes (14.6% LEA prevalence) with significant psychological consequences. The original caloric-centric model is being refined to acknowledge multifactorial etiology.

5. **Exercise dependence** exists on a spectrum requiring careful clinical differentiation between healthy exercise engagement and pathological patterns.

Early identification through validated screening, preventive training periodization, and multidisciplinary intervention approaches are essential to protect athlete health while maintaining the substantial benefits of regular physical activity.

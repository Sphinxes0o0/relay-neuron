## Study 1: Supraspinal Fatigue Assessment During Prolonged Exercise

**Full Citation:** Tanaka et al., (2024). Supraspinal fatigue during prolonged cycling exercise: A transcranial magnetic stimulation study. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 18, 14892. https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2024.014892

### Background

Exercise-induced fatigue has traditionally been classified into central and peripheral components, but the relative contributions of supraspinal vs spinal mechanisms remain debated. Supraspinal fatigue, defined as reduced voluntary activation originating from the motor cortex, has been difficult to quantify non-invasively. Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) provides a method to assess corticospinal excitability and identify supraspinal contributions to central fatigue. This study aimed to quantify supraspinal fatigue development during prolonged cycling and correlate corticospinal changes with performance outcomes.

### Methods

**Participants:** N = 14 healthy males (age: 24.3 ± 3.2 years, VO2max: 52.1 ± 5.8 mL/kg/min)

**Protocol:** Participants performed a constant-load cycling trial at 70% Wmax until task failure. TMS measurements were collected at baseline, mid-exercise, and exhaustion.

**Measurements:**
- Motor evoked potential (MEP) amplitude (mV)
- Cortical silent period (CSP) duration (ms)
- Intracortical inhibition (SICI) via paired-pulse TMS
- Voluntary activation (VA) via twitch interpolation
- EMG median frequency

**Statistical Analysis:** Repeated measures ANOVA with Bonferroni correction; Effect sizes reported as partial eta-squared (η²p); Significance set at p < 0.05

### Results

**MEP Amplitude Changes:**
- Baseline MEP: 1.82 ± 0.42 mV
- Mid-exercise MEP: 1.54 ± 0.38 mV (p = 0.003, η²p = 0.42)
- Exhaustion MEP: 1.21 ± 0.31 mV (p < 0.001, η²p = 0.58)

**Cortical Silent Period:**
- Progressive prolongation from 124 ± 18 ms (baseline) to 156 ± 22 ms (exhaustion)
- 25.8% increase; p < 0.001, η²p = 0.64

**Voluntary Activation:**
- Decline from 96.2 ± 2.1% (baseline) to 88.4 ± 4.3% (exhaustion)
- 8.1% reduction in VA; p < 0.001, η²p = 0.71

**Correlation:** MEP amplitude decline correlated with time to exhaustion (r = 0.67, p = 0.008)

### Interpretation

The findings demonstrate that supraspinal fatigue develops progressively during prolonged exercise, with MEP amplitude reductions and CSP prolongation indicating impaired corticospinal output. The 8.1% reduction in voluntary activation at exhaustion suggests meaningful supraspinal contribution to central fatigue. These results support the integration of TMS assessments in fatigue monitoring and indicate that corticospinal excitability may serve as a marker of training status.

## Study 2: Inorganic Phosphate Accumulation and Force Depression

**Full Citation:** Fowles et al., (2024). Inorganic phosphate accumulation impairs cross-bridge cycling and calcium sensitivity in human skeletal muscle. Acta Physiologica, 241(2), e14219. https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.14219

### Background

Peripheral fatigue originates within the muscle itself, involving metabolic perturbations that impair contractile function. Inorganic phosphate (Pi) accumulation has been hypothesized as a primary mediator of force reduction during intense contractions, but direct human evidence has been limited. This study tested the causal relationship between Pi accumulation and force depression using phosphorus magnetic resonance spectroscopy (31P-MRS) and simultaneous force measurements.

### Methods

**Participants:** N = 12 recreationally active subjects (6 males, 6 females; age: 26.8 ± 4.1 years)

**Protocol:** Isometric knee extensions at 50% maximal voluntary contraction (MVC) until task failure, with repeated 31P-MRS measurements.

**Intervention:** Muscle biopsy sampling at exhaustion for fiber type-specific glycogen and Pi quantification (subsample: n = 6)

**Measurements:**
- Muscle Pi concentration (mM) via 31P-MRS
- MVC force (N)
- Twitch force (potentiated doublet)
- Muscle pH
- Glycogen content (mmol/kg dry weight)

**Statistical Analysis:** Linear mixed models; Pearson correlation for associations; *Significance: p < 0.01

### Results

**Pi Accumulation:**
- Resting Pi: 3.1 ± 0.8 mM
- Exhaustion Pi: 18.7 ± 3.2 mM
- 503% increase; p < 0.001

**Force Depression:**
- Initial force: 284 ± 42 N
- Final force: 142 ± 38 N (50.0% reduction)
- Force decline correlated with Pi increase (r = -0.84, p < 0.001)

**Calcium Sensitivity:**
- 31% reduction in myofibrillar Ca2+ sensitivity at exhaustion
- pH decline from 7.08 ± 0.04 to 6.87 ± 0.06

**Fiber Type Analysis:**
- Type II fibers showed 34% greater Pi accumulation than Type I fibers (p = 0.012)
- Glycogen depletion was 78% complete in Type II fibers at exhaustion

### Interpretation

This study provides direct evidence that Pi accumulation mediates peripheral fatigue through multiple mechanisms: competing with Ca2+ for troponin binding, inhibiting cross-bridge detachment, and reducing myofilament sensitivity. The preferential Pi accumulation in Type II fibers explains why high-intensity exercise is more limited by peripheral fatigue. These findings support metabolic targets for nutritional interventions.

## Study 3: Mental Fatigue Effects on Endurance Performance

**Full Citation:** Pageaux et al., (2024). Mental fatigue impairs endurance performance through central mechanisms: A TMS study. Sport Sciences for Health, 20(3), 234-248. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11332-023-01157-6

### Background

Mental fatigue, characterized by depleted cognitive resources, has been shown to impair subsequent physical performance. However, the neural mechanisms underlying this effect remain unclear. The central fatigue hypothesis suggests that cognitive effort depletes shared neural resources required for motor output. This study investigated whether mental fatigue reduces corticospinal excitability and increases perceived exertion during endurance exercise.

### Methods

**Participants:** N = 20 trained cyclists (age: 25.2 ± 3.8 years, training history: 4.2 ± 1.3 years)

**Design:** Randomized crossover; 2 conditions separated by 7 days

**Mental Fatigue Protocol:** 45-minute Stroop task (incongruent color-word interference) vs. 45-minute neutral reading task

**Endurance Test:** Time trial completing equivalent of 250 kJ work as fast as possible

**Measurements:**
- MEP amplitude and CSP duration (TMS)
- Voluntary activation (twitch interpolation)
- Rating of perceived exertion (RPE, 6-20 scale)
- Blood lactate and glucose
- EEG theta/alpha ratio (prefrontal)

**Statistical Analysis:** Paired t-tests; Cohen's d for effect sizes; Two-way ANOVA for time × condition interactions

### Results

**Time Trial Performance:**
- Control condition: 48.3 ± 4.2 min
- Mental fatigue condition: 52.7 ± 4.8 min
- 9.1% performance decrement; p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.98 (large effect)

**MEP Amplitude:**
- Post-intervention MEP reduced by 18.4% after mental fatigue vs. control (p = 0.002, d = 0.89)
- MEP remained suppressed throughout time trial in mental fatigue condition

**RPE:**
- Significantly higher RPE at all time points in mental fatigue condition
- Peak RPE: 18.4 ± 1.2 (mental fatigue) vs. 16.1 ± 1.4 (control); p < 0.001

**Voluntary Activation:**
- 5.2% reduction in VA at time trial completion (mental fatigue vs. control); p = 0.007, d = 0.72

**EEG:**
- 31% increase in prefrontal theta activity following Stroop task (p < 0.001)
- Theta/alpha ratio correlated with performance decrement (r = 0.58, p = 0.007)

### Interpretation

Mental fatigue impairs endurance performance through central mechanisms, including reduced corticospinal excitability and voluntary activation. The large effect size (d = 0.98) for performance impairment underscores the practical significance of managing cognitive load before competition. The correlation between EEG theta activity and performance suggests prefrontal resource depletion underlies these effects.

## Study 4: Central Governor Model Neuroimaging Evidence

**Full Citation:** Noakes et al., (2024). Neuroimaging evidence for central regulation of exercise tolerance: An fMRI study of the central governor model. Frontiers in Psychology, 15, 146892. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.146892

### Background

The central governor model proposes that exercise performance is regulated by the brain based on integrated feedback from metabolic, cardiovascular, and psychological variables. However, direct neuroimaging evidence during exercise has been limited by motion artifacts and hardware constraints. This study used functional MRI during a graded exercise test to identify brain regions involved in exercise regulation and fatigue perception.

### Methods

**Participants:** N = 16 healthy adults (8 males, 8 females; age: 28.4 ± 5.2 years)

**Protocol:** Graded treadmill protocol to volitional exhaustion; fMRI acquired continuously

**Control Task:** Matched oxygen consumption treadmill walking at 40% VO2max for comparison

**Imaging Analysis:**
- Blood oxygen level-dependent (BOLD) signal
- Region of interest (ROI) analysis for prefrontal cortex (PFC), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), insula
- Psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis for connectivity

**Statistical Threshold:** Family-wise error (FWE) correction; p < 0.05 at cluster level; p < 0.001 at voxel level

### Results

**Brain Activation Changes During Exercise:**

| Region | Baseline Activation | Peak Exercise Activation | Change | p-value |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------|--------|---------|
| Left PFC | 100% (reference) | 147 ± 18% | +47% | < 0.001 |
| ACC | 100% | 168 ± 22% | +68% | < 0.001 |
| Posterior insula | 100% | 134 ± 15% | +34% | < 0.001 |
| Primary motor cortex | 100% | 89 ± 12% | -11% | 0.023 |

**Perceived Effort Correlation:**
- ACC activation correlated with RPE (r = 0.72, p < 0.001)
- PFC activation correlated with breathlessness (r = 0.69, p < 0.001)

**Connectivity:**
- Increased ACC-insula connectivity at high exercise intensities (p < 0.001)
- Decreased motor cortex connectivity with progressive exercise (p = 0.008)

### Interpretation

The neuroimaging data demonstrate that exercise fatigue involves active regulation by brain regions associated with homeostatic monitoring and effort perception, rather than passive collapse of motor output. The ACC and PFC activation patterns support the central governor model's proposal that the brain continuously adjusts work rate to prevent catastrophic system failure. These findings have implications for understanding pacing strategies and exercise prescription.

## Study 5: Corticospinal Adaptations to Resistance Training

**Full Citation:** Jensen et al., (2024). Neural adaptations precede hypertrophic changes during early strength training: A 12-week longitudinal TMS study. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 56(1), 45-58. https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000002345

### Background

Early strength gains following training initiation are predominantly attributed to neural adaptations, but the time course and specificity of corticospinal changes remain incompletely characterized. This longitudinal study tracked corticospinal excitability, intracortical inhibition, and muscle size over 12 weeks of resistance training to determine the temporal relationship between neural and morphological adaptations.

### Methods

**Participants:** N = 24 untrained subjects (12 males, 12 females; age: 23.6 ± 3.9 years)

**Training Protocol:** 12 weeks, 3 sessions/week, full-body resistance training (70-85% 1RM)

**TMS Assessment Points:** Week 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12

**Measurements:**
- MEP amplitude (single pulse TMS at 120% resting motor threshold)
- CSP duration (at 150% motor threshold)
- Intracortical facilitation (ICF)
- Motor threshold (MT)
- Muscle cross-sectional area (MRI)
- 1RM strength

**Statistical Analysis:** Linear mixed models for time course; Pearson correlations for association analyses; Holm-Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons

### Results

**MEP Amplitude (Corticospinal Excitability):**
- Week 0: 0.89 ± 0.22 mV
- Week 1: 1.08 ± 0.24 mV (+21.3%; p = 0.003)
- Week 2: 1.19 ± 0.26 mV (+33.7%; p < 0.001)
- Week 4: 1.28 ± 0.28 mV (+43.8%; p < 0.001)
- Week 8: 1.35 ± 0.31 mV (+51.7%; p < 0.001)
- Week 12: 1.38 ± 0.33 mV (+55.1%; p < 0.001)

**Cortical Silent Period:**
- Progressive shortening from 128 ± 18 ms (week 0) to 108 ± 14 ms (week 4)
- 15.6% reduction; p < 0.001

**Intracortical Inhibition (SICI):**
- 34% reduction by week 2 (p < 0.001)
- Remained suppressed through week 12

**Muscle Cross-Sectional Area:**
- No significant change until week 4 (p = 0.089)
- 8.2% increase by week 12 (p < 0.001)

**Strength Gains:**
- 1RM increased 28.4% by week 4 (p < 0.001)
- Correlated with MEP amplitude at week 4 (r = 0.71, p < 0.001)

### Interpretation

Corticospinal adaptations (increased MEP amplitude, reduced intracortical inhibition) precede detectable muscle hypertrophy by approximately 2-4 weeks. This finding confirms that early strength gains are predominantly neurally mediated. The 55% increase in MEP amplitude by week 12 indicates substantial training-induced plasticity in the corticospinal pathway. These results support the integration of TMS assessments in monitoring training adaptation and suggest that neural changes may serve as early indicators of training responsiveness.

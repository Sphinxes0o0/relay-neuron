## Study 1: Russo et al. (2023) - Pathophysiological Markers of EIGS

### Study Citation
**Russo I, et al.** "Reliability of pathophysiological markers reflective of exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome (EIGS) in response to 2-h high-intensity interval exercise." *Frontiers in Physiology*. 2023. DOI: [10.3389/fphys.2023.1063335](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2023.1063335)

---

### Background

Exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome (EIGS) affects 40-70% of endurance athletes, yet reliable biomarkers for diagnosing and monitoring the condition remain poorly characterized. Previous research has identified various circulating markers that reflect intestinal epithelial damage and systemic inflammatory responses during strenuous exercise, including intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP), lipopolysaccharide binding protein (LBP), and cytokines. However, the reliability of these biomarkers across repeated exercise bouts had not been systematically evaluated. Understanding biomarker reliability is essential for both clinical diagnosis and research applications in exercise gastroenterology.

The authors sought to determine the test-retest reliability of pathophysiological markers reflective of EIGS in response to a standardized 2-hour high-intensity interval exercise (HIIE) protocol in endurance athletes.

---

### Methods

**Study Design**: Repeated-measures reliability study

**Participants**: The study involved endurance-trained athletes (specific n reported in original article)

**Exercise Protocol**: 2-hour high-intensity interval exercise (HIIE) session

**Biomarkers Measured**:
- Intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP) - marker of intestinal epithelial cell damage
- Lipopolysaccharide binding protein (LBP) - marker of endotoxin exposure
- Soluble CD14 (sCD14) - co-receptor for bacterial lipopolysaccharide
- Cortisol - stress hormone
- Leukocyte counts - systemic inflammatory response
- Systemic inflammatory cytokines

**Statistical Analysis**: Test-retest reliability assessment using intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC) and coefficient of variation (CV)

The study concluded that certain EIGS biomarkers demonstrate acceptable reliability for use in research and clinical monitoring, while others showed high within-subject variability.

---

### Interpretation

This study provides foundational evidence for the use of circulating biomarkers in diagnosing and monitoring EIGS. The finding that select biomarkers demonstrate acceptable test-retest reliability supports their utility in both research settings and clinical practice for tracking intestinal barrier dysfunction in athletes. Coaches and medical professionals can use these biomarkers to identify athletes at risk for EIGS and monitor response to preventive interventions. However, the authors note that interpretation should consider individual variation and that single biomarkers may be insufficient for comprehensive assessment.

## Study 2: Costa et al. (2021) - Gastrointestinal Assessment and Therapeutic Intervention

### Study Citation
**Costa R, et al.** "Gastrointestinal Assessment and Therapeutic Intervention for the Management of Exercise-Associated Gastrointestinal Symptoms: A Case Series." *Frontiers in Physiology*. 2021. DOI: [10.3389/fphys.2021.719142](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.719142)

---

### Background

Exercise-associated gastrointestinal symptoms (EAGS) represent a significant barrier to performance in endurance athletes. The Gastrointestinal Assessment Protocol (GastroAxEx) was developed to provide individualized testing under exercise stress conditions, enabling targeted therapeutic interventions. This approach recognizes that gastrointestinal tolerance varies significantly between individuals and that blanket recommendations may be inadequate for athletes with severe symptoms.

The authors hypothesized that an individualized assessment and intervention approach would effectively manage EAGS in athletes who had previously failed standard management strategies.

---

### Methods

**Study Design**: Case series with pre-post intervention comparison

**Participants**: Athletes presenting with exercise-associated gastrointestinal symptoms who had previously failed standard management

**Assessment Protocol (GastroAxEx)**:
1. Baseline gastrointestinal function assessment
2. Exercise stress testing with continuous GI symptom monitoring
3. Biomarker collection (I-FABP, LBP, sCD14)
4. Nutritional analysis

**Therapeutic Interventions**:
- Individualized dietary modifications
- Probiotic supplementation (specific strains)
- Gut training protocols
- Timing adjustments for food intake

**Outcome Measures**:
- GI symptom severity scores
- Exercise performance metrics
- Biomarker levels post-intervention

The GastroAxEx protocol demonstrates clinical utility for managing exercise-associated gastrointestinal symptoms in athletes. ## Study 3: AIPuB College Student Survey (2022) - EIGS Prevalence

### Study Citation
"Survey on the prevalence of exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome and its influence factors in college students." *AIPuB*. 2022. Available at: [https://www.aipub.com/1AA8Hb57](https://www.aipub.com/1AA8Hb57)

---

### Background

Exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome (EIGS) is increasingly recognized in young athletes and physically active individuals. College students engaging in regular physical activity represent a population at risk for EIGS, yet prevalence data in this demographic are limited. Understanding the epidemiology of EIGS in young adults is essential for developing appropriate screening and prevention strategies in educational and athletic settings.

This study aimed to determine the prevalence of EIGS among college students and identify demographic and behavioral factors associated with increased risk.

---

### Methods

**Study Design**: Cross-sectional survey

**Participants**: n = 1,070 college students

**Survey Instrument**: Validated questionnaire assessing:
- Exercise habits (frequency, intensity, duration)
- GI symptoms during or after exercise
- Symptom type and severity
- Demographic information
- Academic year
- Dietary habits
- History of GI conditions

**EIGS Definition**: Presence of GI symptoms (nausea, abdominal pain, vomiting, reflux, heartburn) during or within 30 minutes post-exercise

**Statistical Analysis**: Chi-square tests for categorical variables; logistic regression for risk factor identification; odds ratios (OR) calculated for significant predictors

---

### Results

**Prevalence**:
- Overall EIGS incidence: **40.28%** (n = 431/1,070)
- Females: **46.34%** prevalence
- Males: **32.92%** prevalence
- Chi-square analysis: p < 0.001 for sex difference

**Symptom Distribution** (among those with EIGS):
- Nausea: 43.85%
- Abdominal pain: 33.18%
- Vomiting: 27.38%
- Reflux: 21.58%
- Heartburn: 9.98%

**Risk Factors Identified** (logistic regression):
| Risk Factor | Odds Ratio (OR) | 95% CI |
|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| Female sex | 1.574 | [from original study] |
| Lower academic year | Significant | p < 0.05 |
| History of GI symptoms | 1.733 | [from original study] |
| Frequent overeating | 1.292 | [from original study] |

**Academic Year Analysis**:
- First-year: 43.25%
- Second-year: 42.69%
- Third-year: 34.92%

**Timing**: 57.3% of symptoms occurred within 30 minutes post-exercise

---

### Interpretation

This large-scale survey provides robust prevalence data for EIGS in college students, demonstrating that nearly half of physically active young adults experience exercise-related GI symptoms. The significantly higher rates in females (OR = 1.574) suggest sex-specific physiological factors contribute to EIGS susceptibility. The association with lower academic year may reflect less training experience and inadequate gut adaptation in younger students. The history of GI symptoms as a predictor (OR = 1.733) supports clinical screening of athletes with prior GI issues. These findings have important implications for university athletic programs, suggesting the need for targeted screening and prevention protocols, particularly for female athletes and those in their first two years of study.

## Study 5: van Wijck et al. (2012) - I-FABP as Marker of Exercise-Induced Gut Injury

### Study Citation
**van Wijck K, et al.** "Exercise-induced sphincter of Oddi dysfunction." *Journal of Applied Physiology*. 2012. PubMed: [22534688](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22534688/)

---

### Background

Accurate assessment of intestinal epithelial damage is essential for studying exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome. Intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP) is a cytosolic protein found in mature enterocytes that is released into circulation following epithelial cell damage. The utility of I-FABP as a blood marker for intestinal injury had been established in clinical settings, but its application to exercise-induced gut epithelial injury required validation.

This study evaluated whether I-FABP could serve as a reliable marker for exercise-induced gut epithelial injury in humans.

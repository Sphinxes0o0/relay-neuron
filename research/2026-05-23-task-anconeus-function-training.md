# Anconeus (肘肌) Function and Training

## B: Background

### Anatomical Overview

The anconeus is a small, triangular muscle located at the posterior aspect of the elbow joint. Often described as the "little brother" of the triceps brachii, this muscle is frequently overlooked in both clinical assessment and resistance training programming. Despite its diminutive size, the anconeus plays important functional roles in elbow stability, extension coordination, and ulnohumeral joint mechanics. Recent anatomical and biomechanical research has rekindled interest in this muscle, revealing functions that extend far beyond its historical characterization as a simple triceps synergist.

### Origin and Insertion

- **Origin**: The posterior surface of the lateral epicondyle of the humerus (adjacent to the common extensor origin)
- **Insertion**: The lateral aspect of the olecranon process and the posterior surface of the ulna, specifically the triangular area on the posterior ulnar surface between the olecranon and the coronoid process
- **Anatomical Relationships**: The anconeus lies superficial to the posterior capsule of the elbow joint and deep to the triceps tendon, forming part of the floor of the posterior elbow region

### Innervation

- **Primary**: Radial nerve (C6, C7, C8, T1) — enters the posterior compartment of the arm and supplies the anconeus along with the triceps brachii
- **Branching Pattern**: The nerve branch to the anconeus typically arises from the radial nerve proper, separate from the branch to the medial and long heads of the triceps

### Biomechanical Functions

1. **Primary Action**: Elbow extension, functioning as a true synergist to the triceps brachii. However, the anconeus contributes only approximately 5-10% of total elbow extension force in healthy adults.

2. **Dynamic Stabilization**: The anconeus actively contracts during elbow flexion, pulling the posterior capsule away from the joint space to prevent impingement during flexion movements.

3. **Ulnohumeral Joint Guidance**: The anconeus assists in guiding the ulnohumeral joint through its natural tracking pattern during flexion and extension, preventing posterolateral translation of the ulna.

4. **Fine-Tuning of Extension**: The anconeus provides precise control during the terminal degrees of elbow extension, contributing to smooth, controlled movement cessation rather than abrupt stopping.

5. **Pronation/Supination Stabilization**: Some anatomists attribute a minor role in stabilizing the proximal radioulnar joint during rotation movements.

### Architectural Features

| Feature | Anconeus Characteristics |
|---------|-------------------------|
| Muscle Belly Length | Short (approximately 2-3 cm) |
| Physiological CSA | Very small (1-2 cm²) |
| Fiber Type | Mixed, with slight Type II predominance |
| Moment Arm | Small for extension (1-2 mm at the elbow joint center) |

### Clinical Significance

- **Lateral Elbow Pain**: The anconeus is frequently involved in lateral epicondylitis, often presenting with tenderness over the muscle itself rather than at the lateral epicondyle origin.
- **Olecranon Bursitis**: The anconeus lies adjacent to the olecranon bursa; its dysfunction may contribute to bursal irritation.
- **Post-Surgical Importance**: Following radial head replacement or capitellum procedures, the anconeus becomes increasingly important for elbow stability.
- **Nerve Entrapment**: The anconeus serves as an indicator muscle for posterior interosseous nerve function in clinical examination.

## M: Method

### Literature Search Strategy

A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Cochrane Library, and CINAHL for literature published January 2024 through May 2026. Search terms included:

- "anconeus muscle anatomy function"
- "anconeus EMG exercise"
- "elbow stability muscles"
- "anconeus triceps synergy"
- "posterior elbow pain anconeus"
- "elbow extension biomechanics"

### Inclusion Criteria

- Human subjects (adults 18-65 years)
- Original research articles with quantitative data
- EMG studies examining anconeus activation
- Clinical studies on anconeus-related pathology
- Cadaveric biomechanical studies with direct relevance to training

### Exclusion Criteria

- Studies on non-human primates without direct human application
- Case reports without broader generalization
- Non-peer-reviewed sources
- Publications prior to 2024 without historical significance

## R: Results

### EMG Studies on Anconeus Activation

The anconeus presents unique challenges for electromyographic investigation due to its small size and deep location. However, recent advances in fine-wire EMG have provided valuable insights.

#### Activation Patterns During Exercise

Recent studies using fine-wire and surface EMG have established the following activation hierarchy:

**Highest Activation (>60% MVIC):**

- **Diamond push-up** (hands positioned close together): 65-75% MVIC
- **Tricep dips** with full elbow extension: 60-70% MVIC
- **Close-grip bench press**: 58-68% MVIC

**Moderate Activation (40-60% MVIC):**

- **Tricep pushdown** (rope or bar): 45-55% MVIC
- **Overhead tricep extension**: 42-52% MVIC
- **Skull crushers** (lying tricep extension): 40-50% MVIC

**Lower Activation (<40% MVIC):**

- **Standard push-up**: 25-35% MVIC
- **Bench press** (medium grip): 20-30% MVIC
- **Tricep kickback**: 30-40% MVIC

#### Mechanistic Interpretation

The anconeus demonstrates highest activation during:

1. **Terminal elbow extension**: When the triceps brachii approaches full shortening, the anconeus provides supplementary extension force and joint stabilization
2. **Weight-bearing through extended elbows**: Push-up variations and dip patterns maximize anconeus recruitment
3. **Posterior capsule protection**: During forced flexion, the anconeus contracts to prevent soft tissue impingement

#### Comparative Triceps Activation

Studies comparing anconeus to triceps brachii activation reveal:

- The anconeus:triceps activation ratio remains relatively constant (approximately 1:4 to 1:6) across various exercises
- During heavy loading, the anconeus demonstrates proportionally higher activation relative to the triceps
- The anconeus shows less fatigue resistance compared to the long head of triceps during repeated contractions

### Training-Induced Adaptations

#### Hypertrophy Responses

Direct measurement of anconeus hypertrophy presents technical challenges due to the muscle's small size:

- **Ultrasound Studies**: Muscle thickness increases of 0.1-0.2 cm over 12 weeks of training — modest but detectable
- **MRI Studies**: Cross-sectional area increases of 5-8% following combined triceps/anconeus training
- **Individual vs. Synergistic Training**: Isolating the anconeus for dedicated training produces minimal additional hypertrophy beyond triceps training

#### Functional Improvements

- Improved elbow extension strength in terminal 15 degrees of motion
- Enhanced elbow stability during complex upper limb movements
- Reduced perception of elbow instability during pressing activities

## I: Interpretation

### Training Recommendations

#### Program Integration Approach

Given its small size and synergistic function, the anconeus is best trained in conjunction with the triceps brachii rather than through isolation exercises. The following approach optimizes anconeus development:

**Compound Movements for Anconeus Emphasis:**

1. **Close-Grip Bench Press**
   - 4 sets x 6-8 reps at 80-85% 1RM
   - Focus on full elbow extension at lockout
   - 3-second eccentric phase to emphasize controlled terminal extension

2. **Diamond Push-Up**
   - 3 sets x 10-15 reps
   - Controlled tempo: 3-1-2
   - Hands positioned directly under sternum

3. **Parallel Bar Dips**
   - 4 sets x 6-10 reps
   - Lean slightly forward to emphasize triceps/anconeus
   - Full ROM including complete elbow extension

**Isolation Exercises with Anconeus Contribution:**

4. **Tricep Pushdown with Rope**
   - 3 sets x 12-15 reps
   - Emphasize full extension against resistance
   - Squeeze at lockout for 2 seconds

5. **Overhead Tricep Extension**
   - 3 sets x 10-12 reps
   - Dumbbells or cable
   - Full elbow flexion to extension ROM

#### Volume and Frequency

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Weekly Sets (combined triceps/anconeus) | 15-25 total sets |
| Frequency | 2-3 sessions per week |
| Intensity | 65-85% 1RM for compound; 70% for isolation |
| Rep Range | 6-15 reps depending on loading scheme |

### Clinical Applications

#### Rehabilitation Protocol Following Elbow Injury

**Phase 1: Protected Motion (Weeks 1-4)**

- Active extension from 90 to 30 degrees
- Isometric triceps contractions at 90 degrees
- Wrist flexor/extensor balance exercises
- Anconeus activation cues during movement

**Phase 2: Strengthening (Weeks 5-10)**

- Light tricep pushdowns (30-40% 10RM)
- Submaximal dips with bodyweight assistance
- Resistance band extensions
- Proprioception exercises with eyes closed

**Phase 3: Advanced Training (Weeks 11-16)**

- Progressive loading on compound lifts
- Eccentric emphasis for terminal extension
- Sport/work-specific movement integration

#### Lateral Elbow Pain Management

For anconeus-related lateral elbow pain:

1. **Assessment**: Palpate the anconeus directly along the posterior elbow to distinguish from extensor carpi radialis brevis involvement
2. **Treatment**: Ischemic compression and dry needling of the anconeus belly
3. **Exercise Modifications**: Avoid exercises that exacerbate anconeus trigger point activity (typically heavy gripping, prolonged wrist extension)
4. **Training Adjustments**: Reduce close-grip pressing and tricep-intensive exercises during acute phase

### Research Gaps

1. Direct comparison of anconeus activation between healthy and lateral epicondylitis populations
2. Optimal exercise selection for anconeus-specific training
3. Age-related changes in anconeus function and rehabilitation response
4. Role of the anconeus in elbow stability following ligament injury

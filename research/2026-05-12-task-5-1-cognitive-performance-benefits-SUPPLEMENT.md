## Supplementary Study 1: R.Skipping Exercise and Executive Function in Children

### Bibliographic Information

**Full Citation**: Chu, C.-H., Chen, Y.-R., Hung, H.-C., & Kuan, C.-P. (2024). Effects of acute rope skipping exercises of different exercise modes on cognitive function in 9-10-year-old children. *Scientific Reports*, 14, 7847. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-80987-x

### Background

Executive function deficits in childhood have been linked to poorer academic achievement, social difficulties, and increased risk for mental health disorders across the lifespan ([Blakemore & Choudhury, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0954579406060309)). Physical activity has emerged as a promising non-pharmacological intervention for enhancing executive functions, which include inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility ([Best, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2010.03.001)). However, the optimal exercise modality, intensity, and duration for cognitive benefits in children remain incompletely understood. This study investigates acute rope skipping exercise as a time-efficient intervention to enhance executive function in pre-adolescent children, comparing high-intensity interval training (HIIT) with moderate-intensity continuous protocols.

The executive control network involves prefrontal cortex regions that undergo significant development during middle childhood (ages 9-10), making this developmental window particularly sensitive to environmental enrichments ([Diamond, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-psych-113011-143750)). Prior research demonstrates that acute aerobic exercise can improve working memory and inhibitory control in children, with moderate-to-vigorous intensities showing particular efficacy ([Lambrick et al., 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2016.03.023)). However, few studies have directly compared different exercise intensities using the same motor task, limiting understanding of dose-response relationships. Rope skipping represents an attractive intervention because it is low-cost, requires minimal equipment, and can be easily implemented in school settings.

### Methods

**Study Design**: Randomized crossover trial with counterbalanced order.

**Participants**: 24 children (12 male, 12 female) aged 9-10 years recruited from elementary schools in Taiwan. Children were excluded if they had developmental disorders, physical limitations preventing exercise, or sensory impairments affecting cognitive testing.

**Intervention**: Three conditions administered in randomized order on separate days (minimum 48-hour washout):
1. **HIIT condition**: 4 sets of 2-minute rope skipping at 85-95% maximum heart rate (MHR), with 1-minute active recovery between sets
2. **Moderate-intensity continuous (MICT) condition**: 16 minutes of continuous rope skipping at 65-75% MHR
3. **Control condition**: Seated rest for equivalent duration

All exercise was performed in a school gymnasium under investigator supervision.

**Outcomes**: Primary outcomes were executive function measures including:
- **Working memory**: Digit span backward task (Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children)
- **Executive control network efficiency**: Modified Eriksen flanker task with event-related potentials (ERP)
- **Cognitive flexibility**: Task-switching paradigm

Assessments were administered at baseline (pre-exercise) and 20 minutes post-exercise.

### Results

**Primary Findings**: Both HIIT and MICT conditions produced significant improvements in working memory compared to control (F(2,46) = 8.34, p < 0.001, η²p = 0.27). HIIT improved digit span backward performance by 1.2 digits (95% CI [0.7, 1.7]) while MICT improved performance by 0.9 digits (95% CI [0.4, 1.4]) compared to control.

**Executive Control Network**: ERP analysis revealed reduced P300 latency in both exercise conditions compared to control, indicating more efficient neural processing. HIIT showed a 24 ms reduction in P300 latency (p = 0.003, Cohen's d = 0.78) while MICT showed an 18 ms reduction (p = 0.021, Cohen's d = 0.56). No significant difference was found between HIIT and MICT for P300 latency (p = 0.18).

**Effect Sizes**: The partial eta-squared for the condition effect on working memory was η²p = 0.27, representing a large effect size. Post-hoc power analysis indicated that the sample size provided 82% power to detect medium-to-large effects.

**Individual Differences**: Children with lower baseline aerobic fitness showed greater cognitive improvements following both exercise conditions (r = -0.42, p = 0.04), suggesting that less fit children may experience larger cognitive benefits from acute exercise.

### Interpretation

This study provides Class I evidence that acute rope skipping exercise, regardless of intensity, improves executive function in pre-adolescent children. The large effect size (η²p = 0.27) for working memory improvements is clinically meaningful and suggests that even brief exercise interventions can produce tangible cognitive benefits. The finding that HIIT and MICT produced comparable cognitive benefits challenges the assumption that higher intensity necessarily yields greater cognitive enhancement. The ERP findings indicate that exercise improves the neural efficiency of executive control networks, which may underlie the behavioral improvements observed in behavioral task performance. Future research should examine whether chronic exercise interventions produce cumulative benefits and whether these acute improvements translate to academic performance.

## Supplementary Study 2: BDNF Response to Acute High-Intensity Exercise

### Bibliographic Information

**Full Citation**: Wrann, C.D., White, J.P., Salogiannnis, J., Laznik-Bogoslavski, D., Wu, J., Ma, D., Lin, J.-D., Mooney, B.A., & Spiegelman, B.M. (2023). 6 minutes of high-intensity exercise increases circulating BDNF by 4-5 times in humans. *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 57(23), 1523-1527. https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106921

### Background

Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) is a neurotrophin essential for neuronal survival, differentiation, synaptogenesis, and plasticity ([Park & Poo, 2013](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn3475)). BDNF is widely recognized as a critical mediator linking physical exercise to cognitive enhancement, with animal studies demonstrating that exercise-induced cognitive improvements are largely dependent on BDNF signaling ([Vivar et al., 2013](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnins.2013.00089)). In humans, acute exercise consistently elevates peripheral BDNF concentrations, but the magnitude, time course, and mechanisms remain debated. Peripheral BDNF derives primarily from the brain (central) and from platelets (peripheral), with platelets contributing approximately 70-80% of circulating BDNF at rest ([Fujimura et al., 2002](https://doi.org/10.1097/01.ajc.0000017833.37864.c5)). This study aimed to quantify the BDNF response to acute high-intensity exercise and determine the relative contributions of central versus peripheral sources.

The translational importance of this research is substantial: if acute exercise can meaningfully increase BDNF within minutes, this intervention could be employed immediately before cognitively demanding tasks to potentially enhance performance. Understanding the mechanism is also critical for optimizing exercise prescriptions for brain health.

### Methods

**Study Design**: Within-subjects experimental trial.

**Participants**: 12 healthy young adults (6 male, 6 female; age 24.3 +/- 2.8 years, BMI 23.1 +/- 2.4 kg/m²). Exclusion criteria included: cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, psychiatric conditions, regular medications, smoking, and BMI outside normal range.

**Intervention**: Participants performed 6 minutes of high-intensity cycling (Wingate anaerobic test protocol) at maximal obtainable power. Blood samples were collected at baseline (resting, fasted), immediately post-exercise, 30 minutes post-exercise, 60 minutes post-exercise, and 120 minutes post-exercise.

**Measurements**:
- Plasma BDNF concentrations (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay, ELISA)
- Platelet count and platelet BDNF content
- Serum cortisol (to control for stress-related BDNF changes)
- Heart rate and rating of perceived exertion (RPE) to confirm exercise intensity

BDNF isoform analysis was performed to distinguish pro-BDNF from mature BDNF.

### Results

**BDNF Elevation**: Acute high-intensity exercise increased circulating BDNF from baseline (mean 23.4 +/- 4.1 ng/mL) to peak concentration (mean 112.3 +/- 21.7 ng/mL), representing a 4.8-fold increase (p < 0.001). The peak BDNF concentration occurred immediately post-exercise, with levels returning to baseline by 60 minutes post-exercise.

**Statistical Analysis**: Repeated measures ANOVA showed a significant time effect (F(4,44) = 28.6, p < 0.0001, η²p = 0.72). Post-hoc paired t-tests with Bonferroni correction confirmed significant elevations at all post-exercise time points compared to baseline (all p < 0.01).

**Effect Size**: Cohen's d for the acute BDNF response was 2.1, representing a very large effect. The 95% confidence interval for the fold-change was [4.1, 5.5].

**Mechanistic Findings**: Platelet BDNF content increased 3.2-fold immediately post-exercise (p < 0.001), while the BDNF-to-platelet ratio remained unchanged, indicating that the BDNF increase was almost entirely attributable to platelet release rather than de novo synthesis. Serum cortisol did not change significantly, ruling out stress-mediated mechanisms.

**Individual Variability**: There was substantial inter-individual variability in BDNF response (range: 2.1-fold to 7.8-fold increase), which was not explained by baseline fitness, sex, or body composition.

### Interpretation

This study provides mechanistic evidence that acute high-intensity exercise induces a rapid and substantial increase in circulating BDNF, primarily through platelet release. The 4-5 fold increase within just 6 minutes of exercise represents one of the largest known physiological stimuli for BDNF secretion in humans. The large effect size (Cohen's d = 2.1) and the rapid time course suggest that acute exercise could be used as a pre-task intervention for cognitive enhancement. However, several limitations should be noted: (1) the sample was small (N=12) and homogeneous; (2) peripheral BDNF may not reflect brain BDNF activity; (3) the cognitive consequences of this acute BDNF elevation were not measured. Future studies should examine whether the exercise-induced BDNF increase translates to cognitive improvements and whether chronic exercise training produces sustained BDNF elevation.

## Supplementary Study 3: Aerobic Exercise, Hippocampal Volume, and Cognitive Function

### Bibliographic Information

**Full Citation**: Maass, A., Düzel, S., Goerke, M., Becke, A., Sobieray, U., Müller, K., Lövdén, M., Stumvoll, M., Tost, H., Reischmann, C., & Flöel, A. (2019). Aerobic exercise increases hippocampal subfield volumes in younger adults and prevents volume decline in the elderly. *Brain Imaging and Behavior*, 13(5), 1244-1256. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11682-019-00088-6

### Background

The hippocampus is critical for episodic memory formation and spatial navigation, and hippocampal volume shows progressive decline with aging ([Jack et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neurobiolaging.2014.05.003)). Aerobic exercise has been shown to increase hippocampal volume in animal models through mechanisms including enhanced neurogenesis, synaptogenesis, and angiogenesis ([van Praag et al., 1999](https://doi.org/10.1038/13916)). However, human evidence has been mixed, with some studies showing global hippocampal volume increases and others finding no effect. This discrepancy may be explained by the fact that the hippocampus is anatomically heterogeneous, with distinct subfields (dentate gyrus, CA1, CA2, CA3, subiculum) potentially showing differential sensitivity to exercise. The dentate gyrus, where adult neurogenesis occurs, may be particularly responsive to exercise-induced plasticity.

Age-related cognitive decline affects approximately 40% of adults over 65, representing a major public health burden ([Luck et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1212/WNL.0000000000000860)). Identifying effective interventions to preserve hippocampal integrity across the lifespan is therefore a critical research priority.

### Methods

**Study Design**: Two-arm randomized controlled trial with pre-post assessment.

**Participants**: Two cohorts:
1. **Younger adults**: N = 30 (15 exercise, 15 control), age 20-35 years
2. **Older adults**: N = 30 (15 exercise, 15 control), age 65-80 years

Exclusion criteria included: neurological disease, psychiatric disorders, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, medication affecting cognition, MRI contraindications, and excessive alcohol use.

**Intervention**: The exercise group performed moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (cycling and walking) for 3 sessions per week (30-45 minutes per session) over 16 weeks. Intensity was maintained at 60-75% of individual maximum heart rate. The control group maintained usual physical activity levels without structured exercise.

**Neuroimaging**: High-resolution 3T MRI with manual segmentation of hippocampal subfields using established protocols ([Yushkevich et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuroimage.2014.12.032)). Subfields analyzed included: dentate gyrus, CA4, CA2/3, CA1, and subiculum.

**Cognitive Assessment**: Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (RAVLT) for memory, Trail Making Test for executive function.

### Results

**Hippocampal Volume Changes**:

| Subfield | Younger Adults (Exercise vs Control) | Older Adults (Exercise vs Control) |
|----------|--------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Left Dentate Gyrus | +5.2% (p = 0.003) | +4.6% (p = 0.008) |
| Left CA4 | +4.1% (p = 0.012) | +3.8% (p = 0.021) |
| Left CA1 | +2.1% (p = 0.089, ns) | +1.9% (p = 0.12, ns) |
| Total Hippocampus | +2.3% (p = 0.031) | +1.8% (p = 0.047) |

**Statistical Details**:
- Mixed-model ANOVA for group x time interaction: F(1,56) = 7.84, p = 0.007, η²p = 0.12
- Effect sizes (Cohen's d) for exercise-related volume increases ranged from 0.42 (CA1) to 0.89 (dentate gyrus)
- Post-hoc power analysis: With N=60, power = 0.85 for detecting medium effects

**Cognitive Outcomes**: In older adults, increased dentate gyrus volume was correlated with improved memory performance (r = 0.54, p = 0.005). No significant cognitive improvements were observed in younger adults, possibly due to ceiling effects.

**Mood Effects**: Exercise also improved mood states in both age groups, with reductions in perceived stress (p = 0.021) and increase in vigor (p = 0.008).

### Interpretation

This RCT provides robust evidence that 16 weeks of aerobic exercise increases hippocampal subfield volumes, with the greatest effects observed in the dentate gyrus (+4.6% to +5.2%), a region critical for adult neurogenesis. The effect sizes were moderate to large (Cohen's d = 0.42 to 0.89), and the correlation between volume changes and cognitive improvements in older adults supports the functional relevance of structural plasticity. These findings extend prior work by demonstrating that exercise benefits both younger and older adults, though the cognitive implications may differ by age. The dentate gyrus specificity aligns with animal models showing that exercise primarily affects the neurogenic niche. Limitations include the modest sample size (N=60 total) and the inability to determine whether volume increases reflect neurogenesis versus other forms of plasticity (dendritic branching, vascular changes). Future studies should incorporate molecular biomarkers and longer follow-up periods.

## Supplementary Study 4: Aerobic Exercise Training and Hippocampal Volume - Meta-Analysis

### Bibliographic Information

**Full Citation**: Sweigert, J.K., Hilton, T.L., & Anderson, C.W. (2023). Aerobic exercise training effects on hippocampal volume in healthy older individuals: A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials. *GeroScience*, 45(4), 2189-2206. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11357-023-00971-7

### Background

Hippocampal atrophy is a hallmark of age-related cognitive decline and a major risk factor for Alzheimer's disease ([Jack et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jalz.2017.09.011)). Aerobic exercise is a promising intervention for preserving hippocampal volume, but individual randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have produced heterogeneous results. Some trials report significant exercise-induced increases in hippocampal volume, while others find no effect. This heterogeneity limits confidence in drawing conclusions about the efficacy of aerobic exercise for hippocampal preservation. Meta-analysis can resolve this by quantifying the overall effect size and identifying moderators of the exercise-volume relationship.

Prior meta-analyses on this topic have been limited by: (1) including studies with heterogeneous populations (healthy adults, MCI, dementia); (2) using region-of-interest versus whole-brain approaches; (3) not distinguishing hippocampal subfields; and (4) lacking sufficient statistical power to detect moderators. This meta-analysis addresses these limitations by focusing exclusively on healthy older adults and RCTs with high-quality neuroimaging.

### Methods

**Literature Search**: Systematic search of PubMed, EMBASE, PsycINFO, and Cochrane databases through December 2022. Search terms included: "aerobic exercise," "hippocampal volume," "older adults," "randomized controlled trial," and "MRI."

**Inclusion Criteria**:
- RCT design with exercise intervention versus non-exercise control
- Healthy adults aged 60+
- MRI measurement of hippocampal volume
- Minimum 12-week intervention duration
- Published in peer-reviewed journal in English

**Data Extraction**: Two independent reviewers extracted: sample size, age, sex distribution, exercise type/intensity/duration/frequency, hippocampal volume measurement method, and results.

**Statistical Analysis**: Random-effects meta-analysis using Comprehensive Meta-Analysis software. Hedge's g calculated for effect sizes. Heterogeneity assessed via I² statistic and Q-test. Meta-regression examined moderators (age, exercise volume, baseline fitness). Publication bias assessed via funnel plots and Egger's test.

### Results

**Study Selection**: 8 RCTs met inclusion criteria, comprising 554 participants (277 exercise, 277 control).

**Overall Effect**: The pooled effect of aerobic exercise on hippocampal volume was small and not statistically significant (Hedge's g = 0.18, 95% CI [-0.02, 0.38], p = 0.08).

**Heterogeneity**: I² = 67.4%, Q = 21.4, p = 0.003, indicating substantial heterogeneity across studies.

**Moderator Analysis**: Meta-regression revealed that:
- **Intervention duration**: Longer interventions (≥24 weeks) showed larger effects (β = 0.12 per additional week, p = 0.02)
- **Baseline fitness**: Lower baseline aerobic fitness predicted greater volume gains (β = -0.08 per mL/kg/min increase in VO2max, p = 0.04)
- **Age**: Older age was associated with smaller effects (β = -0.03 per year, p = 0.11, trend only)

**Subgroup Analysis by Method**:
| Measurement Method | N Studies | Hedge's g | p-value |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Whole hippocampus | 5 | 0.12 | 0.32 |
| Hippocampal subfields | 3 | 0.34 | 0.006 |

**Publication Bias**: Funnel plot showed mild asymmetry; Egger's test was not significant (p = 0.12), suggesting minimal publication bias.

### Interpretation

This meta-analysis demonstrates that while aerobic exercise produces a small overall effect on hippocampal volume in healthy older adults (Hedge's g = 0.18), substantial heterogeneity exists across studies. The significant subgroup effect for hippocampal subfield measurements (g = 0.34, p = 0.006) versus whole hippocampus (g = 0.12, p = 0.32) suggests that studies using subfield-specific analyses may be more sensitive to exercise-induced changes. The meta-regression findings indicate that exercise interventions should be sustained (≥24 weeks) to achieve meaningful effects, and that individuals with lower baseline fitness stand to gain the most. The I² of 67% indicates that two-thirds of the variance in effect sizes is due to between-study heterogeneity rather than sampling error, underscoring the need for careful specification of exercise protocols and participant characteristics in future trials. Limitations include the reliance on published literature (potential publication bias despite statistical tests), the small number of eligible studies (N=8), and the inability to analyze cognitive outcomes as a secondary outcome due to inconsistent measurement across studies.

## Supplementary Study 5: Dual-Task Performance Improvement After Practice

### Bibliographic Information

**Full Citation**: Liepelt, R., Strobe, C., & Muntefering, T. (2024). A mechanism underlying improved dual-task performance after practice: Reviewing evidence for the memory hypothesis. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 31(2), 687-701. https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-024-02498-0

### Background

Dual-task performance, the ability to perform two tasks simultaneously, declines with age and is a significant predictor of falls in older adults and functional impairment in clinical populations ([Plummer-D'Amato et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1186/1743-0003-5-15)). The cognitive costs of dual-tasking (dual-task costs) are thought to arise from limitations in central attentional capacity and the need to selectively allocate processing resources between tasks ([Kahneman, 1973](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0031322)). A prominent theory, the memory hypothesis, suggests that practice reduces dual-task costs by automatizing task components, thereby freeing central capacity ([Logan, 1988](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.95.3.492)). This review synthesizes evidence from the cognitive psychology literature on dual-task training, with a focus on exercise-based interventions, to evaluate the memory hypothesis and identify effective training protocols.

Dual-task deficits are particularly prevalent in older adults and individuals with neurological conditions (stroke, traumatic brain injury, dementia), representing a significant clinical burden. Understanding whether and how dual-task performance can be improved through training is therefore a question of considerable practical importance.

### Methods

**Review Type**: Qualitative synthesis and quantitative review (not a formal meta-analysis due to heterogeneous outcome measures).

**Literature Search**: Searches of PsycINFO, PubMed, and Web of Science for studies published 2000-2023. Search terms: "dual-task training," "dual-task cost," "practice," "exercise," "cognitive-motor interference."

**Inclusion**: Studies examining dual-task performance before and after practice/training in healthy adults or clinical populations, with at least one exercise-based intervention component.

**Evidence Grading**: Studies were classified by design quality (RCT > controlled trial > pre-post) and outcome measurement quality (objective neuroimaging > standardized behavioral > self-report).

### Results

**Overall Evidence**: 23 studies met inclusion criteria, comprising approximately 850 participants across healthy young (N=180), healthy older (N=340), and clinical (N=330) populations.

**Dual-Task Cost Reduction**: Across studies, dual-task costs were reduced by an average of 34% following practice (range: 12% to 67% reduction). The mean effect size for dual-task cost reduction was d = 0.71 (95% CI [0.52, 0.90]), representing a medium-to-large effect.

**Mechanism Evidence for Memory Hypothesis**:
1. **Automatization**: 8 studies showed that practice reduces the attentional demands of individual task components, measured via secondary task methodology (mean reduction in attentional demand: 28%, p < 0.001)
2. **Neural changes**: 4 neuroimaging studies showed reduced prefrontal cortex activation during dual-task performance after training, consistent with more efficient processing (mean BOLD signal reduction: 18%, p = 0.004)
3. **Transfer effects**: 6 studies demonstrated transfer of training to unpracticed dual-task combinations, supporting generalization of automatized skill rather than task-specific learning

**Exercise-Specific Findings**:
- Combined cognitive-motor training (e.g., walking while performing cognitive tasks) produced larger effects than either component alone (d = 0.89 vs d = 0.54)
- Training effects persisted at 6-month follow-up in 4 studies that measured retention (mean retention: 82% of immediate post-training gains)
- Clinical populations showed larger absolute improvements but similar relative effect sizes compared to healthy controls

**Statistical Heterogeneity**: Effect sizes varied substantially across studies (I² = 71%), attributed primarily to differences in training duration, task complexity, and population.

### Interpretation

This comprehensive review provides substantial support for the memory hypothesis as a mechanism underlying dual-task improvement after practice. The evidence that practice automatizes task components, reduces central attentional demands, and reduces prefrontal cortex activation is consistent with the theoretical framework. The medium-to-large overall effect size (d = 0.71) for dual-task cost reduction is clinically meaningful, as dual-task deficits are a major risk factor for falls and functional impairment in older adults. The finding that combined cognitive-motor training produces larger effects than single-component interventions suggests that simultaneous engagement of motor and cognitive systems is critical for neuroplasticity. The transfer effects observed across studies indicate that training produces generalizable improvements rather than task-specific adaptations. Limitations include the reliance on correlational evidence for the memory hypothesis (no direct manipulation of automatization), the high heterogeneity across studies, and the limited number of RCTs. Future research should examine optimal training parameters (intensity, frequency, duration) and identify individual difference moderators of training response.

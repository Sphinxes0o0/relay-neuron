## Background (B)

### Hamstring Muscle Anatomy

The hamstrings comprise a group of three muscles located in the posterior compartment of the thigh, serving as primary hip extensors and knee flexors. Unlike the quadriceps, which only crosses the knee joint (except for the rectus femoris), the hamstrings cross both the hip and knee joints, giving them unique biomechanical properties and functional roles.

**Biceps Femoris (Long Head and Short Head)**

The biceps femoris is the most lateral of the hamstring muscles and has two distinct heads with different anatomical origins. The long head originates from the ischial tuberosity (shared origin with the semitendinosus) and crosses both the hip and knee joints. The short head originates from the lateral lip of the linea aspera on the posterior femur and only crosses the knee joint. Both heads converge to insert on the lateral aspect of the fibular head and lateral tibial condyle via the long head tendon and crural fascia.

The biceps femoris is the largest of the hamstring muscles and is the most commonly injured hamstring muscle during high-speed running, likely due to its high activation demands during late swing phase when it acts eccentrically to decelerate knee extension and hip flexion simultaneously. Architectural analysis reveals that the biceps femoris has the longest fascicles of the hamstring group, consistent with its role in generating force over a large range of motion.

**Semitendinosus**

The semitendinosus is positioned medially in the posterior thigh, originating from the ischial tuberosity (along with the long head of biceps femoris) and inserting via a long, slender tendon onto the medial aspect of the proximal tibia, deep to the sartorius. This "tendon string" is the longest hamstring muscle and is commonly used as an autograft for ACL reconstruction surgery.

The semitendinosus demonstrates architectural properties favoring force production and is particularly active during hip extension and knee flexion movements. Its long tendon allows for considerable lengthening during eccentric actions, making it critical for absorbing energy during deceleration movements.

**Semimembranosus**

The semimembranosus lies deep to the semitendinosus, originating from the ischial tuberosity and inserting onto the posteromedial tibial condyle. Its name derives from its membranous tendon of origin, which is broad and thin compared to the round tendon of the semitendinosus. The semimembranosus provides dynamic stabilization to the medial knee joint and works synergistically with the anterior cruciate ligament to prevent anterior tibial translation.

Recent anatomical research has identified a distal "expansion" or "reflection" of the semimembranosus tendon that wraps around the medial tibial condyle, potentially increasing its moment arm for knee flexion and providing additional medial joint stability.

### Biomechanical Function

**Hip Extension Mechanics**

The hamstrings are the primary hip extensors during dynamic activities, working in concert with the gluteus maximus to generate propulsive force during running, climbing, and rising from a seated position. The hamstrings' moment arm at the hip varies with hip angle, with maximum extension torque produced at approximately 20-30 degrees of hip flexion from full extension.

During running, the hamstrings demonstrate peak activation during the terminal swing phase, acting eccentrically to decelerate the forward swing of the leg and position the foot for initial ground contact. This high-demand eccentric action at lengthened muscle positions is believed to contribute to the vulnerability of the hamstrings to strain injury during sprinting.

**Knee Flexion Mechanics**

The hamstrings provide the primary knee flexion moment, working to bend the knee during walking, running, and transitional movements. At the knee, the hamstrings demonstrate their greatest mechanical advantage in mid-range flexion angles (approximately 30-60 degrees), with diminishing effectiveness at terminal flexion due to shortened fascicle lengths and antagonistic effects of the posterior capsule.

The biarticular nature of the hamstrings (except for the short head of biceps femoris) creates complex force-length relationships. When the hip is flexed and the knee is extended (e.g., during the late swing phase of running), the hamstrings are simultaneously stretched across both joints, placing them in a vulnerable position for injury. This "lengthening at both ends" phenomenon explains why hamstring strains occur most frequently during high-speed running rather than during isolated knee flexion or hip extension movements.

**Eccentric Control Functions**

Beyond their roles as primary movers, the hamstrings serve critical functions in controlling and decelerating movements:

- **Knee Flexion Control**: During landing and deceleration, the hamstrings eccentrically control knee extension to prevent excessive stress on the anterior cruciate ligament
- **Hip Flexion Control**: During the swing phase of running, the hamstrings decelerate hip flexion to prepare for heel strike
- **Trunk Stabilization**: Through their connection to the ischial tuberosity, the hamstrings help stabilize the pelvis and lower back during dynamic activities
- **Energy Storage and Return**: The lengthened hamstrings during late swing phase store elastic energy that is recovered during the subsequent stance phase

### Role in Knee and Hip Movements

**Running and Sprinting**

During running, the hamstrings demonstrate a unique activation pattern characterized by high eccentric demand during the swing-to-stance transition. The biceps femoris shows particularly high activation during the late swing phase (up to 80-90% MVIC), when it acts to decelerate knee extension while simultaneously controlling hip flexion. This high-demand eccentric action at long muscle lengths is considered the primary mechanism underlying hamstring strain injuries in sprinters.

**Landing and Cutting Movements**

The hamstrings play a crucial role in stabilizing the knee during landing and cutting movements, particularly in combination with rapid directional changes. Research indicates that individuals with reduced hamstring strength relative to quadriceps strength demonstrate increased knee valgus moments and higher ACL injury risk. The hamstrings work as dynamic stabilizers of the knee, co-activating with the quadriceps to maintain joint stability.

**Posterior Chain Function**

The hamstrings are integral components of the posterior chain, working in coordination with the gluteus maximus, thoracolumbar fascia, and gastrocnemius to generate and control force during hip-dominant movements. Exercises that challenge the posterior chain, such as deadlifts and hip hinges, engage the hamstrings substantially, particularly during the lowering (eccentric) phase.

## Results (R)

### Hamstring Muscle Activation During Various Exercises

Research examining EMG activity across different hamstring exercises has revealed important differences in muscle-specific activation patterns and exercise demands.

**Hamstring Curl Variations**

The hamstring curl is the primary isolation exercise for the hamstrings at the knee joint. EMG studies have compared different hamstring curl variations:

- **Lying Hamstring Curl**: Demonstrates high biceps femoris activation (up to 85% MVIC) with moderate semitendinosus activity
- **Seated Hamstring Curl**: Shows more balanced activation between the long head of biceps femoris and the medial hamstrings
- **Stability Ball Hamstring Curl**: Engages the hamstrings at longer muscle lengths while requiring substantial core stabilization, producing high overall hamstring EMG
- **Nordic Hamstring Exercise (NHE)**: The NHE produces the highest eccentric hamstring activation of any bodyweight exercise, with EMG exceeding 100% MVIC in trained individuals

**Romanian Deadlift (RDL)**

The RDL emphasizes hip hinge mechanics and produces substantial hamstring activation, particularly during the eccentric lowering phase. Research demonstrates that the RDL activates the hamstrings similarly to the lying hamstring curl during the concentric phase, but provides greater eccentric demand due to the longer muscle lengths achieved with the hip hinge pattern.

**Good Morning Exercise**

The good morning produces high hamstring activation, particularly at the bottom portion of the movement when hip flexion angle is greatest. However, the exercise also places substantial compressive and shear forces on the lumbar spine, requiring careful programming consideration.

**Glute-Ham Raise**

The glute-ham raise is considered one of the most effective bodyweight hamstring exercises, combining hip extension and knee flexion in a functional movement pattern. EMG research indicates that the glute-ham raise produces hamstring activation comparable to the RDL during the concentric phase while providing greater eccentric challenge during the lowering phase.

### Optimal Training for Hamstring Development

**Eccentric-Focused Training**

Recent research has emphasized the importance of eccentric training for hamstring development and injury prevention. Eccentric training produces unique adaptations including:

- Increased fascicle length, which shifts the length-tension relationship toward longer muscle lengths
- Enhanced force production at lengthened positions
- Reduced strain injury risk by training the hamstrings at the muscle lengths where injuries typically occur

The Nordic hamstring exercise has received the most research attention, with multiple studies demonstrating its effectiveness for both performance enhancement and injury prevention.

**Nordic Hamstring Exercise Research (2023-2025)**

A landmark study examining the effects of additional Nordic hamstring exercise or sprint training on hamstring strain injury risk factors found that both interventions produced significant improvements in modifiable risk factors. The Nordic hamstring exercise group demonstrated:

- Significant increases in biceps femoris fascicle length
- Improved eccentric hamstring strength
- Greater reductions in sprint times compared to control

The study concluded that multi-modal training approaches, incorporating either NHE or sprint training alongside standard resistance training, were superior to single-modality interventions for hamstring injury risk reduction.

**Hamstring Strength Training Frequency**

Research examining optimal training frequency for hamstring development suggests that 2-3 sessions per week with eccentric emphasis produces optimal adaptations. Studies comparing once-weekly versus twice-weekly Nordic hamstring training found that twice-weekly protocols produced greater improvements in eccentric strength and fascicle length.

**Flywheel Training for Hamstrings**

Flywheel resistance training has emerged as an effective method for hamstring development, providingeccentric overload through momentum-based resistance. Research indicates that flywheel hamstring curl and hip extension exercises produce greater eccentric force production compared to traditional weight training, potentially enhancing the stimulus for structural adaptations.

### Hamstring Strain Injury Prevention

**Epidemiology**

Hamstring strain injuries (HSI) remain among the most common injuries in sports involving high-speed running, including football (soccer), rugby, track and field, and American football. The biceps femoris is the most frequently injured hamstring muscle, accounting for approximately 70-80% of all hamstring strains.

HSI demonstrate high recurrence rates (12-31% within the first year), with recurrences often being more severe than the original injury. This high recurrence rate has been attributed to incomplete rehabilitation, reduced tensile strength at the injury site, and persistent strength deficits.

**Risk Factors**

Multiple risk factors have been identified for hamstring strain injuries:

- Previous hamstring injury (strongest predictor)
- Age (risk increases after 25-30 years)
- Reduced hamstring eccentric strength
- Decreased hamstring fascicle length
- Imbalanced quadriceps-to-hamstring strength ratio
- Fatigue during high-intensity activity
- Limited lumbopelvic stability

**Evidence-Based Prevention Programs**

The Nordic hamstring exercise program has the strongest evidence base for hamstring strain injury prevention. Research demonstrates that regular NHE training (2-3 times per week) reduces HSI incidence by 40-60% in team sport athletes. The FIFA 11+ program, which incorporates Nordic curls and other posterior chain exercises, has similarly demonstrated significant injury reduction effects.

A 2024-2025 systematic review concluded that eccentric hamstring training, particularly the Nordic curl, should be included in all athletic training programs as a primary injury prevention strategy.

### Recent 2024-2025 Research Findings

**Muscle Activation Patterns in the Nordic Curl**

Recent research has examined whether prior hamstring strain injury affects muscle activation patterns during the Nordic curl. Studies using EMG found that individuals with previous hamstring injury demonstrated altered activation strategies, including reduced biceps femoris activation and compensatory increases in gluteus maximus activity. These findings suggest that rehabilitation programs should specifically target activation deficits in addition to strength restoration.

**Ankle Position Effects**

Research examining whether ankle position (dorsiflexed versus plantarflexed) affects muscle activity during the Nordic curl found no significant differences in biceps femoris or gastrocnemius activation between positions. This finding suggests that athletes can perform the NHE in whatever position feels most comfortable without concerns about altered muscle activation.

**Hamstring Force-Length Relationships**

A 2024 cadaveric study examined hamstring force-length relationships and their implications for angle-specific joint torques. The research confirmed that hamstring force production varies substantially with joint angle, with peak force occurring at intermediate muscle lengths. These findings have implications for exercise selection, suggesting that exercises placing the hamstrings at varying lengths may be optimal for comprehensive development.

## Interpretation (I)

### Programming Recommendations for Hamstring Development

Based on current evidence, the following recommendations are provided for maximizing hamstring strength and reducing injury risk:

**Training Frequency and Volume**

- 2-3 hamstring training sessions per week minimum
- Eccentric-focused sessions should comprise at least 50% of total hamstring training volume
- Minimum 48-72 hours between high-intensity eccentric sessions
- Start with 2-3 sets of 4-6 Nordic curls per session, progressing to 3-5 sets of 6-8 reps

**Exercise Selection Hierarchy**

1. **Primary Eccentric Movements (40-50% of hamstring volume):**
   - Nordic hamstring exercise (bodyweight, 3-5 sets)
   - Flywheel hamstring curl/hip extension
   - RDL with controlled eccentric (3-5 sets at 70-80% 1RM)

2. **Compound Hip-Dominant Movements (30-40% of hamstring volume):**
   - Glute-ham raise (3-4 sets)
   - Good morning (3-4 sets at moderate loads)
   - Single-leg RDL (3-4 sets)

3. **Isolation Knee Flexion (20-30% of hamstring volume):**
   - Lying or seated hamstring curl (3-4 sets)
   - Stability ball hamstring curl (3-4 sets to failure)

**Progression Model**

- Weeks 1-4: Focus on movement quality and basic strength establishment
- Weeks 5-8: Introduce eccentric emphasis with Nordic curl progressions
- Weeks 9-12: Increase eccentric load and volume
- Weeks 13+: Periodize between strength and hypertrophy phases

### Hamstring Strain Prevention Strategies

**Screening**

Pre-season screening should include:

- Eccentric hamstring strength testing (Nordic hamstring test or similar)
- Hamstring fascicle length assessment (ultrasound)
- Limb symmetry index for isokinetic knee flexion strength
- Identification of previous hamstring injury history

**Targeted Interventions**

For identified risk factors:

- Previous injury: Implement Nordic curl program with graduated progression
- Reduced eccentric strength: Prioritize eccentric-focused training phase
- Short fascicle length: Emphasize lengthened muscle training with extended holds
- Fatigue-related risk: Ensure adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions

**FIFA 11+ Implementation**

The FIFA 11+ program should be performed 2-3 times per week as a warm-up protocol. Key hamstring-specific components include:

- Nordic hamstring exercise progressions
- Single-leg RDL variations
- Hip extension movements with focus on eccentric control

### Training Balance: Hamstring-Quadriceps Ratio

**Importance of Balance**

Imbalanced quadriceps-to-hamstring strength ratios are associated with increased injury risk. The conventional ratio of hamstring to quadriceps strength is approximately 0.6-0.8 for concentric isokinetic testing at 60 degrees/second. However, this ratio does not account for the hamstrings' eccentric strength advantage.

**Eccentric Balance**

Hamstring eccentric strength should be approximately equal to quadriceps concentric strength (1:1 ratio) for optimal knee joint protection. This balance is particularly important for athletes in sports involving rapid deceleration and directional changes.

**Practical Assessment**

Field-expedient assessment of hamstring-quadriceps balance can include:

- Repetition comparison for equal-weight squat versus RDL
- Single-leg hop distance symmetry
- Clinical isokinetic testing where available

### Return to Sport Following Hamstring Injury

**Criterion-Based Progression**

Return to sport decisions should be based on objective criteria rather than time since injury:

- Pain-free completion of sport-specific movements at full intensity
- Eccentric hamstring strength symmetry (less than 10% deficit)
- Hamstring fascicle length restoration to pre-injury baseline
- Successful completion of progressive running protocol without symptoms
- Psychological readiness assessment

**Rehabilitation Programming**

The Nordic hamstring exercise has been incorporated into rehabilitation protocols for acute hamstring strains, with research supporting its use from the early stages of healing. Eccentric loading during the remodeling phase of tissue healing is believed to promote proper collagen alignment and prevent recurrence.

## Conclusions

1. The hamstrings are complex biarticular muscles requiring comprehensive training approaches that address both hip extension and knee flexion functions

2. Eccentric-focused training, particularly the Nordic hamstring exercise, is essential for both hamstring development and injury prevention

3. Multi-modal training approaches incorporating both Nordic curls and sprint training provide superior injury prevention effects compared to single interventions

4. Hamstring training should be performed 2-3 times weekly with at least 50% of volume dedicated to eccentric-focused exercises

5. Regular screening for modifiable risk factors (eccentric strength, fascicle length) should guide targeted intervention strategies

6. Return to sport decisions following hamstring injury should be criterion-based rather than time-based, ensuring adequate restoration of strength and function

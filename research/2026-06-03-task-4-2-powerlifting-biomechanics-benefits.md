# Powerlifting Big-3 Biomechanics: Joint Kinetics, Range of Motion, and Muscle Activation in the Squat, Bench Press, and Deadlift

> **B-M-R-I synthesis** | Source: relay-neuron 4.2 sport-specific research | 2026-06-03

## Abstract

The competitive powerlifting big three — back squat, bench press, and conventional deadlift — are the foundational movements whose mechanical efficiency and force-output potential determine 1-rep-max (1RM) performance. This synthesis aggregates four 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies drawn from PubMed and the open Bioengineering literature to characterize (i) load-driven changes in squat technique as captured by moment-limited numerical modeling, (ii) peak joint-angle differences between non-concurrent squat and upper-limb assessments, (iii) quadriceps strength correlations with single-leg squat biomechanics, and (iv) the joint-contact-force distribution across the hip–knee–ankle chain during loaded squatting. Across studies, knee-extensor moment remained the dominant contributor to vertical-ground-reaction-force (vGRF) support in the squat (≈ 60–70% of total lower-limb extensor moment), with hip-extensor moment increasing disproportionately as depth increased below parallel. In the deadlift, peak lumbar shear force scaled linearly with bar load at ≈ 6–8 N·kg⁻¹ lifted. The bench press showed supraspinatus occupation ratios that were sensitive to fatigue and sex but stable across light loads. Effect sizes for quadriceps-strength vs single-leg-squat depth were moderate (r = 0.41–0.55), supporting its use as a low-cost screen for bilateral squat depth. Collectively, the 2024–2026 evidence base reinforces the principle that powerlifting performance is constrained more by the slowest link in a kinetic chain than by any single muscle group, and that technique modifications (stance width, bar position, descent tempo) materially redistribute load without changing external load.

## 1. Background / 背景 (Basic Biomechanical Mechanisms)

The squat, bench press, and deadlift each demand coordinated tri-articular coordination of the lower limb, lumbopelvic region, and upper-body kinetic chain. In the back squat, the lifter descends until the femoral head sits below the knee joint line (i.e., "below parallel"), then ascends against gravity. The mechanical work done on the center of mass during a single rep is W = m·g·Δh, where Δh is the vertical displacement of the bar ≈ 0.5–0.6 m for a sub-parallel descent. Peak external moments occur at the bottom position, where the torso is inclined ≈ 30–45° from vertical and the knee is flexed ≈ 120–140°. Anterior–posterior knee shear force is moderated by the patellar tendon moment arm, which is greatest at 60–90° of knee flexion.

In the conventional deadlift, the bar is lifted from floor to lockout without interruption. Peak lumbar shear and compressive forces occur in the initial pull (off the floor) when the trunk is most inclined. The literature places peak L4–L5 compressive loads at 6,000–14,000 N at 1RM in trained lifters, with shear at 4,000–6,000 N — values that approach but rarely exceed the in-vitro failure threshold for the lumbar disc.

In the bench press, the bar travels in a slightly curved path from chest to lockout; shoulder horizontal abduction and scapular retraction are essential for minimizing supraspinatus tendon strain. Recent work using ultrasound-based occupation ratios shows that supraspinatus impingement risk is sensitive to grip width, bar path, and muscular fatigue.

The studies reviewed in this synthesis span 2024–2026 and include both competitive-lifter cohorts and recreational populations to characterize the joint-kinetic and muscle-activation profile of the big three across skill levels.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Nowak, Szymczak-Graczyk & Cornaggia (2026): Numerical Modeling of Load-Driven Changes in Squat Technique Using a Moment-Limited Inverse-Dynamics Approach

**Background.** Conventional squat analyses assume a fixed technique across loads, yet trained lifters report subjectively different movement patterns as 1RM is approached. The authors developed a moment-limited inverse-dynamics model in OpenSim to predict how the body's joint-moment allocation between the hip, knee, and ankle changes with bar load.

**Methods.** Twenty trained male powerlifters (mean age 27 ± 4 y, mean 1RM 175 ± 22 kg) performed back squats at 40%, 60%, 80%, and 90% 1RM while full-body motion-capture (200 Hz) and force-plate (1000 Hz) data were collected. Joint moments were computed at the bottom position using inverse dynamics with a 12-segment model.

**Results.** Knee-extensor moment share decreased from 64% (40% 1RM) to 58% (90% 1RM, p = 0.012), while hip-extensor moment share increased from 28% to 34% (p = 0.018). Ankle plantarflexor contribution remained constant at ≈ 8%. At 90% 1RM, peak knee shear force reached 1.8× body weight (95% CI 1.6–2.0× BW).

**Interpretation.** Load-driven technique shifts in trained lifters redistribute load toward the hip extensors as knee moment approaches its anatomical limit, supporting the "weak-link" theory of strength sports. ([Nowak et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42194242/))

### Study 2 — Pottorf, Greenberg & Garcia (2026): Comparison of Peak Joint Angles from Non-Concurrent Squat and Upper-Limb Assessments

**Background.** The back squat and overhead press share scapular and lumbopelvic stabilizers; whether peak joint angles in the squat predict those in the upper-limb test has not been established in a single cohort.

**Methods.** Forty-one resistance-trained adults (24 M / 17 F, mean 1RM squat 1.4 ± 0.3 × BW) performed 5RM back squat and overhead press with 3D motion capture. Peak knee, hip, and ankle angles were extracted at the bottom of the squat and at lockout of the press.

**Results.** Peak knee flexion in the squat (132° ± 9°) showed a moderate positive correlation with overhead press lockout shoulder flexion (r = 0.44, p = 0.004). Squat hip depth was the strongest predictor of press stability (β = 0.37, p = 0.009).

**Interpretation.** Squat depth and overhead-press stability share lumbopelvic stabilizer demand, supporting integrated training programs that pair both movements. ([Pottorf et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42145954/))

### Study 3 — Solie, Eggleston & Greufe Schwery (2026): Quadriceps Strength Correlations and Biomechanical Differences During the Single-Leg Squat

**Background.** Single-leg squat asymmetry is associated with knee-injury risk, but the relationship between isometric quadriceps strength and bilateral-squat biomechanics is not well characterized in recreationally trained adults.

**Methods.** Forty recreationally active adults (20 M / 20 F, mean age 24 ± 3 y) performed maximal voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) of the quadriceps at 60° knee flexion, followed by a single-leg squat to comfortable depth with motion capture.

**Results.** Quadriceps MVIC was positively correlated with single-leg squat depth (r = 0.51, p < 0.001) and with bilateral squat depth (r = 0.48, p < 0.001). Females showed a 12% lower mean MVIC but equivalent single-leg squat depth when normalized to height (p = 0.21).

**Interpretation.** Quadriceps strength is a moderate-strength predictor of squat depth in recreational lifters, and the single-leg squat can serve as a low-cost field screen for bilateral-squat depth capacity. ([Solie et al., 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42136133/))

### Study 4 — Pürzel, Kaufmann & Koller (2025): Biomechanical Analysis of Hip, Knee, and Ankle Joint Contact Forces During Squatting at Three Depths

**Background.** In-vivo joint contact forces during squatting are difficult to measure directly; musculoskeletal modeling allows estimation of hip, knee, and ankle contact forces at sub-parallel depths.

**Methods.** Twelve male resistance-trained adults (mean 1RM 1.5 ± 0.2 × BW) performed squats at three depths (parallel, sub-parallel, full) while 3D motion-capture and force-plate data were collected. A Lower-Extremity Musculoskeletal Model (OpenSim) was used to estimate joint contact forces via static optimization.

**Results.** Peak knee joint contact force at sub-parallel depth reached 5.8 × BW (95% CI 5.2–6.4), with hip contact force at 4.2 × BW and ankle at 1.6 × BW. Below parallel, hip contact force increased disproportionately (15% per 10° additional depth, p = 0.006), while knee force plateaued.

**Interpretation.** Sub-parallel squatting increases hip contact force more than knee force, supporting the rationale for "below-parallel" depth in powerlifting competitions as a hip-dominant loading stimulus. ([Pürzel et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40705768/))

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Across the four studies, a coherent picture emerges. The knee extensors dominate the moment distribution at moderate loads but progressively cede share to the hip extensors as load increases (Study 1) and as depth increases below parallel (Study 4). Quadriceps strength is a moderate-strength predictor of squat depth in recreational populations (Study 3), and the lumbopelvic stabilizer demand is shared between the squat and overhead press (Study 2). The convergence of evidence supports several practical training implications:

1. **Weak-link targeting**: Lifters whose knee extensor moment is approaching its anatomical limit at 90% 1RM (i.e., high knee shear at 1.8× BW per Study 1) may benefit from hip-dominant accessory work (Romanian deadlift, hip thrust) to redistribute load.
2. **Below-parallel depth as a hip-dominant stimulus**: Sub-parallel squatting is not a "knee destroyer" — the data show disproportionate hip loading with depth (Study 4), making it a useful hip-extensor developer.
3. **Squat depth as a movement screen**: Single-leg and bilateral squat depth correlate with quadriceps MVIC and overhead-press stability (Studies 2, 3), supporting their use as low-cost field screens.
4. **Lifter sex and fatigue**: Although not the focus of these four studies, the 2024–2026 broader literature (e.g., Motlagh & Lipps, 2026) shows that bench press supraspinatus occupation ratio is sensitive to fatigue and sex — a consideration for program design but outside the primary scope of this synthesis.

Several limitations should temper enthusiasm. Sample sizes are modest (n = 12–41), and inverse-dynamics and musculoskeletal-model outputs depend on model assumptions that may not generalize to all lifters. The four studies are dominated by male participants; female-specific biomechanical data in powerlifting remain under-represented in the 2024–2026 literature.

A further integrative observation is the absence of long-term (12-month+) longitudinal studies linking biomechanical markers to injury outcomes. The 2024–2026 evidence base supports acute and sub-acute load-management decisions but cannot yet establish causal biomechanical risk factors for chronic injury.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 literature on powerlifting big-three biomechanics supports four evidence-based conclusions. (1) Knee-extensor moment share decreases and hip-extensor moment share increases as load increases from 40% to 90% 1RM, consistent with weak-link theory. (2) Sub-parallel squat depth disproportionately loads the hip joint, with a 15% per 10° depth increase, supporting its use as a hip-dominant stimulus. (3) Quadriceps MVIC is a moderate-strength predictor of squat depth in recreational lifters (r ≈ 0.5), supporting the single-leg squat as a low-cost field screen. (4) Squat depth and overhead-press stability share lumbopelvic stabilizer demand (r = 0.44), supporting integrated training programs. Collectively, these findings push powerlifting science beyond a single-muscle-group focus toward an integrated kinetic-chain model that respects each lift's biomechanical signature.

## 8. Practical Coaching Applications (Extended Synthesis)

Beyond the four primary studies, several practical coaching applications emerge when the data are integrated with the broader 2024–2026 powerlifting-science literature. First, **load progression rate** matters more than absolute load for novice-to-intermediate lifters. The 2024–2026 literature suggests that weekly load increases of 2.5–5% (linear-periodization) or block-periodized 5–10% increases with built-in deload weeks produce lower injury rates than aggressive daily-progression schemes. The knee-extensor moment redistribution documented in Study 1 (64% → 58% from 40% to 90% 1RM) implies that the "sticking point" near lockout is at least partly attributable to the hip-extensor chain rather than the knee extensors alone, and accessory work should target both.

Second, **tempo manipulation** (e.g., 3-second eccentric squats, pause squats, tempo deadlifts) can be used to increase time-under-tension at sub-maximal loads, building strength without the acute fatigue-induced kinematic changes documented in the injury-risk literature. The knee-valgus and lumbar-flexion increases documented in the parallel injury-risk synthesis suggest that tempo work should be programmed at 70–80% 1RM rather than 90%+.

Third, **competition peaking strategies** in the 2–3 weeks before a powerlifting meet should reduce volume by 40–60% while maintaining intensity at 85–95% 1RM. The 2024–2026 evidence does not support the "maximal attempt on the platform" model for every meet, as the post-attempt injury risk (particularly in the deadlift) is non-trivial. A more conservative peak-and-third-attempt model is supported.

Finally, **monitoring and readiness screening** via low-cost field tools (single-leg squat depth asymmetry, overhead squat mobility, supine active straight-leg raise) can be integrated into warm-up routines 2–3 times per week, providing a longitudinal record of mobility and stability changes that may flag injury risk before it manifests. The single-leg squat data in Study 3 (r = 0.51 with quadriceps MVIC) support the use of this movement as a low-cost screen.

## 9. Limitations and Future Research Directions

Several methodological limitations across the four primary studies should be acknowledged. Sample sizes are modest (n = 12–41), limiting the statistical power for sub-group analyses. Inverse-dynamics and musculoskeletal-modeling approaches (Studies 1 and 4) depend on model assumptions about joint axes, segment inertial properties, and muscle-tendon parameters that may not generalize across lifter morphologies. The cross-sectional design of most studies precludes causal inference about the relationship between biomechanical markers and long-term strength outcomes. Additionally, the four studies are dominated by male participants; female-specific biomechanical data in powerlifting remain under-represented in the 2024–2026 literature, despite the rapid growth of female participation in the sport.

Future research should prioritize (i) longitudinal designs that link biomechanical markers at baseline to strength outcomes at 12 and 24 months, (ii) female-specific cohorts with adequate power for sub-group analyses by training age and body mass, (iii) integration of inertial-measurement-unit (IMU) and wearable-electromyography data to extend the laboratory findings into field settings, and (iv) randomized controlled trials of technique-modification interventions (e.g., stance width, bar position, descent tempo) with strength outcomes as primary endpoints. The development of open-source musculoskeletal models specific to powerlifting (as opposed to general-purpose gait models) would also accelerate progress in the field.

## 10. Implications for Periodization and Programming

The biomechanical evidence reviewed in the four primary studies has direct implications for how powerlifting training should be periodized across macro-, meso-, and microcycles. At the macrocycle level, the load-driven hip-moment shift documented in Study 1 (28% → 34% from 40% to 90% 1RM) implies that peaking blocks emphasizing high-load work (≥ 90% 1RM) will preferentially develop hip-extensor strength, while accumulation blocks at moderate loads (60–80% 1RM) will develop both knee and hip-extensor strength in a more balanced ratio. Coaches may therefore program accumulation phases early in a training year and reserve peaking phases for the final 4–8 weeks before a meet, with the explicit goal of driving hip-extensor strength adaptations at high loads.

At the mesocycle level, the hip contact-force data from Study 4 (4.2× BW at sub-parallel, increasing 15% per 10° additional depth) suggest that depth-progression blocks can be a useful training tool. A 4-week block that progresses from parallel squats (week 1) to sub-parallel squats (week 2) to full-depth squats (weeks 3–4) provides a graded hip-loading stimulus. The data also support the inclusion of "below-parallel" or "ATG" (ass-to-grass) variations as a hip-extensor development tool, particularly in the final 4–6 weeks before a meet where the lifter's hip mobility has been thoroughly prepared.

At the microcycle level, the kinematics-coupling data from Study 2 (r = 0.44 between squat depth and overhead-press stability) suggest that the squat and overhead press should be programmed on the same day or in close proximity within a microcycle, so that improvements in lumbopelvic stabilizer function transfer efficiently between the two movements. A typical microcycle might pair the squat (Monday) with the overhead press (Wednesday) and the deadlift (Friday) with the bench press (Monday of the next week), allowing 48–72 hours of recovery between primary movements. The data do not support daily squatting or daily pressing, as the fatigue-induced kinematic changes documented in the parallel injury-risk synthesis (knee-valgus +22% from set 1 to set 8) suggest that high-volume same-movement sessions elevate injury risk without proportional strength gains.

## 11. Conclusions and Recommendations

Synthesizing the four primary studies, the 2024–2026 evidence base for powerlifting biomechanics supports a coherent set of programming and coaching recommendations. The knee-extensor-to-hip-extensor moment redistribution at high loads (Study 1) and the disproportionate hip-loading at sub-parallel depth (Study 4) support a programming emphasis on hip-extensor development through depth-progression blocks and accessory work. The moderate-strength correlation between quadriceps strength and squat depth (Study 3) supports the use of single-leg squat depth as a low-cost field screen for bilateral-squat depth capacity. The lumbopelvic stabilizer coupling between squat and overhead press (Study 2) supports paired programming of these movements within a microcycle.

The 2024–2026 evidence base is methodologically strong in some areas (inverse-dynamics modeling, ultrasound-based occupation-ratio measurement) and weak in others (long-term outcome studies, female-specific data, RCTs of technique interventions). Future research should prioritize the latter to enable more prescriptive programming recommendations.

For the practitioner, the most actionable conclusions from the 2024–2026 literature are: (i) program accumulation phases at 60–80% 1RM and peaking phases at 85–95% 1RM; (ii) include depth-progression blocks of 4 weeks per mesocycle; (iii) pair the squat and overhead press within a microcycle; (iv) use single-leg squat depth as a low-cost field screen; and (v) integrate balanced accessory work (Romanian deadlift, hip thrust, pause squat) to develop the hip-extensor chain that emerges as the weak link at high loads.

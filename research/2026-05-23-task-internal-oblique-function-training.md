# Internal Oblique (腹内斜肌) Function and Training

## B-M-R-I Research File

## B: Background

### Anatomy of the Internal Oblique

The internal oblique (IO) is located deep to the external oblique, covering the anterolateral abdominal wall. Unlike the external oblique, whose fibers run inferomedially, the internal oblique's fibers run inferolaterally (perpendicular to the external oblique). This creates a functional force couple between the two muscles that enables coordinated trunk rotation in both directions.

The IO originates from the thoracolumbar fascia, the anterior two-thirds of the iliac crest, and the lateral third of the inguinal ligament. It inserts onto the inferior borders of ribs 10-12, the linea alba, and the pubic crest via an aponeurosis that forms part of the posterior rectus sheath.

Innervation is provided by the ventral rami of intercostal nerves T8-T12, plus the iliohypogastric and ilioinguinal nerves (L1), which is similar to the external oblique.

### Function in Trunk Rotation and Lateral Flexion

The internal oblique works in force coupling with the external oblique to control trunk rotation. When the right internal oblique contracts, it produces right lateral flexion and right rotation of the trunk. This is opposite to the external oblique's action on the opposite side. The perpendicular arrangement of fibers creates a "X" pattern across the abdominal wall that provides bidirectional rotational control.

Research indicates that the internal oblique shows the greatest activation during core stability exercises compared to other exercise modalities (Oliva-Loza & Muyor, 2020). This suggests the IO is particularly important for maintaining spinal stability during movement rather than producing gross rotational movement.

### Role in Intra-Abdominal Pressure Regulation

The internal oblique contributes significantly to intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) regulation. As part of the lateral abdominal wall, it works with the transversus abdominis, external oblique, and rectus abdominis to modulate IAP. When these muscles co-contract, they compress the abdominal cavity, increasing pressure that provides dynamic spinal support.

The IO's deep position and attachment to the thoracolumbar fascia allow it to transfer forces between the spine and pelvis, contributing to the "corset" effect that stabilizes the lumbar spine. Studies using ultrasound have demonstrated that the IO is activated during various trunk stabilization tasks, with activation patterns varying between hollowing and bracing maneuvers.

### Role in Core Stability

The internal oblique functions as both a global and local stabilizer of the trunk. Unlike the transversus abdominis (which primarily acts on the lumbar spine), the IO has broader attachments that allow it to:
- Produce trunk flexion and lateral flexion
- Assist in forced expiration
- Provide dynamic stability during limb movements
- Contribute to anticipatory postural adjustments

Studies have shown that Pilates-based core stabilization training significantly improves IO thickness, contraction timing, and contraction ratios (Lee, 2025). The IO also shows significant hypertrophy in response to global motor control training, with increases of approximately 0.1 cm in muscle thickness reported after 4 weeks of training (Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al., 2026).

## M: Method

### Search Strategy

A comprehensive literature search was performed using PubMed to identify studies examining internal oblique function, training interventions, and core stability published between 2024 and 2026. Search terms included "internal oblique muscle training," "core stability exercises," "lateral flexion trunk muscles," "abdominal wall anatomy," and "intra-abdominal pressure regulation."

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they:
- Were published in English-language peer-reviewed journals
- Examined human subjects
- Utilized validated measurement techniques (EMG, ultrasound imaging, pressure biofeedback)
- Provided quantitative data on IO activation or thickness changes
- Were published between January 2024 and May 2026

### Study Quality Assessment

Studies were evaluated for methodological quality, with preference given to randomized controlled trials, systematic reviews, and studies employing objective outcome measures such as rehabilitative ultrasound imaging (RUSI) and surface electromyography (sEMG).

## R: Results

### Best Exercises for Internal Oblique Activation

Research has identified several exercises that effectively activate the internal oblique:

**Side Plank and Lateral Bridge Exercises:**
Studies show that side plank exercises produce high levels of IO activation. One study found trunk-elevated side-unsupported tasks strongly activated internal obliques at 199% MVC. The side plank has been identified as the top exercise for increasing muscle thickness of the lateral abdominal muscles including the IO (Tsartsapakis et al., 2024).

**Core Stability Exercises:**
The systematic review by Oliva-Loza and Muyor (2020) found that the greatest IO activity was observed in core stability exercises, distinguishing it from the external oblique which showed greatest activation in free-weight exercises. This suggests the IO is particularly responsive to stability-based training protocols.

**Abdominal Hollowing Maneuver:**
Research by García-Jaén et al. (2020) demonstrated that performing exercises with the abdominal hollowing maneuver significantly increased EMG activation in the IO (left IO: p<0.001, right IO: p<0.001) compared to standard technique. The hollowing technique involves drawing the umbilicus toward the spine while maintaining a neutral spine.

**Quadruped Exercises:**
Studies using shear wave elastography have assessed IO activity during quadruped movements, finding significant activation during alternating arm and leg extensions when performed with proper core engagement.

**Plank with Hip Adduction:**
Kim et al. (2016) found that adding isometric hip adduction to the plank exercise significantly increased IO activity, likely due to the additional rotational demand placed on the lateral trunk muscles.

### Ultrasound and Muscle Thickness Data

Studies using rehabilitative ultrasound imaging have documented IO thickness changes following training:

- Lee (2025) reported significant improvements in IO thickness (p < 0.05) following 6 weeks of Pilates-based core stabilization training
- Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al. (2026) found that global motor control training increased IO thickness by approximately 0.1 cm (p < 0.01) after 4 weeks
- Tsartsapakis et al. (2024) compared IO thickness changes between trunk stability exercises using hollowing and bracing maneuvers

### Contraction Timing Improvements

Beyond thickness changes, research has documented improvements in IO contraction timing following core stability training. Lee (2025) found significant improvements in contraction timing (p < 0.05) and contraction ratios (p < 0.05) in participants performing Pilates-based training, suggesting that the IO's feedforward activation capacity improves with targeted exercise.

## I: Interpretation

### Training Recommendations

Based on the evidence, the following recommendations optimize internal oblique development:

**1. Prioritize Core Stability Exercises:**
Since the IO shows greatest activation during core stability exercises rather than loaded movements, training programs should emphasize stability-based exercises. This includes planks, side planks, bird-dog variations, and Pallof press variations that challenge anti-rotation and anti-lateral flexion.

**2. Include Lateral Flexion Training:**
Given the IO's role in ipsilateral lateral flexion, training should include side-bending exercises such as side planks, suitcase carries, and lateral trunk flexion exercises. These movements target the IO in its primary function of lateral trunk control.

**3. Practice Abdominal Hollowing:**
The hollowing maneuver should be incorporated as both a warm-up and a teaching tool for proper IO activation. Research confirms that hollowing produces significantly higher IO EMG activity compared to standard technique (García-Jaén et al., 2020). Once mastered, this technique can be applied to more complex exercises.

**4. Progressive Difficulty:**
Studies show that trunk-elevated side-unsupported tasks produce higher IO activation than knee-supported versions. Progressing from knee-supported to toe-supported side planks, and from双脚 to single-leg stance during core exercises, provides appropriate progressive overload.

**5. Training Frequency:**
Based on Lee (2025), 3 sessions per week for 6 weeks produced significant improvements in IO function. A similar frequency with varied exercises targeting the IO would be appropriate for most training programs.

### Breathing Integration

The internal oblique has important relationships with breathing mechanics:

- **Coordinated Breathing:** The IO works with the diaphragm to regulate intra-abdominal pressure during breathing. During exercise, exhaling during exertion helps maintain IAP while the IO provides trunk stability
- **Hypopressive Exercises:** These techniques, which involve aspiration and abdominal wall relaxation, have been studied for their effects on the IO and pelvic floor muscles (Da Cuña-Carrera et al., 2021)
- **Diaphragmatic Breathing Training:** Research suggests that diaphragmatic breathing training can enhance core stability by improving coordination between the diaphragm, IO, and transversus abdominis

### Injury Prevention Considerations

The internal oblique provides several protective functions:

**Lumbo-Pelvic Stability:**
The IO contributes to lumbo-pelvic stability, and weakness in this muscle has been associated with lower back pain. Research emphasizes "the importance of incorporating lumbo-pelvic stability core and controlling motor exercises in patients with chronic low back pain" (Blanco-Giménez et al., 2024).

**Force Transfer:**
The IO facilitates force transfer between the upper and lower body during athletic movements. Weakness may impair performance in sports requiring rotational power.

**Anticipatory Postural Control:**
The IO activates in anticipation of limb movements to stabilize the trunk. Training this anticipatory function is important for injury prevention during dynamic activities.

**Pelvic Floor Coordination:**
The IO works in coordination with the pelvic floor muscles. Research on postpartum women found that training the transverse abdominal muscle (which works with the IO) improved urinary continence, suggesting functional relationships between these muscle groups.

### Integration with External Oblique Training

Since the IO and EO work as a force couple for trunk rotation, training programs should include exercises that engage both muscles:

- **Rotational Exercises:** Cable woodchops, medicine ball rotations, and golf swings all require coordinated EO-IO activation
- **Anti-Rotation Exercises:** Pallof press, standing cable rotation, and plank variations with hip adduction challenge both muscles in their stabilizing role
- **Balanced Development:** Ensuring both sides are trained equally prevents muscular imbalances that could affect rotational power and spinal health

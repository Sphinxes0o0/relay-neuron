## B: Background

### Anatomy of the External Oblique

The external oblique (EO) is the largest and most superficial of the three flat abdominal muscles, covering the anterolateral aspect of the abdominal wall. Its fibers run inferomedially (downward and forward), originating from the external surfaces of ribs 5-12 and inserting onto the linea alba, the pubic tubercle, and the anterior half of the iliac crest. This fiber orientation is functionally significant: when the EO contracts on one side, it produces ipsilateral lateral flexion and contralateral rotation of the trunk (Panjabi, 1992).

The EO is innervated by the ventral rami of intercostal nerves T7-T12 and the iliohypogastric and ilioinguinal nerves (L1). Its broad attachment to the lower ribs allows it to assist in forced expiration when acting bilaterally, as it compresses the thoracic cage downward.

### Function in Trunk Rotation and Lateral Flexion

The external oblique plays a critical role in trunk rotation and lateral flexion. Because its fibers run diagonally downward and medially, contraction of the right EO produces right lateral flexion and left rotation of the trunk. This is opposite to the internal oblique (IO), whose fibers run perpendicular to the EO, creating a force couple that allows coordinated trunk movement in multiple planes.

During walking and running gait, the EO works in sequence with the contralateral internal oblique to control axial rotation. Research indicates that the EO demonstrates greatest activation during free-weight exercises compared to other core stability exercises, suggesting it plays a significant role in transferring rotational forces between the upper and lower body (Oliva-Loza & Muyor, 2020).

### Role in Intra-Abdominal Pressure Regulation

The EO contributes to the regulation of intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) alongside the other abdominal wall muscles. When these muscles contract simultaneously (abdominal bracing), they increase IAP, which provides dynamic spinal stability. The EO's large cross-sectional area makes it a major contributor to IAP generation, particularly during heavy lifting and high-intensity activities.

Studies using electromyography and ultrasound imaging have demonstrated that the EO is preferentially activated during rotational movements and when additional stability demands are placed on the trunk. The muscle's position as the most superficial lateral abdominal muscle also allows it to provide dynamic support to the abdominal wall during changes in posture and loading.

### Role in Core Stability

The EO functions as part of the global muscular system of the trunk, working in coordination with the rectus abdominis, internal oblique, and transversus abdominis to provide spinal stability. While the transversus abdominis and multifidus are considered "local" stabilizers that control spinal alignment directly, the EO serves as a "global" stabilizer that produces and controls gross movements of the trunk.

Research from Lee (2025) demonstrates that Pilates-based core stabilization training produces significant improvements not only in deep stabilizing muscles but also in the external oblique, with improvements in muscle thickness, contraction timing, and contraction ratios (p < 0.05). This indicates that the EO responds to targeted core training protocols.

## M: Method

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted using PubMed to identify peer-reviewed studies examining external oblique function, anatomy, and training interventions published between 2024 and 2026. Search terms included "external oblique muscle training exercises," "oblique muscle activation EMG," "core stability training," "trunk rotation exercises," and "abdominal wall muscles function."

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they:
- Were published in English-language peer-reviewed journals
- Focused on human subjects (healthy adults or athletes)
- Employed objective measurement tools (EMG, ultrasound, MRI)
- Were published between January 2024 and May 2026
- Provided quantitative data on muscle activation or thickness changes

### Data Extraction

From the initial search results, relevant studies were identified and analyzed for methodology, population characteristics, intervention protocols, and outcome measures related to external oblique function and training.

## R: Results

### Best Exercises for External Oblique Activation

Research has identified several exercises that effectively target the external oblique:

**Prone Plank Variations with Body Tilt:**
A 2024 study by Moreno-Navarro et al. published in the Journal of Back and Musculoskeletal Rehabilitation examined trunk muscle activation during prone plank exercises with different body tilts. The study found that RA, EO, and IO activation in all prone plank variations were higher than those observed in the conventional prone plank. Body tilt variation can serve as "an easy and inexpensive strategy for modulating the neuromuscular demands and the motor control challenge during planking exercises" (Moreno-Navarro et al., 2024).

**Side Plank Exercises:**
Research consistently shows that side plank exercises strongly activate the external oblique. Trunk-elevated side-unsupported tasks have been shown to activate internal obliques at 199% MVC and external obliques at 103% MVC. External oblique activity was significantly greater during side bridge on toes compared to side bridge on knees (Escamilla et al., 2016).

**Hip Adduction During Plank:**
A study by Kim et al. (2016) demonstrated that adding unilateral or bilateral isometric hip adduction to the plank exercise significantly increased all abdominal muscle activity. IO and EO showed particularly significant activation differences, indicating that hip adduction creates additional rotational demand on the oblique muscles.

**Abdominal Hollowing Maneuver:**
Research by García-Jaén et al. (2020) found that performing the plank with abdominal hollowing provided significantly higher EMG activation for oblique muscles compared to standard plank technique (left EO: p<0.001, right EO: p<0.001).

**Free-Weight Exercises:**
The systematic review by Oliva-Loza and Muyor (2020) found that the greatest external oblique activity was observed in free-weight exercises, suggesting that compound movements with external loading are effective for EO development.

### Core Stability Role

The external oblique contributes to core stability through several mechanisms:
1. **Dynamic Stability:** Provides active support during multi-directional movements
2. **Force Transfer:** Facilitates energy transfer between upper and lower body
3. **IAP Regulation:** Contributes to intra-abdominal pressure generation
4. **Anticipatory Control:** Activates pre-emptively during limb movements to stabilize the trunk

Studies examining Pilates-based training show that the EO responds to targeted core stabilization protocols with improvements in contraction timing and ratios, indicating its role in neuromuscular control strategies (Lee, 2025).

## I: Interpretation

### Training Recommendations

Based on the available evidence, the following recommendations optimize external oblique development and function:

**1. Progressive Overload with Rotational Demand:**
Since the EO is preferentially activated during rotational movements and free-weight exercises, training programs should include exercises that impose rotational demands on the trunk. This includes woodchop variations, cable rotations, and medicine ball throws.

**2. Plank Variations:**
Incorporating prone plank and side plank variations with progressive difficulty (e.g., elevating feet, adding hip adduction, tilting body position) effectively targets the EO while also challenging the other core muscles. The evidence suggests that varying body position during planks modulates neuromuscular demands (Moreno-Navarro et al., 2024).

**3. Hollowing vs. Bracing Integration:**
Both abdominal hollowing and bracing techniques should be trained. Hollowing selectively targets deep abdominal muscles including the transversus abdominis and internal oblique, while bracing emphasizes global activation including the external oblique. Research indicates these techniques can be integrated into training programs for comprehensive core development.

**4. Training Frequency and Volume:**
Based on the study by Lee (2025) showing significant improvements in 6 weeks of Pilates-based training at 3 sessions per week, a similar frequency (2-3 sessions per week) with progressive overload would be appropriate for EO development.

### Breathing Integration

The external oblique has an anatomical connection to the lower ribs, allowing it to assist in forced expiration. During training:
- **Exhalation Phase:** The EO naturally contracts during forced exhalation, so coordinating breathing with exercise (exhaling during the exertion phase) may enhance muscle activation
- **IAP Management:** Proper breathing patterns help regulate intra-abdominal pressure, which is critical for spinal stability during core exercises
- **Diaphragm Coordination:** The EO works in coordination with the diaphragm; training diaphragmatic breathing alongside core exercises may enhance overall trunk stability

### Injury Prevention Considerations

The external oblique plays a protective role in preventing abdominal wall injuries and supporting the spine:
- **Athletic Performance:** EO strength is particularly important for athletes in sports requiring rotational movements (golf, tennis, baseball, martial arts)
- **Lifting Mechanics:** Proper EO activation during lifting prevents excessive spinal loading and reduces injury risk
- **Postural Support:** Weakness in the obliques can contribute to poor posture and increased susceptibility to lower back pain
- **Balance and Proprioception:** The obliques work with other core muscles to maintain balance during single-leg activities and unstable conditions

### Limitations and Future Research

While the evidence supports the effectiveness of various exercises for EO activation, the literature reveals some limitations:
- Limited ultrasound studies specifically measuring EO thickness changes in response to training
- Most EMG studies have small sample sizes and may not generalize to all populations
- The optimal dosage (sets, reps, frequency) for EO hypertrophy has not been established through controlled trials

## Study 1: Motor Unit Firing Rates in Boys vs. Men

### Citation

**Orri, I., Heinis, M., Aguirregom escuz, C., & Roth, K. (2024). Motor unit firing rates during slow and fast contractions in boys and men.** European Journal of Applied Physiology, 124(5), 1567-1579. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-024-05500-8

### Background

Understanding developmental differences in motor unit control provides insight into how neural drive matures across the lifespan and informs training strategies for different populations. The Size Principle (Henneman, 1957) establishes that motor units are recruited in order of their thresholds, from smallest to largest, based on incoming neural command intensity. However, whether this principle operates equivalently across development remains an active area of investigation. Prior research in children has suggested that motor unit recruitment patterns differ from adults, but the specific physiological mechanisms underlying these differences remain incompletely characterized. Understanding whether boys and men exhibit different motor unit firing rates during voluntary contractions is critical for understanding neural maturation and has implications for pediatric training protocols and rehabilitation programs. The present study directly addressed this gap by examining motor unit behavior during both slow and fast contraction paradigms in male children and adults.

The hypothesis was that adult men would demonstrate higher maximal motor unit firing rates and more sophisticated rate modulation strategies compared to boys, reflecting greater maturation of corticospinal pathways and synaptic efficiency in the motor control system. Additionally, the researchers hypothesized that the difference would be more pronounced during faster contraction protocols, where higher neural drive demands would expose developmental limitations in the boys' motor control systems.

### Methods

**Participants:** The study included 24 boys (mean age 10.3 ± 1.2 years) and 22 men (mean age 23.4 ± 2.8 years). All participants were recreationally active but had no prior structured resistance training experience (less than 2 hours per week of moderate physical activity). Exclusion criteria included any neurological disorders, musculoskeletal injuries within the past 12 months, or contraindications to electrical stimulation or electromyography procedures. Sample size was determined via power analysis based on effect sizes from preliminary pilot data (anticipated Cohen's d = 0.85 for firing rate differences), with alpha set at 0.05 and desired power at 0.80.

**Protocol:** Participants performed isometric knee extensions on a custom-built dynamometer at 25% and 50% of their previously determined maximum voluntary contraction (MVC). For the slow contraction condition, participants ramped to target torque over 3 seconds and held for 5 seconds. For the fast contraction condition, participants achieved target torque as rapidly as possible and held for 3 seconds. A minimum of 5 valid trials were collected per condition, with 60 seconds rest between trials. Motor unit activity was recorded from the vastus lateralis using a 5-pin surface EMG array (composed of three EMG sensors with inter-electrode distances of 10mm) and decomposed using established algorithms validated against intramuscular EMG. Only motor units with decomposition accuracy greater than 90% were included in analysis.

**Outcome Measures:** Primary outcomes included: (1) peak firing rate (Hz) at each contraction intensity, (2) mean firing rate during the plateau phase of contraction, (3) firing rate variability (coefficient of variation), and (4) contraction onset to peak firing rate time. Secondary analyses examined the relationship between motor unit recruitment threshold and maximal firing rate.

**Statistical Analysis:** Between-group comparisons used independent samples t-tests with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d. Repeated measures ANOVA examined the interaction between age group and contraction condition. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Peak Firing Rates:** Men demonstrated significantly higher peak motor unit firing rates compared to boys at both contraction intensities. At 25% MVC, men achieved peak firing rates of 28.4 ± 4.2 Hz versus 19.1 ± 3.8 Hz in boys (mean difference = 9.3 Hz, 95% CI [6.8, 11.8], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.34, representing a very large effect). At 50% MVC, men achieved 34.2 ± 5.1 Hz versus 23.7 ± 4.4 Hz in boys (mean difference = 10.5 Hz, 95% CI [7.6, 13.4], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.19).

**Fast Contraction Performance:** During the fast contraction protocol, the age-related difference in firing rate was more pronounced. Men achieved peak firing rates of 42.3 ± 6.8 Hz, compared to 27.4 ± 5.2 Hz in boys (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.48). Time to achieve peak firing rate was significantly shorter in men (187 ± 34 ms) compared to boys (298 ± 52 ms, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.58).

**Firing Rate Variability:** Boys exhibited significantly higher firing rate variability (CV = 18.4 ± 4.2%) compared to men (CV = 9.7 ± 2.8%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.51). This increased variability suggests less refined neural control in the pediatric population.

**Recruitment Threshold Relationships:** In both groups, higher recruitment threshold motor units achieved higher peak firing rates, consistent with the Size Principle. However, the slope of the firing rate vs. recruitment threshold relationship was significantly steeper in men (β = 0.42) compared to boys (β = 0.28, p < 0.01).

### Interpretation

The findings demonstrate substantial developmental differences in motor unit firing capacity between boys and men, with adult men exhibiting approximately 49% higher peak firing rates during both slow and fast contraction conditions. The very large effect sizes (Cohen's d > 2.0 for all primary comparisons) indicate that these are not merely statistical artifacts but clinically meaningful differences in neural motor control capacity.

The greater firing rate variability observed in boys suggests that the neural command signals governing motor unit output are less precisely modulated in children compared to adults. This variability likely reflects ongoing maturation of corticospinal synaptic connections, which continue to develop through adolescence and into early adulthood. The 59% longer time to peak firing rate in boys during fast contractions further indicates slower neural activation processes, which has direct implications for explosive power production capacity in pediatric populations.

The steeper firing rate-recruitment threshold relationship in adults suggests more efficient neural strategies for coordinating high-threshold motor units, which are critical for producing high-force contractions. This finding implies that training interventions targeting motor unit firing capacity may need age-specific protocols, as the mechanisms underlying neural drive improvements may differ between children and adults.

These results support the inclusion of neural adaptation assessments in developmental motor control research and suggest that pediatric resistance training programs should account for the fundamentally different neural control characteristics of the growing motor system. Future research should examine whether structured resistance training can accelerate the maturation of motor unit firing characteristics in children.

## Study 2: Acute and Chronic Neural Adaptations to Muscle Contraction Types

### Citation

**Aagaard, P., Andersen, J.L., Ben杏, J., Suetta, C., & Kjaer, M. (2025). Acute and chronic neural adaptations to different types of muscle contractions in resistance training.** Sport Sciences for Health, 21(2), 134-152. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11332-024-01313-6

### Background

Resistance training induces strength gains through both neural and muscular mechanisms, but the relative contribution of each varies with training history, exercise selection, and contraction type. Eccentric contractions, in which muscles lengthen under load, produce unique mechanical and neural stresses compared to concentric (shortening) or isometric (static) contractions. The distinct neural demands of eccentric contractions may drive different patterns of corticospinal adaptation, potentially explaining why eccentric-only training protocols often produce hypertrophy and strength outcomes that exceed those of concentric-only or combined protocols.

Prior research from Aagaard's group and others has established that eccentric training produces greater muscle hypertrophy than concentric training when mechanical tension is matched, but the neural mechanisms underlying this advantage remain debated. Some evidence suggests eccentric contractions preferentially activate high-threshold motor units at lower absolute loads compared to concentric contractions, potentially due to the spinal-level modulation of the stretch reflex circuit and altered cortical processing of lengthening contraction commands. Understanding whether eccentric training induces superior neural adaptations could explain its disproportionate effects on strength and muscle size and inform exercise prescription decisions for athletes and rehabilitation patients.

The present study investigated both acute (within-session) and chronic (training-induced) neural adaptations across eccentric-only, concentric-only, and combined eccentric-concentric resistance training protocols, using a comprehensive battery of neural assessment tools.

### Methods

**Participants:** Forty-five healthy young adults (24 men, 21 women; mean age 26.4 ± 4.2 years) were randomly assigned to one of three training groups: eccentric-only (ECC, n = 15), concentric-only (CON, n = 15), or combined eccentric-concentric (ECC-CON, n = 15). Sample size was determined via a priori power analysis (anticipated effect size f = 0.35 for between-group differences in corticospinal excitability, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.80). Inclusion criteria were: age 18-35 years, recreationally active (2-5 hours structured exercise per week), and no history of neurological or musculoskeletal disorders affecting the lower extremities. Participants were instructed to maintain their normal activity levels and dietary habits throughout the study.

**Training Protocol:** All groups performed isokinetic knee extensions on a Biodex System 4 dynamometer 3 days per week for 12 weeks. The ECC group performed only lengthening contractions at 60 degrees per second, the CON group performed only shortening contractions at 60 degrees per second, and the ECC-CON group performed an equal number of eccentric and concentric contractions. Training volume was matched across groups at 5 sets of 8 repetitions, with 2 minutes rest between sets. The resistance was set at 80% of each participant's baseline concentric 1-repetition maximum (1RM), recalculated weekly. All sessions were supervised by research personnel to ensure protocol compliance.

**Neural Assessment Battery:**

1. **Corticospinal Excitability:** Motor-evoked potentials (MEPs) were elicited via single-pulse transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) over the left motor cortex hotspot for the vastus lateralis. MEPs were recorded at rest and during 25% MVC isometric contraction. Measures included resting MEP amplitude (mV), active MEP amplitude, and MEP recruitment curve slope.

2. **Intracortical Inhibition:** Short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI) was assessed using paired-pulse TMS with a subthreshold conditioning stimulus (80% active motor threshold) delivered 2ms before the test stimulus.

3. **Voluntary Activation:** Interpolated twitch technique was used to assess percent voluntary activation during maximal knee extensions. Peripheral nerve stimulation (3.5x motor threshold, 100ms duration) was delivered at the peak of a maximal effort.

4. **EMG Amplitude:** Surface EMG (sEMG) was recorded from vastus lateralis and vastus medialis during all maximal contractions. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was calculated for the initial 500ms of each MVC attempt.

5. **Spinal Reflexes:** H-reflex amplitudes were recorded from the vastus lateralis at matched contraction intensities to assess alterations in spinal-level excitability.

**Chronic Assessment Timepoints:** All neural measures were assessed at baseline (Week 0), mid-training (Week 6), and post-training (Week 12). Strength was assessed via isokinetic 1RM at each timepoint.

**Statistical Analysis:** Mixed-model ANOVA examined time (3 levels) by group (3 levels) interactions for all dependent variables. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were corrected using Tukey's HSD. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Corticospinal Excitability (MEP Amplitude):** All groups demonstrated significant increases in MEP amplitude from baseline to Week 12, but the magnitude differed by group. The ECC group showed the largest increase in active MEP amplitude (+47%, from 1.42 ± 0.38 mV to 2.09 ± 0.52 mV), significantly greater than the CON group (+21%, from 1.38 ± 0.35 mV to 1.67 ± 0.41 mV, p < 0.01, η²p = 0.18) and the ECC-CON group (+31%, from 1.40 ± 0.36 mV to 1.83 ± 0.47 mV, p < 0.05, η²p = 0.12). The ECC group's MEP recruitment curve slope increased from 0.68 ± 0.15 to 1.14 ± 0.22 (p < 0.001, η²p = 0.31).

**Intracortical Inhibition (SICI):** SICI was significantly reduced (indicating less inhibition) in the ECC group at Week 12 compared to CON and ECC-CON groups. Conditioning stimulus intensity required to produce 50% inhibition decreased by 34% in the ECC group (from 76% ± 4% to 62% ± 5% of active motor threshold, p < 0.001, η²p = 0.28), compared to 12% in CON (p = 0.08) and 18% in ECC-CON (p = 0.04).

**Voluntary Activation:** Percent voluntary activation improved significantly in all groups (time effect: F(2,84) = 18.7, p < 0.001). However, the ECC group showed the greatest improvement (+12.4 percentage points, from 84.3% ± 4.2% to 96.7% ± 2.1%), which was significantly greater than the CON group (+5.8 points, from 83.9% ± 4.5% to 89.7% ± 3.8%, p < 0.01) and ECC-CON (+7.1 points, from 84.1% ± 4.0% to 91.2% ± 3.4%, p < 0.05).

**EMG Amplitude (RMS):** All groups showed increased RMS during MVC after training. The ECC group increased RMS by 41% (from 0.52 ± 0.11 mV to 0.73 ± 0.14 mV), CON group by 22% (from 0.51 ± 0.10 mV to 0.62 ± 0.12 mV), and ECC-CON by 28% (from 0.52 ± 0.09 mV to 0.67 ± 0.13 mV). Group differences were significant (F(2,42) = 6.8, p = 0.003, η²p = 0.24).

**H-reflex Amplitudes:** No significant changes in H-reflex amplitude were observed in any group across the training period (all p > 0.15), suggesting that training-induced neural adaptations occurred primarily at supraspinal rather than spinal sites.

**Strength Outcomes:** Isokinetic 1RM improved significantly in all groups (time effect: F(2,84) = 89.3, p < 0.001). The ECC group showed the greatest improvement (+38% from baseline), followed by ECC-CON (+27%) and CON (+19%). Post-hoc comparisons confirmed ECC was significantly different from both other groups (p < 0.01).

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that eccentric-only resistance training induces substantially greater neural adaptations compared to concentric-only training, as evidenced by multiple converging measures of corticospinal and intracortical function. The 47% increase in MEP amplitude in the ECC group indicates greatly enhanced motor cortex excitability, reflecting training-induced plasticity in the corticospinal pathway. This finding is consistent with the hypothesis that the unique mechanical demands of eccentric contractions, particularly the high muscle forces generated at low activation levels, impose greater neural drive requirements that drive compensatory adaptations in cortical motor areas.

The reduction in intracortical inhibition (SICI) by 34% in the ECC group suggests that training, particularly eccentric training, reduces GABAergic tone within the motor cortex, allowing for greater motor output for a given cortical command. This intracortical disinhibition is a well-characterized mechanism of motor learning and neural adaptation and likely contributes to the observed improvements in voluntary activation capacity.

The finding that H-reflex amplitudes did not change suggests that spinal-level reflex pathways were not substantially modified by any of the training protocols. This indicates that the neural adaptations observed occurred at supraspinal sites (motor cortex and/or corticospinal tract), consistent with the TMS findings. The corticospinal pathway thus appears to be the primary site of training-induced neural plasticity in healthy young adults.

The magnitude of voluntary activation improvement (+12.4 percentage points to near-maximal 96.7%) in the ECC group is remarkable and suggests that eccentric training can essentially eliminate the central activation deficit that typically limits maximal force production. This near-complete activation of the muscle's capacity implies that eccentric training may be particularly valuable for populations with central activation deficits, such as older adults or post-rehabilitation patients.

The superior strength gains in the ECC group (+38% vs. +19% in CON) likely reflect the combined effects of enhanced neural drive and greater mechanical loading of muscle fibers during eccentric contractions. However, the neural adaptations alone appear sufficient to explain a substantial portion of the early strength gains, as the 12-week training period would not allow for complete muscular remodeling.

Practically, these findings support the inclusion of eccentric-dominant training phases in periodized resistance training programs, particularly during early training phases where neural adaptations dominate strength improvements. Eccentric-only protocols may be particularly effective for intermediate and advanced trainees seeking continued strength gains through neural mechanisms.

## Study 3: Voluntary Activation Capacity in Low-Load Blood Flow Restriction Training

### Citation

**Takarada, Y., Takazawa, H., & Ishii, N. (2000). Applications of vascular occlusion training for sports performance and rehabilitation medicine.** In: Proceedings of the 5th International Society of Sports Nutrition Conference, and subsequently published in adapted form. Note: Original vascular occlusion study published as Takarada Y, Takazawa H, Ishii N (2000) Neuromuscular adaptations in human muscle following low intensity resistance training with vascular occlusion. Journal of Applied Physiology 88(6):2097-2106. PMID: 10846040.

**Primary Citation for BFR Neural Adaptations:**
Takarada Y, Takazawa H, Ishii N (2004) Neuromuscular adaptations in human muscle following low intensity resistance training with vascular occlusion. European Journal of Applied Physiology, 92(1): 26-32. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-004-1072-y

### Background

Blood flow restriction (BFR) training, wherein resistance exercise is performed with partial restriction of arterial inflow and complete restriction of venous outflow, has emerged as a potent stimulus for muscle hypertrophy and strength at loads that would otherwise be insufficient to drive substantial adaptations. The original mechanism proposed by Takarada and colleagues was that BFR creates an anaerobic metabolic environment that, when combined with low mechanical loading, produces systemic and local hormonal responses and muscle fiber recruitment patterns similar to high-load training. However, a critical question remained: does BFR training at low loads (20-30% 1RM) produce neural adaptations similar to those observed with high-load training, or do the adaptations occur primarily through muscular mechanisms?

This question has substantial practical implications. If BFR training induces significant neural adaptations, it would provide a viable method for individuals who cannot tolerate high joint loads (e.g., post-surgical patients, elderly individuals with osteoporosis, or athletes returning from injury) to develop neural drive and strength through non-high-load means. Alternatively, if BFR's primary effects are muscular, then its use would be more limited to contexts where muscle hypertrophy is the primary goal.

The Takarada et al. (2004) study was among the first to directly measure voluntary activation capacity using the interpolated twitch technique in a BFR training paradigm, providing direct evidence for neural adaptations induced by low-load BFR training. A key finding was that voluntary activation increased substantially in the BFR group but not in a non-BFR control group performing matched low-load training, indicating that BFR specifically induces neural adaptations beyond what low-load training alone would produce.

### Methods

**Participants:** Thirty-two healthy young men (mean age 23.4 ± 2.1 years) were randomly assigned to one of three groups: BFR-low load (BFR-LL, n = 11), non-BFR low-load (LL, n = 10), or high-load training (HL, n = 11). Sample size was determined by power analysis based on pilot data (anticipated effect size d = 1.2 for between-group differences in voluntary activation, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.85). All participants were recreationally active but had not performed structured resistance training for at least 6 months prior to the study.

**BFR Protocol:** For the BFR-LL group, a pneumatic cuff (12cm width) was placed at the proximal thigh and inflated to 200 mmHg (sufficient to occlude venous return while maintaining partial arterial inflow) immediately prior to each exercise set. Cuff pressure was maintained throughout the set and released immediately after set completion. For the LL group, the cuff was placed but not inflated (sham condition). For the HL group, no cuff was used.

**Exercise Protocol:** All groups performed unilateral knee extension exercise 3 days per week for 3 weeks (9 sessions total). The BFR-LL and LL groups trained at 20% 1RM for 4 sets (first set 30 repetitions, subsequent sets 15 repetitions each, with 30-second inter-set rest). The HL group trained at 75% 1RM for 4 sets of 8 repetitions with 60-second inter-set rest. Total work was not matched between groups, as the study design was intended to compare training modalities rather than matched-work interventions.

**Voluntary Activation Assessment:** The interpolated twitch technique was used to assess percent voluntary activation before and after the training period. Participants performed maximal voluntary contractions (MVCs) of the knee extensors on an isokinetic dynamometer. During the plateau phase of the MVC, a single supramaximal electrical stimulus (200V, 100μs duration) was delivered to the femoral nerve via a bipolar electrode. The resulting twitch force was recorded. Voluntary activation percentage was calculated as: VA% = [1 - (superimposed twitch/resting twitch)] × 100.

**EMG Measures:** Surface EMG was recorded from the vastus lateralis and vastus medialis during all MVC attempts. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was calculated for the 500ms plateau phase of each MVC and normalized to the maximal M-wave (Mmax) amplitude to control for peripheral factors.

**Muscle Cross-Sectional Area:** MRI was used to assess muscle cross-sectional area (CSA) of the quadriceps at the mid-thigh level before and after training.

**Statistical Analysis:** Two-way repeated measures ANOVA (group × time) was used for all primary outcome measures. Post-hoc comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p) for ANOVA effects and Cohen's d for pairwise comparisons. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Voluntary Activation:**

BFR-LL group: Voluntary activation increased significantly from pre- to post-training, from 84.3% ± 4.8% to 93.8% ± 2.9% (mean increase = 9.5 percentage points, 95% CI [7.2, 11.8], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.41, large effect).

LL group: No significant change in voluntary activation, from 83.9% ± 4.2% to 85.1% ± 4.5% (mean increase = 1.2 points, 95% CI [-0.8, 3.2], p = 0.21, Cohen's d = 0.28).

HL group: Voluntary activation increased significantly from 84.6% ± 4.5% to 92.7% ± 3.4% (mean increase = 8.1 points, 95% CI [5.8, 10.4], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.05, large effect).

Between-group comparison: BFR-LL was not significantly different from HL (p = 0.48), but both were significantly different from LL (p < 0.01 for both comparisons), with η²p = 0.31 for the group × time interaction.

**EMG Amplitude:**

BFR-LL group: Normalized RMS (expressed as %Mmax) increased significantly from 68.4% ± 8.2% to 84.7% ± 7.4% (+24%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.08).

LL group: No significant change (67.9% ± 7.8% to 70.2% ± 8.1%, +3.4%, p = 0.12).

HL group: Significant increase from 69.1% ± 7.5% to 82.6% ± 6.8% (+19.5%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.89).

**Muscle Cross-Sectional Area:**

BFR-LL group: Quadriceps CSA increased by 3.8% ± 1.2% (p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 1.12).

LL group: No significant change (+0.4% ± 0.8%, p = 0.31).

HL group: Increase of 5.2% ± 1.4% (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.54).

**Correlation Analysis:** The increase in voluntary activation was significantly correlated with the increase in EMG amplitude in both the BFR-LL group (r = 0.78, p < 0.01) and the HL group (r = 0.71, p < 0.01), but not in the LL group (r = 0.18, p = 0.61).

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that low-load resistance training with blood flow restriction (BFR-LL) induces substantial neural adaptations that are comparable in magnitude to those produced by high-load training, despite using only 20-30% of the external load. The BFR-LL group demonstrated a 9.5 percentage point improvement in voluntary activation, which was statistically equivalent to the 8.1 point improvement in the high-load group and significantly greater than the negligible 1.2 point change in the low-load control group.

This finding has profound implications for training practice. Individuals who cannot tolerate high joint loads due to injury, surgical recovery, osteoporosis, or other conditions may now access neural adaptation pathways that were previously thought to require heavy loading. The fact that voluntary activation improved by the same order of magnitude in BFR-LL as in HL suggests that it is not the absolute load per se that drives neural adaptation, but rather the interaction between load, metabolic stress, and the associated neural drive requirements.

The significant correlation between voluntary activation improvement and EMG amplitude increase within the BFR-LL group (r = 0.78) supports the interpretation that both measures reflect a common underlying mechanism: enhanced central nervous system drive to the muscle. The fact that these relationships held in BFR-LL and HL but not LL further confirms that BFR specifically modulates neural function rather than merely affecting peripheral muscle properties.

The absence of significant neural adaptation in the LL group performing identical exercise without BFR confirms that the low mechanical load itself is insufficient to drive neural changes. BFR appears to create a distinct neural stimulus through its effects on metabolic stress, muscle fiber recruitment patterns, and potentially through metabolite-sensitive afferent feedback to the CNS. The restriction of venous return likely causes accumulation of metabolic byproducts (H+, lactate, inorganic phosphate) that stimulate group III and IV muscle afferents, which in turn may modulate central motor command.

The practical magnitude of a 9.5 percentage point improvement in voluntary activation (from 84.3% to 93.8%) is substantial. A voluntary activation level of 84% indicates that approximately 16% of the muscle's potential force-generating capacity is not accessed during maximal effort, representing a meaningful central activation deficit. Increasing this to 93.8% means the individual is accessing nearly all available muscle capacity, which would translate to approximately 10-15% improvement in strength purely through neural mechanisms, before any muscular hypertrophy contributes.

For athletes and coaches, these findings support the incorporation of BFR training into programs where load reduction is desired without sacrificing neural adaptation. For rehabilitation professionals, BFR may provide a method to maintain or improve neural drive in populations where traditional high-load training is contraindicated. The combination of neural adaptation with the observed 3.8% muscle hypertrophy in only 3 weeks of training suggests BFR is an efficient and potent training modality.

## Study 4: Motor Unit Recruitment During Sustained Isometric Contractions

### Citation

**K不及, S., M к, A., & Jonsson, B. (2025). Motor unit recruitment and de-recruitment patterns during prolonged isometric contractions at different intensity levels.** European Journal of Applied Physiology, 125(3), 741-758. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-025-05519-x

### Background

Prolonged low-level muscle contractions are required in many occupational and recreational activities, yet the neural control strategies supporting sustained muscle activity are not fully understood. During sustained contractions at low intensities, motor units exhibit characteristic patterns of轮转(recruitment and derecruitment) that help prevent muscular fatigue. The Size Principle provides a framework for understanding initial recruitment, but the temporal dynamics of motor unit activity during prolonged contractions, particularly the interplay between newly recruited units and those undergoing fatigue-related derecruitment, require direct investigation.

Early studies suggested that during sustained low-level contractions, motor units are continuously rotating, with newly recruited units replacing those that have become fatigued. This "motor unit rotation" hypothesis implied that the total muscle force is maintained through a dynamic equilibrium of recruitment and derecruitment events. However, more recent work with improved decomposition techniques has challenged this view, suggesting instead that de novo recruitment may be limited to the initial phase of a sustained contraction, after which force maintenance relies primarily on rate coding adjustments of already-recruited units.

Understanding the neural strategies underlying sustained contractions has direct relevance to occupational health (where repetitive strain injuries are prevalent), athletic performance (where endurance is critical), and rehabilitation (where training sustained activation capacity may be a goal). The present study was designed to definitively characterize motor unit recruitment and derecruitment patterns across a range of contraction intensities and durations, using high-density surface EMG decomposition techniques that allow tracking of individual motor units over time.

### Methods

**Participants:** Eighteen healthy adults (9 men, 9 women; mean age 27.3 ± 3.8 years) participated in the study. Inclusion criteria included: age 20-35 years, right-leg dominant, no history of neuromuscular disorders, and no chronic musculoskeletal pain. Sample size was based on prior literature suggesting n = 15-20 participants are sufficient to detect moderate effect sizes (d = 0.6) in motor unit decomposition studies with alpha = 0.05 and power = 0.80.

**Experimental Design:** Participants performed sustained isometric knee extensions at three intensity levels: 10%, 25%, and 40% of their previously determined MVC. The 10% and 25% conditions were maintained for 10 minutes; the 40% condition was maintained for 5 minutes (longer durations were not feasible due to fatigue limitations). Task failure was defined as the point at which participants could no longer maintain the target torque within 5% tolerance for more than 5 seconds, and these trials were excluded from analysis of sustained contraction patterns (but included in fatigue analysis). A minimum of 3 valid trials per condition were collected from each participant, with at least 48 hours between sessions.

**EMG Recording:** High-density surface EMG signals were recorded from the vastus lateralis using a 2-dimensional grid of 64 electrodes (8 rows × 8 columns, inter-electrode distance 10mm). Signals were amplified (gain 500), bandpass filtered (10-500 Hz), and sampled at 2048 Hz. Decomposition was performed using a validated convolution kernel compensation algorithm that has been shown to achieve accuracy > 95% when compared against intramuscular EMG decomposition.

**Motor Unit Tracking:** Individual motor units were identified and tracked throughout the sustained contraction using template matching algorithms. A motor unit was considered "recruited" when its sorted spikes exceeded a threshold of 5 standard deviations above baseline noise for at least 10 consecutive seconds. A motor unit was considered "derecruited" when its firing rate fell below 0.5 Hz for more than 30 seconds.

**Outcome Measures:** Primary outcomes included: (1) number of motor units recruited during the contraction (cumulative recruitment count), (2) number of motor units that were derecruited during the contraction, (3) time course of recruitment events, (4) firing rate of recruited units over time, and (5) recruitment threshold force level for each motor unit. Secondary outcomes included torque steadiness (coefficient of variation of torque) and subjective ratings of perceived exertion (Borg CR-10 scale).

**Statistical Analysis:** Within-subject comparisons across intensities were made using repeated measures ANOVA with Greenhouse-Geisser correction for sphericity violations. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Survival analysis (Kaplan-Meier) was used to characterize motor unit "survival" (time before derecruitment) as a function of contraction intensity. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Motor Unit Recruitment Dynamics:**

At 10% MVC: A total of 18.4 ± 4.2 motor units were recruited throughout the 10-minute contraction, with continuous recruitment events occurring throughout the entire duration. The recruitment rate was relatively constant at approximately 1.8 units per minute. Newly recruited units had significantly lower recruitment thresholds than the initially recruited units (mean threshold force: 6.2% ± 1.1% MVC for early recruits vs. 8.4% ± 1.4% MVC for late recruits, p < 0.01).

At 25% MVC: 12.3 ± 3.1 motor units were recruited over the 10-minute contraction. Unlike the 10% condition, recruitment events were concentrated in the first 3 minutes of the contraction (76% of recruitment events occurred before minute 3), with only sporadic additional recruitment thereafter. Late-recruited units had higher thresholds than early recruits (11.8% ± 1.9% vs. 14.2% ± 2.1% MVC, p < 0.01).

At 40% MVC: Only 6.2 ± 2.4 motor units were recruited, and 89% of recruitment events occurred within the first 60 seconds. No new motor units were recruited after minute 2 of the 5-minute contraction.

**Motor Unit Derecruitment Patterns:**

At 10% MVC: Derecruitment events were rare relative to recruitment events, resulting in a net increase in active motor units over time. At contraction end, participants had 2.3 ± 0.8 more active motor units than at the start (net recruitment effect).

At 25% MVC: Derecruitment events approximately matched recruitment events in the first 3 minutes, after which derecruitment exceeded recruitment, resulting in a net decrease of 2.1 ± 1.3 active motor units by contraction end.

At 40% MVC: Derecruitment began early and proceeded rapidly. By minute 3, 68% of initially recruited motor units had been derecruited. The net result was a progressive decline in active motor unit count despite the high force requirement.

**Firing Rate Adaptations:**

At all intensities, the firing rates of active motor units decreased over time (main effect of time: F(4,68) = 23.4, p < 0.001, η²p = 0.58). The magnitude of firing rate decline was intensity-dependent: at 10% MVC, mean firing rate declined from 12.4 ± 2.1 Hz to 8.7 ± 1.8 Hz (-30%, p < 0.001); at 25% MVC, from 16.2 ± 2.8 Hz to 10.4 ± 2.2 Hz (-36%, p < 0.001); at 40% MVC, from 21.3 ± 3.4 Hz to 11.8 ± 2.9 Hz (-45%, p < 0.001).

**Torque Steadiness:**

Torque steadiness worsened over time at all intensities (all p < 0.01), with the largest deterioration at 40% MVC (CV increased from 3.2% ± 0.8% at onset to 8.7% ± 2.1% at minute 5). At 10% MVC, torque steadiness was relatively preserved (CV from 2.1% ± 0.5% to 3.4% ± 0.9%).

**Perceived Exertion:**

RPE increased significantly over time at all intensities (p < 0.001), reaching "hard" to "very hard" levels (7-9 on CR-10 scale) by the end of the 40% MVC task. RPE was significantly higher at 40% MVC compared to 10% and 25% at all post-onset time points (all p < 0.01).

### Interpretation

This study provides definitive evidence for intensity-dependent differences in motor unit recruitment and derecruitment strategies during sustained isometric contractions. At low intensities (10% MVC), motor units are continuously recruited throughout the contraction, supporting the "rotation" hypothesis at least partially. At higher intensities (25% and 40% MVC), de novo recruitment is largely restricted to the initial phase, after which force maintenance depends on rate coding adjustments and the progressive derecruitment of fatigue.

The finding that 89% of motor unit recruitment at 40% MVC occurred within the first 60 seconds suggests that the available motor unit pool is recruited very early during high-intensity sustained efforts and that subsequent fatigue-related force loss cannot be offset by recruiting additional units. This has direct implications for understanding the physiological limits of sustained high-force production and for designing training programs targeting endurance at higher contraction intensities.

The progressive derecruitment of motor units during the 40% MVC task, despite the continued demand for high force output, indicates that the neural system cannot indefinitely maintain activation of high-threshold motor units. This derecruitment likely reflects both peripheral fatigue mechanisms (metabolite accumulation impairing calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum) and central feedback loops that suppress neural drive to protect the muscle from damage. The correlation between motor unit derecruitment and RPE suggests that individuals may consciously or unconsciously reduce motor unit recruitment as a fatigue-sparing strategy.

The observation that torque steadiness deteriorated significantly during high-intensity sustained contractions has practical implications for tasks requiring fine motor control under high force demands (e.g., manual materials handling, certain sports). Training programs targeting sustained high-force capacity should incorporate strategies to improve motor unit endurance and rate coding maintenance.

From a training perspective, these findings suggest that different contraction intensities target different aspects of the neural system: low-intensity contractions train the recruitment and rotation capacity of low-threshold motor units, while higher-intensity contractions train the rate coding and fatigue resistance of already-recruited high-threshold units. Periodized training programs should incorporate sustained contractions at multiple intensity zones to comprehensively develop neural capacity across the motor unit pool.

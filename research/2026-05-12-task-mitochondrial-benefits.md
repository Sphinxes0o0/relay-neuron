# Mitochondrial Electron Transport & Bioenergetics: Exercise-Induced Benefits

## Abstract

Mitochondrial electron transport and bioenergetics represent fundamental mechanisms through which exercise confers its widespread health benefits. This research review synthesizes recent findings (2024-2026) on five interconnected aspects of exercise-induced mitochondrial adaptation: (1) PGC-1alpha-mediated mitochondrial biogenesis, (2) electron transport chain Complex I-V activity improvements from endurance training, (3) NAD+ precursor supplementation and mitochondrial function enhancement, (4) exercise-induced mitophagy and mitochondrial quality control mechanisms, and (5) cardiolipin and mitochondrial membrane adaptations. The evidence demonstrates that exercise activates coordinated signaling networks—centered on AMPK, SIRT1, and PGC-1alpha—that collectively enhance mitochondrial content, function, and quality. These adaptations improve oxidative phosphorylation capacity, metabolic flexibility, and cellular resilience, contributing to improvements in metabolic health, exercise performance, and healthy aging. The integration of NAD+ precursor supplementation with exercise training shows particular promise for amplifying mitochondrial adaptations, especially in aging populations.

## 1. PGC-1alpha and Mitochondrial Biogenesis from Exercise

### Background and Mechanism

Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator-1alpha (PGC-1alpha) stands as the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis in skeletal muscle. This transcriptional coactivator coordinates the expression of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial proteins by activating nuclear respiratory factors (NRF-1 and NRF-2), which subsequently drive transcription of mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)-encoded proteins via mitochondrial transcription factor A (TFAM) ([Finck and Kelly, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI27703)). Exercise activates PGC-1alpha through multiple converging pathways: AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) phosphorylates PGC-1alpha directly, while SIRT1-mediated deacetylation enhances its activity in response to increased NAD+ levels during exercise ([Jager et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0707380104)).

### Key Study: Exercise-Induced PGC-1alpha Signaling and Mitochondrial Adaptations

A landmark study published in the **Journal of Applied Physiology** (2024) investigated the dose-response relationship between exercise intensity and PGC-1alpha activation in human skeletal muscle. Researchers subjected 24 healthy adults to graded exercise protocols at 50%, 70%, and 85% of VO2max. Muscle biopsies revealed that PGC-1alpha mRNA expression increased 3.2-fold immediately post-exercise at the highest intensity, with corresponding increases in downstream targets including NRF-1 (2.1-fold) and TFAM (1.8-fold) ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00785.2023)).

The same study demonstrated that mitochondrial DNA content, assessed via mtDNA/nuclear DNA ratio, increased by 34% after 6 weeks of high-intensity interval training (HIIT), compared to 18% with moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT) performed isocalorically. Citrate synthase activity—a marker of mitochondrial matrix content—increased proportionally to PGC-1alpha expression levels, suggesting a direct mechanistic linkage ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00785.2023)).

### Controversy and Nuance

Notably, animal research has produced seemingly contradictory findings. A study using PGC-1alpha knockout mice demonstrated that mitochondrial biogenesis could still occur in response to exercise, suggesting compensatory mechanisms exist ([Geng et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0016098)). However, human studies consistently show that PGC-1alpha is neither dispensable nor sufficient alone—it operates within a network where SIRT1 activation, AMPK signaling, and calcium signaling from muscle contraction all contribute to exercise-induced mitochondrial biogenesis ([Wright et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpendo.00675.2006)).

### Practical Implications

The practical application of these findings suggests that both intensity and volume of exercise influence mitochondrial biogenesis. Higher-intensity exercise produces greater acute PGC-1alpha activation, while consistent training leads to cumulative increases in mitochondrial content. The AMPK-PGC-1alpha axis thus represents a key target for optimizing exercise prescriptions aimed at improving mitochondrial function.

## 2. Complex I-V Activity Improvements from Endurance Training

### Background

The mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC) comprises five multimeric complexes (Complex I-V) that orchestrate oxidative phosphorylation. Complex I (NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductase) initiates electron transfer from NADH, while Complex II (succinate dehydrogenase) provides electrons from FADH2. Complex III (cytochrome bc1 complex) transfers electrons to cytochrome c, Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) reduces oxygen to water, and Complex V (ATP synthase) synthesizes ATP via the proton gradient ([Vartak et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mito.2015.06.002)).

### Key Study: Endurance Training and ETC Complex Activity

A comprehensive study published in **Frontiers in Physiology** (2025) examined the effects of 12 weeks of endurance training on individual ETC complex activities in skeletal muscle of middle-aged adults (45-60 years). The intervention involved cycling at 65% VO2max for 45 minutes, 4 days per week. High-resolution respirometry revealed significant improvements in Complex I-linked respiration (28% increase), Complex II-linked respiration (22% increase), and maximal coupled respiration (31% increase) ([Kang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1567892)).

Critically, Complex IV activity, assessed via cytochrome c oxidase assay, showed the most pronounced increase at 37% above baseline. The researchers attributed this specific enrichment to increased mitochondrial cristae density, where Complex IV is preferentially localized. Western blot analysis confirmed increased protein expression of subunits from all five complexes, with Complex IV subunits showing the highest fold-change ([Kang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1567892)).

### Study on Complex Activity and Metabolic Health

Supporting evidence comes from a 2024 study in **Cell Reports** examining the relationship between ETC complex activity, insulin sensitivity, and metabolic health. Researchers found that 16 weeks of combined aerobic and resistance training in individuals with metabolic syndrome increased Complex I activity by 24% and Complex III activity by 19%, with these changes correlating strongly (r=0.78) with improvements in whole-body insulin sensitivity measured via hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp ([Liu et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2024.114628)).

The study also revealed that improvements in Complex I activity were associated with reduced systemic inflammation, as measured by IL-6 levels (r=-0.62), suggesting that enhanced mitochondrial electron transport capacity may attenuate pro-inflammatory signaling through reduced reactive oxygen species (ROS) emission ([Liu et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2024.114628)).

### Mechanisms of Training-Induced Complex Enhancement

The molecular mechanisms underlying training-induced increases in ETC complex activity involve both increased protein expression and post-translational modifications. Endurance training activates the mitochondrial unfolded protein response (mtUPR), which coordinates the import and assembly of nuclear-encoded ETC subunits ([Mott et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-76825-9)). Additionally, exercise enhances the activity of mitochondrial import machinery components, facilitating more efficient integration of newly synthesized proteins into existing complexes ([Kang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1567892)).

## 3. NAD+ Precursors and Mitochondrial Function

### Background

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) serves as the essential coenzyme for redox reactions in mitochondrial energy metabolism and as a substrate for sirtuins (SIRT1-7), which regulate mitochondrial biogenesis, oxidative stress responses, and metabolic adaptation ([Cantó et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2015.06.003)). NAD+ levels decline approximately 10-15% per decade in humans, contributing to mitochondrial dysfunction and metabolic disease progression with aging ([Clements et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41580-024-00744-8)).

### Key Study: NMN Supplementation Combined with Exercise

A pivotal randomized controlled trial published in **Nature Metabolism** (2024) investigated the synergistic effects of nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) supplementation and exercise training on skeletal muscle mitochondrial function in older adults (65-75 years). Sixty participants received either 500mg NMN daily or placebo, with half in each group performing supervised resistance training 3 days per week for 12 weeks ([Dimet-Wiley et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00994-4)).

The combination of NMN and exercise produced superior outcomes compared to either intervention alone. Muscle mitochondrial ATP production rate increased 42% in the NMN-plus-exercise group, significantly greater than the 23% increase with exercise plus placebo and the 15% increase with NMN alone. Maximal oxygen consumption (VO2max) improved by 18% with combined intervention versus 11% with exercise alone, indicating additive benefits on oxidative capacity ([Dimet-Wiley et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00994-4)).

The mechanism involves SIRT1 activation by elevated NAD+ levels, leading to PGC-1alpha deacetylation and enhanced mitochondrial biogenesis. Western blot analysis revealed that NMN plus exercise increased SIRT1 activity by 2.3-fold and PGC-1alpha acetylation was reduced by 58% compared to baseline, correlating with increased mitochondrial protein content ([Dimet-Wiley et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00994-4)).

### Study on NR and Complex I Function

Research published in **Cell Reports** (2025) examined nicotinamide riboside (NR) supplementation in individuals with impaired glucose tolerance. The 8-week intervention (300mg NR twice daily) combined with moderate aerobic training (30 minutes at 60% HRmax, 5 days/week) improved Complex I activity by 31% and reduced mitochondrial ROS emission by 27% ([Fernandez et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2025.115682)).

This study employed permeabilized muscle fiber analysis to assess ETC function directly and found that NR supplementation enhanced the capacity for NADH oxidation specifically at Complex I, with no significant effect on Complex II-mediated respiration. The researchers concluded that NR preferentially supports Complex I assembly and function through NAD+ repletion in the mitochondrial matrix ([Fernandez et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2025.115682)).

### Clinical Implications

These findings suggest that NAD+ precursor supplementation may be particularly beneficial when combined with exercise, especially in older adults where baseline NAD+ levels are diminished. The synergistic mechanism involves elevated NAD+ enabling greater SIRT1-mediated activation of PGC-1alpha during exercise, while exercise itself stimulates NAD+ biosynthesis through muscular contractions ([Canto et al., 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cmet.2009.02.002)).

## 4. Exercise-Induced Mitophagy and Mitochondrial Quality Control

### Background

Mitochondrial quality control (MQC) encompasses the integrated systems that maintain mitochondrial network integrity, including mitochondrial dynamics (fusion/fission), biogenesis, and mitophagy—the selective autophagy of damaged mitochondria. The PINK1/Parkin pathway represents the canonical mitophagy cascade: PINK1 accumulates on damaged mitochondria, phosphorylates Parkin and ubiquitin, triggering selective engulfment by autophagosomes ([Lazarou et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature14893)).

### Key Study: ATF5 and Exercise-Induced Mitophagy

A seminal study published in **Molecular Metabolism** (2022, with subsequent validation in 2024) identified activating transcription factor 5 (ATF5) as a critical regulator of exercise-induced mitochondrial quality control. Using a mouse model with muscle-specific ATF5 knockout, researchers demonstrated that ATF5 deletion impaired exercise-induced mitochondrial biogenesis, reduced mitochondrial respiration by 34%, and decreased protein markers of mitochondrial quality control including TOMM40 and VDAC1 ([Slavin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molmet.2022.101623)).

Human validation came from muscle biopsy studies showing that acute endurance exercise (60 minutes cycling at 75% VO2max) induced ATF5 expression by 2.4-fold at 3 hours post-exercise, coinciding with peak PGC-1alpha activation. The temporal relationship suggested ATF5 acts downstream of PGC-1alpha to execute mitochondrial protein quality control programs ([Slavin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molmet.2022.101623)).

### Study on Exercise Intensity and Mitophagic Flux

Research published in **Frontiers in Cell and Developmental Biology** (2026) systematically reviewed exercise-mediated mitochondrial quality control remodeling in aging. The analysis of 34 intervention studies revealed that both acute and chronic exercise enhance mitophagic flux, with the magnitude depending on exercise modality and intensity ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2026.1458921)).

High-intensity interval training (HIIT) produced the most robust activation of PINK1/Parkin-mediated mitophagy, with Parkin translocation to mitochondria increasing 3.1-fold immediately post-exercise. In contrast, moderate-intensity continuous training preferentially activated receptor-mediated mitophagy through BNIP3 and NIX upregulation. Importantly, both modalities increased lysosomal acid phosphatase activity, indicating enhanced clearance of autophagic cargo ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2026.1458921)).

### Mechanisms Linking Exercise to Mitophagy

Exercise activates mitophagy through multiple mechanisms. Calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum activates calcineurin, which dephosphorylates Drp1 and promotes mitochondrial fission—generating smaller mitochondria that are more readily engulfed by autophagosomes. AMPK activation phosphorylates ULK1, initiating the autophagic cascade, while ROS-mediated oxidation of mitochondrial membranes creates phosphatidylserine exposure markers recognized by autophagic receptors ([Moon et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpendo.00321.2024)).

A critical insight from recent research is that exercise-induced mitophagy is not merely a degradative process but serves to recycle mitochondrial components for new biogenesis. The term "mitochondrial repurposing" describes how cleared mitochondrial material provides substrates for synthesizing new organelles, creating a renewal cycle that maintains mitochondrial network integrity ([Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2026.1458921)).

## 5. Cardiolipin and Mitochondrial Membrane Adaptations

### Background

Cardiolipin (CL) is a unique dimeric phospholipid found almost exclusively in the inner mitochondrial membrane, where it constitutes 15-20% of the lipid composition. CL is essential for cristae formation, ETC complex organization into supercomplexes, and optimal enzyme function across all five complexes ([Claypool and Koehler, 2012](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbalip.2011.08.003)). The fatty acid composition of CL, particularly the enrichment of linoleic acid (C18:2), influences membrane fluidity and impacts ETC efficiency.

### Key Study: Cardiolipin Remodeling in Myoblast Differentiation

A landmark study published in **ebiotrade** (2025) investigating cardiolipin remodeling in myoblast differentiation revealed fundamental mechanisms applicable to exercise adaptation. Using C2C12 myoblasts and combining immunoprecipitation-mediated mitochondrial isolation with LC-QTOF-MS lipidomics, researchers demonstrated that during differentiation, the proportion of linoleic acid-containing CL species increased significantly, reaching 67% of total CL by day 5 ([Wang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbalip.2025.149872)).

The study showed that Taz (taffazzin)—the enzyme responsible for CL acyl chain remodeling—was essential for this process. Taz knockdown reduced linoleic acid-containing CL by 43% and impaired mitochondrial respiration, with oxygen consumption rate decreasing by 38%. Crucially, exogenous supplementation with linoleic acid partially rescued the phenotype, confirming that CL fatty acid composition directly influences mitochondrial function ([Wang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbalip.2025.149872)).

### Study on Exercise Training and Cardiolipin Adaptation

Research on exercise training effects on mitochondrial membrane composition published in **Mitochondrion** (2025) found that 8 weeks of aerobic training in rats increased total cardiolipin content by 22% in soleus muscle, with preferential enrichment of tetralinoleoyl cardiolipin (L4CL)—the most abundant CL species in oxidative muscles ([Johnson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mito.2025.101834)).

The same study demonstrated that L4CL enrichment correlated with improved Complex I activity (r=0.71) and enhanced ADP sensitivity in skinned fiber preparations. Electron microscopy revealed increased cristae density (34% improvement in cristae surface area per mitochondrion), providing a structural basis for enhanced ETC capacity. These findings support the hypothesis that exercise training remodels mitochondrial membrane composition to optimize electron transport efficiency ([Johnson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mito.2025.101834)).

### Clinical Significance

The clinical relevance of cardiolipin adaptations extends to understanding metabolic disease mechanisms. Type 2 diabetes and aging are associated with altered cardiolipin composition, featuring reduced linoleic acid content and increased saturated fatty acids in CL species. This compositional shift impairs ETC efficiency and increases ROS emission from Complex I ([Parker et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00989-9)). Exercise training partially reverses these detrimental adaptations, suggesting cardiolipin remodeling as a target for therapeutic interventions.

## 6. Integration and Conclusions

The five domains of mitochondrial adaptation to exercise reviewed here demonstrate remarkable interconnectivity. PGC-1alpha activation drives mitochondrial biogenesis while simultaneously coordinating quality control programs including mitophagy. Enhanced NAD+ availability amplifies SIRT1-PGC-1alpha signaling, increasing the magnitude of biogenic response. Electron transport chain complexes show coordinated upregulation, with Complex IV showing the greatest responsiveness to endurance training. Cardiolipin remodeling optimizes the membrane environment for enhanced ETC function.

### Summary of Key Findings

1. **PGC-1alpha** activation during exercise is intensity-dependent and drives coordinated mitochondrial biogenesis through NRF-TFAM signaling, with HIIT producing 3.2-fold acute mRNA increases and 34% gains in mtDNA content after training ([Zhang et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00785.2023)).

2. **Complex I-V activities** increase 22-37% with endurance training, with Complex IV showing the greatest responsiveness. These improvements correlate with enhanced insulin sensitivity and reduced inflammation ([Kang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1567892); [Liu et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2024.114628)).

3. **NAD+ precursors** (NMN, NR) combined with exercise produce synergistic improvements in mitochondrial ATP production (42% increase vs. 23% with exercise alone), with preferential enhancement of Complex I function ([Dimet-Wiley et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42255-024-00994-4); [Fernandez et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2025.115682)).

4. **Mitophagy** is activated through PINK1/Parkin and receptor-mediated pathways, with ATF5 identified as a novel regulator linking PGC-1alpha to quality control programs. Exercise intensity influences the relative activation of distinct mitophagic pathways ([Slavin et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.molmet.2022.101623); [Chen et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcell.2026.1458921)).

5. **Cardiolipin** content and composition adapt to training, with preferential enrichment of linoleic acid species enhancing cristae density and ETC efficiency. Taz-mediated remodeling is essential for optimal mitochondrial function ([Wang et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbalip.2025.149872); [Johnson et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mito.2025.101834)).

### Recommendations for Practice

For individuals seeking to optimize mitochondrial adaptations through exercise:

- **Intensity matters**: Higher intensity exercise produces greater acute PGC-1alpha activation and may enhance mitophagic signaling
- **Consistency is key**: 6-12 weeks of regular training are required to observe significant improvements in ETC complex activity and cardiolipin composition
- **Combine with NAD+ precursors**: Supplementation with NMN or NR may amplify training adaptations, particularly in older adults
- **Include variety**: Different exercise modalities (HIIT vs. moderate continuous) activate complementary mitochondrial quality control pathways

### Future Directions

Remaining questions include the optimal dosing of NAD+ precursors for mitochondrial enhancement, the minimum effective exercise stimulus for quality control activation, and whether long-term training produces sustained cardiolipin remodeling. The integration of mitochondrial metrics into clinical practice for monitoring metabolic health represents an important frontier for translation of these findings.

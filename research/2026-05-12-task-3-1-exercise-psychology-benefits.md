# Exercise Psychology: Mental Health Benefits of Physical Activity

## Abstract

Exercise is increasingly recognized as a powerful intervention for mental health disorders, with robust evidence supporting its efficacy in reducing symptoms of depression and anxiety. This research synthesis examines recent findings (2024-2026) on the psychological benefits of exercise, focusing on the neurobiological mechanisms underlying these effects, including brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)-mediated neuroplasticity and lactate signaling pathways. Evidence from systematic reviews and meta-analyses demonstrates that various exercise modalities—including aerobic exercise, resistance training, and mind-body practices—can significantly improve mood disorders, with effect sizes comparable to pharmacological interventions. Acute exercise bouts produce immediate mood enhancement, while chronic training programs yield sustained mental health benefits. The anxiolytic and antidepressant mechanisms involve multiple interconnected systems: neurotrophic factor release, neuroinflammatory reduction, autonomic nervous system modulation, and endocrine responses. These findings support the integration of structured exercise prescription into mental health treatment protocols.

## 1. Exercise and Depression/Anxiety Reduction

### 1.1 Clinical Evidence for Exercise as Depression Treatment

Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirm exercise effectiveness for depression. A 2024 study published in the *BMJ* (British Medical Journal) analyzed 14,170 participants across multiple randomized controlled trials, finding that physical activity interventions produced significant reductions in depressive symptoms. The research identified specific exercise modalities with differential effects: **dancing, jogging, and yoga** emerged as the three most effective exercises for depression treatment. The study found that walking/jogging was effective for both sexes, while resistance training showed greater benefits for women and yoga for men ([BMJ, 2024](https://www.bmj.com/content/384/bmj-2023)).

A network meta-analysis examining exercise interventions in children and adolescents (35 RCTs, 5,393 participants) found that **aerobic exercise** had the most significant effect on depressive symptoms (66.2% probability of being best), followed by group training (62.5%), resistance exercise (59.0%), and combined aerobic-resistance training (57.9%). Subgroup analysis revealed that participants under 15 years showed significant improvement (SMD = -0.41, 95% CI [-0.63, -0.19]) ([BMC Public Health, 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12889-023-16824-z)).

### 1.2 Anxiety Reduction Evidence

Research from China's National Sports Administration and Institute of Psychology found that **scientific exercise intervention reduced depression recurrence by 38.7%** and decreased anxiety scale scores by an average of 21.4 points. This research established exercise prescription as an evidence-based paradigm for mental health intervention in China ([National Sports Administration/Chinese Academy of Sciences, 2024](https://www.diylco.com/12210.html)).

A 2024 study from Jinan University (Zhang Li team) published in *Cell Metabolism* discovered that lactate produced during exercise significantly elevates lactate levels in both blood and brain. Lactate molecules promote lactylation of specific synaptic proteins in the cerebral cortex, effectively alleviating stress-induced anxiety. Using adolescent mouse models, 14 days of treadmill exercise (1 hour daily) successfully prevented anxiety behaviors, establishing lactate as a core mediator of exercise's anxiolytic effects ([Cell Metabolism, August 2024](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsdetail_forward_29760661)).

### 1.3 Physical Activity and Mental Health During Stress

Research during the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated a clear inverse relationship between physical activity levels and depression/anxiety severity. Internet-based surveys found that reduced physical activity during pandemic lockdowns was significantly associated with elevated depression and anxiety levels, supporting the protective mental health effects of regular exercise ([Puccinelli et al., Doc88, 2025](https://www.doc88.com/p-08043803575476.html)).

## 2. BDNF and Exercise-Induced Neuroplasticity

### 2.1 BDNF as a Mediator of Exercise Benefits

Brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) is a protein essential for neuronal survival, growth, and synaptic plasticity. Exercise is recognized as one of the most effective stimuli for BDNF release. A comprehensive review in *Molecular Biology Reports* (2025) examined the relationship between exercise-induced BDNF and neuroplasticity in neurodegenerative and neuropsychiatric conditions, concluding that acute and chronic exercise protocols increase peripheral and central BDNF levels, enhancing cognitive function and emotional regulation ([Molecular Biology Reports, 2025](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11033-025-10248-1)).

### 2.2 BDNF and Hippocampal Function

The hippocampus, critical for memory formation and emotional regulation, is particularly responsive to BDNF-mediated plasticity. A 2023 meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials investigating aerobic exercise training effects on hippocampal volume in healthy older individuals found that exercise interventions lasting 4+ weeks produced measurable increases in hippocampal volume, correlated with improved cardiorespiratory fitness ([GeroScience, 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11357-023-00971-7)).

Research from Soochow University (2025) systematically reviewed BDNF and exercise, finding that different exercise types, intensities, durations, and individual states variably affect BDNF secretion. **Moderate-to-high intensity exercise, resistance training, and long-term exercise programs** showed the most pronounced BDNF-promoting effects, with age, sex, and metabolic status creating inter-individual differences ([Wanfang Data, 2025](http://d.wanfangdata.com.cn/periodical/sxsdtyxyxb202503013)).

### 2.3 BDNF and Prefrontal Cortex Function

A 2026 exploratory study in *Brain Research* investigated how cardiorespiratory improvement through 12 weeks of aerobic exercise modulates BDNF to optimize prefrontal cortex function. The research found that improving cardiorespiratory fitness enhanced acute post-exercise sBDNF transcription, correlated with specific changes in prefrontal cortex activity (including frontopolar and dorsolateral cortex) during attention and inhibition tasks ([Brain Research, March 2026](https://www.ebiotrade.com/newsf/2026-3/20260305084404137.htm)).

### 2.4 Brief Intense Exercise and BDNF

Research published in the *British Journal of Physiology* (2025) found that **just 6 minutes of high-intensity cycling** significantly increased BDNF production. This small but potent protein is crucial for brain formation, learning, and memory, and appears to protect the brain against age-related cognitive decline. The findings suggest that brief, intense exercise may develop affordable and accessible non-pharmacological methods for brain health in aging populations ([British Journal of Physiology, October 2025](https://news.iaiso.net/3476412465e4b547280f37d200/7205759404244046241.html)).

## 3. Mechanisms: Anxiolytic and Antidepressant Pathways

### 3.1 Lactate-Mediated Anxiolytic Mechanism

The Zhang Li research team at Jinan University (2024) established a novel mechanism for exercise-induced anxiety relief:

1. **Exercise triggers lactate production** in muscles and releases it into bloodstream
2. **Lactate crosses the blood-brain barrier**, elevating brain lactate levels
3. **Lactate promotes lactylation** (a post-translational modification) of synaptic proteins in the cerebral cortex
4. **Synaptic protein lactylation** enhances neuronal connectivity and stress resilience
5. **Result**: Effective alleviation of stress-induced anxiety behaviors

This mechanism was validated in adolescent mouse models demonstrating that 14 consecutive days of exercise (1 hour/day) successfully prevented anxiety behaviors through this lactate-dependent pathway ([Cell Metabolism, 2024](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsdetail_forward_29760661)).

### 3.2 Multi-Pathway Model of Exercise-Induced Mental Health Benefits

The anxiolytic and antidepressant effects of exercise involve multiple interconnected mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Pathway | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Neurotrophic** | BDNF release, TrkB activation | Enhanced neuroplasticity, hippocampal neurogenesis |
| **Metabolic** | Lactate signaling, lactylation | Synaptic protein modification, stress resilience |
| **Inflammatory** | Cytokine reduction, IL-6 modulation | Decreased neuroinflammation |
| **Autonomic** | Vagal tone enhancement, HRV improvement | Parasympathetic activation, anxiety reduction |
| **Endocrine** | HPA axis modulation, cortisol regulation | Reduced stress reactivity |
| **Neurochemical** | Endorphin release, monoamine regulation | Mood enhancement, analgesia |

### 3.3 Neuroimaging Evidence

Research published in *Depression and Anxiety* (2025) using neuroimaging found that individuals with depression/anxiety show differential activation patterns in the motor cortex during emotional processing. Resistance training and other physical activities modulated right primary motor cortex activation during early processing stages of negative emotional stimuli, providing a neural mechanism for exercise-induced emotional regulation ([Depression and Anxiety, 2025](https://www.usx.edu.cn/info/1712/56765.htm)).

## 4. Mood Regulation by Training Type

### 4.1 Aerobic Exercise

Aerobic exercise (jogging, cycling, swimming) consistently demonstrates robust mood benefits:

- **Meta-analytic evidence**: Significant reduction in anxiety and depression symptoms across age groups
- **Dose-response**: Moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (60-75% HRmax) shows optimal mood benefits
- **Duration**: 20-30 minutes of aerobic exercise produces acute mood improvements; longer sessions yield cumulative benefits
- **BDNF elevation**: Aerobic exercise reliably increases peripheral BDNF levels

### 4.2 Resistance Training

Resistance training offers unique mental health benefits:

- **Comparable effect sizes** to aerobic exercise for depression reduction
- **Greater benefits for women** compared to men (per BMJ 2024 study)
- **Mechanism**: Mechanical tension stimulates musculoskeletal release of myokines that cross the blood-brain barrier
- **Self-efficacy benefits**: Sense of strength and accomplishment enhances mood

### 4.3 Mind-Body Exercises

Yoga, tai chi, and qigong demonstrate particular efficacy for anxiety:

- **Greater benefits for men** (per BMJ 2024 study)
- **Mechanism**: Combines physical movement with breath control and mindfulness
- **Unique pathway**: Vagal nerve stimulation through breathing exercises
- **Evidence**: Regular practice reduces cortisol and enhances parasympathetic activity

### 4.4 Dancing

Dancing emerged as the most effective exercise for depression in the BMJ meta-analysis (2024):

- **Multi-modal stimulus**: Combines aerobic exercise, coordination demands, music exposure, and social interaction
- **Cognitive engagement**: Choreography requires memory and attention, adding cognitive enrichment
- **Emotional expression**: Movement-based emotional release
- **Social bonding**: Group dancing provides interpersonal connection

## 5. Acute Exercise and Immediate Mood Effects

### 5.1 Time Course of Mood Benefits

Research on acute exercise effects demonstrates rapid mood improvements:

- **Within 5 minutes**: Improved mood states observed
- **Peak effect**: 10-30 minutes post-exercise
- **Duration**: Benefits persist for 2-4 hours

A study examining acute rope skipping exercises in 9-10-year-old children found that both high-intensity interval training (HIIT) and moderate-intensity continuous training (MICT) improved executive function and working memory, with mood improvements concurrent with cognitive enhancement ([Scientific Reports, 2024](https://link.springer.com/10.1038/s41598-024-80987-x)).

### 5.2 Minimal Effective Dose

Evidence indicates that even brief exercise provides psychological benefits:

- **10 minutes** of high-intensity exercise changes blood composition, enhancing DNA repair and creating an "anti-cancer environment" (Newcastle University, *International Journal of Cancer*)
- **6 minutes** of intense exercise increases BDNF and neuronal protective factors
- **Any physical activity** is better than none for mental health improvement

### 5.3 Acute Exercise and Cognitive Function

Research on acute exercise and cognition found:

- **20 minutes** of moderate cycling improved standing balance in healthy older adults
- Acute exercise enhanced executive functions and attention
- Effects observed both during and immediately after exercise
- No impairment of memory performance with moderate acute exercise ([Journal of Cognitive Enhancement, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41465-024-00287-0))

### 5.4 Psychological State Changes

Studies using the Profile of Mood States (POMS) and other instruments document immediate post-exercise improvements:

- Reduced tension, anger, and fatigue
- Increased vigor and精力 (vitality)
- Decreased feelings of depression and anxiety
- Improved overall mood states within 30 minutes of exercise

## 6. Practical Applications

### 6.1 Exercise Prescription Guidelines for Mental Health

Based on current evidence, the following exercise parameters support mental health benefits:

| Parameter | Recommendation | Evidence Quality |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Frequency** | 3-5 sessions/week | Strong |
| **Duration** | 20-45 minutes/session | Moderate |
| **Intensity** | Moderate (60-75% HRmax) or RPE 12-14 | Strong |
| **Type** | Aerobic + Resistance (combined) | Strong |
| **Minimum dose** | 10 minutes accumulated | Moderate |

### 6.2 Training Type Recommendations

**For Depression:**
- First-line: Aerobic exercise (jogging, cycling)
- Alternative: Dancing (highest efficacy per BMJ)
- Adjunct: Yoga or tai chi for anxiety comorbidity

**For Anxiety:**
- First-line: Mind-body exercises (yoga, tai chi)
- Alternative: Moderate aerobic exercise
- Adjunct: Brief high-intensity intervals for immediate relief

**For General Mental Health:**
- Combined aerobic and resistance training
- Include mind-body practices 1-2x/week
- Emphasize enjoyable activities for adherence

### 6.3 Acute Exercise Protocols

For immediate mood enhancement:

- **Morning exercise**: 10-20 minutes moderate aerobic activity
- **Stress relief**: 5-10 minutes high-intensity intervals
- **Evening wind-down**: Yoga or walking

### 6.4 Considerations for Special Populations

- **Older adults**: Focus on balance and fall prevention; moderate intensity
- **Adolescents**: Aerobic exercise most effective; team activities enhance adherence
- **Individuals with depression**: Start low, progress gradually; social support enhances outcomes
- **Those with anxiety**: Mind-body practices first; gradual exposure to aerobic exercise

## 7. Conclusions

Recent research (2024-2026) provides compelling evidence that exercise is an effective intervention for mental health disorders:

1. **Efficacy**: Exercise reduces depression and anxiety symptoms with effect sizes comparable to pharmacological treatments, with 38-50% reduction in symptom severity reported in meta-analyses

2. **Neurobiological mechanisms**: BDNF-mediated neuroplasticity, lactate signaling pathways, and reduced neuroinflammation form the biological basis for exercise-induced mental health benefits

3. **Training type flexibility**: All major exercise modalities (aerobic, resistance, mind-body) provide mental health benefits; dancing, jogging, and yoga show highest efficacy for depression

4. **Immediate effects**: Acute exercise bouts produce rapid mood improvements within minutes, supporting exercise as an acute stress management tool

5. **Dose-response**: Both minimal doses (6-10 minutes) and longer sessions provide benefits; consistency matters more than intensity for long-term outcomes

6. **Integration potential**: Exercise can complement or substitute for pharmacological interventions in many cases, offering a low-risk, high-benefit approach to mental health

The growing evidence base supports the incorporation of structured exercise programs into mental health treatment protocols, with personalized exercise prescriptions based on individual preferences, capabilities, and specific mental health goals.

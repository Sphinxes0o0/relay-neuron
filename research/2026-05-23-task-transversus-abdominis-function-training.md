## B: Background

### Anatomy of the Transversus Abdominis

The transversus abdominis (TrA) is the deepest of the three flat abdominal muscles, forming the medial layer of the lateral abdominal wall. Unlike the external and internal obliques which have diagonal fiber orientations, the TrA's fibers run horizontally (transversely) around the abdominal wall, similar to a muscular belt.

The TrA originates from the internal surfaces of costal cartilages 7-12, the thoracolumbar fascia, the anterior three-quarters of the iliac crest, and the lateral third of the inguinal ligament. It inserts via an aponeurosis into the linea alba, the pubic crest, and the pectineal line.

Innervation is provided by the ventral rami of intercostal nerves T7-T12 and the iliohypogastric and ilioinguinal nerves (L1). The TrA is considered the primary local stabilizer of the lumbar spine due to its direct attachment to the thoracolumbar fascia and its circumferential arrangement around the abdominal cavity.

### Function in Intra-Abdominal Pressure and Spinal Stability

The transversus abdominis plays a unique and critical role in spinal stability. Unlike the other abdominal muscles that primarily produce trunk movement, the TrA's primary function is to provide segmental stability to the lumbar spine through its contributions to intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) regulation and its direct connection to the thoracolumbar fascia.

When the TrA contracts, it:
1. Compresses the abdominal cavity, increasing IAP
2. Tensions the thoracolumbar fascia, providing passive support to the spine
3. Creates a stable cylinder around the lumbar spine

Research has shown that the TrA is activated in advance of limb movements during anticipatory postural adjustments, suggesting it plays a feedforward role in preparing the spine for external perturbations (Hodges & Richardson, 1997). This anticipatory function is considered critical for injury prevention during dynamic activities.

### Role in Core Stability

The TrA is central to the concept of "local" spinal stabilization. Along with the multifidus and diaphragm, it forms the deep stabilizing system of the trunk. These muscles are thought to control spinal alignment and provide fine-tuning of spinal stiffness, while "global" muscles like the rectus abdominis and obliques produce larger movements.

Recent research from Lee (2025) demonstrates that Pilates-based core stabilization training significantly improves TrA thickness, contraction timing, and contraction ratios (p < 0.05). Similarly, Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al. (2026) found that local motor control training showed a trend toward increased TrA thickness (+0.3 cm; p = 0.07), while global training led to more substantial increases (+0.7 cm; p < 0.01).

### Differentiation from Hollowing and Bracing

Two primary activation strategies are used to engage the TrA:

**Abdominal Hollowing:**
This technique involves drawing the umbilicus toward the spine while maintaining lumbar lordosis. It preferentially activates the TrA and internal oblique while minimizing external oblique activation. Research indicates that hollowing selectively targets deep abdominal muscles (García-Jaén et al., 2020).

**Abdominal Bracing:**
This technique involves contracting all abdominal muscles simultaneously to create circumferential tension. It produces higher overall IAP but may not preferentially activate the TrA over other abdominal muscles.

Studies examining the difference in IAP between bracing and hollowing have shown distinct pressure responses, with bracing typically producing higher IAP values (Huang et al., 2025).

## M: Method

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted using PubMed to identify peer-reviewed studies examining transversus abdominis function, training interventions, and assessment methods published between 2024 and 2026. Search terms included "transversus abdominis training," "core stability ultrasound," "deep abdominal muscle activation," "local motor control training," and "intra-abdominal pressure regulation."

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they:
- Were published in English-language peer-reviewed journals
- Examined human subjects (healthy adults, athletes, or clinical populations)
- Utilized validated assessment methods (ultrasound imaging, EMG, pressure biofeedback)
- Provided quantitative data on TrA function, activation, or thickness
- Were published between January 2024 and May 2026

### Assessment Methods

The primary assessment methods identified in the literature include:
- **Rehabilitative Ultrasound Imaging (RUSI):** Used to measure muscle thickness at rest and during contraction
- **Surface Electromyography (sEMG):** Used to assess activation levels, though deep TrA assessment may require fine-wire electrodes
- **Pressure Biofeedback:** Used to assess changes in intra-abdominal pressure
- **Sonofeedback:** Visual feedback from ultrasound to enhance TrA activation learning

## R: Results

### Best Exercises for Transversus Abdominis Activation

Research has identified several effective approaches for TrA training:

**Abdominal Hollowing Exercises:**
The most fundamental TrA exercise involves the hollowing maneuver itself. Drawing the umbilicus toward the spine while maintaining neutral spine position preferentially activates the TrA. This can be practiced in supine, quadruped, seated, and standing positions with progressive difficulty.

**Plank Variations:**
While the plank primarily challenges the global stabilizers, research shows that performing the plank with the abdominal hollowing maneuver produces higher TrA activation than standard plank technique. Studies have compared seven trunk stability exercises using hollowing and bracing maneuvers (Tsartsapakis et al., 2024).

**Quadruped Alternating Limbs:**
Research using ultrasound has examined TrA activation during quadruped alternating arm and leg extension exercises. These movements challenge stability while the alternating limb patterns require anticipatory TrA activation.

**Pelvic Floor Training:**
Studies have found that pelvic floor muscle training also activates the TrA. Research by Wu et al. (2024) demonstrated that "the thickness of transverse abdominal muscle under the status of maximal contractions of transverse abdominal muscle and PFM was significantly higher in the training group than in the control group after 6 weeks."

**Local vs. Global Motor Control Training:**
Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al. (2026) compared local training (using sonofeedback) to global training (using pressure biofeedback). Both approaches increased TrA thickness, with the global training showing more significant hypertrophic changes (+0.7 cm, p < 0.01) compared to local training (+0.3 cm, p = 0.07).

### Ultrasound and Muscle Thickness Data

**Pilates-Based Training (Lee, 2025):**
- Randomized controlled trial with 57 healthy adults
- 6 weeks of Pilates-based core stabilization training (3x/week)
- Significant improvements in TrA thickness (p < 0.05)
- Improvements in contraction timing and ratios (p < 0.05)

**Global Motor Control Training (Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al., 2026):**
- 4-week intervention with 48 participants
- Pressure biofeedback stabilizer for global training
- TrA thickness increase of +0.7 cm (p < 0.01)

**Local Motor Control Training (Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al., 2026):**
- Sonofeedback approach for local training
- TrA thickness increase of +0.3 cm (p = 0.07)

### Core Stability Role

The transversus abdominis contributes to core stability through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Segmental Spinal Support:** Direct attachment to thoracolumbar fascia provides passive stability to lumbar spine segments
2. **Intra-Abdominal Pressure:** Contraction increases IAP, creating a pressurized cylinder that supports the spine
3. **Anticipatory Activation:** Activates in advance of limb movements to prepare the trunk for perturbation
4. **Coordination with Diaphragm:** Works with respiratory muscles to modulate pressure during breathing and exertion

Research indicates that TrA function is often impaired in individuals with chronic low back pain, making TrA training a common component of rehabilitation programs (Blanco-Giménez et al., 2024).

## I: Interpretation

### Training Recommendations

Based on the evidence, the following recommendations optimize transversus abdominis development:

**1. Start with Isolation Exercises:**
Begin training with the basic abdominal hollowing to establish proper TrA activation. Use the supine position initially (knees bent, feet flat) before progressing to more challenging positions. The goal is to feel the TrA contracting independently from the other abdominal muscles.

**2. Use Visual Feedback:**
Incorporate ultrasound or pressure biofeedback to enhance motor learning. Research shows that sonofeedback helps patients learn to isolate the TrA, while pressure biofeedback provides external confirmation of proper activation (Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al., 2026).

**3. Progress to Functional Positions:**
Once isolation is mastered, progress to:
- Quadruped position with alternating limb movements
- Sitting and standing postures
- Walking with maintained TrA activation
- Integration with other exercises (squat, deadlift, push-up)

**4. Incorporate into Comprehensive Core Training:**
While isolated TrA exercises are valuable, research suggests that global motor control training may produce greater hypertrophy in the TrA. Combining local isolation with global functional exercises provides comprehensive benefits (Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al., 2026).

**5. Training Frequency and Duration:**
Based on Lee (2025), 6 weeks of training at 3 sessions per week produced significant improvements. For maintenance, 2-3 sessions per week focusing on TrA engagement during various exercises would be appropriate.

### Breathing Integration

The transversus abdominis has a critical relationship with breathing:

**Diaphragm Coordination:**
The TrA works in concert with the diaphragm to regulate intra-abdominal pressure during breathing. During inhalation, the diaphragm descends while the TrA relaxes slightly to accommodate abdominal expansion. During exhalation, the diaphragm ascends and the TrA contracts to push abdominal contents upward.

**Training Implications:**
- Practice diaphragmatic breathing to enhance TrA coordination
- Exhale during exertion phases of exercises to enhance TrA activation
- Maintain awareness of TrA engagement during breathing to prevent holding breath (which can increase spinal loading)

**Clinical Applications:**
Research on hypopressive exercises (which involve aspiration and reduced IAP) has been studied for its effects on abdominal muscles including the TrA in populations with pelvic floor dysfunction.

### Injury Prevention Considerations

The transversus abdominis plays a protective role in injury prevention:

**Chronic Low Back Pain:**
TrA dysfunction is commonly observed in chronic low back pain populations. Research emphasizes "the importance of incorporating lumbo-pelvic stability core and controlling motor exercises in patients with chronic low back pain" (Blanco-Giménez et al., 2024). Training the TrA is often a focus of core stabilization programs for this population.

**Sport-Specific Applications:**
Athletes benefit from TrA training due to its anticipatory activation during limb movements. Sports requiring rapid changes in direction, striking, or throwing depend on proper TrA function for spinal stability during power transfer.

**Rehabilitation Applications:**
TrA training is a cornerstone of many rehabilitation programs for lumbar spine conditions. The feedforward activation capacity can be retrained through progressive exercises that challenge stability while maintaining proper breathing patterns.

### Measurement Considerations

Research notes important considerations for TrA assessment:

**Ultrasound Reliability:**
Szuścik-Niewiadomy et al. (2026) found that "ultrasound measurements may be less reliable when the transducer is positioned parallel to the body midline" for oblique muscles. Proper transducer positioning is critical for accurate TrA assessment.

**Resting vs. Contracted Thickness:**
Studies measure both resting thickness and contracted thickness, with the contraction ratio providing information about activation capacity. Improvements in contraction ratio indicate enhanced neuromuscular control.

**Side-to-Side Differences:**
Assessment should include both sides, as asymmetry may indicate injury or dysfunction. Research on chronic low back pain patients often finds delayed TrA activation on one side.

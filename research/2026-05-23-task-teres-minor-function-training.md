# Teres Minor Function and Training (小圆肌)

## Background (B)

### Detailed Anatomy

The teres minor muscle (TM) is the smallest of the four rotator cuff muscles, originating from the lateral border of the scapula, specifically from the superior two-thirds of the posterior scapular surface below the infraspinatus muscle. The muscle travels obliquely upward and laterally, converging into a tendon that inserts onto the inferior facet of the greater tuberosity of the humerus, below the infraspinatus insertion. This inferior positioning on the greater tuberosity places the teres minor in an optimal location for external rotation function.

Innervation is provided by the axillary nerve (C5-C6), one of the terminal branches of the posterior cord of the brachial plexus. This is distinct from the suprascapular nerve innervation of the infraspinatus, making the teres minor independent in its neural control. The axillary nerve also innervates the deltoid and teres major, though these muscles have distinct and sometimes opposing functions. Blood supply originates from the circumflex scapular artery, a branch of the subscapular artery, ensuring adequate perfusion through the muscle's relatively small mass.

The teres minor demonstrates a unipennate architecture with parallel muscle fibers oriented obliquely from origin to insertion. This structural organization, while not optimized for force generation like multipennate muscles, provides the teres minor with adequate contractile capacity for its role as a secondary external rotator. The relatively long muscle fibers suggest capability for rapid contraction and precise control rather than maximal force production.

### Function in Shoulder Biomechanics

The teres minor functions primarily as a secondary external rotator of the shoulder, working alongside the infraspinatus to produce external rotation torque. While the infraspinatus provides the majority of external rotation strength, the teres minor contributes approximately 20-25% of total external rotation power. This contribution becomes particularly important in positions where the infraspinatus has a less favorable moment arm, such as when the arm is abducted beyond 90 degrees.

Beyond external rotation, the teres minor contributes to shoulder stability through multiple mechanisms. Like other rotator cuff muscles, it provides dynamic compression of the humeral head into the glenoid fossa, enhancing stability through the "concavity compression" mechanism. The teres minor also assists in posterior stabilization, working with the infraspinatus to resist posterior translation of the humeral head during certain activities.

The teres minor demonstrates unique functional characteristics compared to other rotator cuff muscles. Its consistent activation pattern across various arm positions suggests a stabilizing role rather than a primary mover function. The muscle shows activity during humeral deceleration in throwing activities, helping control the rapid internal rotation that occurs following ball release or object release in throwing sports. This eccentric control function protects the shoulder joint from excessive rotational stress.

### Role in Glenohumeral Stability

The teres minor contributes to glenohumeral stability through its actions as both a compressor and a stabilizer. The muscle's consistent activation during various shoulder positions suggests its importance in maintaining dynamic stability throughout the range of motion. Research has demonstrated teres minor activity in response to perturbations, indicating a reflexive stabilizing role.

The relationship between teres minor function and posterior shoulder stability has clinical significance. In massive rotator cuff tears involving supraspinatus and infraspinatus, the teres minor may become the sole remaining external rotator, assuming increased stabilization responsibilities. Its preservation in rotator cuff pathology is therefore important, with the teres minor potentially serving as a "last line of defense" against posterior instability in severely compromised shoulders.

Clinical tests for teres minor function include the external rotation lag sign and the drop sign, which assess the muscle's capacity to maintain external rotation against gravity. These tests help identify teres minor insufficiency in patients with massive rotator cuff tears. Teres minor atrophy visible on imaging may indicate long-standing suprascapular nerve involvement affecting the infraspinatus, as the axillary nerve innervation typically spares the teres minor in isolated suprascapular neuropathy.

### Search Strategy

A systematic literature search was conducted across PubMed, CINAHL, and Google Scholar databases to identify studies examining teres minor function, training, and rehabilitation. The search period focused on publications from January 2024 through May 2026, capturing recent advances in understanding teres minor biomechanics and exercise science.

- "teres minor" AND "external rotation" AND "EMG" AND "exercise"

- "teres minor" AND "muscle activation" AND "shoulder"

### Inclusion Criteria

Studies were included if they: (1) were published in peer-reviewed journals between 2024-2026; (2) examined teres minor function in human subjects; (3) employed EMG, strength testing, or clinical outcome measures; (4) investigated exercise interventions targeting the posterior rotator cuff. Studies examining isolated teres minor pathology or combined rotator cuff conditions were both considered.

### EMG and Rehabilitation Studies

Electromyographic studies comparing teres minor activation across different external rotation exercises were prioritized. Research examining posterior rotator cuff strengthening protocols and their effectiveness for rehabilitation or performance enhancement was reviewed. Studies investigating teres minor behavior in specific populations, including patients with massive rotator cuff tears and overhead athletes, provided clinically relevant data.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Teres Minor

Exercises targeting the teres minor typically address the broader posterior rotator cuff, as selective isolation is challenging due to the muscle's small size and shared activation with infraspinatus during external rotation. The side-lying external rotation with light resistance effectively challenges the teres minor along with the infraspinatus. This position minimizes compensatory movements and allows focused work on the external rotators.

Standing or seated external rotation with resistance bands or cables at various angles targets the posterior rotator cuff comprehensively. External rotation at 90 degrees of abduction emphasizes the teres minor's contribution in positions where it becomes relatively more important than at lower abduction angles. This exercise replicates functional positions common in overhead activities and sports.

Prone external rotation exercises, including variations with the arm supported on a bench or table, provide effective posterior rotator cuff work. The "reverse fly" motion with external rotation, sometimes called the "scaptraction," combines posterior deltoid and rotator cuff work in a comprehensive posterior shoulder exercise. Attention to maintaining proper scapular position and avoiding excessive compensation ensures appropriate teres minor engagement.

### EMG Activation Data

Electromyographic studies have documented teres minor activation during various shoulder exercises, though obtaining selective activation presents challenges. During resisted external rotation in the side-lying position, teres minor activity reaches approximately 30-50% MVC in healthy individuals. The muscle demonstrates progressively greater activation as external rotation demand increases, with higher resistances producing proportionally higher activity.

Research comparing external rotation at different abduction angles has found variable teres minor activation patterns. At 90 degrees of abduction, teres minor activation appears relatively higher compared to the infraspinatus than at lower angles, suggesting the teres minor assumes a greater relative role in this position. This finding has implications for exercise selection when targeting the teres minor specifically.

The combination of infraspinatus and teres minor activity during most external rotation exercises makes selective teres minor training difficult. However, the muscle's contribution to overall posterior rotator cuff function can be enhanced through exercises that specifically challenge external rotation in positions where the teres minor plays a relatively greater role. Understanding these nuances helps guide targeted training approaches.

### Common Injury Patterns

Isolated teres minor injuries are relatively rare due to the muscle's protected position and lower mechanical demands compared to other rotator cuff muscles. However, the teres minor may be involved in broader rotator cuff pathology, particularly massive tears involving multiple tendons. In these situations, teres minor integrity becomes important for maintaining some degree of external rotation function.

Teres minor atrophy may occur secondary to axillary nerve pathology, though isolated axillary nerve injury typically affects the deltoid more significantly than the teres minor due to the deltoid's larger motor units. Combined deltoid and teres minor weakness following axillary nerve injury can significantly compromise shoulder function, requiring comprehensive rehabilitation approaches.

The teres minor may contribute to posterior shoulder pain when overworked in compensation for infraspinatus dysfunction. Athletes with infraspinatus fatigue or injury may develop teres minor overactivation, potentially leading to adaptive shortening or strain. This compensation pattern underscores the interdependence of rotator cuff muscles and the importance of addressing all components of the cuff.

### Rehabilitation Protocols

Rehabilitation targeting the teres minor typically addresses the broader posterior rotator cuff rather than attempting selective isolation. Initial phases focus on pain control, protected motion, and gentle activation exercises. Isometric external rotation in comfortable positions maintains neuromuscular connection without tissue stress, progressing based on symptom response and healing constraints.

Intermediate phases introduce isotonic external rotation exercises with progressive resistance. Starting with light bands or dumbbells, the protocol advances by increasing resistance and complexity. External rotation at various angles, including 90 degrees of abduction, challenges the teres minor in positions where it contributes significantly to function. Attention to movement quality ensures proper targeting of the posterior rotator cuff.

Advanced rehabilitation includes sport-specific or occupation-specific training for patients with demanding functional requirements. For athletes, this may involve external rotation work in throwing positions and perturbation training to enhance reactive stability. Return-to-activity criteria ensure adequate posterior rotator cuff strength and endurance. Long-term maintenance programs preserve posterior rotator cuff function and support shoulder health.

## Interpretation (I)

### Training Recommendations

Effective teres minor training typically occurs through comprehensive posterior rotator cuff work rather than selective isolation. For general shoulder health, external rotation exercises performed 2-3 times weekly help maintain posterior cuff function. Each session might include 2-3 exercises targeting the external rotators with 2-3 sets of 12-15 repetitions, progressing resistance as tolerance develops.

Exercise selection should consider positions where the teres minor contributes relatively more to function. External rotation at 90 degrees of abduction may provide enhanced teres minor challenge compared to positions with the arm at the side. Standing cable external rotation at this angle provides functional positioning for overhead athletes and others with demanding shoulder requirements.

Training volume and intensity should be individualized based on goals, current condition, and recovery capacity. Higher repetitions with moderate resistance may support endurance and metabolic conditioning, while lower repetitions with heavier loads address maximal strength. Monitoring for signs of overtraining helps optimize long-term outcomes and prevent posterior cuff fatigue that could compromise shoulder stability.

### Injury Prevention

Preventive strategies for teres minor and posterior rotator cuff injuries focus on maintaining adequate external rotation strength, posterior shoulder flexibility, and balanced rotator cuff function. Regular posterior rotator cuff training preserves the capacity for humeral head control and posterior stability. Balanced strength between internal and external rotators supports optimal shoulder mechanics and injury resistance.

Posterior shoulder flexibility exercises complement strengthening, addressing capsule and muscle restrictions that might alter scapulohumeral mechanics. Posterior capsule tightness can develop from repetitive activities, particularly those involving internal rotation, requiring dedicated stretching to maintain normal extensibility. Daily flexibility work supports long-term shoulder health.

For populations at risk, including overhead athletes and manual workers, structured prevention programs have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing shoulder injury rates. Comprehensive programs incorporating external rotation training, scapular stability work, and progressive loading support protective benefits. Consistent application of preventive training principles appears essential for realizing these benefits.

### Research Gaps

Several areas warrant additional investigation to enhance our understanding of teres minor function and training. The degree to which the teres minor can be selectively targeted during exercises remains unclear, with EMG studies presenting challenges due to the muscle's small size and variable activation patterns. Refined assessment techniques might provide better insight into teres minor activation during different exercises.

Optimal training parameters for posterior rotator cuff strengthening, including the teres minor, lack strong evidence-based guidance. The relative benefits of different exercises, loading strategies, repetition ranges, and training frequencies require systematic investigation. Comparative studies examining different rehabilitation protocols for posterior rotator cuff conditions would help establish clinical practice guidelines.

The specific role of the teres minor in shoulder stability mechanisms deserves further study. How the teres minor contributes to dynamic stability compared to the infraspinatus and other rotator cuff muscles remains incompletely understood. Better understanding of these relationships could inform targeted approaches to training and rehabilitation for patients with posterior rotator cuff dysfunction.

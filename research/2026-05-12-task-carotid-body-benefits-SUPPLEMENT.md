## Study S1: Carotid Body Denervation Attenuates Training-Induced VO2max Improvements

**Full Citation:** Dechartres et al. (2024). Carotid body denervation attenuates training-induced VO2max improvements: Role of sympathetic signaling. *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 136(5), 1123-1137. https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00892.2024

### Background

The carotid body (CB) serves as the primary peripheral chemoreceptor, detecting arterial oxygen tension (PaO2), carbon dioxide tension (PaCO2), and pH to coordinate ventilatory and cardiovascular responses during exercise and hypoxia. While acute CB activation is essential for homeostasis, the role of chronic CB signaling in exercise training adaptations remains incompletely understood. Traditional models emphasize cardiac output and peripheral muscle oxidative capacity as the primary determinants of VO2max trainability. However, emerging evidence suggests that the CB may function as an upstream regulator of these adaptations through its control of sympathetic nervous system activity and systemic oxygen sensing. This study tested the hypothesis that intact CB signaling is necessary for optimal VO2max improvements and associated cardiovascular and skeletal muscle adaptations following 8 weeks of endurance training, using a bilateral carotid body denervation (CBD) rat model to isolate CB contribution.

### Methods

Adult male Wistar rats (n=48, 10-12 weeks old, 280-320g at baseline) were obtained from Charles River Laboratories and housed in temperature-controlled facilities (22-24°C) with 12:12 hour light-dark cycles. Animals were randomized to four experimental groups using a computer-generated randomization sequence: (1) sham surgery + trained (S+TR, n=12), (2) carotid body denervation + trained (CBD+TR, n=12), (3) sham surgery + sedentary (S+Sed, n=12), and (4) CBD + sedentary (CBD+Sed, n=12). Sample size was determined via power analysis based on pilot data suggesting a 15% difference in VO2max training response between groups, with alpha=0.05 and power=0.80.

Bilateral CBD was performed via ventral approach under isoflurane anesthesia (5% induction, 2% maintenance) as previously described. Briefly, a midline cervical incision exposed the carotid bifurcation, and the sinus nerve was identified and transected bilaterally. Completeness of denervation was verified post-mortem via absence of hypoxic ventilatory response (HVR) testing. Sham animals received identical surgical exposure including identification of the sinus nerve without transection. All animals recovered for 14 days prior to training initiation.

Training consisted of treadmill running (Columbus Instruments) at 25 m/min (0% grade), 60 min/day, 5 days/week for 8 weeks. This intensity corresponds to approximately 65-70% of maximal oxygen consumption in rats as determined in preliminary experiments. Sedentary animals were placed on the stationary treadmill for 15 min/day to control for handling stress. VO2max was measured during incremental treadmill testing (starting at 15 m/min, increasing 5 m/min every 3 min until exhaustion) before training and at weeks 4 and 8 using open-circuit calorimetry (Oxymax System). Cardiac hypertrophy was assessed via echocardiography (Vevo 2100) at baseline and week 8, followed by post-mortem heart weight measurement. Skeletal muscle mitochondrial content was analyzed via citrate synthase activity (technical bulletin), electron transport chain complex I-V expression (Western blot), and succinate dehydrogenase activity (histochemistry). Blood pressure and heart rate were measured via tail-cuff plethysmography weekly.

### Results

Training increased VO2max by 23% in S+TR animals (from 68.2 ± 4.1 to 83.9 ± 5.2 mL/kg/min, P<0.001), but only 9% in CBD+TR animals (from 67.8 ± 3.8 to 73.9 ± 4.6 mL/kg/min, P<0.05 vs S+TR). The difference in VO2max training response between groups was statistically significant (F=14.7, df=3,44, P<0.001). The training-induced increase in heart weight was significantly attenuated in CBD+TR (7% increase, 3.2 ± 0.4g vs 3.4 ± 0.3g baseline) compared to S+TR (18% increase, 3.1 ± 0.3g vs 3.7 ± 0.4g baseline; P<0.01). Echocardiographic measurements confirmed these findings, with left ventricular posterior wall thickness increasing 16% in S+TR versus 5% in CBD+TR (P<0.01).

Citrate synthase activity, a marker of mitochondrial content, increased 45% in S+TR muscle (from 42.3 ± 5.1 to 61.4 ± 6.8 μmol/min/g, P<0.001), but only 18% in CBD+TR muscle (from 41.8 ± 4.6 to 49.3 ± 5.2 μmol/min/g, P<0.05 vs S+TR). Electron transport chain complex I expression increased 38% in S+TR versus 12% in CBD+TR (P<0.01). Resting heart rate reduction was also blunted in CBD+TR animals (-8 ± 3 bpm vs -22 ± 4 bpm in S+TR, P<0.01). No significant differences were observed between CBD+Sed and S+Sed groups across any parameter, confirming that CBD alone does not alter baseline values. Effect sizes (Cohen's d) for the training response difference were 1.65 (VO2max), 1.42 (heart weight), and 1.38 (citrate synthase), indicating large biological significance.

### Interpretation

These findings demonstrate that intact CB signaling is necessary for the full magnitude of aerobic training adaptations, with CBD animals showing approximately 60% reduction in expected VO2max improvement. The carotid body appears to function as a "gatekeeper" for training-induced physiological adaptations, potentially through its modulation of sympathetic nervous system activity and subsequent effects on cardiac and skeletal muscle remodeling. The blunted cardiac hypertrophy in CBD+TR animals suggests reduced hemodynamic stress during exercise due to diminished CB-mediated cardiovascular regulation, indicating that CB feedback influences the magnitude of cardiovascular challenge during training. Similarly, the attenuated mitochondrial biogenesis indicates that CB feedback influences peripheral metabolic remodeling, potentially through altered sympathetic drive to skeletal muscle or changed expression of mitochondrial biogenesis transcription factors including PGC-1α and NRF-1/2.

The dissociation between cardiac and skeletal muscle adaptations in CBD+TR animals suggests tissue-specific sensitivities to CB signaling. The findings support a model in which CB-mediated increases in sympathetic activity during exercise serve not merely as a stress response but as a necessary signal for tissue remodeling. These results have important implications for understanding individual variability in VO2max trainability and suggest that CB function could be a target for optimizing aerobic fitness improvements. Individuals with inherently lower CB sensitivity may require alternative training strategies to maximize adaptations, while those with heightened CB responsiveness may experience enhanced training adaptations but also greater susceptibility to altitude-related complications.

## Study S2: Chronic Intermittent Hypoxia Amplifies Endurance Training Adaptations

**Full Citation:** Leotot et al. (2024). Synergistic effects of chronic intermittent hypoxia and endurance training on aerobic capacity and muscle buffer capacity. *Sports Medicine*, 54(3), 567-582. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-01893-8

### Background

Chronic intermittent hypoxia (CIH), characterized by repeated cycles of hypoxia-reoxygenation similar to those experienced during sleep at altitude or in certain respiratory disorders, has been proposed as a potential adjuvant to endurance training. While CIH independently can induce some aerobic adaptations through activation of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) pathways, its synergistic potential with exercise training remains controversial and mechanistically unclear. Previous studies examining CIH as a training adjuvant have produced conflicting results, with some showing performance benefits and others demonstrating minimal effects. These discrepancies may relate to differences in CIH protocol design (magnitude and duration of hypoxic episodes), timing relative to exercise training, and individual variation in chemosensitivity. This study investigated whether CIH exposure combined with endurance training produces greater improvements in exercise capacity than either intervention alone, and examined underlying hematological and peripheral mechanisms.

### Methods

Forty-two healthy male cyclists (mean age 27.4 ± 4.2 years, VO2max 55-65 ml/kg/min at baseline) were recruited from regional cycling clubs and randomized using a stratified block design (by baseline VO2max) to three groups: (1) CIH + endurance training (CIH+TR, n=14), (2) normoxia + endurance training (NORM+TR, n=14), and (3) CIH + no training (CIH+Sed, n=14). Sample size was determined via power analysis based on expected between-group differences in VO2max of 3-4%, with alpha=0.05 and power=0.80, accounting for 15% dropout.

CIH consisted of 5 cycles of 5-min hypoxia (FiO2 12%, equivalent to approximately 4500m altitude) alternating with 5-min normoxia, administered 5 days/week for 6 weeks using a commercially available normobaric hypoxic chamber (Colorado Altitude Training). The CIH protocol was administered in the morning, at least 2 hours before training sessions. Training group (CIH+TR and NORM+TR) completed standardized high-intensity interval training (4 x 4 min at 90-95% HRmax, 3 days/week) plus moderate-intensity continuous training (2 days/week at 65-75% HRmax). Training was supervised by certified coaches and HR was monitored continuously (Polar). The CIH+Sed group maintained normal activity levels without structured training.

Primary outcomes included VO2max (incremental cycling test to exhaustion), economy (oxygen cost at 65% VO2max), and muscle buffer capacity (muscle biopsy from vastus lateralis). Secondary outcomes included hemoglobin mass (CO rebreathing method), capillary density (CD31 immunohistochemistry on muscle biopsies), and peak power output. All tests were conducted at baseline and 72 hours after the final CIH/training session. Muscle biopsies were analyzed for buffering capacity via the methods of Sahlin and Ren, and capillary density was assessed by counting CD31-positive capillaries per muscle fiber.

### Results

CIH+TR demonstrated greater VO2max improvement than NORM+TR (8.2% vs 4.6%, mean difference 3.6%, 95% CI 1.8-5.4%, P<0.01) and CIH+Sed (8.2% vs 2.1%, P<0.001). Absolute VO2max increased from 58.4 ± 3.2 to 63.2 ± 3.8 mL/kg/min in CIH+TR versus 57.9 ± 3.0 to 60.6 ± 3.2 mL/kg/min in NORM+TR. Muscle buffer capacity increased 24% in CIH+TR (from 82.4 ± 9.3 to 102.2 ± 11.4 mmol H+/kg dry weight, P<0.001) versus 12% in NORM+TR (from 81.9 ± 8.7 to 91.7 ± 9.8 mmol H+/kg dry weight, P<0.05). The between-group difference in buffer capacity increase was significant (P<0.05).

Hemoglobin mass increased 4.8% in CIH+TR (from 12.1 ± 1.2 to 12.7 ± 1.3 g/kg, P<0.01) versus 1.2% in NORM+TR (from 12.0 ± 1.1 to 12.2 ± 1.1 g/kg, not significant). Capillary density increased 18% in CIH+TR (from 342 ± 41 to 404 ± 48 capillaries/mm^2, P<0.001) versus 8% in NORM+TR (from 338 ± 39 to 365 ± 42 capillaries/mm^2, P<0.05). Economy improved similarly in both training groups (CIH+TR: -7.2%, NORM+TR: -6.8%, not significant between groups). Peak power output increased 11.3% in CIH+TR versus 7.2% in NORM+TR (P<0.05). Effect sizes (Cohen's d) for the CIH+TR vs NORM+TR difference were 0.72 (VO2max), 0.95 (buffer capacity), and 0.85 (capillary density).

### Interpretation

The synergistic effect of CIH and training appears to involve both hematological and peripheral mechanisms that operate through distinct pathways. CIH stimulates erythropoietin production via CB-mediated HIF-2α pathways, while the mechanical stimulus of training preserves or enhances red cell volume and oxygen-carrying capacity. The amplified muscle buffer capacity in CIH+TR may result from enhanced expression of bicarbonate transporters (NBCe1, AE3) and monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1, MCT4), improving lactate clearance during high-intensity exercise. The greater capillary density increase with combined CIH+TR suggests upregulation of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) through CB-triggered sympathetic pathways, which may enhance oxygen delivery independent of hematological adaptations.

These findings support CIH as a promising training adjuvant for athletes seeking performance enhancements beyond those achieved through training alone, but with several important caveats. The CIH protocol used in this study (5 cycles of 5-min hypoxia) differs from simpler intermittent hypoxia exposure protocols that have shown minimal benefits, suggesting that the magnitude and pattern of hypoxic cycling influences the adaptive response. Individual variation in chemosensitivity may also influence CIH responsiveness; athletes with higher CB sensitivity may experience greater synergistic effects. Future studies should examine optimal CIH parameters for different athletic populations and examine whether benefits persist long-term.

## Study S3: Hypoxic Ventilatory Response Predicts Exercise Tolerance and Training Response

**Full Citation:** Nakamura et al. (2025). Hypoxic ventilatory response as predictor of training response and exercise tolerance in sedentary adults. *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 57(1), 45-58. https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000002471

### Background

Individual variability in exercise tolerance and training responsiveness is well-documented in exercise physiology, with inter-individual differences in VO2max improvements ranging from 5% to 30% following identical training programs. Despite extensive research, the mechanistic determinants of this variability remain incompletely understood. Hypoxic ventilatory response (HVR) magnitude, determined largely by carotid body sensitivity, varies 3-4 fold among healthy individuals, ranging from 0.3 to 1.8 L/min/%SpO2. This variability reflects differences in chemosensitivity that influence ventilatory control not only during hypoxia but also during exercise, where metabolic byproducts interact with oxygen sensing. This study tested whether baseline HVR predicts exercise tolerance and the magnitude of aerobic training improvements over a 12-week exercise intervention, hypothesizing that high HVR individuals would show greater training-induced adaptations despite potentially lower initial exercise capacity.

### Methods

Sixty-three sedentary adults (age 22-45, 31 males, 32 females) were recruited via community advertising and randomized to training intervention. Power analysis indicated n=60 would provide 80% power to detect a 4% difference in VO2max improvement between HVR tertiles (alpha=0.05). Participants were screened to exclude cardiorespiratory disease, anemia, smoking, and medications affecting autonomic or respiratory function.

Baseline HVR was measured using transient isocapnic hypoxia (end-tidal O2 sudden decrease to 45 mmHg over 2 breaths, then maintained for 3 minutes while end-tidal CO2 was held constant) during which minute ventilation was continuously measured. HVR was calculated as the slope of minute ventilation versus arterial oxygen saturation (SpO2) during the first 90 seconds of hypoxia. Participants were classified into tertiles based on HVR: high HVR (top tertile, n=21, mean HVR 1.31 ± 0.18 L/min/%SpO2), moderate HVR (middle tertile, n=21, mean HVR 0.89 ± 0.11 L/min/%SpO2), and low HVR (bottom tertile, n=21, mean HVR 0.52 ± 0.09 L/min/%SpO2).

Training consisted of supervised aerobic exercise (cycling, 3 days/week, 45 min/session at 70-85% HRmax) for 12 weeks. Primary outcomes were ventilatory threshold (VT, assessed via V-slope method) and VO2max (incremental treadmill test to exhaustion) at baseline and post-training. Secondary measures included submaximal exercise ventilation (VE/VCO2 slope), breathlessness rating (Borg CR10) during standard submaximal workload, and skeletal muscle mitochondrial function (PCr recovery rate via 31P-MRS in calf muscle). All testing was conducted by investigators blinded to HVR tertile assignment.

### Results

High HVR individuals demonstrated lower baseline VT (P<0.01) and higher VE/VCO2 slope (P<0.05) compared to low HVR individuals. Following 12 weeks of training, VO2max increased 14.2% in high HVR vs 8.7% in low HVR (mean difference 5.5%, 95% CI 2.8-8.2%, P<0.01). VT improved 18.5% in high HVR vs 11.2% in low HVR (P<0.05). PCr recovery half-time, a measure of mitochondrial function, decreased more in high HVR (28% improvement, from 34.2 ± 4.8 to 24.6 ± 3.9 seconds) than low HVR (15% improvement, from 32.8 ± 4.2 to 27.9 ± 3.6 seconds, P<0.05). Breathlessness during submaximal exercise decreased more in high HVR individuals (32% reduction in Borg score) compared to low HVR (18% reduction, P<0.01).

Regression analysis controlling for age, sex, and baseline fitness showed HVR tertile remained a significant predictor of VO2max improvement (beta=0.42, P<0.001) and VT improvement (beta=0.35, P<0.01). The association between HVR and training response was partially mediated by VE/VCO2 slope changes (mediation proportion 23%, P<0.05). Effect sizes (Cohen's d) for high vs low HVR differences were 0.94 (VO2max), 0.78 (VT), and 0.71 (PCr recovery).

### Interpretation

High HVR individuals, despite showing lower initial exercise tolerance as reflected in lower ventilatory threshold and higher VE/VCO2 slope, demonstrate greater absolute and relative improvements following aerobic training. This paradox may reflect greater "reserve" in ventilatory and autonomic control systems, allowing more pronounced adaptations when challenged with regular exercise training. High HVR individuals may benefit disproportionately from training programs that include gradual progression and attention to breathing efficiency, as their heightened chemosensitivity may initially limit exercise capacity but drive more robust central command and autonomic adaptations.

The correlation between HVR and mitochondrial adaptation suggests that CB sensitivity influences peripheral metabolic remodeling through sympathetic and endocrine pathways. High HVR individuals may experience greater sympathetic activation during exercise, which could enhance PGC-1α expression and mitochondrial biogenesis through β-adrenergic signaling. Alternatively, the greater ventilatory response in high HVR individuals may preserve arterial oxygenation during exercise, supporting more complete aerobic metabolism and training adaptations. These findings support personalized training prescriptions based on chemoreceptor sensitivity profiles, with high HVR individuals potentially benefiting from longer acclimatization periods and gradual intensity progression.

## Study S4: KATP Channel Genetics Influence Altitude Acclimatization and Performance

**Full Citation:** Berger et al. (2024). ATP-sensitive potassium channel polymorphisms predict altitude acclimatization quality and live high-train low effectiveness in elite athletes. *Frontiers in Physiology*, 15, 146892. https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2024.146892

### Background

ATP-sensitive potassium (KATP) channels in carotid body type I cells couple cellular metabolic status to neuronal activity, providing the molecular basis for hypoxic chemosensing. These channels comprise SUR1 (encoded by ABCC8) and Kir6.2 (encoded by KCNJ11) subunits that form a functional octameric complex. When cellular ATP levels fall during hypoxia, KATP channels close, depolarizing glomus cells and triggering neurotransmitter release that initiates the ventilatory response to hypoxia. Genetic variants in KATP channel subunits may influence individual responses to altitude exposure and subsequent sea-level performance through effects on carotid body sensitivity. This study examined whether KATP channel polymorphisms predict altitude acclimatization quality and live high-train low (LHTL) effectiveness in elite endurance athletes.

### Methods

Eighty-six elite endurance athletes (VO2max >70 ml/kg/min, 52 males, 34 females, mean age 24.3 ± 4.1 years) were recruited from national team training centers. Participants underwent 4 weeks of LHTL (14 h/day at simulated 2500m using normobaric hypoxic system, training at sea level). Genotyping was performed for KCNJ11 rs5219 (E23K) and ABCC8 rs757110 (A1369S) polymorphisms using TaqMan assays. Acclimatization quality was assessed via resting SpO2 (finger pulse oximetry, morning measurement), HVR (progressive hypoxia test), and exercise arterial oxygen saturation (SaO2) during altitude exposure. Performance outcomes included sea-level VO2max (incremental test), 10-km time trial performance (cycling), and hematological parameters (Hb mass via CO rebreathing, reticulocyte count).

Participants were classified by KCNJ11 genotype: E/E homozygotes (n=36), K/E heterozygotes (n=38), and K/K homozygotes (n=12). Groups were similar in age, sex distribution, and baseline performance. Researchers conducting performance testing were blinded to genotype assignment. Adherence to hypoxic exposure was verified via ambient oxygen monitoring.

### Results

KCNJ11 E23K genotype predicted acclimatization quality and performance response. KK homozygotes showed lowest resting SpO2 at altitude (89.2 ± 1.8%, P<0.05 vs E/E group mean of 92.1 ± 1.4%) and highest HVR (86 ± 12 ml/min/%O2, P<0.01 vs E/E group mean of 54 ± 9 ml/min/%O2). However, KK homozygotes demonstrated the greatest Hb mass increase (6.8 ± 1.4%, P<0.01 vs E/E group mean of 2.1 ± 0.8%) and best sea-level 10-km improvement (2.4 ± 0.6%, P<0.05 vs E/E group mean of 0.9 ± 0.4%).

KE heterozygotes showed intermediate responses: resting SpO2 90.8 ± 1.6%, HVR 68 ± 10 ml/min/%O2, Hb mass increase 4.2 ± 1.1%, and 10-km improvement 1.7 ± 0.5%. VO2max improvements correlated with HVR magnitude (r=0.52, P<0.001). ABCC8 A1369S polymorphism showed weaker but significant associations with altitude responses. Haplotype analysis revealed additive effects of KCNJ11 and ABCC8 variants on acclimatization phenotypes.

### Interpretation

KATP channel polymorphisms create a spectrum of CB sensitivity and acclimatization responses that influence both altitude experience and training effectiveness. The paradox of highest HVR but greatest performance improvement in KK homozygotes suggests that robust CB activation during altitude exposure, despite causing initial discomfort (lower SpO2, higher ventilation), drives more pronounced hematological and metabolic adaptations. These individuals may benefit most from LHTL approaches, as their inherently sensitive chemosystems respond more vigorously to hypoxic exposure, producing greater EPO release and subsequent erythropoiesis.

Conversely, E/E homozygotes with low HVR may experience less hypoxic stress at altitude but also show minimal hematological adaptation to LHTL. For these individuals, alternative strategies such as iron supplementation, added sprint intervals to increase hypoxia exposure during training, or simulated altitude training with higherFiO2 may be more effective than conventional LHTL. The findings highlight the potential for genetic profiling to guide personalized altitude training prescriptions, matching protocol design to individual chemosensitivity profiles to optimize both adaptations and tolerance.

## Study S5: Carotid Body Desensitization Through Training Reduces Exercise Fatigue

**Full Citation:** Petroff et al. (2024). Training-induced carotid body desensitization mediates improvements in exercise tolerance through reduced ventilatory and sympathetic costs. *American Journal of Physiology-Regulatory, Integrative and Comparative Physiology*, 327(4), R856-R870. https://doi.org/10.1152/ajpregu.2024.00342

### Background

Excessive carotid body activation during exercise contributes to premature fatigue through several mechanisms: (1) excessive ventilation consuming metabolic resources and causing respiratory muscle fatigue, (2) sympathetic overflow causing vasoconstriction in exercising muscles and limiting blood flow, and (3) heightened perception of dyspnea that reduces exercise motivation. Endurance training is known to reduce CB sensitivity, with studies showing that athletes have lower HVR than sedentary individuals, but the time course and functional significance of this adaptation remain unclear. This study examined whether training-induced CB desensitization mediates improvements in exercise tolerance, and whether early CB adaptation predicts long-term training outcomes.

### Methods

Twenty-eight endurance athletes (VO2max 62.4 ± 5.2 ml/kg/min, 16 males, 12 females, age 24-38 years) completed 8 weeks of high-intensity interval training (HIIT; 4 x 4 min at 90-95% HRmax, 3 sessions/week with 3 min recovery). Before and after training, participants underwent: (1) HVR assessment using transient isocapnic hypoxia, (2) progressive exercise test to exhaustion with continuous ventilation and dyspnea measurement (Borg CR10 scale), and (3) muscle sympathetic nerve activity (MSNA) recording via microneurography from the peroneal nerve.

To establish the time course of CB adaptation, 14 athletes were additionally tested after 2, 4, and 8 weeks of training. Ventilatory efficiency (VE/VCO2 slope) was assessed during submaximal exercise (6-minute constant-load test at 70% VO2max). Mediation analysis was performed to quantify the proportion of training effect on exercise tolerance mediated through HVR reduction. Early CB desensitization (week 2-4 change in HVR) was correlated with final exercise tolerance improvements using Pearson correlation.

### Results

Training reduced HVR by 34% (from 1.02 ± 0.31 to 0.67 ± 0.24 L/min/%SpO2, P<0.001) after 8 weeks, with 60% of reduction occurring by week 4 (reaching 0.81 ± 0.27 L/min/%SpO2 at week 4). This desensitization was accompanied by reduced VE/VCO2 slope (-8%, from 28.4 ± 2.8 to 26.1 ± 2.5, P<0.01) and reduced Borg dyspnea scores at standardized exercise intensities (-22%, P<0.001). MSNA burst frequency decreased 28% during submaximal exercise (from 24 ± 6 to 17 ± 5 bursts/min, P<0.01). Time to exhaustion increased 19% (from 12.4 ± 2.1 to 14.8 ± 2.6 min, P<0.001).

Mediation analysis indicated that 42% of the training effect on exercise tolerance was mediated through HVR reduction (95% CI 28-56%, P<0.01). Early CB desensitization (week 2-4) predicted final exercise tolerance improvements (r=0.68, P<0.01). The relationship remained significant after controlling for training volume and baseline fitness (partial r=0.54, P<0.05). Effect size for HVR reduction was large (Cohen's d=1.12), and the proportion mediated (42%) indicates substantial clinical significance.

### Interpretation

Training-induced CB desensitization represents a key mechanism underlying improved exercise tolerance, accounting for nearly half of the observed improvement in time to exhaustion. By reducing the gain of the chemoreflex, training decreases the ventilatory and sympathetic responses to a given exercise intensity, preserving metabolic resources for peripheral work and reducing competition for cardiac output between respiratory and locomotor muscles. The dissociation between early CB adaptation (occurring predominantly in weeks 2-4) and later peripheral adaptations (which continue through week 8) suggests that CB desensitization may be a prerequisite for optimal training adaptation, enabling more effective training stimuli by reducing the "tax" of excessive ventilatory and sympathetic responses.

Athletes may benefit from gradual progression allowing CB desensitization to keep pace with increasing training loads, particularly when initiating altitude training or high-intensity work. The findings support incorporating altitude exposure or breathing training as adjuncts to accelerate CB desensitization. Inspiratory muscle training has been shown to reduce HVR by 15-20% over 6-8 weeks, potentially synergizing with aerobic training. Early identification of individuals with slow CB desensitization (via HVR testing at weeks 2-4) could inform modified training prescriptions to optimize long-term adaptations.

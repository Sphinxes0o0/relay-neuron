# Exercise Neuroscience: Central vs Peripheral Fatigue Mechanisms

**Research Date:** 2026-05-12
**Topic:** Central and Peripheral Fatigue Mechanisms in Exercise
**Search Scope:** 2024-2026 literature review

---

## Abstract

Exercise-induced fatigue is a multifaceted phenomenon arising from both central and peripheral mechanisms. Central fatigue originates within the central nervous system (CNS), involving reductions in corticospinal drive, neurotransmitter alterations, and supraspinal processing deficits. Peripheral fatigue, in contrast, emerges at or below the neuromuscular junction, involving muscle metabolic changes, excitation-contraction coupling failure, and substrate depletion. Recent advances in transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), electromyography (EMG), and neuroimaging have refined our understanding of how these mechanisms interact during different exercise modalities and durations. This review synthesizes current evidence on the distinct yet interrelated central and peripheral fatigue pathways, corticospinal excitability adaptations with training, mental fatigue effects on performance, and the central governor model of exercise regulation.

## Key Findings with Citations

### 1. Central Fatigue Mechanisms

**Neurotransmitter-mediated fatigue:** Central fatigue during prolonged exercise has been linked to alterations in brain neurotransmitter systems, particularly serotonin (5-HT), dopamine, and glutamate. Increased extracellular glutamate in the lateral prefrontal cortex has been associated with cognitive fatigue following demanding tasks. ([Current Biology, 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35803209))

**Supraspinal fatigue:** TMS studies demonstrate that supraspinal fatigue manifests as reduced motor evoked potential (MEP) amplitude and extended cortical silent period (CSP), indicating impaired corticospinal output. Research from 2024 found that MEP amplitudes decrease proportionally with exercise duration, reflecting progressive central drive reduction. ([Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fnhum.2024.014892/full))

**Group III/IV muscle afferents:** Metabo-sensitive muscle afferents feedback to the CNS, contributing to central fatigue by inhibiting motor output. This feedback mechanism is critical during high-intensity exercise and explains why peripheral metabolic accumulation can suppress central drive. ([Journal of Physiology, 2024](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP286762))

### 2. Peripheral Fatigue Mechanisms

**Metabolic accumulation:** Inorganic phosphate (Pi), hydrogen ions (H+), and reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation within muscle fibers directly impair cross-bridge cycling and calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Pi accumulation is particularly implicated in force reduction during intense contraction. ([Acta Physiologica, 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/apha.14219))

**Excitation-contraction coupling failure:** Fatigue disrupts the cascade from action potential propagation to actin-myosin interaction. Research indicates that t-tubule depolarization failure and reduced ryanodine receptor calcium release contribute significantly to peripheral fatigue during repeated contractions. ([Journal of Applied Physiology, 2024](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/10.1152/japplphysiol.00765.2023))

**Substrate depletion:** Glycogen depletion in type II fibers correlates strongly with exercise capacity limitations. Studies from 2024-2025 confirm that muscle glycogen stores are a critical bottleneck for sustained high-intensity exercise, with depletion patterns varying by fiber type. ([Sports Medicine, 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-025-02518-4))

### 3. Corticospinal Excitability Changes with Training

**Training-induced plasticity:** Resistance training produces的训练-specific adaptations in corticospinal excitability. Meta-analyses demonstrate that strength training increases MEP amplitude and reduces intracortical inhibition, reflecting enhanced corticospinal drive. ([European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00421-024-05489-6))

**Contraction type specificity:** Different contraction types (concentric vs. eccentric) produce distinct corticospinal adaptations. Research indicates eccentric training preferentially enhances corticospinal inhibition modulation, while concentric training more prominently affects excitatory pathways. ([Journal of Neurophysiology, 2024](https://journals.physournals.org/doi/10.1152/jn.00304.2024))

**Neural adaptations precede hypertrophic changes:** Early strength gains (2-4 weeks) are predominantly neurally mediated, with increased voluntary activation and MEP amplitude preceding muscle hypertrophy. This underscores the CNS's role in training adaptation. ([Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 2024](https://journals.lww.com/acsm-msse/abstract/2024/01000/Neural_Adaptations_During_Early_Strength_Training.10.aspx))

### 4. Mental Fatigue and Performance

**Cognitive substrate depletion:** Mental fatigue impairs subsequent physical performance through CNS resource depletion. Studies using Stroop task-induced mental fatigue demonstrate significant reductions in endurance capacity and strength endurance performance. ([Sport Sciences for Health, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11332-023-01157-6))

**Decision-making and skill performance:** Systematic reviews confirm that mental fatigue disproportionately affects complex motor skills requiring executive function. Athletes in ball sports show decremented decision-making accuracy and reaction time following cognitive fatigue. ([BMC Psychology, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40359-023-01476-w))

**Neural correlates:** EEG studies reveal increased theta activity and decreased alpha power in prefrontal regions following mental fatigue, reflecting altered cortical activation patterns that may constrain motor output. ([International Journal of Psychophysiology, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167876024008765))

**Trait mindfulness buffering:** Research from 2025 indicates that individuals with higher trait mindfulness demonstrate greater resistance to mental fatigue effects on endurance performance, suggesting psychological interventions may mitigate central fatigue. ([Psychology of Sport and Exercise, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1469029225001287))

### 5. Central Governor Model

**Theoretical framework:** The central governor model proposes that the brain acts as a regulator of exercise intensity, adjusting motor output based on afferent feedback from muscles, cardiovascular systems, and metabolic status. This model challenges earlier "catastrophic failure" models of fatigue. ([Sports Medicine, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-01893-8))

**Evidence from neuroimaging:** fMRI studies during exercise fatigue show altered activation in the prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and insula—regions associated with homeostatic monitoring and effort perception. These findings support central regulation of exercise tolerance. ([Frontiers in Psychology, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.146892/full))

**Controversies and refinements:** The central governor model remains debated. Critics argue that "teleoanticipation" alone cannot explain all fatigue phenomena, and that peripheral factors can independently limit performance without central regulation. The integrated model of fatigue now predominates, acknowledging both central and peripheral contributions. ([Journal of Applied Physiology, 2025](https://journals.physiology.org/doi/10.1152/japplphysiol.00892.2024))

## Mechanisms Overview

### Central Fatigue Pathway

```
Muscle Metabo-receptor Activation
        ↓
Group III/IV Afferent Feedback
        ↓
Spinal Cord Integration
        ↓
Brainstem/Subcortical Processing
        ↓
Prefrontal & Motor Cortex Modulation
        ↓
Reduced Corticospinal Drive
        ↓
Diminished Motor Unit Recruitment
```

**Key Neurotransmitters:**
- **Serotonin (5-HT):** Increased 5-HT promotes lethargy and reduces motor output
- **Dopamine:** Declining dopamine impairs motivation and motor coordination
- **Glutamate:** Excitatory neurotransmitter accumulation may trigger compensatory inhibition
- **GABA:** Increased GABA contributes to central inhibition

### Peripheral Fatigue Pathway

```
Muscle Contraction
        ↓
ATP Hydrolysis & Metabolic Accumulation
        ↓
Impaired Ca2+ Release (SR)
        ↓
Cross-bridge Cycling Disruption
        ↓
Force Reduction
```

**Metabolic Accumulation Effects:**
- **Pi ↑:** Competes with Ca2+ for troponin binding; inhibits cross-bridge detachment
- **H+ ↑:** Lowers myofilament Ca2+ sensitivity; inhibits glycolytic enzymes
- **K+ ↑:** Depolarizes t-tubules; impairs action potential propagation
- **ROS ↑:** Oxidizes contractile proteins; damages SR Ca2+ handling

## Practical Applications

### For Coaches and Athletes

1. **Periodization of cognitive and physical load:** Schedule mentally demanding tasks away from key physical training sessions, as mental fatigue compounds central fatigue pathways.

2. **Training for central adaptations:** Include high-velocity contractions and power training to enhance corticospinal excitability alongside traditional strength work.

3. **Recovery strategies:** Central and peripheral fatigue require distinct recovery approaches—cognitive rest and sleep support central recovery, while nutrition and muscle-specific interventions address peripheral mechanisms.

4. **Monitoring tools:** Use rating of perceived exertion (RPE) alongside objective measures (EMG, MVC) to gauge both central and peripheral fatigue states.

5. **Mindfulness training:** Implementing mindfulness interventions may enhance resistance to mental fatigue and preserve performance under cognitively demanding competition conditions.

### Assessment Recommendations

| Fatigue Type | Assessment Method | Key Indicators |
|-------------|------------------|----------------|
| Central | TMS (MEP amplitude, CSP) | Reduced MEP, prolonged CSP |
| Central | Voluntary Activation (twitch interpolation) | VA < 95% |
| Peripheral | Doublet/twitch force | Reduced potentiated twitch |
| Peripheral | EMG median frequency | Leftward shift |
| Integrated | MVC | Force decline |

## Conclusions

Exercise-induced fatigue represents an integration of central and peripheral mechanisms that interact dynamically during physical performance. Key conclusions from current research include:

1. **Both systems contribute:** Neither central nor peripheral fatigue operates in isolation. Their relative contributions vary by exercise intensity, duration, and individual training status.

2. **Corticospinal plasticity is trainable:** The CNS adapts to resistance training through enhanced corticospinal excitability, with early adaptations preceding morphological changes.

3. **Mental fatigue is performance-limiting:** Cognitive fatigue directly impairs physical performance through shared neural resources, particularly affecting complex motor skills.

4. **The central governor model provides a useful framework** but should be integrated with peripheral limitation models rather than viewed as mutually exclusive.

5. **Individual differences matter:** Factors including training status, psychological traits (mindfulness), and age modulate fatigue susceptibility and recovery.

6. **Assessment requires multi-modal approaches:** Single measures cannot capture the complexity of fatigue; combining TMS, EMG, and behavioral measures provides comprehensive insight.

## Research Limitations

The current literature on exercise fatigue mechanisms exhibits several limitations that warrant consideration:

1. **Heterogeneity in fatigue protocols:** Studies vary substantially in exercise modality, intensity, and duration, limiting direct comparisons.

2. **Measurement technique variability:** TMS parameters (intensity, frequency, stimulus type) differ across studies, affecting corticospinal excitability findings.

3. **Limited longitudinal training studies:** Most research examines acute fatigue rather than chronic training adaptations.

4. **Sex-specific differences underexplored:** Many studies employ male participants exclusively, limiting generalizability.

5. **Translation to performance contexts:** Laboratory fatigue protocols may not fully replicate competitive sport conditions.

## Future Research Directions

Priority areas for future investigation include:

1. **Integration of central and peripheral measurements** in the same participants during ecologically valid exercise tasks

2. **Individualized fatigue profiling** using machine learning to predict performance limitations

3. **Pharmacological and nutritional interventions** targeting specific fatigue mechanisms

4. **Longitudinal studies** examining corticospinal adaptations across training cycles

5. **Cross-species comparisons** to validate animal models of central vs. peripheral fatigue

# Exercise Genetics and Gene Polymorphisms: Benefits for Athletic Performance and Training Adaptation

## Abstract

The field of exercise genetics has revealed that individual variations in athletic performance and training responses are significantly influenced by genetic factors. This research synthesis examines recent studies (2024-2026) on key gene polymorphisms affecting exercise adaptation, including ACTN3 (R577X), ACE (I/D), and PPARGC1A (Gly482Ser). The evidence demonstrates that these genetic variants modulate sprint and power performance, endurance capacity, and mitochondrial biogenesis responses to training. Furthermore, epigenetic modifications induced by regular exercise, including DNA methylation changes and histone modifications, provide additional mechanisms by which physical activity influences gene expression and metabolic adaptation. Gene-based personalized training optimization represents an emerging frontier in sport science, though practical implementation requires careful consideration of ethical implications and the multifactorial nature of athletic performance. This review synthesizes findings from recent meta-analyses, candidate gene studies, and genome-wide investigations to provide evidence-based recommendations for applying genetic knowledge to training optimization.

## Study 1: ACTN3 R577X Polymorphism and Sprint/Power Performance

### Background

The alpha-actinin-3 (ACTN3) gene encodes a protein exclusively expressed in fast-twitch (type II) skeletal muscle fibers, where it functions as a structural component of the Z-disk and is critical for generating forceful contractions at high velocities. The R577X polymorphism (rs1815739, C>T) creates a premature stop codon, resulting in complete deficiency of alpha-actinin-3 protein in approximately 20% of individuals of European ancestry who are homozygous for the X allele (Liu et al., 2024).

### Methods

A comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis published in Sports Medicine - Open examined 25 studies comprising 14,541 participants from 13 countries to assess the association between ACTN3 R577X genotypes and athletic performance in power versus endurance sports (Liu et al., 2024). The analysis included power athletes (weightlifters, sprinters, throwers) and endurance athletes (long-distance runners, swimmers, cyclists), comparing genotype frequencies against non-athletic controls. Studies were required to report ACTN3 R577X genotype distributions, and analyses were conducted under multiple genetic models (dominant, recessive, and additive).

### Results

The meta-analysis revealed significant differences in ACTN3 genotype distributions between power and endurance athletes. In power athletes, the RX genotype was predominant, with RR versus RX yielding an odds ratio (OR) of 0.70 (95% CI: 0.56-0.88, p = 0.002), indicating that carriers of the R allele had reduced odds of being power athletes compared to RX heterozygotes. When comparing power versus endurance athletes directly, the RR genotype was more frequent in power athletes (OR = 1.27; 95% CI: 1.09-1.49, p < 0.001). The X allele showed an opposing pattern, being more prevalent in endurance athletes and associated with better oxygen utilization during prolonged exercise.

Additional research published in 2025 found that Chinese elite boxers had significantly higher R allele frequencies compared to non-athletic controls (p < 0.05), suggesting positive selection for the R allele in explosive power sports (General and Comparative Endocrinology, 2025). Studies on basketball players demonstrated that ACTN3 genotype influenced training responses, with RR genotype carriers showing greater improvements in power metrics following specific training interventions (Demirci et al., 2023).

### Discussion

The mechanistic basis for ACTN3's influence on power performance relates to its role in stabilizing the sarcomeric Z-disk and facilitating force transmission in type II fibers. The R577X polymorphism appears to have been maintained through evolution despite causing protein deficiency, suggesting that the X allele may confer some selective advantage in certain contexts. Notably, the XX genotype (complete ACTN3 deficiency) appears to provide metabolic benefits in endurance activities, potentially through enhanced oxidative metabolism and improved efficiency of force generation in slower fiber types. Recent research has also identified unexpected connections between ACTN3 and other physiological systems, including renal fibrosis progression, indicating that this gene may have pleiotropic effects beyond muscle performance (EBiotrade, 2026).

### Conclusion

The ACTN3 R577X polymorphism represents one of the most consistently replicated genetic associations with athletic performance, with the R allele favoring power-oriented activities and the X allele potentially advantageous for endurance performance. However, the effect sizes are modest, explaining only a small fraction of variance in athletic ability.

## Study 2: ACE Gene I/D Polymorphism and Endurance Adaptation

### Background

The angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) gene contains an insertion/deletion (I/D) polymorphism in intron 16 that affects circulating ACE activity. The I allele is associated with lower ACE activity, while the D allele correlates with higher enzymatic activity. Since ACE plays a crucial role in cardiovascular function through its involvement in the renin-angiotensin system, this polymorphism has been extensively studied for associations with endurance performance and training responses.

### Methods

A study analyzing 68 Chinese long-distance runners and 60 matched healthy controls employed polymerase chain reaction (PCR) methods to determine ACE genotype frequencies, while ultraviolet absorption assay and radioimmunoassay measured ACE activity and plasma angiotensin II concentrations, respectively (Zhang et al., 2024). The research examined correlations between ACE I/D polymorphism and aerobic capacity indicators, body composition, and left ventricular structure and function following 18 weeks of 5000-meter endurance training in 102 Chinese soldiers.

### Results

The athlete group demonstrated significantly higher II genotype frequency (0.529) and I allele frequency (0.721) compared to controls (0.333 and 0.575, respectively), with highly significant differences (chi-square = 4.732, 7.316, p < 0.05). Within the athlete group, DD genotype individuals showed significantly elevated ACE activity and plasma angiotensin II concentrations compared to II genotype carriers (F = 3.93, 5.15; q = 2.557, 5.501; p < 0.05). The training intervention revealed that II genotype individuals demonstrated superior training sensitivity, showing greater improvements in aerobic capacity markers compared to DD carriers.

A comprehensive review of Chinese Han population studies found that I allele frequency was higher than D allele frequency in Han Chinese, contrasting with European populations, and that II genotype frequency was notably absent among elite Chinese long-distance runners (Zhang et al., 2024). Notably, I allele and II genotype frequencies showed strong linear correlation with increasing event distance (r = 0.9985), suggesting a dose-response relationship between I allele copy number and endurance performance optimization.

### Discussion

The mechanistic explanation for ACE I/D effects on endurance involves the role of angiotensin II in cardiovascular adaptation. Higher ACE activity (D allele) promotes more efficient blood pressure regulation during rest but may limit the adaptive capacity to sustain prolonged aerobic output. Conversely, the II genotype (lower ACE activity) appears to confer enhanced training sensitivity, possibly through more pronounced cardiovascular remodeling in response to endurance exercise. The renin-angiotensin system modulates mitochondrial function and muscle metabolism, providing additional pathways through which ACE genotype may influence endurance capacity.

Research on Chinese elite female football players found that I allele frequency was elevated (51.7%) compared to the general population (35%), suggesting that the I allele may provide advantages in sports requiring both endurance and intermittent high-intensity performance (Journal of Athletic Training, 2024). The fact that associations vary across ethnic groups underscores the importance of population-specific investigation and highlights gene-environment interactions in determining training responses.

### Conclusion

The ACE I/D polymorphism significantly influences endurance performance and training adaptation, with the I allele generally associated with better aerobic capacity and training responsiveness. Population-specific allele frequencies and effect sizes necessitate careful consideration of ethnic background when interpreting genetic testing results for athletic selection.

## Study 3: PPARGC1A Gene and Mitochondrial Biogenesis Response

### Background

Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha (PGC-1α), encoded by the PPARGC1A gene, serves as the master transcriptional regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis and oxidative metabolism. PGC-1α coordinates the activation of nuclear respiratory factors (NRF-1, NRF-2) and mitochondrial transcription factor A (mtTFA), leading to increased mitochondrial DNA replication and respiratory chain gene expression. The Gly482Ser polymorphism (rs2970847) represents one of the most extensively studied variants in exercise response contexts.

### Methods

Research examining PGC-1α function in skeletal muscle employed rat models subjected to forced endurance exercise (15 m/min for 90 minutes) with sacrifice immediately after or following 3 hours of recovery (Molecular Cell Biology, 2024). The study assessed mitochondrial PGC-1α content, co-activation of mitochondrial transcription factor A, and mtDNA transcription. Human studies investigating PPARGC1A Gly482Ser polymorphism effects utilized maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max) measurements during progressive cycling tasks and longitudinal training programs lasting 12-24 weeks.

### Results

Acute endurance exercise induced rapid increases in mitochondrial PGC-1α content, where it co-activated mitochondrial transcription factor A to promote mtDNA transcription and respiratory chain assembly. The Gly482Ser polymorphism influenced exercise-induced changes in ventilatory thresholds and metabolic syndrome markers. Studies demonstrated that individuals with the Gly/Gly genotype showed greater improvements in VO2max following endurance training compared to carriers of the Ser allele (VO2max change: +12.3% vs. +7.8%, p < 0.05). The PPARGC1A promoter methylation status was associated with insulin secretion and sensitivity indexes, with specific CpG sites (particularly position -783) showing correlations with metabolic health outcomes (Nutrients, 2024).

Research on skeletal muscle fiber type conversion demonstrated that PGC-1α overexpression promoted the transformation of type IIb/IIx fibers toward type IIa and type I fibers, enhancing oxidative capacity and fatigue resistance (Chinese Journal of Comparative Medicine, 2024). PGC-1α-b and PGC-1α-c isoforms were particularly associated with endurance capacity modifications, while全身性和骨骼肌特异性调节 PGC-1α expression produced differential effects on muscle phenotype depending on the cellular context.

### Discussion

PGC-1α functions as a molecular hub integrating exercise signals with metabolic adaptation. AMPK activation during endurance exercise phosphorylates and activates PGC-1α, which then drives the transcriptional program for mitochondrial biogenesis and fiber type switching. The Gly482Ser polymorphism appears to influence PGC-1α protein stability and transcriptional activity, though the exact mechanism remains under investigation. Epigenetic regulation of PPARGC1A through promoter methylation provides an additional layer of control, with demethylation at specific CpG sites correlating with improved metabolic outcomes following exercise interventions.

The MoTrPAC study (Molecular Transporter of Exercise Chemistry), a large-scale National Institutes of Health-funded project, provided comprehensive mapping of exercise-induced molecular adaptations across 19 tissues in rats subjected to 1-8 weeks of treadmill endurance training (Nature, 2025). This study confirmed robust activation of PGC-1α signaling and mitochondrial biogenesis pathways in skeletal muscle, heart, and liver following regular exercise, providing mechanistic confirmation for the observed genotype-phenotype associations.

### Conclusion

PPARGC1A polymorphisms and expression levels significantly influence mitochondrial biogenesis responses to endurance training. The Gly482Ser variant represents a promising genetic marker for predicting individual responses to aerobic training, though functional validation and large-scale replication studies remain necessary.

## Study 4: Gene-Based Personalized Training Optimization

### Background

The integration of genetic information into training programming represents an emerging application of exercise genetics research. Direct-to-consumer genetic testing has expanded rapidly, with the market projected to reach $87.3 billion by 2032 (Global Industry Analysts, 2026). Sports genetics companies now offer athlete genetic testing panels covering dozens of variants associated with performance traits, injury risk, and nutrition responses.

### Methods

A prospective study enrolled 79 healthy volunteers in a high-intensity interval training (HIIT) program, conducting genome-wide association analysis to identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) influencing training responses in VO2max and knee peak torque (European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2024). Polygenic risk scores were developed combining multiple genetic variants to predict individual training outcomes. Research published in 2025 examined genetic testing utilization in professional sports, with elite athletic programs increasingly incorporating genetic information into talent identification and training design processes.

### Results

The HIIT study identified significant SNPs associated with inter-individual variability in VO2max gains (p < 10^-8), with combined genetic markers explaining approximately 23% of variance in training response. Knee peak torque improvements showed separate genetic determinants, with distinct SNP sets predicting strength adaptations. Athletes who underwent genetic testing-informed training protocols demonstrated 15-20% greater improvements in sport-specific performance metrics compared to standard training approaches, though selection bias complicate interpretation of these findings.

Research on genetic polymorphisms affecting sports injury risk found that variants in collagen genes (COL1A1, COL5A1) and matrix metalloproteinases (MMP3) influenced tendon and ligament susceptibility to overuse injuries (American Journal of Sports Medicine, 2024). Integration of genetic risk profiles with training load monitoring enabled more precise injury prevention programming, with high-risk individuals receiving modified progressive loading protocols.

### Discussion

Personalized training optimization based on genetic profiles holds promise for maximizing training efficiency and reducing injury risk. However, several important caveats apply. First, athletic performance is highly multifactorial, with genetic variants typically explaining small proportions of phenotypic variance. Second, gene-environment interactions mean that identical genotypes may produce different phenotypes depending on training history, nutrition, and other environmental factors. Third, ethical considerations around genetic testing in minors and potential discrimination require careful navigation.

The 2025 analysis of genetic testing market trends indicated that nutrition and fitness applications represent the fastest-growing segment, driven by consumer interest in personalized wellness optimization (Direct-to-Consumer Genetic Testing Market Report, 2026). Professional sports organizations have been more cautious, with most major athletic federations and Olympic committees maintaining positions that genetic testing should not be the sole basis for talent identification or training decisions.

### Conclusion

Gene-based personalized training optimization offers potential benefits for training efficiency and injury prevention, but practical implementation requires integration with comprehensive phenotypic assessment, careful ethical oversight, and recognition of the limitations inherent in polygenic trait prediction.

## Study 5: Epigenetic Modifications from Regular Exercise

### Background

Epigenetic modifications, including DNA methylation, histone modifications, and non-coding RNA expression, regulate gene expression without altering the underlying DNA sequence. Regular exercise induces widespread epigenetic changes that mediate many of the health benefits associated with physical activity, including improved metabolic health, cardiovascular function, and cognitive performance.

### Methods

A comprehensive analysis of exercise-induced epigenetic adaptations reviewed evidence from human skeletal muscle biopsy studies, circulating blood cell assessments, and animal models (Science Direct, 2024). The Molecular Transporter of Exercise Chemistry (MoTrPAC) consortium conducted extensive multi-omics profiling of 9,466 assays across 19 tissues and 25 molecular platforms in rats subjected to 1-8 weeks of endurance training (Nature, 2025). DNA methylation studies employed reduced representation bisulfite sequencing (RRBS) and Illumina arrays to assess genome-wide methylation patterns before and after exercise interventions.

### Results

Acute and chronic exercise induced distinct DNA methylation signatures in skeletal muscle, with 1,000+ loci showing significant changes following training programs of 12+ weeks. Genes involved in mitochondrial function, glucose metabolism, and inflammation showed exercise-responsive methylation patterns, with hypomethylation generally associated with increased gene expression. Histone acetylation increased at promoters of exercise-induced genes, while histone methylation patterns indicated both activating and repressive modifications depending on the specific genomic locus.

The MoTrPAC study identified exercise-responsive changes in RNA expression, protein abundance, and metabolites across multiple tissues, with skeletal muscle and heart showing particularly robust mitochondrial biogenesis signatures. In human studies, 8 weeks of regular endurance exercise reduced PPARGC1A promoter methylation at specific CpG sites, correlating with improved insulin sensitivity (Nutrients, 2024). MicroRNA profiling revealed exercise-induced changes in circulating miRNAs that may serve as biomarkers of training adaptation.

### Discussion

Exercise initiates epigenetic modifications through multiple mechanisms, including calcium signaling, AMPK activation, and NAD+-dependent deacetylase pathways. These signals converge on chromatin-modifying enzymes to alter the accessibility of exercise-responsive genes. The persistence of exercise-induced epigenetic changes appears to depend on continued physical activity, with some marks reverting toward baseline within weeks of detraining.

Research on skeletal muscle stem cells demonstrated that exercise promoted satellite cell activation through Mettl3-mediated m6A methylation of MyoD, enhancing muscle growth and repair in adolescent subjects (Journal of Orthopaedic Translation, 2025). This finding has important implications for understanding exercise benefits across the lifespan, particularly for maintaining muscle mass and function during aging.

### Conclusion

Regular exercise induces widespread epigenetic adaptations that contribute to the metabolic and functional benefits of physical activity. The reversibility of these changes underscores the importance of sustained exercise participation for maintaining health benefits. Epigenetic biomarkers may eventually enable monitoring of training responses and adherence.

## Mechanisms

### ACTN3 and Muscle Fiber Function

ACTN3 protein localizes to the Z-disk of sarcomeres in type II (fast-twitch) skeletal muscle fibers, where it cross-links actin filaments and anchors them to the Z-disk structure. This localization enables efficient force transmission during rapid contractions. The R577X polymorphism causes a premature stop codon, with the X allele producing no functional protein. Complete deficiency of alpha-actinin-3 is relatively common (approximately 1 in 5 individuals of European ancestry), indicating that the null genotype is not detrimental to basic muscle function. This is likely because ACTN2, a closely related protein, can partially compensate for ACTN3 deficiency. However, the absence of ACTN3 shifts muscle fiber characteristics toward slower, more oxidative phenotypes, which may benefit endurance performance while disadvantaging maximal power output.

### ACE and Cardiovascular Adaptation

ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme) regulates the renin-angiotensin system by converting angiotensin I to the potent vasoconstrictor angiotensin II. The I/D polymorphism in intron 16 of the ACE gene influences circulating ACE activity, with the D allele associated with higher activity and the I allele with lower activity. During endurance exercise, the renin-angiotensin system modulates cardiovascular responses, including blood pressure regulation and cardiac remodeling. Lower ACE activity (I allele) may facilitate more pronounced adaptive changes in cardiac structure and function in response to endurance training, improving stroke volume and aerobic capacity. The I allele has been consistently associated with elite endurance performance across multiple populations, though effect sizes are modest.

### PPARGC1A and Mitochondrial Biogenesis

PGC-1α (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha) serves as the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis, coordinating nuclear and mitochondrial genome expression to increase mitochondrial content and oxidative capacity. Exercise activates PGC-1α through multiple signaling pathways, including AMPK, p38 MAPK, and calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase. Activated PGC-1α co-activates transcription factors including NRF-1, NRF-2, and PPARγ, driving expression of mitochondrial proteins and replication of mitochondrial DNA. The Gly482Ser polymorphism in PPARGC1A affects protein stability and transcriptional activity, influencing individual responses to endurance training. Epigenetic regulation through promoter methylation provides an additional control mechanism, with exercise-induced demethylation enhancing PGC-1α expression and mitochondrial function.

### Exercise-Induced Epigenetic Changes

Regular exercise induces epigenetic modifications through several interconnected mechanisms. First, exercise increases NAD+ levels and activates sirtuin deacetylases, which deacetylate histones and PGC-1α to promote transcriptional activity. Second, exercise activates AMPK, which phosphorylates chromatin-remodeling enzymes. Third, calcium signaling through calcineurin and calmodulin-dependent kinases influences histone modification status. Fourth, metabolic intermediates generated during exercise (such as lactate and succinate) can serve as substrates or inhibitors for chromatin-modifying enzymes. Together, these mechanisms produce coordinated changes in DNA methylation and histone modification that enhance expression of exercise-adaptive genes while suppressing genes associated with metabolic dysfunction.

## Practical Applications

### Talent Identification

Genetic information may supplement traditional talent identification methods, particularly when combined with physiological testing and performance metrics. The ACTN3 R577X genotype can provide supplementary information regarding potential for power versus endurance sports, though should not be used as the sole selection criterion. Similarly, ACE I/D genotype may inform predictions about endurance training responsiveness, though environmental factors remain dominant determinants of ultimate performance capacity.

### Training Programming

Knowledge of an individual's genetic profile may enable more precise training prescription. Athletes with the ACTN3 RR genotype may benefit from greater emphasis on power-oriented training and may show superior adaptations to velocity-based training methods. Those with the XX genotype may show greater responsiveness to endurance training and may require additional motivation during high-intensity power training. ACE II genotype individuals may demonstrate superior adaptations to aerobic endurance training, while DD genotype carriers may require modified approaches to optimize cardiovascular adaptations.

### Injury Prevention

Genetic testing for collagen and matrix metalloproteinase gene variants may identify athletes at elevated risk for tendinopathy and ligament injuries. High-risk individuals may benefit from modified training progressions, enhanced recovery protocols, and targeted strengthening exercises to mitigate injury susceptibility. Integration of genetic risk profiles with load monitoring and movement quality assessment enables comprehensive injury prevention strategies.

### Nutrition Optimization

Genetic variants affecting metabolism (including PPARGC1A and other genes involved in nutrient sensing and utilization) may inform personalized nutrition recommendations. However, the interaction between genetics and nutrition for athletic performance remains complex and requires individualized assessment by qualified professionals.

### Caveats and Considerations

Practical application of exercise genetics requires awareness of important limitations. First, genetic variants typically explain small proportions of phenotypic variance, with environmental factors (training history, nutrition, recovery, psychological factors) often having larger effects. Second, gene-environment interactions mean that optimal training approaches cannot be determined from genetic information alone. Third, ethical considerations include privacy protection, avoidance of genetic discrimination, and appropriate use in minors. Fourth, the rapidly evolving nature of the field requires continuous updating of recommendations as new evidence emerges.

## Conclusions

Recent research (2024-2026) has substantially advanced understanding of how genetic variation influences exercise responses and athletic performance. The ACTN3 R577X polymorphism represents one of the most robust associations with power versus endurance athletic status, with the R allele favoring sprint and power performance while the X allele shows associations with endurance capacity. The ACE I/D polymorphism influences cardiovascular adaptation to endurance training, with the I allele generally associated with enhanced training responsiveness and elite endurance performance. PPARGC1A polymorphisms and expression levels modulate mitochondrial biogenesis responses, with implications for aerobic capacity development.

Epigenetic mechanisms, including DNA methylation, histone modifications, and non-coding RNA expression, mediate many of the beneficial effects of regular exercise on metabolic health and physical function. Exercise-induced epigenetic changes are widespread, tissue-specific, and reversible, underscoring the importance of sustained exercise participation.

Gene-based personalized training optimization represents an emerging application of exercise genetics research, with potential benefits for training efficiency, injury prevention, and performance development. However, practical implementation requires careful integration with comprehensive phenotypic assessment, recognition of the multifactorial nature of athletic performance, and adherence to ethical principles.

The individualization of exercise prescription based on genetic information holds promise for optimizing training outcomes, though the current evidence base is insufficient to support definitive, gene-based training recommendations for most athletic populations. Ongoing research, including large-scale studies like MoTrPAC, continues to elucidate the complex interactions between genetic makeup and exercise adaptation, advancing the field toward more precise, personalized approaches to exercise prescription.

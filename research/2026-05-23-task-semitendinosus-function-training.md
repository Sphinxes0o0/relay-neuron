## Background (B)

### Anatomical Structure and Fiber Architecture

The semitendinosus is one of three muscles comprising the hamstring group, positioned medially in the posterior thigh. The muscle derives its name from its unusually long, slender distal tendon, which has historically been used as an autograft for anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) reconstruction surgery. This tendinous structure runs nearly the entire length of the muscle belly, making the semitendinosus appear cord-like and distinguishing it from the broader, more fleshy semimembranosus (IMAIOS, 2023) [1].

**Proximal Attachment**

The semitendinosus originates from the ischial tuberosity via a common tendon shared with the long head of the biceps femoris. This shared origin means that force generated by the semitendinosus during hip extension is transmitted through the same bony attachment as the biceps femoris long head. The tendinous origin is substantial, allowing for secure attachment of the muscle to the pelvis during high-force hip extension activities.

**Distal Attachment and Tendon Length**

The semitendinosus tendon descends along the medial aspect of the posterior thigh, passing posterior to the medial femoral condyle before wrapping around the medial tibial condyle. The tendon inserts onto the medial surface of the proximal tibia, specifically at the "pes anserinus" (goosefoot) region, deep to the sartorius muscle and superficial to the gracilis tendon. This medial insertion site is distinct from the semimembranosus insertion, which is more posterior on the medial tibial condyle.

The semitendinosus tendon is remarkable for its length, measuring approximately 25-30 cm in adult males. This long tendon allows the muscle to act across a large range of hip and knee motion while maintaining effective force transmission. The tendon's length also contributes to the muscle's capacity for elastic energy storage and return during dynamic activities.

**Architectural Properties**

Architectural analysis reveals that the semitendinosus demonstrates characteristics favoring force production and series elastic element contribution. The muscle possesses moderately long fascicles with moderate pennation angles, suggesting a balance between force-generating capacity and architectural efficiency. The long distal tendon acts as a substantial series elastic component, storing and releasing energy during stretch-shortening cycle activities.

The physiological cross-sectional area of the semitendinosus is smaller than the biceps femoris but comparable to the semimembranosus. This moderate size reflects the muscle's role as a contributor to hamstring function rather than the primary force generator.

**Innervation**

The semitendinosus is innervated by the tibial portion of the sciatic nerve (L5, S1, S2 nerve roots), the same innervation as the long head of biceps femoris and semimembranosus. This shared innervation means the medial hamstrings typically demonstrate coordinated activation patterns during functional tasks.

**Vascular Supply**

The arterial supply to the semitendinosus originates from the profunda femoris artery (deep femoral artery) through muscular branches. The proximal portion of the muscle receives supply from the inferior gluteal artery's contribution, while the distal portion is supplied by perforating branches from the profunda femoris. The vascular architecture supports the muscle's metabolic demands during both aerobic and anaerobic activities.

### Function in Hip Extension and Knee Flexion

**Hip Extension Mechanics**

The semitendinosus serves as a primary hip extensor, contributing to the hamstring group's collective action of extending the hip joint. During hip extension, the muscle generates extension torque through its ischial tuberosity origin, working synergistically with the gluteus maximus and the other hamstring muscles.

The semitendinosus demonstrates its greatest hip extension moment when the hip is flexed to moderate angles (20-40 degrees), a position commonly encountered during the late swing phase of running and the initial contact phase of stance. At these angles, the muscle's moment arm at the hip is favorable for force generation.

During running, the semitendinosus shows substantial activation during the late swing phase, contributing to hip extension moment generation and working eccentrically to decelerate hip flexion. While the biceps femoris typically demonstrates higher activation levels during running, the semitendinosus contributes meaningfully to overall hamstring function.

**Knee Flexion Mechanics**

The semitendinosus provides the primary medial knee flexion moment among the hamstring muscles. When acting to flex the knee, the muscle's tendon wraps around the medial tibial condyle, creating a moment arm that is effective throughout a substantial portion of the knee flexion range.

The medial insertion of the semitendinosus gives it a mechanical advantage for generating medial rotation of the tibia when the knee is flexed. This medial rotation component works in opposition to the biceps femoris, which generates lateral tibial rotation. The opposing rotation actions of the medial and lateral hamstrings contribute to rotational knee stability.

The semitendinosus demonstrates substantial activation during hamstring curl exercises, with EMG studies showing that it contributes significantly to overall hamstring activation during both concentric and eccentric phases of knee flexion movements. The muscle's long tendon allows it to operate effectively at various knee flexion angles.

**Biarticular Function and Force Dynamics**

As a biarticular muscle crossing both the hip and knee joints, the semitendinosus demonstrates complex force-length relationships that influence its function during dynamic activities. When the hip is flexed and the knee is extended, the semitendinosus is lengthened across both joints, placing it in a mechanically disadvantaged position for force generation.

This lengthening at both joints is particularly relevant during the late swing phase of running, when the semitendinosus simultaneously controls hip flexion and knee extension while operating at lengthened muscle lengths. The muscle's series elastic element (long distal tendon) may provide some protection against strain injury by absorbing energy during unexpected lengthening.

**Role in Knee Joint Stability**

Beyond its primary functions of hip extension and knee flexion, the semitendinosus contributes to dynamic knee stability through multiple mechanisms:

- **Posterior Tibial Translation Resistance**: The semitendinosus works with the semimembranosus to resist anterior tibial translation, complementing the ACL's static restraint
- **Medial Knee Stabilization**: The muscle's medial insertion provides dynamic support to the medial knee joint
- **Rotational Control**: Medial tibial rotation during knee flexion contributes to overall knee rotational stability

Research examining the semitendinosus's role in ACL function suggests that the muscle may provide meaningful dynamic stabilization, particularly when the ACL is compromised. This finding has implications for rehabilitation following ACL injury.

### ACL Reconstruction Autograft Considerations

The semitendinosus tendon is commonly used as an autograft for ACL reconstruction surgery. This clinical application has generated substantial research on the muscle's functional capacity following tendon harvest.

**Functional Consequences of Tendon Harvest**

Following semitendinosus tendon harvest for ACL reconstruction, several functional changes have been documented:

- Reduced knee flexion strength, particularly at 90 degrees flexion
- Altered hamstring activation patterns during dynamic tasks
- Compensatory increases in biceps femoris activation
- Potential for reduced medial knee stability if gracilis is also harvested

Rehabilitation following semitendinosus harvest should address the resulting strength deficits through progressive hamstring strengthening. The remaining hamstring muscles (biceps femoris and semimembranosus) typically compensate substantially, but residual deficits may persist, particularly in deep knee flexion strength.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Semitendinosus Activation

**Hamstring Curl Variations**

The hamstring curl is the primary isolation exercise for the semitendinosus at the knee joint. EMG studies examining different hamstring curl variations have revealed important activation differences:

**Lying Hamstring Curl**: This exercise produces substantial semitendinosus activation, with EMG studies showing approximately 70-80% MVIC during the concentric phase. The muscle's medial position is optimally aligned with the line of pull during this exercise.

**Seated Hamstring Curl**: The seated variation limits hip extension contribution, potentially focusing more activation on the semitendinosus at the knee joint. Research suggests comparable or slightly reduced semitendinosus activation compared to the lying variation.

**Stability Ball Hamstring Curl**: This challenging variation requires the semitendinosus to work at lengthened positions while maintaining hip extension against gravity. The exercise produces high overall hamstring EMG, with the semitendinosus contributing substantially to the overall activation pattern.

**Nordic Hamstring Exercise**

While the Nordic hamstring exercise is typically associated with biceps femoris activation, the semitendinosus also demonstrates substantial engagement during the eccentric lowering phase. Research indicates that the semitendinosus is activated to approximately 80-100% MVIC during the eccentric phase of the Nordic curl, making this exercise valuable for training both the lateral and medial hamstrings.

The lengthened muscle position during the Nordic curl provides the same injury-prevention benefits for the semitendinosus as for the biceps femoris, training the muscle at the lengths where strains typically occur.

**Romanian Deadlift (RDL)**

The RDL emphasizes hip hinge mechanics and produces substantial semitendinosus activation during the eccentric lowering phase. The hip-dominant position stretches the semitendinosus across the hip joint, activating it throughout the movement. EMG research suggests that the semitendinosus contributes approximately equally to the biceps femoris during the RDL eccentric phase.

The RDL is particularly effective for training the semitendinosus at lengthened hip positions, complementing the Nordic curl's emphasis on lengthened knee positions. Together, these exercises provide comprehensive coverage of the semitendinosus's functional range.

**Single-Leg RDL**

The single-leg RDL variation increases the demand on the semitendinosus for hip stabilization while maintaining the knee flexion component. This exercise is particularly effective for identifying and addressing lateral imbalances between limbs.

### EMG Activation Levels

Fine-wire EMG studies have provided activation data for the semitendinosus during various exercises:

- Lying hamstring curl (concentric): 70-80% MVIC
- Seated hamstring curl: 65-75% MVIC
- Stability ball hamstring curl: 75-85% MVIC
- Nordic hamstring exercise (eccentric): 80-100% MVIC
- Romanian deadlift (eccentric): 65-80% MVIC
- Single-leg RDL: 70-85% MVIC

These values indicate that the semitendinosus is effectively recruited across a range of exercises, with highest activation during exercises that challenge knee flexion specifically.

### Training Adaptations Following Tendon Harvest

**Hamstring Strength After ACL Reconstruction**

Research examining hamstring strength following semitendinosus tendon harvest for ACL reconstruction reveals consistent patterns:

- Concentric knee flexion strength deficits of 10-20% persist even after rehabilitation
- Eccentric strength deficits may be more pronounced than concentric deficits
- The semimembranosus compensates substantially, increasing its activation
- Biceps femoris also demonstrates compensatory increases in activation

A 2024 study found that athletes returning to sport following semitendinosus harvest showed residual weakness in deep knee flexion (beyond 90 degrees), suggesting that the semitendinosus contributes uniquely to terminal knee flexion strength.

**Rehabilitation Approaches**

Effective rehabilitation following semitendinosus harvest includes:

- Early activation exercises targeting remaining hamstring muscles
- Progressive resistance training beginning with isometric exercises
- Advancement to concentric exercises at 4-6 weeks
- Eccentric training introduction at 8-12 weeks
- Sport-specific progression beginning at 4-6 months

Studies suggest that the remaining hamstring muscles can compensate sufficiently for most functional activities, though athletes may demonstrate subtle deficits in tasks requiring high knee flexion moments.

### Semitendinosus vs. Semimembranosus Activation

Research comparing medial hamstring activation reveals that the semitendinosus and semimembranosus demonstrate similar activation patterns during most exercises, though subtle differences exist:

- During knee flexion exercises, the semitendinosus may demonstrate higher activation in the early range (0-45 degrees)
- During hip extension exercises, both muscles contribute approximately equally
- During the Nordic curl, the semitendinosus shows slightly higher activation than the semimembranosus

These similarities suggest that exercises targeting the medial hamstrings effectively recruit both muscles, though specific exercise modifications may emphasize one over the other if desired.

## Interpretation (I)

### Programming Recommendations for Semitendinosus Development

**Exercise Selection Strategy**

Based on available evidence, the following recommendations address semitendinosus-specific development:

1. **Primary Knee Flexion Exercise (40-50% of medial hamstring volume):**
   - Lying or seated hamstring curl
   - Stability ball hamstring curl for increased challenge
   - 3-4 sets of 8-12 repetitions

2. **Primary Hip-Dominant Exercise (30-40% of volume):**
   - Romanian deadlift with controlled eccentric
   - Single-leg RDL for increased demand
   - 3-4 sets of 6-10 repetitions at 70-80% 1RM

3. **Eccentric Emphasis (20-30% of volume):**
   - Nordic hamstring exercise
   - RDL with 3-second eccentric phase
   - 3-4 sets of 6-8 repetitions

**Training Frequency and Volume**

Medial hamstring training should follow similar frequency recommendations to the overall hamstring group:

- 2-3 sessions per week minimum
- 48-72 hours between intensive sessions
- Progressive overload through load, volume, or complexity increases

**Special Considerations for Athletes with Previous Semitendinosus Harvest**

Athletes who have undergone semitendinosus tendon harvest require modified training approaches:

- Greater emphasis on compound hip-dominant movements
- Acceptance of potential terminal knee flexion strength deficits
- Focus on functional outcomes rather than normalized strength values
- Regular assessment of bilateral strength symmetry

### Injury Prevention Applications

**Hamstring Strain Prevention**

While the biceps femoris is most commonly injured during running, the semitendinosus is also vulnerable to strain injuries, particularly during activities involving simultaneous hip flexion and knee extension. Eccentric training protocols should address both medial and lateral hamstring vulnerabilities.

**Medial Knee Stability**

For athletes with medial knee instability or valgus tendency, semitendinosus strengthening may provide meaningful dynamic stabilization. The muscle's medial insertion and medial rotation action help counteract lateral knee structures that are typically emphasized.

**ACL Injury Risk Reduction**

The semitendinosus's role in resisting anterior tibial translation suggests that strengthening may provide protective effects for the ACL. Research examining hamstring strength and ACL injury risk suggests that stronger hamstrings are associated with reduced ACL injury rates, particularly in female athletes.

### Rehabilitation Applications

**Following Hamstring Strain**

Rehabilitation following medial hamstring strain should follow a progressive protocol:

1. Pain management and protected range of motion
2. Isometric activation exercises
3. Short-range knee flexion against light resistance
4. Full-range knee flexion and hip extension
5. Eccentric loading as tissue tolerance allows
6. Sport-specific movement integration
7. Criterion-based return to sport

**Following ACL Reconstruction**

When semitendinosus has been harvested, rehabilitation focuses on:

- Protecting the graft harvest site in early weeks
- Gradually loading the remaining hamstring muscles
- Addressing quadriceps-hamstring balance
- Restoring full range of motion
- Progressive strengthening emphasizing functional positions
- Sport-specific conditioning before return to play

### Functional Integration

**Running Mechanics**

The semitendinosus contributes to overall hamstring function during running, with activation patterns that complement the biceps femoris. The muscle's medial insertion provides balance to the lateral force contributions of the biceps femoris, contributing to overall posterior thigh force production.

**Cutting and Pivoting**

During cutting movements, the semitendinosus works to decelerate leg swing and provide medial knee stability. Athletes in sports requiring rapid directional changes benefit from strong medial hamstring function for optimal performance and injury prevention.

**Kicking and Change of Direction**

The semitendinosus's role in hip extension and knee flexion makes it important for kicking mechanics. The muscle contributes to the swing phase of kicking and assists with rapid hip extension during the kick execution.

## Conclusions

1. The semitendinosus is a biarticular muscle providing hip extension and knee flexion with medial rotation capability

2. Hamstring curl exercises effectively target the semitendinosus, with highest activation during knee flexion isolation

3. The semitendinosus tendon is commonly harvested for ACL reconstruction, resulting in residual strength deficits that require targeted rehabilitation

4. Eccentric training benefits the semitendinosus by increasing fascicle length and training at injury-prone lengths

5. The semitendinosus contributes to dynamic knee stability, particularly for medial joint protection and anterior tibial translation resistance

6. Comprehensive hamstring training should address both medial and lateral hamstring functions through varied exercise selection

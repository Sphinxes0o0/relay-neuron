# Erector Spinae: Function and Training (Iliocostalis, Longissimus, Spinalis)

## Background (B)

### Anatomy and Segmental Innervation

The erector spinae is a large muscle group comprising three primary muscles running bilaterally along the length of the spine. The iliocostalis, the most lateral component, originates from the iliac crest and sacrum and inserts onto the angles of the ribs and cervical transverse processes. The longissimus, the middle and largest component, extends from the transverse processes of lumbar through cervical vertebrae to the mastoid process. The spinalis, the most medial and smallest component, connects spinous processes from the sacrum to the axis (Park et al., 2014).

Segmental innervation derives from the dorsal rami of spinal nerves, with each muscle receiving fibers from multiple spinal levels. The longissimus thoracis et lumborum receives innervation from the dorsal rami of L1-L5, while the iliocostalis lumborum is supplied by L2-S1 dorsal rami. This segmental innervation allows for graded control of spinal extension and rotation across multiple vertebral levels.

The erector spinae muscles demonstrate complex architectural organization. The fascicles are oriented obliquely, creating functional divisions that permit both pure extension and combined movements. The longissimus possesses the largest physiological cross-sectional area among the three muscles, correlating with its primary role in producing extensor moments across the lumbar and thoracic spine.

### Postural vs Movement Functions

The erector spinae serves dual functional roles that differ substantially between postural maintenance and dynamic movement contexts. During postural tasks, the erector spinae demonstrates continuous low-level activation patterns that maintain vertical alignment against gravitational loads. Research using intramuscular and surface electromyography demonstrates that erector spinae activity increases proportionally with trunk inclination angle, reflecting the need to counteract increasing gravitational moments as the torso moves forward from neutral (Park et al., 2014).

During dynamic movements, the erector spinae demonstrates task-dependent activation patterns. During gait, the muscles show phasic activity during late swing and early stance phases, contributing to trunk stabilization as the lower extremities generate ground reaction forces. During lifting tasks, the erector spinae activates in anticipation of load perturbation, demonstrating feedforward control mechanisms that prepare the spine for upcoming mechanical demands.

The transition between postural and movement functions involves complex coordination with abdominal muscles. Studies examining trunk muscle activation patterns during sitting balance tasks have identified individuals classified as "balancers" who demonstrate greater symmetrical activation of opposite muscle groups, suggesting adaptive strategies for maintaining spinal stability (ScienceDirect, 2005).

### Role in Spinal Stability

The erector spinae contributes critically to spinal stability through both passive and active mechanisms. Passive stability arises from the anatomical arrangement of the muscles relative to the vertebral column, creating an extensor moment arm that resists flexion moments generated by body weight and external loads. Active stability involves the muscles' capacity to generate tension that increases spinal stiffness, thereby enhancing the spine's resistance to perturbation.

Biomechanical models of spinal stability demonstrate that antagonistic muscle pairs, including the erector spinae and abdominal muscles, exhibit self-stabilizing behavior. Using Ljapunov's theory, researchers have shown that the geometric arrangement of these muscles with respect to the instantaneous center of rotation provides inherent stability during various loading conditions. The antagonistic relationships between paraspinal and abdominal muscles create force couples that maintain spinal alignment under physiological loads (ResearchGate, 2005).

Recent computational simulations examining deep core muscle weakness have highlighted the compensatory role of the superficial longissimus thoracis. When deep core muscles including the multifidus are weakened, the superficial erector spinae demonstrates increased activation as a compensatory strategy. However, this compensation comes with elevated spinal loading, potentially increasing injury risk over time (ScienceDirect, 2018).

## Results (R)

### EMG Studies on Back Extension Exercises

Electromyographic investigations of back extension exercises reveal differential activation patterns across the erector spinae components. During the prone trunk extension exercise, surface EMG studies demonstrate high activation of the thoracic erector spinae, with the longissimus thoracis showing greater activity than the iliocostalis. However, individuals with slouched thoracic posture demonstrate altered muscle activation patterns, with reduced thoracic erector spinae activity and altered thoracic-lumbar ratio compared to those with neutral posture (Park et al., 2014).

The Sorensen test (Biering-Sørensen test) remains the gold standard for evaluating back extensor endurance. This isometric test requires subjects to maintain a horizontal trunk position against gravity while prone, with the pelvis and lower extremities stabilized. Studies have established that performance on this test correlates with the potential for developing low back pain, making it a valuable clinical assessment tool. Research using modified versions of the test has demonstrated that individuals with chronic low back pain show earlier onset of muscle fatigue and reduced endurance times compared to asymptomatic controls (ResearchGate, 2007).

Loading condition studies during various back extension exercises demonstrate that spinal compression forces vary substantially depending on exercise execution. At L4-L5 levels, compressive loads during unsupported back extensions can exceed 3000 N, while lumbar hyperextension exercises performed with proper pelvic support demonstrate significantly lower loading profiles. These findings have important implications for exercise prescription in populations with spinal pathology (BMC Sports Science, 2017).

### Deadlift and Good Morning Effects

The deadlift represents a compound movement that places substantial demands on the erector spinae. Biomechanical analyses reveal that peak erector spinae activation occurs during the initial phase of the lift when the bar is lifted from the floor, with activity decreasing as the bar passes knee height. The lumbar erector spinae demonstrates higher activation during conventional deadlift compared to sumo style, reflecting differences in trunk inclination angles between the two variations.

Research examining muscle activation during good morning exercises demonstrates that this movement preferentially targets the lumbar erector spinae and hamstrings. Surface EMG studies show that the good morning produces significantly higher erector spinae activation relative to gluteal muscles compared to Romanian deadlifts, making it useful for targeted back extensor training. However, the movement also generates substantial compressive loads on the lumbar spine, necessitating proper technique and appropriate loading progressions.

The posterior chain muscles, including the erector spinae, demonstrate coordinated activity during hip extension movements. Studies comparing leg curl, good morning, glute-ham raise, and Romanian deadlift exercises demonstrate that the good morning and glute-ham raise produce the highest erector spinae activation among hamstring-dominant movements. However, co-activation patterns with the abdominal muscles are essential for maintaining spinal stability during these movements (ResearchGate, 2013).

### Optimal Training for Back Health

Recent research has identified several principles for optimal back extensor training. A 2025 study published in Nature Scientific Reports investigated the effects of aquatic exercise versus standard care on paraspinal muscle morphology in individuals with chronic low back pain. The 10-week intervention demonstrated that aquatic exercise produced significant improvements in multifidus and erector spinae muscle volume at multiple lumbar levels (L1-L2 through L5-S1), while also reducing fatty infiltration. Importantly, the aquatic environment reduces spinal loading during exercise, allowing individuals with pain-related fear to participate more comfortably (Nature Scientific Reports, 2025).

A 2024 study examining paraspinal muscle oxygenation and mechanical efficiency found that individuals with chronic low back pain demonstrate reduced tissue oxygenation and mechanical efficiency during trunk extension exercise compared to asymptomatic controls. These findings suggest that impaired muscle metabolism may contribute to the functional limitations observed in chronic low back pain populations. The study also found positive correlations between tissue oxygenation and exercise-induced pain exacerbation, indicating that muscle oxygenation may be related to persisting and disabling low back pain (Scientific Reports, 2024).

The association between lumbar paraspinal muscle quality and activation patterns has been further elucidated by a 2025 European Spine Journal study. This cross-sectional analysis of 102 participants found that muscle quality parameters, including cross-sectional area, functional cross-sectional area, fat area, and fat infiltration, significantly influence EMG activity during standardized lifting tasks. Individuals with chronic low back pain demonstrated altered activation patterns in both erector spinae and multifidus muscles, suggesting that muscle quality changes contribute to impaired motor control in these populations (European Spine Journal, 2025).

### Evidence-Based Recommendations

Based on the reviewed literature, the following evidence-based recommendations emerge for training the erector spinae while promoting back health:

First, exercise progression should emphasize gradual loading with attention to individual tolerance levels. Research indicates that both insufficient and excessive training can lead to negative outcomes, with the former failing to stimulate adaptive responses and the latter potentially contributing to tissue overload.

Second, variety in exercise selection appears beneficial for comprehensive muscle development. The evidence suggests that combining exercises targeting different portions of the erector spinae complex, from compound movements like deadlifts to isolated exercises like prone trunk extensions, provides comprehensive stimulus for muscle adaptation.

Third, the integration of core stabilization exercises alongside back extension training is essential. The coordinated activity between erector spinae and abdominal muscles during functional tasks highlights the importance of training these muscle groups in combination rather than isolation.

## Interpretation (I)

### Low Back Pain Prevention

The relationship between erector spinae function and low back pain prevention is multifaceted. Evidence indicates that both muscle endurance and muscle quality are important factors in preventing the onset and recurrence of low back pain. Longitudinal research tracking individuals from childhood to early adulthood has demonstrated that persistently high intramuscular adipose tissue levels in the lumbar multifidus at ages 13 and 16 are associated with greater odds of developing low back pain (OR = 2.98, 95% CI: 1.17-7.58), suggesting that early muscle quality changes may predispose individuals to future pain episodes (Nature Scientific Reports, 2022).

The finding that paraspinal muscle oxygenation is reduced in chronic low back pain populations has important implications for prevention strategies. If impaired muscle metabolism contributes to pain development, interventions targeting muscle blood flow and metabolic capacity may prove beneficial for prevention. Regular exercise that promotes muscle perfusion, combined with appropriate loading to stimulate tissue adaptation, represents a rational approach to maintaining paraspinal muscle health.

Postural considerations also emerge from the literature. Individuals with altered thoracic posture demonstrate different muscle activation patterns during back extension exercises, suggesting that postural assessment should be incorporated into exercise prescription. Correcting underlying postural dysfunction may improve the effectiveness of back extensor training and reduce injury risk.

### Training Programming

For athletic populations, the evidence supports a structured approach to back extensor training that includes both strength and endurance components. The Sorensen test provides a useful benchmark for assessing back extensor endurance, with training programs demonstrating improvements in both endurance time and EMG fatigue parameters following targeted interventions.

The principle of specificity suggests that training should target the relevant muscle groups through movements that mimic functional demands. For athletes requiring spinal stability during complex movements, training should progress from isolated exercises to compound movements that challenge coordination between paraspinal and other core muscles. The deadlift and its variations remain foundational exercises for developing functional back extensor strength, provided they are performed with appropriate technique and progressive loading.

For rehabilitation populations, the 2025 aquatic exercise study provides compelling evidence for the benefits of reduced-load training environments. The ability to exercise without substantial spinal compression may be particularly valuable for individuals with pain-related fear or those early in the rehabilitation process. As tolerance improves, gradual transition to land-based exercises with progressive loading can maintain the gains achieved in the aquatic environment.

Training frequency recommendations from the literature suggest that back extensor training should occur 2-3 times per week to achieve meaningful adaptations. However, the optimal frequency depends on training status, with more advanced individuals potentially requiring higher frequencies or volumes to continue progressing.

### Future Research Directions

Several important questions remain unanswered in the literature and represent opportunities for future research. First, the longitudinal effects of different exercise interventions on paraspinal muscle morphology and function require further investigation. While cross-sectional studies have identified relationships between muscle quality and pain status, causality cannot be determined without prospective designs.

Second, the optimal parameters for back extensor training, including intensity, volume, and exercise selection, remain to be clearly established. Randomized controlled trials comparing different training approaches would provide valuable information for exercise prescription.

Third, the interaction between genetic factors and training responses in paraspinal muscles warrants investigation. Understanding individual differences in trainability may eventually allow for personalized exercise recommendations based on individual characteristics.

Fourth, the mechanisms underlying the relationship between paraspinal muscle changes and low back pain development require further elucidation. Whether muscle changes precede pain development or result from pain-related disuse remains unclear from current evidence.

Finally, the integration of emerging technologies, including real-time ultrasound imaging biofeedback and neuromuscular electrical stimulation, into back extensor training programs represents an important frontier. These technologies may enhance the ability to selectively activate specific muscle groups and monitor training adaptations over time.

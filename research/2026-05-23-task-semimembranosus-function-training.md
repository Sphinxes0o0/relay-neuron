## Background (B)

### Anatomical Structure and Fiber Architecture

The semimembranosus is the most medial and deepest of the three hamstring muscles, positioned beneath the semitendinosus on the posterior thigh. Despite sharing its name's membrane-like origin tendon characteristic, the semimembranosus is architecturally and functionally distinct from its medial neighbor (IMAIOS, 2023) [1].

**Proximal Attachment**

The semimembranosus originates from the ischial tuberosity via a broad, thin tendon that contrasts sharply with the round, cord-like tendon of the semitendinosus. This expanded origin tendon is approximately 10-15 cm in length and descends from the ischial tuberosity to the muscle belly. The tendon's broad attachment area allows for substantial force transmission from the muscle to the pelvis during hip extension activities.

The semimembranosus shares its ischial origin with the semitendinosus and biceps femoris long head, though its tendon is anatomically distinct and positioned posterolaterally to the semitendinosus origin. This proximal arrangement influences the muscle's moment arm at the hip joint.

**Distal Attachment and Insertion Complex**

The semimembranosus inserts onto a complex insertion site on the posteromedial aspect of the medial tibial condyle. Unlike the semitendinosus, which inserts at the pes anserinus, the semimembranosus has a more posterior insertion that provides direct dynamic stabilization to the medial knee joint.

Recent anatomical research has identified a distal "expansion" or "reflection" of the semimembranosus tendon that wraps around the medial tibial condyle. This expansion increases the muscle's effective moment arm for knee flexion and may contribute to its stabilizing function. The tendon's multiple attachment sites create a fan-like arrangement that distributes force across the posteromedial knee structures.

Additional expansions of the semimembranosus tendon contribute to the oblique popliteal ligament and the fascia covering the popliteal fossa. These connections link the semimembranosus to posterior knee structures, potentially enhancing its stabilizing role.

**Architectural Properties**

Architectural analysis reveals that the semimembranosus demonstrates characteristics favoring force production. The muscle possesses relatively short fascicles compared to the biceps femoris, with moderate pennation angles that suggest a focus on force-generating capacity rather than excursion capability.

The physiological cross-sectional area of the semimembranosus is comparable to the semitendinosus, both being smaller than the biceps femoris. This moderate size reflects the muscle's role as a contributor to medial hamstring function rather than the primary force generator of the hamstring group.

**Innervation**

The semimembranosus is innervated by the tibial portion of the sciatic nerve (L5, S1, S2 nerve roots), the same innervation as the other medial hamstrings. This shared innervation pattern ensures coordinated activation of the semitendinosus and semimembranosus during functional tasks.

**Vascular Supply**

The arterial supply to the semimembranosus originates from the profunda femoris artery (deep femoral artery) through muscular branches. Perforating arteries from the femoral artery provide additional supply to the distal muscle belly. The vascular architecture supports the muscle's metabolic demands during dynamic activities.

### Function in Hip Extension and Knee Flexion

**Hip Extension Mechanics**

The semimembranosus serves as a primary hip extensor, contributing to the collective hip extension moment generated by the hamstring group. During hip extension, the muscle generates force through its ischial tuberosity origin, working synergistically with the gluteus maximus and other hamstring muscles.

The semimembranosus demonstrates its greatest hip extension moment at intermediate hip flexion angles, with optimal force production occurring when the hip is flexed approximately 20-40 degrees from full extension. At this position, the muscle's moment arm at the hip is favorable for force generation during activities such as rising from a seated position or generating propulsive force during running.

**Knee Flexion Mechanics**

The semimembranosus provides the most posteromedial knee flexion moment among the hamstring muscles, giving it unique mechanical advantages for medial knee stabilization. When acting to flex the knee, the muscle's posteromedial insertion position creates a moment arm that contributes substantially to posterior drawer resistance.

The semimembranosus's distal expansion wrapping around the medial tibial condyle may increase its effective moment arm for knee flexion, particularly in the terminal range of flexion. This anatomical feature suggests that the semimembranosus contributes uniquely to deep knee flexion strength.

**Medial Rotation Function**

The semimembranosus generates medial rotation of the tibia when the knee is flexed, working in concert with the semitendinosus to produce medial tibial rotation. This action opposes the lateral tibial rotation produced by the biceps femoris, contributing to overall rotational knee balance.

The medial rotation function of the semimembranosus is particularly relevant during the screw-home mechanism of the knee, where tibial medial rotation accompanies terminal knee extension. The muscle's eccentric control of this rotation may be important for preventing rotational knee injuries.

**Biarticular Force Dynamics**

As a biarticular muscle, the semimembranosus demonstrates force-length characteristics that influence its function during dynamic activities. When the hip is flexed and the knee is extended, the muscle is lengthened across both joints, placing it in a mechanically challenged position.

This lengthening at both joints is particularly relevant during the late swing phase of running, when the semimembranosus must simultaneously control hip flexion and knee extension while operating at lengthened muscle lengths. While the biceps femoris typically demonstrates higher activation during running, the semimembranosus contributes meaningfully to overall hamstring function.

**Role in Knee Joint Stability**

The semimembranosus is increasingly recognized for its role in dynamic knee stabilization, particularly for the medial compartment:

**Posterior Knee Stability**: The semimembranosus works with the semitendinosus to resist anterior tibial translation, providing dynamic support that complements the ACL's static restraint function. Research suggests that the medial hamstrings may contribute meaningfully to knee stability, particularly when the ACL is compromised.

**Medial Compartment Load Distribution**: The semimembranosus's posteromedial insertion helps distribute load across the medial compartment during weight-bearing activities. This function may be particularly important during activities involving medial knee stress, such as pivoting or cutting.

**Popliteal Muscle Coordination**: The semimembranosus has anatomical connections to the popliteal muscle, which itself is important for knee kinematic control. This connection suggests a functional relationship that may influence overall knee stability.

### Distinction from Semitendinosus

While the semimembranosus and semitendinosus share several functional characteristics, important differences exist:

| Characteristic | Semimembranosus | Semitendinosus |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Tendon shape | Broad, thin | Round, cord-like |
| Insertion | Posteromedial tibial condyle | Pes anserinus (medial tibia) |
| Fascicle length | Shorter | Longer |
| Deep position | Deepest medial hamstring | Superficial to semimembranosus |
| Popliteal connection | Yes | No |
| ACL stabilization | Potentially greater | Lesser |

These anatomical differences suggest that the two medial hamstrings may have partially distinct functional roles, with the semimembranosus potentially more important for deep knee flexion strength and posterior knee stabilization.

## Results (R)

### Best Exercises for Semimembranosus Activation

**Hamstring Curl Variations**

The hamstring curl is the primary isolation exercise for the semimembranosus at the knee joint. EMG studies examining different hamstring curl variations reveal important activation patterns:

**Lying Hamstring Curl**: This exercise produces substantial semimembranosus activation, with research indicating approximately 65-75% MVIC during the concentric phase. The posteromedial insertion of the semimembranosus is aligned with the line of pull during lying curl variations.

**Seated Hamstring Curl**: The seated variation limits hip extension contribution, potentially focusing more activation on the semimembranosus at the knee joint. Some research suggests that the seated position may reduce semimembranosus activation compared to the lying position due to altered hip angle.

**Stability Ball Hamstring Curl**: This challenging variation requires the semimembranosus to work at lengthened positions while maintaining hip extension against gravity. The exercise produces high overall hamstring EMG, with the semimembranosus contributing substantially to the overall activation pattern.

**Nordic Hamstring Exercise**

While the Nordic hamstring exercise is typically emphasized for biceps femoris training, the semimembranosus also demonstrates substantial engagement during the eccentric lowering phase. Research indicates that the semimembranosus is activated to approximately 70-90% MVIC during the eccentric phase of the Nordic curl.

The lengthened muscle position during the Nordic curl provides injury-prevention benefits for the semimembranosus by training the muscle at the lengths where strains typically occur. This eccentric training effect is a key component of comprehensive hamstring injury prevention programs.

**Romanian Deadlift (RDL)**

The RDL emphasizes hip hinge mechanics and produces substantial semimembranosus activation during the eccentric lowering phase. The hip-dominant position stretches the semimembranosus across the hip joint, activating it throughout the movement. Research suggests that the semimembranosus contributes approximately equally to the biceps femoris during the RDL eccentric phase.

The RDL is particularly effective for training the semimembranosus at lengthened hip positions. The combination of hip extension demand and the lengthened muscle position provides a comprehensive stimulus for the muscle's structural adaptations.

**Single-Leg RDL**

The single-leg RDL variation increases the demand on the semimembranosus for hip stabilization while maintaining the knee flexion component. This exercise is particularly effective for identifying and addressing lateral imbalances between limbs.

### EMG Activation Levels

Fine-wire EMG studies have provided activation data for the semimembranosus during various exercises:

- Lying hamstring curl (concentric): 65-75% MVIC
- Seated hamstring curl: 55-70% MVIC
- Stability ball hamstring curl: 70-80% MVIC
- Nordic hamstring exercise (eccentric): 70-90% MVIC
- Romanian deadlift (eccentric): 60-75% MVIC
- Single-leg RDL: 65-80% MVIC

These values indicate that the semimembranosus is effectively recruited across a range of exercises, with highest activation during exercises that challenge knee flexion at lengthened positions.

### Medial vs. Lateral Hamstring Activation

Research comparing medial and lateral hamstring activation reveals important patterns:

**During Knee Flexion Exercises**: The medial hamstrings (semitendinosus and semimembranosus) demonstrate higher activation than the biceps femoris during isolated knee flexion, likely due to their more favorable moment arms for this action.

**During Hip Extension Exercises**: Both medial and lateral hamstrings contribute substantially, with biceps femoris typically showing higher activation due to its larger physiological cross-sectional area.

**During the Nordic Curl**: Both medial and lateral hamstrings demonstrate high activation, with some research suggesting preferential biceps femoris activation during the eccentric lowering phase.

### Semimembranosus and Knee Stability

**ACL Function and Medial Hamstrings**

Research examining the semimembranosus's role in knee stability suggests that the muscle provides meaningful dynamic stabilization to the ACL. Studies using various methodologies have demonstrated that:

- The semimembranosus activates in response to anterior tibial translation, suggesting a protective reflex
- Individuals with stronger medial hamstrings demonstrate greater knee stability during dynamic tasks
- The semimembranosus may compensate partially for ACL deficiency during functional activities

A 2024 study found that hamstring co-contraction significantly reduces anterior tibial translation, with the medial hamstrings contributing approximately 40-50% of the total hamstring-induced anterior drawer resistance. This finding supports the inclusion of medial hamstring strengthening in ACL injury prevention and rehabilitation programs.

**Medial Knee Injury Prevention**

The semimembranosus's posteromedial insertion gives it unique mechanical advantages for resisting valgus forces at the knee. Athletes with weak medial hamstrings may be at increased risk for medial knee injuries during pivoting and cutting activities.

Research examining the relationship between medial hamstring strength and medial knee injury risk suggests that strengthening the semimembranosus may provide protective effects, particularly for athletes in sports involving lateral direction changes.

### Injury Patterns Specific to Semimembranosus

**Proximal Tendinopathy**

The semimembranosus origin at the ischial tuberosity is a site of proximal tendinopathy, often presenting as deep posterior gluteal or thigh pain. This condition is less common than biceps femoris proximal injuries but may occur in athletes with repetitive hip extension demands.

**Distal Tendon Pathology**

The semimembranosus distal tendon is susceptible to degeneration at its insertion on the medial tibial condyle. This condition may present as posteromedial knee pain and may be confused with medial meniscal pathology.

**Muscle Belly Strains**

While less common than biceps femoris strains, the semimembranosus muscle belly can sustain strain injuries during high-speed running or stretching activities. These injuries typically present with localized posterior thigh pain and may be confirmed with MRI.

## Interpretation (I)

### Programming Recommendations for Semimembranosus Development

**Exercise Selection Strategy**

Based on available evidence, the following recommendations address semimembranosus-specific development:

1. **Primary Knee Flexion Exercise (40-50% of medial hamstring volume):**
   - Lying hamstring curl with focus on full range of motion
   - Stability ball hamstring curl for increased lengthened challenge
   - 3-4 sets of 8-12 repetitions

2. **Primary Hip-Dominant Exercise (30-40% of volume):**
   - Romanian deadlift with controlled eccentric phase
   - Single-leg RDL for unilateral development
   - 3-4 sets of 6-10 repetitions at 70-80% 1RM

3. **Eccentric Emphasis (20-30% of volume):**
   - Nordic hamstring exercise
   - RDL with 3-5 second eccentric phase
   - 3-4 sets of 6-8 repetitions

**Training Frequency and Volume**

Medial hamstring training should follow similar frequency recommendations to the overall hamstring group:

- 2-3 sessions per week minimum
- 48-72 hours between intensive sessions
- Progressive overload through load, volume, or complexity increases
- Emphasis on the eccentric phase for structural adaptations

**Medial-Lateral Balance**

While both medial and lateral hamstrings require attention, the emphasis should be on comprehensive posterior chain development rather than medial-specific isolation. Exercises that recruit both medial and lateral hamstrings (RDL, Nordic curl) should form the foundation of training programs.

### Knee Stability Applications

**ACL Injury Prevention**

For athletes at risk for ACL injuries, medial hamstring strengthening may provide meaningful protective effects:

- Include hamstring curl exercises to develop medial hamstring strength
- Emphasize hamstring co-contraction during dynamic activities
- Address any medial hamstring strength deficits identified through screening
- Consider sport-specific tasks that challenge medial hamstring function

**Return to Sport Following ACL Reconstruction**

Following ACL reconstruction, medial hamstring function should be assessed and addressed:

- Screen for residual medial hamstring strength deficits
- Include targeted medial hamstring exercises in rehabilitation
- Progress to sport-specific tasks that challenge knee stability
- Use criterion-based progression rather than time-based return decisions

**Medial Knee Pain**

For individuals with medial knee pain, semimembranosus strengthening may provide relief:

- Strengthen through hamstring curl variations with appropriate loading
- Include hip-dominant movements that challenge medial hamstring function
- Progress to functional positions that replicate daily or sport demands

### Rehabilitation Applications

**Following Hamstring Strain**

Rehabilitation following medial hamstring strain should follow a progressive protocol:

1. Pain management and protected range of motion (days 1-7)
2. Isometric activation exercises targeting all hamstrings (weeks 1-2)
3. Short-range knee flexion against light resistance (weeks 2-3)
4. Full-range knee flexion and hip extension exercises (weeks 3-4)
5. Eccentric loading as tissue tolerance allows (weeks 4-6)
6. Sport-specific movement integration (weeks 6-12)
7. Criterion-based return to sport assessment

**Special Considerations for Deep Knee Flexion**

For athletes requiring strong deep knee flexion (e.g., wrestling, martial arts, gymnastics):

- Emphasize hamstring curl exercises through full range
- Include exercises that challenge medial hamstring at terminal flexion
- Progress cautiously to avoid overloading healing tissue
- Assess bilateral symmetry before return to full activity

### Functional Integration

**Running Mechanics**

The semimembranosus contributes to overall hamstring function during running, with activation patterns that complement the other hamstring muscles. The muscle's posteromedial insertion provides balance to the lateral force contributions of the biceps femoris, contributing to overall posterior thigh force production.

**Cutting and Pivoting**

During cutting movements, the semimembranosus works to decelerate leg swing, provide medial knee stability, and control rotational forces. Athletes in sports requiring rapid directional changes benefit from strong medial hamstring function for optimal performance and injury prevention.

**Posterior Chain Coordination**

The semimembranosus functions as part of the posterior chain, coordinating with the gluteus maximus, biceps femoris, and gastrocnemius. Training should address posterior chain integration through compound movements rather than isolated medial hamstring work.

## Conclusions

1. The semimembranosus is the deepest and most posteromedial of the hamstring muscles, with unique anatomical features including distal tendon expansions around the medial tibial condyle

2. The semimembranosus contributes to hip extension, knee flexion, and medial tibial rotation, with emerging evidence for its role in dynamic knee stabilization

3. Hamstring curl and RDL exercises effectively target the semimembranosus, with highest activation during knee flexion isolation and hip hinge movements

4. The semimembranosus likely contributes to ACL protection through dynamic resistance to anterior tibial translation

5. Eccentric training benefits the semimembranosus by increasing fascicle length and training at injury-prone lengths

6. Comprehensive hamstring training should address both medial and lateral hamstring functions through varied exercise selection

7. The semimembranosus may have distinct functional roles from the semitendinosus, particularly for deep knee flexion and posterior medial knee stabilization

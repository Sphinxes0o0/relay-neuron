# Combat Sports Energy Systems: Aerobic, Anaerobic, and Autonomic Adaptations in Boxing and MMA

**Research Date:** 2026-06-03
**Type:** Sport-Specific Research Batch A2 — Topic 4.3 Benefits
**Discipline:** Combat Sports (Boxing, MMA, Taekwondo, Karate)

---

## Abstract / 摘要

Combat sports impose one of the most metabolically heterogeneous demands in competitive athletics. A single MMA bout (3 × 5 min) requires repeated maximal-effort striking, grappling, and clinching interspersed with sub-maximal recovery, while an Olympic boxing match (3 × 3 min) similarly toggles between alactic, lactic, and aerobic energy systems. The present B-M-R-I synthesis aggregates four 2024–2026 peer-reviewed studies (PubMed-indexed; n = 12–40 athletes per study) to characterize the modern evidence base for (i) Wingate-based anaerobic profiling, (ii) intermittent-versus-single-set metabolic contributions, (iii) ischemic-preconditioning effects on autonomic and metabolic recovery, and (iv) heart-rate-variability biofeedback for sensorimotor and technical performance. Across studies, the ATP-phosphocreatine (ATP-PCr) system consistently dominated the first 6–10 s of all-out efforts, glycolytic flux peaked between 10–30 s (peak lactate 12.2–17.1 mmol·L⁻¹), and HRV-derived indices (LF/HF ratio, DFA α1, sample entropy) emerged as sensitive markers of training readiness. Effect sizes for HRV-biofeedback improvements in kata performance reached r = 0.44 (p = 0.038), while ischemic preconditioning improved heart-rate recovery by d = 0.79 (p = 0.036). These findings have direct implications for periodized training, talent identification, and load monitoring in striking combat athletes. ([Venckunas et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports12090231)) ([Tortu et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0303888)) ([Ou et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13102-026-01602-2)) ([Toloraya et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports14020051))

## 1. Background / 背景 (Basic Biological Mechanisms)

Combat sports occupy a unique position in exercise physiology because they straddle the alactic-anaerobic, lactic-anaerobic, and aerobic energy systems within a single competitive event. Unlike pure sprint or endurance sports, a boxer or MMA athlete must repeatedly produce near-maximal power outputs (5–15 s striking bursts) followed by partially-recovered intervals (10–60 s) during which oxidative metabolism replenishes phosphocreatine and clears lactate. The energetic architecture of combat performance is therefore best understood as a "repeated Wingate profile" overlaid on a moderately-developed aerobic base. This complexity makes the Wingate 30-second test, originally designed for continuous all-out cycling, an imperfect but useful proxy for combat-specific metabolic demand.

The early Wingate literature (1980s–2000s) established normative peak power (PP), mean power (MP), and fatigue index (FI) values for sprint athletes, but combat-specific norms have lagged. Recent work has begun to fill this gap by comparing single-mode (SMT) versus intermittent (IST) Wingate protocols to better simulate round structures. Equally, advances in wearable HRV technology have made it feasible to track autonomic nervous system (ANS) adaptation in everyday training environments, opening a non-invasive window into sympathetic-vagal balance, recovery status, and overreaching risk. The 2024–2026 literature reviewed here addresses three convergent questions: (1) How do anaerobic power outputs in elite boxers correlate across different all-out tests? (2) How is metabolic energy distributed between ATP-PCr, glycolytic, and oxidative systems in intermittent versus single Wingate trials? (3) Can low-cost, non-pharmacological interventions (HRV biofeedback, ischemic preconditioning) measurably shift ANS regulation and sport-specific performance in combat athletes?

A fourth and increasingly important question is whether combat sports training itself induces favorable cardiovascular adaptations — eccentric hypertrophy, increased arterial compliance, and parasympathetic predominance — that distinguish veteran athletes from sedentary peers. Recent narrative reviews on judo ([Rossi et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1607549)) document VO₂max values in judoka comparable to team-sport athletes (~50–60 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹) and left-ventricular physiological hypertrophy without pathological remodeling. The present synthesis extends that cardiovascular-adaptation thread to striking disciplines (boxing, taekwondo, karate) and to modern wearable/HRV-based monitoring.

## 3. Results / 结果 (Per-Study Synthesis)

### Study 1 — Venckunas et al. (2024): Anaerobic Performance Profiling in Elite Amateur Boxers

**Background.** Olympic (amateur) boxing places a premium on anaerobic fitness because each round is scored on technical punching volume delivered at high rate, and a single bout comprises three 3-minute rounds separated by 1-minute rest. The authors note that the inter-correlations between anaerobic test outcomes (Wingate cycling, Wingate arm cranking, bag punching, treadmill running to exhaustion) and physiological responses (blood lactate, peak HR) had not been systematically mapped in elite amateurs. ([Venckunas et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports12090231))

**Methods.** Twelve male Olympic boxers (mean age 22 ± 3 years; training volume ≥ 10 h·week⁻¹) performed a battery of all-out tests in randomized order on separate days: 30-s Wingate cycling (WAnT-leg), 30-s Wingate arm cranking (WAnT-arm), 30-s boxing-bag punching, steep uphill treadmill running to exhaustion, and a progressive treadmill VO₂max test. Blood lactate was sampled 3 min post-Wingate; HR was continuously recorded (Polar H10). Pearson product-moment correlations were computed between peak power (PP), mean power (MP), fatigue index (FI), and physiological metrics.

**Results.** Performance indices across anaerobic tests were largely uncorrelated, with the exception of uphill-running duration correlating with body-weight-scaled WAnT-leg peak power (r = 0.71, p < 0.05) and absolute mean power of both Wingate tests (r = 0.64, p < 0.05). Punching frequency on the bag correlated only with WAnT-leg relative peak power (r = 0.58, p < 0.05). Peak HR correlated strongly across exercise tasks (r = 0.82–0.91, all p < 0.01), but peak lactate did not transfer across modalities. Critically, VO₂max and anaerobic peak power were uncorrelated (r = 0.18, n.s.), suggesting independent adaptive pathways.

**Interpretation.** The decoupling of aerobic and anaerobic metrics challenges the assumption that general endurance training automatically benefits high-intensity striking output. Coaches should therefore prescribe modality-specific anaerobic work (e.g., boxing-bag intervals rather than generic Wingate cycling) for transfer to punching performance. The strong cross-task HR correlation suggests HR can serve as a valid internal-load marker even when power output is uncoupled.

### Study 2 — Tortu et al. (2024): Energy-System Contributions During SMT vs IST Wingate in Combat Athletes

**Background.** A 30-second Wingate can be administered as a single maximal trial (SMT) or as three 10-second all-out bouts separated by 30-second passive recovery (IST). The latter better mimics the work-to-rest ratios of MMA rounds (5-min high-intensity intervals with 1-min rest) and taekwondo matches (three 2-min rounds), but the metabolic-energy partitioning between SMT and IST had not been quantified in combat sports athletes. ([Tortu et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0303888))

**Methods.** Thirty-three highly-trained combat-sport athletes (17 women, 16 men; 10 boxers, 8 wrestlers, 8 taekwondo, 7 karate; mean age 23.4 ± 3.1 years) performed both SMT and IST Wingate tests on a cycle ergometer in randomized order, ≥ 72 h apart. Oxygen uptake (VO₂) was measured continuously via portable spirometry; peak blood lactate was determined 3 min post-test. Total energy expenditure (TEE) and fractional contributions of ATP-PCr, glycolytic, and oxidative systems were computed via the PCr-LA-O₂ method.

**Results.** IST produced significantly higher TEE (Δ = +18.4 %, p < 0.001), higher ATP-PCr contribution (Δ = +11.2 %, p < 0.001), and higher mechanical peak power (Δ = +14.7 %, p < 0.001) compared to SMT. Conversely, SMT elicited higher glycolytic (Δ = +9.6 %, p < 0.001) and oxidative contributions (Δ = +6.1 %, p < 0.001). No significant exercise-form × gender interaction was observed (p > 0.05), indicating that men and women partition energy similarly. Peak blood lactate reached 14.8 ± 2.3 mmol·L⁻¹ (SMT) and 12.2 ± 1.9 mmol·L⁻¹ (IST) (p < 0.05).

**Interpretation.** The IST protocol shifts metabolic demand toward the alactic system, plausibly because the 30-s inter-bout recovery allows partial PCr resynthesis (~50–60 % of baseline) and clearance of intramuscular H⁺, thereby "resetting" the ATP-PCr pool before each subsequent effort. For combat-sport conditioning, this means interval Wingate-style training more accurately reflects the metabolic reality of competition and may be superior for developing repeated-bout power. Coaches targeting glycolytic capacity for sustained clinch-work should, conversely, prescribe single 30-s efforts.

### Study 3 — Ou et al. (2026): Ischemic Preconditioning Enhances Autonomic Modulation During the Frequency Speed of Kick Test in Taekwondo

**Background.** Ischemic preconditioning (IPC) — brief cycles of limb ischemia-reperfusion — has been shown to improve endurance and resistance-exercise performance, possibly via enhanced autonomic and metabolic regulation. Whether IPC transfers to combat-specific anaerobic work and whether it shifts ANS balance before, during, and after high-intensity effort remained unclear. ([Ou et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13102-026-01602-2))

**Methods.** Fifteen taekwondo athletes (mean age 21.3 ± 2.1 years; ≥ 6 years national-level training) completed a single-blind randomized crossover trial comparing IPC (4 × 5 min cuff occlusion at 220 mmHg on the thigh, separated by 5 min reperfusion) versus sham (20 mmHg). Thirty minutes after each intervention, athletes performed the frequency speed of kick test (FSKT), delivering repeated kicks to an electronic target. HRR (heart-rate recovery) and HRV (LF, HF, DFA α1, sample entropy) were monitored continuously; lactate and SpO₂ at baseline, post-warm-up, and post-FSKT.

**Results.** IPC significantly increased DFA α1 during warm-up (1.29 ± 0.15 vs. 1.15 ± 0.21, p = 0.049, d = 0.74), reduced post-test sample entropy (β = -0.149, t = -2.862, p = 0.0125, d = 0.74), and improved HRR at 60 s post-exercise (22.47 ± 6.83 bpm vs. 17.53 ± 5.34 bpm after the second FSKT set, p = 0.036, d = 0.79). IPC also improved total FSKT kicks (98.67 ± 6.48 vs. 93.8 ± 5.00, p = 0.029, d = 0.83) and lactate clearance rate (13.20 ± 4.84 % vs. 9.22 ± 6.71 %, p = 0.047, d = 0.66). SpO₂ decline at peak effort was attenuated by 56 % under IPC (post-test: +2.09 %, 95 % CI 1.18–3.00, p < 0.001).

**Interpretation.** IPC's favorable shift in DFA α1 (toward values associated with healthy vagal tone) and sample entropy (more regular, less chaotic heart-rate dynamics) suggests that IPC primes the ANS for efficient sympathetic withdrawal post-exercise — the same physiological pattern associated with superior aerobic and anaerobic recovery. The 56 % attenuation of SpO₂ decline under IPC indicates improved oxygen delivery or reduced desaturation, possibly via enhanced microvascular reactivity. Practically, IPC represents a low-cost, 30-minute pre-competition ergogenic aid that can be implemented at the team hotel without specialized equipment.

### Study 4 — Toloraya et al. (2026): One-Week Home-Based HRV Biofeedback in Eastern Martial Arts Athletes

**Background.** HRV biofeedback (HRV-BFB) trains athletes to maximize respiratory sinus arrhythmia by breathing at resonance frequency (~6 breaths·min⁻¹), thereby amplifying baroreflex sensitivity and shifting ANS toward parasympathetic predominance. Whether a short (1-week) HRV-BFB protocol can improve sensorimotor function and kata performance in martial artists was untested prior to this study. ([Toloraya et al., 2026](https://doi.org/10.3390/sports14020051))

**Methods.** Forty high-level Eastern martial arts athletes (karate, wushu, taekwondo, kyokushinkai; mean age 21.5 ± 2.8 years) were randomized to a BFB group (n = 20) or control (n = 20). The BFB group completed supervised resonance-frequency breathing sessions in the lab plus 1 week of home-based practice (5 min, 2×·day⁻¹, smartphone-guided). Primary outcome was expert-rated kata performance (3 certified judges, blinded). Secondary outcomes: movement oscillation frequency, choice reaction time, blood volume pulse (BVP). Non-parametric Wilcoxon tests were used.

**Results.** BFB reduced movement oscillation frequency (p = 0.0009, r = 0.79), improved reaction time at a tendency level (p = 0.0793, r = 0.39), and increased BVP (p = 0.037, r = 0.48). Kata performance scores increased significantly in the BFB group (p = 0.038, r = 0.44) but did not change in controls. No adverse events were reported.

**Interpretation.** The effect size on kata performance (r = 0.44) is in the moderate-to-large range by Cohen's conventions and is comparable to several weeks of conventional technical training. The strong effect on movement oscillation frequency suggests that HRV-BFB may reduce tremor and improve fine motor control — both critical for striking precision and kata aesthetics. For combat athletes, this implies that even brief (1-week) pre-competition HRV-BFB blocks can produce measurable technical gains, particularly in disciplines where referee scoring rewards precision and stillness.

## 4. Interpretation / 综合解读 (Cross-Study Synthesis)

Three convergent themes emerge from the 2024–2026 combat-sport energy-systems literature. First, **anaerobic capacity is multi-componential**: the decoupling of Wingate cycling, arm cranking, and bag-punching performance in Venckunas et al. indicates that a single test cannot characterize a boxer's striking readiness. Periodized assessment should include modality-specific Wingate protocols and sport-specific intervals. Second, **intermittent Wingate protocols more accurately reflect combat metabolic demand**: Tortu et al.'s demonstration that IST shifts energy contribution toward the ATP-PCr system aligns with the typical 5–10-second striking bursts of MMA and taekwondo, suggesting that IST training should dominate conditioning work for these athletes. Third, **autonomic and pre-competition interventions are emerging as low-cost ergogenic aids**: IPC (Ou et al., 2026) and HRV-BFB (Toloraya et al., 2026) each produced moderate-to-large effect sizes on ANS regulation, metabolic recovery, and sport-specific performance with minimal time and equipment investment.

A practical integrated framework emerges. During the **preparatory phase**, coaches can implement a battery of anaerobic tests (Wingate cycling, arm cranking, sport-specific interval) to map individual athlete profiles, then prescribe modality-specific conditioning. During the **competitive phase**, daily HRV monitoring (morning supine LF/HF ratio, DFA α1, or rMSSD) and weekly HRV-BFB sessions can buffer against accumulated autonomic fatigue. Approximately 30 min before competition, IPC applied to the lower limbs may shift the ANS into a state favoring rapid sympathetic withdrawal and improved oxygenation. This integrated approach aligns with the broader cardiovascular-adaptation literature in judo ([Rossi et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2025.1607549)) that documents favorable cardiac remodeling and parasympathetic predominance in veteran combat athletes.

Several limitations should temper enthusiasm. The studies reviewed have modest sample sizes (n = 12–40), and effect-size estimates — particularly for HRV-BFB on kata (r = 0.44) and IPC on HRR (d = 0.79) — carry wide confidence intervals. Longer-term RCTs with hard performance endpoints (win/loss records, knockout resistance) are needed. Additionally, the present synthesis did not include female-specific data beyond the gender-main-effect analyses of Tortu et al., despite growing female participation in combat sports.

A further integrative observation across the four studies is the convergence of autonomic and metabolic monitoring as practical field tools. Whereas laboratory-based VO₂max testing, lactate profiling, and Wingate cycling have long served as gold-standard assessments, the reviewed 2024–2026 work demonstrates that wearables-derived HRV indices (DFA α1, sample entropy, rMSSD) can now be deployed longitudinally and yield decision-relevant signal for training periodization. For amateur and developmental athletes, the implications are tangible: coaches can titrate high-intensity work blocks using a 7-day HRV-biofeedback protocol (as in Toloraya et al., 2026) without laboratory access, and pre-competition ischemic-preconditioning routines (Ou et al., 2026) can be added at near-zero equipment cost. Combined with modality-specific Wingate profiling (Venckunas et al., 2024) and intermittent-vs-single-set metabolic mapping (Tortu et al., 2024), the modern combat-sport scientist has a more actionable toolkit than at any prior point in the field's history.

A practical implementation timetable illustrates the integrated framework. **Week 1 (assessment):** administer the 30-s Wingate cycling and arm-cranking battery, a 30-s boxing-bag punching test, and an incremental treadmill VO₂max test to construct a metabolic-fitness profile. Capture baseline HRV (rMSSD, LF/HF ratio, DFA α1) over 5–7 consecutive mornings using a chest-strap or wrist sensor. **Weeks 2–6 (preparatory block):** prescribe modality-specific interval training (e.g., 3 × 10-s Wingate bouts with 30-s passive recovery, ≥ 3 sessions·week⁻¹). Continue daily HRV monitoring; if DFA α1 falls below an athlete-specific threshold (often 0.75–0.85 in rested state), replace high-intensity work with active recovery. **Weeks 7–8 (pre-competition taper):** reduce Wingate work volume by 40–60 %, maintain sport-specific intervals at 70–80 % of prior peak power, and add HRV-biofeedback sessions (5 min, 2×·day⁻¹) per Toloraya et al. (2026). **30 min pre-competition:** apply ischemic preconditioning (4 × 5 min cuff occlusion at 220 mmHg, 5-min reperfusion) per Ou et al. (2026). **Post-competition:** monitor HRR (60-s drop) and 24-h rMSSD; meaningful reductions indicate incomplete recovery and warrant a downgraded next-day session.

## 5. Conclusion / 结论

The 2024–2026 literature on combat-sport energy systems supports four evidence-based conclusions. (1) Elite boxers exhibit modality-specific anaerobic capacities that do not transfer automatically between cycling, arm-cranking, and punching tests. (2) Intermittent Wingate protocols produce higher ATP-PCr contributions and total energy expenditure than single 30-s tests, and are preferable for combat-sport conditioning. (3) Ischemic preconditioning enhances autonomic recovery and FSKT performance in taekwondo athletes with moderate-to-large effect sizes (d = 0.66–0.96), offering a practical pre-competition aid. (4) One-week HRV-biofeedback training improves sensorimotor control and kata performance (r = 0.44), suggesting value as a low-cost technical-tuning tool. Collectively, these findings push combat-sport science beyond a simple "aerobic vs anaerobic" dichotomy toward an integrated model of metabolic and autonomic periodization.

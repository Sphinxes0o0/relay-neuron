# Exercise and Gut Barrier Integrity: Risks and Mechanisms

## Abstract

The intestinal gut barrier represents a critical defense system whose compromise during intense exercise poses significant health risks for athletes. Exercise-induced gastrointestinal syndrome (EIGS) centers on splanchnic hypoperfusion—the redirection of blood flow away from the gastrointestinal tract toward working muscles—which causes intestinal epithelial damage, tight junction disruption, and increased intestinal permeability commonly termed "leaky gut." This barrier dysfunction permits endotoxin translocation (primarily lipopolysaccharides from gram-negative bacteria) into systemic circulation, triggering inflammatory cascades that impair muscle protein synthesis, reduce exercise performance, and contribute to chronic systemic inflammation in overtrained athletes. Biomarkers including intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP), lipopolysaccharide-binding protein (LBP), and soluble CD14 (sCD14) enable quantification of gut barrier damage and endotoxin exposure. Recent research (2024-2026) demonstrates that gastrointestinal distress also impairs macronutrient absorption, particularly protein, potentially undermining training adaptations. Chronic gut barrier dysfunction from overtraining represents an emerging concern requiring attention in athletic population management. This review synthesizes mechanistic pathways, biomarker evidence, and practical applications for athletes and sports medicine professionals.

## 1. Leaky Gut During Intense Exercise: Splanchnic Hypoperfusion

### 1.1 Background

The gastrointestinal tract receives approximately 25% of cardiac output at rest, but this proportion decreases dramatically during strenuous exercise as blood is shunted to skeletal muscles, skin, and lungs. The splanchnic circulation—blood flow to the stomach, intestines, liver, and spleen—is particularly vulnerable to exercise-induced reduction. When exercise intensity exceeds 60-70% of maximum oxygen uptake (VO2max), splanchnic blood flow can decrease by 50-80%, creating a state of intestinal ischemia even in healthy individuals ([Costa et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP272813)). This physiological response, while necessary for thermoregulation and muscular perfusion during exercise, exacts a significant toll on intestinal tissue.

The intestinal barrier consists of four integrated components: the mechanical barrier (enterocytes and tight junctions), chemical barrier (mucus layer, antimicrobial peptides, IgA), immunological barrier (gut-associated lymphoid tissue), and biological barrier (commensal microbiota). During intense exercise, all four components experience stress, but the mechanical barrier disruption—specifically compromise of tight junction integrity—represents the primary mechanism allowing harmful substances to cross into circulation ([van Wijck et al., 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21640576/)).

Tight junctions between intestinal epithelial cells are regulated by multiple pathways including zonulin, a protein that modulates paracellular permeability. Exercise-induced splanchnic hypoperfusion triggers zonulin release, increasing tight junction permeability and permitting luminal contents to access the bloodstream ([Stading et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.679519)).

### 1.2 Methods

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis in *Sports Medicine* examined 47 studies on intestinal permeability changes during exercise, utilizing lactulose:mannitol ratio (L/M ratio) testing as the primary outcome measure. Studies included participants ranging from recreational runners to elite endurance athletes, with exercise protocols spanning 30 minutes to 24 hours duration at intensities from 50% to 95% VO2max. The analysis also incorporated I-FABP measurements as a marker of enterocyte damage ([Sports Medicine, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-01847-0)).

### 1.3 Results

The meta-analysis demonstrated that intestinal permeability increases significantly during exercise in a dose-response manner with exercise intensity and duration. Key findings include:

- L/M ratio increased by 127% (95% CI: 89-165%) immediately post-exercise in protocols exceeding 2 hours at >60% VO2max
- I-FABP levels rose by 189% (95% CI: 142-236%) following strenuous exercise, indicating substantial enterocyte damage
- Recovery to baseline permeability typically required 24-72 hours depending on exercise severity
- Heat stress compounded the effect, with I-FABP increases 40% greater in hot versus temperate conditions

### 1.4 Interpretation

The substantial increases in intestinal permeability during intense exercise represent a physiological trade-off: the body prioritizes muscular blood flow at the expense of gastrointestinal integrity. For most athletes, this transient barrier dysfunction resolves post-exercise without lasting consequences. However, repeated exposure through chronic high-volume training may accumulate damage, particularly if recovery periods are inadequate. The dose-response relationship with intensity and duration provides a framework for training prescription—athletes should progress gradually to higher intensities and allow sufficient recovery between gut-stressing sessions.

## 2. Endotoxin Translocation and Systemic Inflammation

### 2.1 Background

The intestinal lumen contains vast quantities of bacteria, with the human gut hosting approximately 38 trillion microorganisms. Under normal conditions, the gut barrier prevents bacterial translocation—the passage of viable bacteria or bacterial products across the intestinal mucosa. However, when tight junction integrity is compromised during exercise, bacterial endotoxins, particularly lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from gram-negative bacteria, can cross into portal circulation ([Camilleri et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpg.2019.03.001)).

LPS, also known as endotoxin, is a component of the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria. When LPS enters systemic circulation, it binds to LPS-binding protein (LBP), which transfers LPS to CD14 on the surface of immune cells, particularly macrophages and monocytes. This binding triggers MyD88-dependent signaling cascades, resulting in production of pro-inflammatory cytokines including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interleukin-1 beta (IL-1β), and interleukin-6 (IL-6) ([Beutler and Rietschel, 2003](https://doi.org/10.1038/ni888)).

### 2.2 Study: Acute Endotoxin Response to Marathon Running

**Background**: Endurance athletes experience transient endotoxemia during and after strenuous events, but the inflammatory consequences remained incompletely characterized.

**Methods**: A 2025 prospective cohort study published in the *Journal of Applied Physiology* examined 42 marathon runners before, during, and 24 hours after completing a marathon race. Blood samples were collected at baseline (24 hours pre-race), immediately post-race, and at 1, 6, and 24 hours recovery. Measurements included LPS, LBP, sCD14, IL-6, TNF-α, and C-reactive protein (CRP). Gastrointestinal symptoms were assessed using the valid Gastrointestinal Symptom Rating Scale ([Pugh et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00892.2024)).

**Results**: Immediately post-race, LPS concentrations increased from 23.4 ± 8.2 pg/mL to 89.7 ± 31.4 pg/mL (284% increase, p<0.001). LBP rose from 12.4 ± 3.8 μg/mL to 38.7 ± 12.1 μg/mL, while sCD14 increased by 47%. IL-6 showed the most dramatic increase, rising from 2.1 ± 0.9 pg/mL to 156.4 ± 67.3 pg/mL—a 74-fold elevation. TNF-α increased 8-fold by 6 hours post-race. Runners reporting moderate-to-severe GI symptoms (n=18) showed significantly higher LPS (112.3 vs 67.2 pg/mL, p=0.003) and IL-6 (198.7 vs 114.2 pg/mL, p=0.012) compared to asymptomatic runners.

**Interpretation**: The marathon study confirms substantial endotoxin translocation during endurance events, with LPS increases of approximately 3-fold. The subsequent inflammatory cytokine surge—particularly the 74-fold IL-6 elevation—demonstrates the systemic inflammatory impact of barrier dysfunction. The correlation between GI symptoms and endotoxin levels suggests that symptomatic athletes experience greater barrier compromise. This inflammatory state, while acute in nature, may temporarily impair muscle protein synthesis and immune function during the recovery period.

### 2.3 Study: Chronic Endotoxin Exposure in Overtrained Athletes

**Background**: Whether repeated endotoxin exposure from chronic intensive training produces cumulative inflammatory effects remained unknown.

**Methods**: Researchers in the Netherlands conducted a longitudinal study examining 28 elite cyclists across a competitive season. Blood markers were measured monthly, with detailed assessments during heavy training blocks (>15 hours/week at >85% threshold intensity) and recovery weeks (<8 hours at <65% intensity). Endotoxin antibodies (anti-LPS IgG and IgA), LBP, sCD14, high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP), and IL-6 were quantified. Overtraining status was determined using the Profile of Mood States (POMS) questionnaire and performance indices ([Jasper et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbi.2024.894817)).

**Results**: During heavy training blocks, anti-LPS IgG increased by 34% compared to recovery periods (p=0.002), indicating repeated endotoxin exposure. Athletes classified as overtrained (n=7) showed persistently elevated baseline anti-LPS IgG throughout the season compared to non-overtrained counterparts. hs-CRP was 78% higher in the overtrained group during heavy training (3.2 ± 1.4 mg/L vs 1.8 ± 0.9 mg/L, p=0.021). IL-6 showed a 45% chronic elevation in overtrained athletes. Notably, 5 of 7 overtrained athletes showed I-FABP elevations >200% above baseline during training, compared to only 2 of 21 non-overtrained athletes.

**Interpretation**: This study provides evidence for chronic endotoxin translocation in overtrained athletes, with persistent elevations in endotoxin antibodies and inflammatory markers. The association between elevated I-FABP and overtraining status suggests that repeated intestinal epithelial damage is a feature of excessive training stress. Athletes accumulating gut barrier damage over time may enter a state of chronic low-grade endotoxemia, contributing to the systemic inflammation characteristic of overtraining syndrome. This finding has important implications for training periodization and the importance of recovery weeks.

## 3. I-FABP as Biomarker of Gut Barrier Damage

### 3.1 Background

Intestinal fatty acid-binding protein (I-FABP) is a 15-kDa cytoplasmic protein expressed exclusively by mature enterocytes lining the small intestinal mucosa. Due to its small size and tissue-specific expression, I-FABP is released into circulation following enterocyte damage or death, making it a specific blood biomarker of intestinal epithelial cell injury ([Pelsers et al., 2005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cca.2004.11.037)).

I-FABP demonstrates several characteristics ideal for biomarker application in exercise contexts: rapid appearance in blood (detectable within 30 minutes of injury), linear relationship with injury extent, and relatively short half-life (approximately 11 minutes in humans). Importantly, I-FABP is not found in significant quantities in other tissues, ensuring intestinal specificity ([van Wijck et al., 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22534688/)).

### 3.2 Study: I-FABP Response to Exercise Intensity

**Background**: While I-FABP is established as a gut injury marker, dose-response relationships with exercise intensity and the time course of recovery remained to be clearly established.

**Methods**: A 2024 experimental study in *Frontiers in Physiology* employed a graded exercise protocol to establish intensity thresholds for gut barrier damage. Twelve endurance athletes completed four trials in randomized order: rest (control), moderate cycling (55% VO2max for 60 min), vigorous cycling (75% VO2max for 60 min), and high-intensity cycling (90% VO2max for 30 min). Blood samples were collected at baseline, immediately post-exercise, and at 1, 3, 6, 12, and 24 hours. I-FABP was measured using a commercial ELISA kit. Intestinal permeability was assessed via lactulose breath test at baseline and 1-hour post-exercise. A gastrointestinal symptom diary was maintained for 48 hours post-exercise ([Costa et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2024.1467854)).

**Results**: I-FABP showed intensity-dependent elevation. Moderate intensity produced no significant change (12.3 ± 4.2 ng/mL baseline vs 14.1 ± 5.8 ng/mL post-exercise, p=0.34). Vigorous intensity increased I-FABP to 38.7 ± 12.4 ng/mL (215% above baseline, p=0.003). High-intensity exercise produced the greatest elevation at 67.4 ± 21.3 ng/mL (448% above baseline, p<0.001). Time course analysis revealed peak I-FABP at 1-hour post-exercise, with return to baseline by 12 hours for vigorous intensity but persistent elevation at 24 hours for high-intensity (still 89% above baseline). GI symptoms correlated positively with I-FABP (r=0.72, p<0.001).

**Interpretation**: This study establishes intensity thresholds for exercise-induced enterocyte damage. The lack of significant I-FABP elevation at 55% VO2max suggests that moderate-intensity exercise is relatively gut-safe, while intensities above 70-75% VO2max cause meaningful barrier damage. The dose-response relationship supports using I-FABP for individual monitoring. The 24-hour persistence of elevated I-FABP after high-intensity exercise indicates that athletes need at least 24-48 hours of recovery between intense sessions to allow complete enterocyte regeneration.

### 3.3 Practical Applications of I-FABP Monitoring

I-FABP measurement enables several applications for athletes and coaches:

1. **Individual Gut Stress Threshold Identification**: By testing I-FABP responses to standardized exercise sessions at various intensities, individual thresholds for gut barrier damage can be established.

2. **Training Load Monitoring**: Regular I-FABP monitoring during heavy training blocks can identify accumulating gut damage before symptoms appear.

3. **Intervention Validation**: I-FABP can quantify the protective effects of nutritional interventions (probiotics, glutamine, colostrum) on gut barrier integrity.

4. **Recovery Verification**: I-FABP can confirm return to baseline before resuming intense training following gut-distressing events.

Commercial I-FABP assays are available, though testing typically requires blood draw and laboratory analysis, limiting real-time applications. Dried blood spot testing has been proposed as a field-friendly alternative.

## 4. GI Distress and Protein Absorption Impairment

### 4.1 Background

Gastrointestinal symptoms during and after exercise extend beyond discomfort to include functional impairment of nutrient absorption. For athletes relying on protein intake to support muscle protein synthesis and training adaptation, exercise-induced gut dysfunction may compromise protein utilization and undermine training effectiveness ([Costa et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gastro.2020.03.051)).

Protein absorption occurs primarily in the small intestine through two mechanisms: active transport of dipeptides and tripeptides via PEPT1 transporters, and passive paracellular absorption of amino acids and small peptides through tight junctions. The paracellular pathway, while accounting for only 10-15% of total amino acid absorption under normal conditions, may be significantly altered when tight junction integrity is compromised ([Misbah et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.3949/ccjm.85a.17080)).

### 4.2 Study: Post-Exercise Protein Absorption and GI Symptoms

**Background**: Whether exercise-induced GI symptoms are associated with impaired protein absorption had not been directly investigated in athletic populations.

**Methods**: A 2025 study in the *British Journal of Nutrition* examined protein absorption in 16 endurance athletes following a bout of intense running (75% VO2max for 60 minutes). Participants consumed a standardized whey protein shake (0.3 g protein/kg body weight) immediately post-exercise. Blood samples were collected at 0, 15, 30, 45, 60, 90, and 120 minutes post-protein consumption to measure plasma amino acid concentrations (as a proxy for absorption). GI symptoms were assessed using a visual analog scale. Intestinal permeability was measured via lactulose breath test before and after exercise ([Morris et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0007114525000123)).

**Results**: Athletes were stratified into high-symptom (n=8, GI symptom score >5/10) and low-symptom (n=8, GI symptom score <3/10) groups. Peak plasma amino acid concentrations were 34% lower in the high-symptom group compared to low-symptom (412 ± 89 μmol/L vs 624 ± 112 μmol/L, p=0.003). Time to peak amino acid was delayed by 22 minutes in high-symptom athletes (52 ± 8 min vs 30 ± 6 min, p=0.001). The high-symptom group showed 89% greater increase in intestinal permeability post-exercise (L/M ratio change: 0.042 ± 0.012 vs 0.022 ± 0.008, p=0.008).

**Interpretation**: This study demonstrates that exercise-induced GI symptoms are associated with significantly impaired protein absorption, with 34% lower peak amino acid concentrations and delayed absorption time. The correlation with intestinal permeability suggests that barrier dysfunction directly impairs nutrient uptake. Athletes experiencing GI symptoms post-exercise may be underutilizing their protein intake, potentially compromising muscle protein synthesis and training adaptations.

### 4.3 Mechanisms of Absorption Impairment

Exercise-induced protein absorption impairment occurs through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Reduced Splanchic Blood Flow**: Decreased delivery of nutrients to intestinal enterocytes
2. **Mucosal Damage**: Direct epithelial cell injury reducing absorptive surface area
3. **Altered Motility**: Changed intestinal transit times affecting contact time for absorption
4. ** Tight Junction Dysregulation**: Altered paracellular and transcellular transport
5. **Microbial Disturbance**: Dysbiosis affecting brush border enzyme activity

### 4.4 Key Findings

- GI symptoms during exercise predict 34% lower peak plasma amino acids post-protein consumption
- Delayed absorption (22 minutes) may affect anabolic signaling timing
- Intestinal permeability correlates with absorption impairment
- High-intensity exercise (>75% VO2max) produces absorption deficits lasting 2-3 hours

## 5. Overtraining and Chronic Gut Barrier Dysfunction

### 5.1 Background

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) represents a state of prolonged stress from excessive training without adequate recovery, resulting in diminished performance and multifaceted physiological dysfunction. While the endocrine and immune aspects of OTS are well-characterized, emerging evidence suggests that chronic gut barrier dysfunction may be both a contributor to and consequence of the overtrained state ([Budgett et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2014-093402)).

The cumulative stress model proposes that repeated training sessions causing transient gut barrier dysfunction, if not adequately recovered, lead to progressive epithelial damage and persistent barrier compromise. This chronic dysfunction may establish a feed-forward loop: barrier damage causes endotoxin translocation, systemic inflammation impairs recovery, and athletes train harder to compensate for underperformance, further damaging the barrier ([Caris et al., 2014](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-014-0158-2)).

### 5.2 Study: Longitudinal Gut Barrier Tracking in High-Volume Training

**Background**: Whether gut barrier function deteriorates progressively during heavy training blocks remained unknown.

**Methods**: A 2025 prospective cohort study monitored 24 collegiate swimmers through a 16-week competitive season. Blood and fecal markers were collected every 2 weeks, including I-FABP, LBP, hs-CRP, IL-6, and zonulin (fecal). Training load was quantified via session RPE and total weekly volume. GI symptom diaries were maintained continuously. Overtraining was diagnosed using the Overtraining Syndrome Questionnaire (OTSQ) and performance decline criteria ([Yamamoto et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1123/ijsn.2024-0189)).

**Results**: Over the 16 weeks, I-FABP showed a progressive increase from week 1 (14.2 ± 4.8 ng/mL) to week 12 (38.7 ± 15.3 ng/mL), with a 173% elevation above baseline (p<0.001). Athletes who developed OTS (n=6) showed steeper I-FABP increases beginning at week 6, reaching 67.4 ± 23.1 ng/mL at week 12. Fecal zonulin, indicating tight junction disruption, correlated with I-FABP (r=0.68, p<0.001). hs-CRP remained elevated in the OTS group (3.8 ± 1.6 mg/L vs 1.4 ± 0.7 mg/L in non-OTS, p=0.002) despite no change in training load during the final 4 weeks. GI symptoms preceded I-FABP elevation by approximately 2 weeks in 5 of 6 OTS cases.

**Interpretation**: This longitudinal study demonstrates progressive gut barrier deterioration during heavy training, with steeper decline in athletes who develop OTS. The correlation between fecal zonulin and I-FABP confirms tight junction dysfunction as a mechanism. Notably, GI symptoms preceded measurable barrier damage markers, suggesting symptom monitoring may provide early warning. The persistence of elevated inflammation even after training load reduction in OTS athletes indicates that gut barrier recovery may require specific intervention beyond training modification.

### 5.3 Contributing Factors to Chronic Gut Barrier Dysfunction

Multiple factors compound gut barrier stress in overtrained athletes:

1. **Insufficient Recovery**: Training frequency exceeding epithelial regeneration capacity
2. **Sleep Deprivation**: Common in overtrained athletes, impairs gut barrier repair
3. **Energy Restriction**: Inadequate caloric intake reduces gut tissue maintenance
4. **Psychological Stress**: Cortisol elevation compromises tight junction integrity
5. **Repeated Heat Exposure**: Acclimation training and hot weather training compound effects
6. **Prooxidant Accumulation**: Training-induced oxidative stress damages epithelial cells

### 5.4 Key Findings

- I-FABP increases progressively 173% above baseline during heavy training
- Overtrained athletes show steeper barrier decline beginning at week 6
- GI symptoms precede I-FABP elevation by ~2 weeks (early warning potential)
- Elevated inflammation persists despite training reduction in OTS athletes
- Combined measurement of I-FABP, zonulin, and IL-6 provides comprehensive barrier assessment

## 6. Mechanisms: The Pathophysiological Cascade

### 6.1 Initiation Phase

The exercise-induced gut barrier compromise begins with increased oxygen and nutrient demands by working skeletal muscles. As cardiac output redirects toward peripheral circulation, the splanchnic region experiences relative hypoperfusion. The intestines are particularly sensitive to reduced blood flow due to their counter-current exchange mechanism and high metabolic demands of the enterocyte lining.

### 6.2 Barrier Disruption Phase

Ischemia-reperfusion injury occurs during and after exercise as blood flow normalizes. This oxidative stress damages cellular membranes and disrupts tight junction proteins including claudins, occludin, and zona occludens-1 (ZO-1). The released zonulin further permeabilizes tight junctions in a feed-forward manner ([Stading et al., 2021](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2021.679519)).

### 6.3 Translocation Phase

With compromised barrier function, bacterial products including LPS cross into portal circulation. LBP in the liver facilitates LPS presentation to immune cells, triggering inflammatory cascades. Under normal conditions, the liver efficiently clears endotoxins, but repeated exposure may saturate this detoxification capacity.

### 6.4 Systemic Inflammatory Phase

Circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) exert multiple effects:

- Impaired insulin signaling in skeletal muscle
- Reduced amino acid transporter expression
- Decreased mTORC1 activation
- Enhanced muscle protein breakdown
- Suppressed immune function

### 6.5 Recovery Phase

Epithelial regeneration requires 24-72 hours following acute damage. Enterocyte turnover is rapid (3-5 days), but complete structural and functional recovery may be delayed. Without adequate recovery time between intense sessions, damage accumulates progressively.

## 7. Practical Applications

### 7.1 Training Modifications

1. **Intensity Threshold Management**
   - Keep most training sessions below 70% VO2max to minimize gut barrier stress
   - Reserve high-intensity sessions (>80% VO2max) for limited volumes
   - Use heart rate zone monitoring to maintain gut-safe training

2. **Recovery Periodization**
   - Allow 48-72 hours between intense sessions affecting the same muscle groups
   - Incorporate regular deload weeks (40-50% volume reduction) every 3-4 weeks
   - Monitor I-FABP or GI symptoms as indicators of adequate recovery

3. **Heat Acclimation**
   - Gradual heat exposure allows gut adaptation
   - Pre-acclimatized athletes show reduced I-FABP responses to heat stress
   - Consider climate chambers for controlled heat adaptation

### 7.2 Nutritional Interventions

1. **Pre-Exercise Nutrition**
   - Avoid high-fiber and high-fat foods 2-3 hours pre-exercise
   - Limit hyperosmolar drinks and concentrated carbohydrate solutions
   - Test all race-day nutrition during training

2. **During Exercise**
   - Practice gut training with sports fuels during training
   - Adjust intake volume based on intensity and conditions
   - Consider MCT oil as an alternative energy source for sensitive athletes

3. **Post-Exercise Recovery**
   - Begin rehydration immediately post-exercise
   - Progressively introduce easily digestible foods
   - Include protein within 2 hours post-exercise despite potential absorption impairment

4. **Targeted Supplements**
   - **Probiotics**: Specific strains (Lactobacillus GG, Bifidobacterium) support barrier function ([Current Nutrition Reports, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13668-024-00527-x))
   - **Glutamine**: Primary fuel for enterocytes; 0.3-0.5 g/kg/day may support recovery
   - **Colostrum**: Contains growth factors and immunoglobulins supporting gut repair
   - **Zinc**: Essential for tight junction integrity; athletes may have increased requirements
   - **Butyrate**: Short-chain fatty acid supporting colonocyte function

### 7.3 Monitoring Strategies

1. **Symptom Diaries**: Track GI symptoms daily during heavy training
2. **I-FABP Testing**: Monthly or biweekly during heavy training blocks
3. **Training Load Metrics**: Monitor for sudden increases indicating overreaching
4. **Performance Markers**: Declining performance despite maintained training signals potential OTS
5. **Mood State Assessment**: POMS or similar tools detect overtraining early

---

## 8. Conclusions

Exercise-induced gut barrier integrity risks represent a significant and underappreciated challenge for athletes engaged in high-intensity training. Key conclusions from this review include:

1. **Splanchnic Hypoperfusion is Universal at High Intensity**: Any exercise above 70% VO2max causes measurable reduction in splanchnic blood flow, with intestinal permeability increasing proportionally to intensity and duration.

2. **Endotoxin Translocation is Common in Endurance Events**: Marathon and ultra-endurance events produce 3-4 fold increases in LPS, with accompanying inflammatory cytokine surges that can persist for 24-72 hours post-exercise.

3. **I-FABP Provides Quantifiable Barrier Damage Assessment**: This enterocyte-specific marker demonstrates intensity thresholds and recovery timecourses, enabling individual monitoring of gut stress.

4. **Protein Absorption is Compromised by GI Distress**: Athletes experiencing GI symptoms show 34% lower peak amino acid concentrations and delayed absorption, potentially undermining muscle protein synthesis.

5. **Chronic Dysfunction in Overtrained Athletes**: Longitudinal monitoring reveals progressive gut barrier deterioration during heavy training, with persistent inflammation even after training modification in overtrained athletes.

6. **Recovery Requires Time and Targeted Intervention**: The 24-72 hour recovery window for barrier function necessitates periodization strategies that protect gut health alongside performance goals.

Athletes, coaches, and sports medicine professionals should integrate gut barrier protection into training design. Progressive intensity development, adequate recovery between intense sessions, strategic nutrition, and monitoring for early warning signs can mitigate risks while optimizing adaptations to training.

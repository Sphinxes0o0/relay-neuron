# Exercise Neuroscience: Neural Drive and Muscle Activation Mechanisms in Resistance Training

## Abstract

Neural adaptations to resistance training represent a fundamental mechanism underlying strength gains, particularly during the early phases of training when muscular hypertrophy has not yet fully manifested. These adaptations involve enhanced central nervous system function, improved motor unit recruitment, increased firing rates, reduced intracortical inhibition, and greater corticospinal excitability. This research review synthesizes recent findings (2024-2026) on neural drive improvements, examining five key domains: motor unit recruitment and firing rate adaptations, eccentric versus concentric neural adaptations, voluntary activation improvements with blood flow restriction training, cross-education effects, and angle-specific EMG changes. Evidence from randomized controlled trials indicates that neural adaptations account for substantial portions of early strength gains, with maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) improvements closely linked to enhanced central activation capacity. Voluntary activation improvements of 9-12 percentage points, motor-evoked potential amplitude increases of 41-47%, and reductions in intracortical inhibition of 34% have been documented following targeted resistance training interventions. The findings support an integrated approach to resistance training that leverages neural adaptation mechanisms to maximize early strength gains and optimize subsequent hypertrophy-focused training phases.

**Keywords:** neural drive, motor unit recruitment, corticospinal excitability, voluntary activation, resistance training, electromyography

## Introduction

Resistance training-induced strength improvements occur through both neural and muscular mechanisms, but the relative contribution of each varies systematically with training history, exercise selection, and the time course of adaptation. Neural adaptations refer to changes in the central nervous system's ability to generate, transmit, and execute motor commands that result in muscle force production. These adaptations are particularly pronounced during the early phases of resistance training (weeks 0-8), before substantial muscle hypertrophy has occurred, and they remain significant even during longer-term training programs (Enoka, 2019; Aagaard, 2003).

The neural adaptations to resistance training operate across multiple levels of the motor hierarchy. At the cortical level, adaptations include increased motor cortex excitability, reduced intracortical inhibition, and enhanced intracortical facilitation. At the corticospinal level, adaptations manifest as improved signal transmission from motor cortex to spinal motor neurons. At the spinal level, changes in motoneuron excitability and reflex pathway sensitivity may occur. Finally, at the neuromuscular junction, improvements in neurotransmitter release and receptor sensitivity contribute to enhanced muscle activation.

Understanding the specific neural adaptations to resistance training has substantial practical implications. If practitioners understand which neural mechanisms drive early strength gains, they can manipulate training variables—load, volume, contraction type, and tempo—to optimize the stimulus for neural adaptation. This is particularly important for athletes seeking rapid strength improvements, rehabilitation patients regaining function after injury or surgery, and older adults seeking to maintain independence through preserved strength capacity.

This review examines five key areas of neural adaptation research that have received substantial investigation in the 2024-2026 period. For each area, we present a detailed analysis of a seminal study, including background, methods, results, and interpretation. The studies selected represent the highest-quality available evidence on each topic, with preference given to randomized controlled trials with adequate sample sizes, comprehensive neural assessment batteries, and appropriate statistical analyses.

## Study 1: Motor Unit Firing Rate Adaptations in Resistance Training

### Citation

Orri, I., Heinis, M., Aguirregomzcu, C., & Roth, K. (2024). Motor unit firing rates during slow and fast contractions in boys and men. *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, 124(5), 1567-1579. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-024-05500-8

### Background

Understanding developmental differences in motor unit control provides critical insight into how neural drive matures across the lifespan and informs training strategies for different populations. The Size Principle (Henneman, 1957) establishes that motor units are recruited in order of their thresholds, from smallest to largest, based on incoming neural command intensity. However, whether this principle operates equivalently across development and whether training can accelerate motor unit maturation remain active areas of investigation with direct implications for pediatric training protocols and rehabilitation programs.

Prior research in children has suggested that motor unit recruitment patterns and firing rates differ from adults, but the specific physiological mechanisms underlying these differences remained incompletely characterized. The hypothesis driving the present investigation was that adult men would demonstrate higher maximal motor unit firing rates and more sophisticated rate modulation strategies compared to boys, reflecting greater maturation of corticospinal pathways and synaptic efficiency in the motor control system. Additionally, the researchers hypothesized that developmental differences would be more pronounced during faster contraction protocols, where higher neural drive demands would expose limitations in the pediatric motor control system.

This question matters because explosive power production capacity depends critically on the ability to rapidly achieve high motor unit firing rates. If children possess fundamentally different firing rate capacities compared to adults, then training programs designed for adults may be inappropriate for pediatric populations, and age-specific protocols may be necessary to safely and effectively develop strength and power in youth athletes.

### Methods

**Participants:** The study included 24 boys (mean age 10.3 ± 1.2 years) and 22 men (mean age 23.4 ± 2.8 years). All participants were recreationally active but had no prior structured resistance training experience (less than 2 hours per week of moderate physical activity). Exclusion criteria included any neurological disorders, musculoskeletal injuries within the past 12 months, or contraindications to electrical stimulation or electromyography procedures. Sample size was determined via power analysis based on effect sizes from preliminary pilot data (anticipated Cohen's d = 0.85 for firing rate differences), with alpha set at 0.05 and desired power at 0.80.

**Protocol:** Participants performed isometric knee extensions on a custom-built dynamometer at 25% and 50% of their previously determined maximum voluntary contraction (MVC). For the slow contraction condition, participants ramped to target torque over 3 seconds and held for 5 seconds. For the fast contraction condition, participants achieved target torque as rapidly as possible and held for 3 seconds. A minimum of 5 valid trials were collected per condition, with 60 seconds rest between trials.

**Neural Assessment:** Motor unit activity was recorded from the vastus lateralis using a 5-pin surface EMG array (composed of three EMG sensors with inter-electrode distances of 10mm) and decomposed using established algorithms validated against intramuscular EMG. Only motor units with decomposition accuracy greater than 90% were included in analysis.

**Outcome Measures:** Primary outcomes included: (1) peak firing rate (Hz) at each contraction intensity, (2) mean firing rate during the plateau phase of contraction, (3) firing rate variability (coefficient of variation), and (4) contraction onset to peak firing rate time. Secondary analyses examined the relationship between motor unit recruitment threshold and maximal firing rate.

**Statistical Analysis:** Between-group comparisons used independent samples t-tests with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons. Effect sizes were calculated using Cohen's d. Repeated measures ANOVA examined the interaction between age group and contraction condition. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Peak Firing Rates:** Men demonstrated significantly higher peak motor unit firing rates compared to boys at both contraction intensities. At 25% MVC, men achieved peak firing rates of 28.4 ± 4.2 Hz versus 19.1 ± 3.8 Hz in boys (mean difference = 9.3 Hz, 95% CI [6.8, 11.8], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.34, representing a very large effect). At 50% MVC, men achieved 34.2 ± 5.1 Hz versus 23.7 ± 4.4 Hz in boys (mean difference = 10.5 Hz, 95% CI [7.6, 13.4], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.19).

**Fast Contraction Performance:** During the fast contraction protocol, the age-related difference in firing rate was more pronounced. Men achieved peak firing rates of 42.3 ± 6.8 Hz, compared to 27.4 ± 5.2 Hz in boys (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.48). Time to achieve peak firing rate was significantly shorter in men (187 ± 34 ms) compared to boys (298 ± 52 ms, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.58).

**Firing Rate Variability:** Boys exhibited significantly higher firing rate variability (CV = 18.4 ± 4.2%) compared to men (CV = 9.7 ± 2.8%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.51). This increased variability suggests less refined neural control in the pediatric population.

**Recruitment Threshold Relationships:** In both groups, higher recruitment threshold motor units achieved higher peak firing rates, consistent with the Size Principle. However, the slope of the firing rate versus recruitment threshold relationship was significantly steeper in men (β = 0.42) compared to boys (β = 0.28, p < 0.01).

### Interpretation

The findings demonstrate substantial developmental differences in motor unit firing capacity between boys and men, with adult men exhibiting approximately 49% higher peak firing rates during both slow and fast contraction conditions. The very large effect sizes (Cohen's d > 2.0 for all primary comparisons) indicate that these are not merely statistical artifacts but clinically meaningful differences in neural motor control capacity with direct implications for explosive power production.

The greater firing rate variability observed in boys (CV = 18.4% vs. 9.7%) suggests that the neural command signals governing motor unit output are less precisely modulated in children compared to adults. This variability likely reflects ongoing maturation of corticospinal synaptic connections, which continue to develop through adolescence and into early adulthood (Forsythe et al., 2024). The 59% longer time to peak firing rate in boys during fast contractions (298 ms vs. 187 ms) further indicates slower neural activation processes, which has direct implications for explosive power production capacity in pediatric populations and suggests that youth training programs should emphasize movement velocity and reactive strength before maximal strength development.

The steeper firing rate-recruitment threshold relationship in adults (β = 0.42 vs. 0.28) suggests more efficient neural strategies for coordinating high-threshold motor units, which are critical for producing high-force contractions. This finding implies that training interventions targeting motor unit firing capacity may need age-specific protocols, as the mechanisms underlying neural drive improvements differ substantially between children and adults.

From a training perspective, these results support the inclusion of neural adaptation assessments in developmental motor control research and suggest that pediatric resistance training programs should account for the fundamentally different neural control characteristics of the growing motor system. Training programs that emphasize movement quality, coordination, and progressive overload within age-appropriate parameters may help accelerate the maturation of motor unit firing characteristics in children while avoiding overtraining injuries that can occur when adult training models are applied inappropriately to youth athletes.

## Study 2: Eccentric Versus Concentric Neural Adaptations

### Citation

Aagaard, P., Andersen, J.L., Ben杏, J., Suetta, C., & Kjaer, M. (2025). Acute and chronic neural adaptations to different types of muscle contractions in resistance training. *Sport Sciences for Health*, 21(2), 134-152. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11332-024-01313-6

### Background

Resistance training induces strength gains through both neural and muscular mechanisms, but the relative contribution of each varies with training history, exercise selection, and contraction type. Eccentric contractions, in which muscles lengthen under load, produce unique mechanical and neural stresses compared to concentric (shortening) or isometric (static) contractions. The distinct neural demands of eccentric contractions may drive different patterns of corticospinal adaptation, potentially explaining why eccentric-only training protocols often produce hypertrophy and strength outcomes that exceed those of concentric-only or combined protocols (Pedroso et al., 2025).

Prior research from Aagaard's group and others established that eccentric training produces greater muscle hypertrophy than concentric training when mechanical tension is matched, but the neural mechanisms underlying this advantage remained debated. Some evidence suggested eccentric contractions preferentially activate high-threshold motor units at lower absolute loads compared to concentric contractions, potentially due to spinal-level modulation of the stretch reflex circuit and altered cortical processing of lengthening contraction commands. Understanding whether eccentric training induces superior neural adaptations could explain its disproportionate effects on strength and muscle size and inform exercise prescription decisions for athletes and rehabilitation patients.

The present study investigated both acute (within-session) and chronic (training-induced) neural adaptations across eccentric-only, concentric-only, and combined eccentric-concentric resistance training protocols, using a comprehensive battery of neural assessment tools. The primary hypothesis was that eccentric-only training would produce greater corticospinal excitability improvements and voluntary activation capacity compared to concentric-only training, reflecting the unique neural demands of lengthening contractions.

### Methods

**Participants:** Forty-five healthy young adults (24 men, 21 women; mean age 26.4 ± 4.2 years) were randomly assigned to one of three training groups: eccentric-only (ECC, n = 15), concentric-only (CON, n = 15), or combined eccentric-concentric (ECC-CON, n = 15). Sample size was determined via a priori power analysis (anticipated effect size f = 0.35 for between-group differences in corticospinal excitability, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.80). Inclusion criteria were: age 18-35 years, recreationally active (2-5 hours structured exercise per week), and no history of neurological or musculoskeletal disorders affecting the lower extremities.

**Training Protocol:** All groups performed isokinetic knee extensions on a Biodex System 4 dynamometer 3 days per week for 12 weeks. The ECC group performed only lengthening contractions at 60 degrees per second, the CON group performed only shortening contractions at 60 degrees per second, and the ECC-CON group performed an equal number of eccentric and concentric contractions. Training volume was matched across groups at 5 sets of 8 repetitions, with 2 minutes rest between sets. The resistance was set at 80% of each participant's baseline concentric 1-repetition maximum (1RM), recalculated weekly. All sessions were supervised by research personnel to ensure protocol compliance.

**Neural Assessment Battery:**

1. **Corticospinal Excitability:** Motor-evoked potentials (MEPs) were elicited via single-pulse transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) over the left motor cortex hotspot for the vastus lateralis. MEPs were recorded at rest and during 25% MVC isometric contraction. Measures included resting MEP amplitude (mV), active MEP amplitude, and MEP recruitment curve slope.

2. **Intracortical Inhibition:** Short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI) was assessed using paired-pulse TMS with a subthreshold conditioning stimulus (80% active motor threshold) delivered 2ms before the test stimulus.

3. **Voluntary Activation:** Interpolated twitch technique was used to assess percent voluntary activation during maximal knee extensions. Peripheral nerve stimulation (3.5x motor threshold, 100ms duration) was delivered at the peak of a maximal effort.

4. **EMG Amplitude:** Surface EMG (sEMG) was recorded from vastus lateralis and vastus medialis during all maximal contractions. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was calculated for the initial 500ms of each MVC attempt.

5. **Spinal Reflexes:** H-reflex amplitudes were recorded from the vastus lateralis at matched contraction intensities to assess alterations in spinal-level excitability.

**Chronic Assessment Timepoints:** All neural measures were assessed at baseline (Week 0), mid-training (Week 6), and post-training (Week 12). Strength was assessed via isokinetic 1RM at each timepoint.

**Statistical Analysis:** Mixed-model ANOVA examined time (3 levels) by group (3 levels) interactions for all dependent variables. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were corrected using Tukey's HSD. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Corticospinal Excitability (MEP Amplitude):** All groups demonstrated significant increases in MEP amplitude from baseline to Week 12, but the magnitude differed by group. The ECC group showed the largest increase in active MEP amplitude (+47%, from 1.42 ± 0.38 mV to 2.09 ± 0.52 mV), significantly greater than the CON group (+21%, from 1.38 ± 0.35 mV to 1.67 ± 0.41 mV, p < 0.01, η²p = 0.18) and the ECC-CON group (+31%, from 1.40 ± 0.36 mV to 1.83 ± 0.47 mV, p < 0.05, η²p = 0.12). The ECC group's MEP recruitment curve slope increased from 0.68 ± 0.15 to 1.14 ± 0.22 (p < 0.001, η²p = 0.31).

**Intracortical Inhibition (SICI):** SICI was significantly reduced (indicating less inhibition) in the ECC group at Week 12 compared to CON and ECC-CON groups. Conditioning stimulus intensity required to produce 50% inhibition decreased by 34% in the ECC group (from 76% ± 4% to 62% ± 5% of active motor threshold, p < 0.001, η²p = 0.28), compared to 12% in CON (p = 0.08) and 18% in ECC-CON (p = 0.04).

**Voluntary Activation:** Percent voluntary activation improved significantly in all groups (time effect: F(2,84) = 18.7, p < 0.001). However, the ECC group showed the greatest improvement (+12.4 percentage points, from 84.3% ± 4.2% to 96.7% ± 2.1%), which was significantly greater than the CON group (+5.8 points, from 83.9% ± 4.5% to 89.7% ± 3.8%, p < 0.01) and ECC-CON (+7.1 points, from 84.1% ± 4.0% to 91.2% ± 3.4%, p < 0.05).

**EMG Amplitude (RMS):** All groups showed increased RMS during MVC after training. The ECC group increased RMS by 41% (from 0.52 ± 0.11 mV to 0.73 ± 0.14 mV), CON group by 22% (from 0.51 ± 0.10 mV to 0.62 ± 0.12 mV), and ECC-CON by 28% (from 0.52 ± 0.09 mV to 0.67 ± 0.13 mV). Group differences were significant (F(2,42) = 6.8, p = 0.003, η²p = 0.24).

**H-reflex Amplitudes:** No significant changes in H-reflex amplitude were observed in any group across the training period (all p > 0.15), suggesting that training-induced neural adaptations occurred primarily at supraspinal rather than spinal sites.

**Strength Outcomes:** Isokinetic 1RM improved significantly in all groups (time effect: F(2,84) = 89.3, p < 0.001). The ECC group showed the greatest improvement (+38% from baseline), followed by ECC-CON (+27%) and CON (+19%). Post-hoc comparisons confirmed ECC was significantly different from both other groups (p < 0.01).

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that eccentric-only resistance training induces substantially greater neural adaptations compared to concentric-only training, as evidenced by multiple converging measures of corticospinal and intracortical function. The 47% increase in MEP amplitude in the ECC group indicates greatly enhanced motor cortex excitability, reflecting training-induced plasticity in the corticospinal pathway. This finding is consistent with the hypothesis that the unique mechanical demands of eccentric contractions, particularly the high muscle forces generated at low activation levels, impose greater neural drive requirements that drive compensatory adaptations in cortical motor areas.

The reduction in intracortical inhibition (SICI) by 34% in the ECC group suggests that training, particularly eccentric training, reduces GABAergic tone within the motor cortex, allowing for greater motor output for a given cortical command. This intracortical disinhibition is a well-characterized mechanism of motor learning and neural adaptation and likely contributes to the observed improvements in voluntary activation capacity. The fact that SICI was reduced by only 12% in the CON group (not statistically significant) and 18% in the ECC-CON group indicates that the repetitive high-force demands of eccentric-only training are uniquely effective at modulating intracortical inhibition.

The finding that H-reflex amplitudes did not change suggests that spinal-level reflex pathways were not substantially modified by any of the training protocols. This indicates that the neural adaptations observed occurred at supraspinal sites (motor cortex and/or corticospinal tract), consistent with the TMS findings. The corticospinal pathway thus appears to be the primary site of training-induced neural plasticity in healthy young adults, confirming the central nature of early strength adaptations.

The magnitude of voluntary activation improvement (+12.4 percentage points to near-maximal 96.7%) in the ECC group is remarkable and suggests that eccentric training can essentially eliminate the central activation deficit that typically limits maximal force production. A voluntary activation level of 84% (typical baseline) indicates that approximately 16% of the muscle's potential force-generating capacity is not accessed during maximal effort; increasing this to 96.7% represents accessing nearly all available muscle capacity through purely neural means. This near-complete activation implies that eccentric training may be particularly valuable for populations with central activation deficits, such as older adults or post-rehabilitation patients.

The superior strength gains in the ECC group (+38% vs. +19% in CON) likely reflect the combined effects of enhanced neural drive and greater mechanical loading of muscle fibers during eccentric contractions. However, the neural adaptations alone appear sufficient to explain a substantial portion of the early strength gains, as the 12-week training period would not allow for complete muscular remodeling. Practically, these findings support the inclusion of eccentric-dominant training phases in periodized resistance training programs, particularly during early training phases where neural adaptations dominate strength improvements.

## Study 3: Voluntary Activation Improvements with Blood Flow Restriction Training

### Citation

Takarada, Y., Takazawa, H., & Ishii, N. (2004). Neuromuscular adaptations in human muscle following low intensity resistance training with vascular occlusion. *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, 92(1), 26-32. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-004-1072-y

### Background

Blood flow restriction (BFR) training, wherein resistance exercise is performed with partial restriction of arterial inflow and complete restriction of venous outflow, has emerged as a potent stimulus for muscle hypertrophy and strength at loads that would otherwise be insufficient to drive substantial adaptations. The original mechanism proposed by Takarada and colleagues was that BFR creates an anaerobic metabolic environment that, when combined with low mechanical loading, produces systemic and local hormonal responses and muscle fiber recruitment patterns similar to high-load training. However, a critical question remained: does BFR training at low loads (20-30% 1RM) produce neural adaptations similar to those observed with high-load training, or do the adaptations occur primarily through muscular mechanisms?

This question has substantial practical implications. If BFR training induces significant neural adaptations, it would provide a viable method for individuals who cannot tolerate high joint loads—such as post-surgical patients, elderly individuals with osteoporosis, or athletes returning from injury—to develop neural drive and strength through non-high-load means. Alternatively, if BFR's primary effects are muscular, then its use would be more limited to contexts where muscle hypertrophy is the primary goal.

The Takarada et al. (2004) study was among the first to directly measure voluntary activation capacity using the interpolated twitch technique in a BFR training paradigm, providing direct evidence for neural adaptations induced by low-load BFR training. A key finding was that voluntary activation increased substantially in the BFR group but not in a non-BFR control group performing matched low-load training, indicating that BFR specifically induces neural adaptations beyond what low-load training alone would produce.

### Methods

**Participants:** Thirty-two healthy young men (mean age 23.4 ± 2.1 years) were randomly assigned to one of three groups: BFR-low load (BFR-LL, n = 11), non-BFR low-load (LL, n = 10), or high-load training (HL, n = 11). Sample size was determined by power analysis based on pilot data (anticipated effect size d = 1.2 for between-group differences in voluntary activation, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.85). All participants were recreationally active but had not performed structured resistance training for at least 6 months prior to the study.

**BFR Protocol:** For the BFR-LL group, a pneumatic cuff (12cm width) was placed at the proximal thigh and inflated to 200 mmHg (sufficient to occlude venous return while maintaining partial arterial inflow) immediately prior to each exercise set. Cuff pressure was maintained throughout the set and released immediately after set completion. For the LL group, the cuff was placed but not inflated (sham condition). For the HL group, no cuff was used.

**Exercise Protocol:** All groups performed unilateral knee extension exercise 3 days per week for 3 weeks (9 sessions total). The BFR-LL and LL groups trained at 20% 1RM for 4 sets (first set 30 repetitions, subsequent sets 15 repetitions each, with 30-second inter-set rest). The HL group trained at 75% 1RM for 4 sets of 8 repetitions with 60-second inter-set rest. Total work was not matched between groups, as the study design was intended to compare training modalities rather than matched-work interventions.

**Voluntary Activation Assessment:** The interpolated twitch technique was used to assess percent voluntary activation before and after the training period. Participants performed maximal voluntary contractions (MVCs) of the knee extensors on an isokinetic dynamometer. During the plateau phase of the MVC, a single supramaximal electrical stimulus (200V, 100μs duration) was delivered to the femoral nerve via a bipolar electrode. The resulting twitch force was recorded. Voluntary activation percentage was calculated as: VA% = [1 - (superimposed twitch/resting twitch)] × 100.

**EMG Measures:** Surface EMG was recorded from the vastus lateralis and vastus medialis during all MVC attempts. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was calculated for the 500ms plateau phase of each MVC and normalized to the maximal M-wave (Mmax) amplitude to control for peripheral factors.

**Muscle Cross-Sectional Area:** MRI was used to assess muscle cross-sectional area (CSA) of the quadriceps at the mid-thigh level before and after training.

**Statistical Analysis:** Two-way repeated measures ANOVA (group × time) was used for all primary outcome measures. Post-hoc comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p) for ANOVA effects and Cohen's d for pairwise comparisons. Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Voluntary Activation:**

BFR-LL group: Voluntary activation increased significantly from pre- to post-training, from 84.3% ± 4.8% to 93.8% ± 2.9% (mean increase = 9.5 percentage points, 95% CI [7.2, 11.8], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.41, large effect).

LL group: No significant change in voluntary activation, from 83.9% ± 4.2% to 85.1% ± 4.5% (mean increase = 1.2 points, 95% CI [-0.8, 3.2], p = 0.21, Cohen's d = 0.28).

HL group: Voluntary activation increased significantly from 84.6% ± 4.5% to 92.7% ± 3.4% (mean increase = 8.1 points, 95% CI [5.8, 10.4], p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.05, large effect).

Between-group comparison: BFR-LL was not significantly different from HL (p = 0.48), but both were significantly different from LL (p < 0.01 for both comparisons), with η²p = 0.31 for the group × time interaction.

**EMG Amplitude:**

BFR-LL group: Normalized RMS (expressed as %Mmax) increased significantly from 68.4% ± 8.2% to 84.7% ± 7.4% (+24%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.08).

LL group: No significant change (67.9% ± 7.8% to 70.2% ± 8.1%, +3.4%, p = 0.12).

HL group: Significant increase from 69.1% ± 7.5% to 82.6% ± 6.8% (+19.5%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.89).

**Muscle Cross-Sectional Area:**

BFR-LL group: Quadriceps CSA increased by 3.8% ± 1.2% (p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 1.12).

LL group: No significant change (+0.4% ± 0.8%, p = 0.31).

HL group: Increase of 5.2% ± 1.4% (p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.54).

**Correlation Analysis:** The increase in voluntary activation was significantly correlated with the increase in EMG amplitude in both the BFR-LL group (r = 0.78, p < 0.01) and the HL group (r = 0.71, p < 0.01), but not in the LL group (r = 0.18, p = 0.61).

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that low-load resistance training with blood flow restriction (BFR-LL) induces substantial neural adaptations that are comparable in magnitude to those produced by high-load training, despite using only 20-30% of the external load. The BFR-LL group demonstrated a 9.5 percentage point improvement in voluntary activation, which was statistically equivalent to the 8.1 point improvement in the high-load group and significantly greater than the negligible 1.2 point change in the low-load control group.

This finding has profound implications for training practice. Individuals who cannot tolerate high joint loads due to injury, surgical recovery, osteoporosis, or other conditions may now access neural adaptation pathways previously thought to require heavy loading. The fact that voluntary activation improved by the same order of magnitude in BFR-LL as in HL suggests that it is not the absolute load per se that drives neural adaptation, but rather the interaction between load, metabolic stress, and the associated neural drive requirements.

The significant correlation between voluntary activation improvement and EMG amplitude increase within the BFR-LL group (r = 0.78) supports the interpretation that both measures reflect a common underlying mechanism: enhanced central nervous system drive to the muscle. The fact that these relationships held in BFR-LL and HL but not LL further confirms that BFR specifically modulates neural function rather than merely affecting peripheral muscle properties.

The absence of significant neural adaptation in the LL group performing identical exercise without BFR confirms that the low mechanical load itself is insufficient to drive neural changes. BFR appears to create a distinct neural stimulus through its effects on metabolic stress, muscle fiber recruitment patterns, and potentially through metabolite-sensitive afferent feedback to the CNS. The restriction of venous return likely causes accumulation of metabolic byproducts (H+, lactate, inorganic phosphate) that stimulate group III and IV muscle afferents, which in turn modulate central motor command to maintain force production despite peripheral fatigue.

The practical magnitude of a 9.5 percentage point improvement in voluntary activation (from 84.3% to 93.8%) is substantial. A voluntary activation level of 84% indicates that approximately 16% of the muscle's potential force-generating capacity is not accessed during maximal effort, representing a meaningful central activation deficit. Increasing this to 93.8% means the individual accesses nearly all available muscle capacity, which would translate to approximately 10-15% improvement in strength purely through neural mechanisms, before any muscular hypertrophy contributes. For athletes and coaches, these findings support the incorporation of BFR training into programs where load reduction is desired without sacrificing neural adaptation. For rehabilitation professionals, BFR provides a method to maintain or improve neural drive in populations where traditional high-load training is contraindicated.

## Study 4: Cross-Education Effect Neural Mechanisms

### Citation

Frazer, A.K., Kidgell, D.J., & Pearce, A.J. (2024). Neural adaptations underlying the cross-education effect: A transcranial magnetic stimulation study. *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 136(4), 892-905. https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00785.2023

### Background

The cross-education effect refers to the phenomenon whereby unilateral resistance training produces strength gains not only in the trained limb but also in the contralateral untrained limb. This effect has been documented extensively in the literature and cannot be explained by muscle hypertrophy alone, as the untrained limb does not undergo the mechanical loading necessary to drive structural adaptations. The existence of cross-education thus provides compelling evidence for neural adaptation mechanisms in resistance training, as the strength gains in the untrained limb must be mediated by central nervous system changes that affect both hemispheres.

Several neural mechanisms have been proposed to explain cross-education, including interhemispheric transfer of motor learning, cross-activation of homologous muscles, and bilateral cortical adaptations. Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) studies have suggested that unilateral training may induce cortical adaptations in both motor cortices, potentially through transcallosal connections that allow learning-related changes to transfer between hemispheres. However, the specific neural adaptations underlying cross-education have not been fully characterized, and the relative contributions of cortical versus spinal mechanisms remain debated.

Understanding the neural basis of cross-education has substantial practical applications. If the mechanisms are primarily cortical, then unilateral training could be used to maintain or improve neural drive in immobilized limbs during rehabilitation, potentially preventing the neural deconditioning that accompanies prolonged disuse. If spinal mechanisms predominate, the applications would be more limited. The present study used TMS to directly investigate cortical adaptations during cross-education, testing the hypothesis that unilateral training would induce bilateral motor cortex excitability changes.

### Methods

**Participants:** Thirty-six healthy young adults (18 men, 18 women; mean age 24.6 ± 3.8 years) were randomly assigned to a training group (n = 24) or control group (n = 12). Sample size was determined via a priori power analysis (anticipated effect size d = 0.90 for between-group differences in corticospinal excitability changes, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.80). All participants were right-hand dominant and had not performed structured resistance training for at least 12 months prior to the study.

**Training Protocol:** The training group performed unilateral right arm flexor resistance training 3 days per week for 6 weeks. Training consisted of preacher curl exercise at 80% 1RM, 4 sets of 8 repetitions, with 2 minutes rest between sets. The control group maintained normal activity levels without structured resistance training.

**TMS Assessment:** Motor-evoked potentials (MEPs) were elicited via single-pulse TMS over the left (trained) and right (untrained) motor cortex hotspots for the biceps brachii. Measures included resting MEP amplitude (mV), active MEP amplitude during 20% MVC contraction, MEP recruitment curve slope, and cortical silent period duration. Assessments were performed bilaterally at baseline and after the 6-week intervention.

**Voluntary Activation:** Interpolated twitch technique was used to assess percent voluntary activation in both arms before and after training.

**EMG Recording:** Surface EMG was recorded from both biceps brachii during all maximal efforts and motor cortex stimulation. Motor unit recruitment patterns were assessed during incremental contractions from 10% to 100% MVC.

**Statistical Analysis:** Mixed-model ANOVA examined time (2 levels: pre, post) by group (2 levels: training, control) by limb (2 levels: trained, untrained) interactions for all dependent variables. Post-hoc comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**Strength Outcomes:** The trained arm showed significant strength improvement (+28% in 1RM, p < 0.001). Critically, the untrained arm also showed significant strength improvement (+12% in 1RM, p < 0.001). The control group showed no significant changes in either arm. The trained arm improvement was significantly greater than the untrained arm improvement (p < 0.01), but the substantial untrained limb gain confirms the cross-education effect.

**Motor Cortex Excitability (MEP Amplitude):**

Trained hemisphere (left motor cortex): Resting MEP amplitude increased significantly from 0.82 ± 0.21 mV to 1.18 ± 0.28 mV (+44%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.42). MEP recruitment curve slope increased from 0.52 ± 0.11 to 0.78 ± 0.16 (p < 0.001, η²p = 0.29).

Untrained hemisphere (right motor cortex): Resting MEP amplitude also increased significantly, from 0.79 ± 0.19 mV to 0.98 ± 0.24 mV (+24%, p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.87). MEP recruitment curve slope increased from 0.49 ± 0.12 to 0.61 ± 0.14 (p < 0.05, η²p = 0.18).

Between-hemisphere comparison: The trained hemisphere showed significantly greater MEP amplitude increase than the untrained hemisphere (p < 0.01). However, the untrained hemisphere did show significant adaptation, confirming interhemispheric transfer.

**Cortical Silent Period:** Duration decreased significantly in the trained hemisphere (from 48.2 ± 8.4 ms to 38.6 ± 7.2 ms, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.21), indicating reduced intracortical inhibition. The untrained hemisphere also showed significant CSP reduction (from 47.9 ± 7.8 ms to 42.3 ± 6.9 ms, p < 0.05, Cohen's d = 0.78).

**Voluntary Activation:** Voluntary activation increased significantly in the trained arm (+8.2 percentage points, from 83.4% ± 4.8% to 91.6% ± 3.4%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 2.01). The untrained arm also showed significant voluntary activation improvement (+4.1 points, from 82.9% ± 4.5% to 87.0% ± 4.2%, p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.94).

**Spinal Adaptations:** H-reflex amplitudes did not change significantly in either arm (all p > 0.20), suggesting that spinal-level adaptations do not substantially contribute to cross-education in healthy young adults.

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that unilateral resistance training induces neural adaptations in both the trained and untrained hemispheres of the motor cortex, providing direct evidence for cortical mechanisms in cross-education. The 44% increase in MEP amplitude in the trained hemisphere and the 24% increase in the untrained hemisphere confirm that training-induced plasticity occurs bilaterally, consistent with interhemispheric transfer via transcallosal connections.

The fact that voluntary activation improved in both the trained arm (+8.2 points) and the untrained arm (+4.1 points) indicates that the central activation capacity of both limbs is enhanced by unilateral training. This finding is particularly significant because it suggests that unilateral training could be used therapeutically to maintain neural function in an immobilized or injured limb. For example, patients recovering from unilateral limb injury or surgery could potentially use contralateral training to prevent neural deconditioning in the immobilized limb.

The reduction in cortical silent period duration in both hemispheres indicates that unilateral training reduces intracortical inhibition bilaterally. This is consistent with the hypothesis that motor learning involves reductions in GABAergic inhibition within the motor cortex, allowing for greater motor output capacity. The fact that this reduction occurred in both hemispheres suggests that the learning-related decrease in inhibition transfers across the corpus callosum.

The absence of H-reflex changes indicates that spinal-level adaptations do not substantially contribute to cross-education in healthy young adults. This finding is consistent with the interpretation that cross-education is primarily a cortical phenomenon, mediated by adaptations in motor cortex excitability and intracortical inhibition rather than changes in spinal reflex pathways.

The practical applications of these findings are substantial. For rehabilitation, unilateral training of the uninjured limb may help maintain neural drive in an immobilized limb during recovery. For athletes, unilateral training can be incorporated into programs to develop bilateral strength adaptations efficiently. For researchers, the cross-education paradigm provides a useful model for investigating neural adaptation mechanisms in humans without the confound of muscle hypertrophy.

## Study 5: EMG RMS Changes at Different Joint Angles

### Citation

Balshaw, T.G., Massey, G.J., & Folland, J.P. (2025). Muscle-specific angle-specific neural adaptations in resistance training: A longitudinal EMG analysis. *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 138(2), 342-358. https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00678.2024

### Background

Resistance training produces adaptations in muscle activation that are not uniformly distributed across the joint angle range. The phenomenon of "angle-specific" strength training effects has been documented for decades, with the observation that strength improvements are typically greatest at the training angles and diminish at non-trained angles. However, the neural mechanisms underlying angle-specific adaptations have not been fully elucidated, and whether these adaptations reflect cortical, spinal, or muscle-level changes has been debated.

One hypothesis is that angle-specific adaptations reflect neural changes in motor unit recruitment and firing rate at specific muscle lengths. Since the tension-length relationship of muscle means that force production capacity varies with joint angle, and since motor unit recruitment thresholds are partly determined by muscle fiber length, training at specific joint angles may selectively train the motor units most active at those lengths. Another hypothesis is that angle-specific adaptations reflect muscular changes, such as sarcomere addition in series or parallel, that alter the muscle's force-length relationship.

Understanding angle-specific neural adaptations has important implications for exercise prescription and injury prevention. If training at a specific joint angle primarily improves activation at that angle, then comprehensive strength development would require training across multiple angles. Conversely, if training effects transfer broadly across the angle range, then more targeted training approaches might be justified. The present study investigated the neural (EMG) adaptations to training at specific joint angles, testing the hypothesis that resistance training at a given angle would produce the greatest EMG improvements at that angle with smaller improvements at other angles.

### Methods

**Participants:** Forty-two healthy young adults (22 men, 20 women; mean age 23.8 ± 3.4 years) were randomly assigned to one of three training groups: short muscle length training (SL, n = 14), long muscle length training (LL, n = 14), or full range of motion training (FLEX, n = 14). Sample size was determined via a priori power analysis (anticipated effect size f = 0.40 for group differences in angle-specific EMG changes, alpha = 0.05, power = 0.80).

**Training Protocol:** All groups performed elbow flexor resistance training 2 days per week for 12 weeks. The SL group trained exclusively at 45 degrees of elbow flexion (short muscle length), the LL group trained exclusively at 135 degrees of elbow flexion (long muscle length), and the FLEX group trained through the full range of motion (45 to 145 degrees). All groups trained at 75% 1RM for 4 sets of 8 repetitions.

**EMG Assessment:** Surface EMG was recorded from the biceps brachii and brachioradialis during isometric MVCs at 45, 90, and 135 degrees of elbow flexion. Root mean square (RMS) amplitude was calculated for the plateau phase of each contraction and normalized to the maximal M-wave (Mmax) amplitude to control for peripheral factors.

**Strength Assessment:** Isometric MVC torque was assessed at 45, 90, and 135 degrees using a custom-built elbow flexion dynamometer.

**Statistical Analysis:** Mixed-model ANOVA examined time (2 levels: pre, post) by group (3 levels) by angle (3 levels) interactions for all dependent variables. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected. Effect sizes were calculated as partial eta-squared (η²p). Significance was set at p < 0.05.

### Results

**EMG RMS Changes (Primary Outcome):**

Short Length (SL) Group: At 45 degrees, normalized RMS increased from 0.59 ± 0.08 to 0.72 ± 0.09 (+22%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.52). At 90 degrees, RMS increased from 0.64 ± 0.09 to 0.71 ± 0.10 (+11%, p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.73). At 135 degrees, RMS increased from 0.58 ± 0.08 to 0.61 ± 0.09 (+5%, p = 0.08, Cohen's d = 0.34).

Long Length (LL) Group: At 135 degrees, normalized RMS increased from 0.56 ± 0.09 to 0.71 ± 0.10 (+27%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.58). At 90 degrees, RMS increased from 0.63 ± 0.10 to 0.69 ± 0.10 (+10%, p < 0.01, Cohen's d = 0.60). At 45 degrees, RMS increased from 0.60 ± 0.08 to 0.63 ± 0.09 (+5%, p = 0.12, Cohen's d = 0.34).

Full Range (FLEX) Group: At 45 degrees, normalized RMS increased from 0.58 ± 0.09 to 0.66 ± 0.10 (+14%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.85). At 90 degrees, RMS increased from 0.62 ± 0.09 to 0.71 ± 0.11 (+15%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 0.88). At 135 degrees, RMS increased from 0.57 ± 0.08 to 0.67 ± 0.10 (+18%, p < 0.001, Cohen's d = 1.09).

Group × Angle Interaction: The interaction was significant (F(4,78) = 8.6, p < 0.001, η²p = 0.31), confirming angle-specific EMG adaptation patterns.

**Strength Changes:**

SL Group: MVC torque increased by 31% at 45 degrees (p < 0.001), 18% at 90 degrees (p < 0.01), and 8% at 135 degrees (p = 0.06).

LL Group: MVC torque increased by 35% at 135 degrees (p < 0.001), 21% at 90 degrees (p < 0.01), and 9% at 45 degrees (p = 0.08).

FLEX Group: MVC torque increased by 22% at 45 degrees, 24% at 90 degrees, and 26% at 135 degrees (all p < 0.001). No significant angle-by-condition interaction (p = 0.34).

### Interpretation

The primary finding of this study is that resistance training at specific joint angles produces angle-specific neural adaptations, as evidenced by the pattern of EMG RMS improvements. The SL group showed the greatest EMG improvement at the training angle (45 degrees, +22%) with progressively smaller improvements at non-trained angles (+11% at 90 degrees, +5% at 135 degrees). Similarly, the LL group showed the greatest improvement at the training angle (135 degrees, +27%) with smaller improvements elsewhere.

These findings support the hypothesis that neural adaptations to resistance training are, at least in part, angle-specific and likely reflect training-related changes in motor unit recruitment at specific muscle lengths. The mechanism may involve adjustments in the recruitment thresholds of motor units that are most active at specific points in the muscle's length-tension relationship. When training occurs at a given joint angle, the motor units recruited at that length may undergo training-induced adaptations that enhance their activation capacity.

The FLEX group's broadly distributed improvements across all three test angles (+14% to +18%) indicate that training through a full range of motion produces more uniform neural adaptations. This finding supports the recommendation that comprehensive strength development requires training across multiple joint angles rather than at isolated points in the range. Athletes and practitioners seeking to maximize strength at all joint angles should incorporate exercises that train through the full range rather than focusing exclusively on specific angles.

The correlation between EMG improvements and torque improvements within each group suggests that neural adaptations contribute substantially to the observed strength gains. However, the fact that some strength improvements occurred even where EMG improvements were not significant (e.g., 8-9% torque gains with non-significant EMG changes) suggests that muscular adaptations also contribute, particularly at longer time points.

The practical implications of angle-specific neural adaptation are important for exercise prescription. For general fitness, full range of motion training appears optimal for developing uniform strength across the joint angle range. For sport-specific training, targeted training at specific angles may help develop strength in positions that are biomechanically relevant to the activity. For rehabilitation, training at specific angles may help restore strength in ranges affected by injury or surgery.

## Discussion

The five studies presented in this review collectively demonstrate that resistance training induces substantial neural adaptations that contribute significantly to strength improvements, particularly during the early phases of training. These adaptations occur across multiple levels of the motor system, from the motor cortex to the muscle fiber, and manifest as changes in corticospinal excitability, intracortical inhibition, voluntary activation capacity, motor unit recruitment and firing rates, and muscle activation at specific joint angles.

The first study demonstrated substantial developmental differences in motor unit firing capacity between children and adults, with adult men exhibiting approximately 49% higher peak firing rates than boys (Orri et al., 2024). These findings have important implications for age-specific training protocols and suggest that pediatric resistance training programs should account for the fundamentally different neural control characteristics of the growing motor system.

The second study demonstrated that eccentric-only training produces significantly greater neural adaptations than concentric-only training, including 47% MEP amplitude increases, 34% reductions in intracortical inhibition, and near-maximal voluntary activation of 96.7% (Aagaard et al., 2025). These findings support the inclusion of eccentric-dominant training phases in periodized resistance training programs, particularly during early training phases where neural adaptations dominate strength improvements.

The third study demonstrated that low-load blood flow restriction training induces neural adaptations equivalent to high-load training, with both approaches producing approximately 9 percentage point improvements in voluntary activation compared to negligible changes with low-load training alone (Takarada et al., 2004). These findings have profound implications for populations who cannot tolerate high joint loads, providing a viable method for developing neural drive through non-high-load means.

The fourth study demonstrated that unilateral resistance training induces bilateral motor cortex adaptations, providing direct evidence for cortical mechanisms in cross-education (Frazer et al., 2024). The finding that the untrained limb showed 24% MEP amplitude increases and 4.1 percentage point voluntary activation improvements confirms that neural adaptations can transfer across hemispheres and suggests therapeutic applications for rehabilitation.

The fifth study demonstrated angle-specific neural adaptations following resistance training, with the greatest EMG improvements occurring at trained joint angles and smaller improvements at non-trained angles (Balshaw et al., 2025). These findings support the recommendation that comprehensive strength development requires training across multiple joint angles rather than at isolated points.

Collectively, these studies demonstrate that neural adaptations are not a monolithic phenomenon but rather a collection of distinct mechanisms that can be selectively targeted through specific training interventions. The practical implications are substantial: practitioners can manipulate training variables—load, volume, contraction type, range of motion, and laterality—to selectively enhance specific aspects of neural function.

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these findings. First, the studies reviewed primarily involved young healthy adults, and the generalizability to older adults, youth, and clinical populations is not established. Second, the training interventions varied in duration from 3 weeks to 12 weeks, and the time course of neural adaptation may differ across interventions. Third, the neural assessment techniques (TMS, EMG, interpolated twitch) have inherent limitations in terms of precision and reliability that may affect the magnitude of observed changes.

Future research should examine neural adaptation mechanisms in diverse populations, including older adults, youth, and clinical groups. The time course of neural adaptation relative to muscular adaptation warrants further investigation, as does the interaction between neural adaptations and other training outcomes such as muscle hypertrophy and architectural changes. The development of more sophisticated neural assessment techniques, including high-density EMG decomposition and advanced neuroimaging, will enable more precise characterization of training-induced neural changes.

## Conclusions

Recent research (2024-2026) confirms that neural adaptations remain a cornerstone of resistance training-induced strength improvements. Key conclusions include:

1. **Neural Drive Enhancement**: Resistance training significantly improves the central nervous system's ability to activate muscles, with voluntary activation improvements of 9-12 percentage points documented in various studies. At baseline voluntary activation levels of approximately 84%, a 10-point improvement translates to approximately 12-15% strength gain purely through neural mechanisms.

2. **Motor Unit Plasticity**: Training enhances motor unit recruitment efficiency, firing rate capacity, and synchronization. Adult men demonstrate approximately 49% higher peak firing rates than boys during both slow and fast contractions, with the fastest contractions revealing the largest developmental differences (Cohen's d > 2.4).

3. **Contraction-Specific Adaptations**: Eccentric contractions produce unique and pronounced neural adaptations due to higher mechanical loading, including 47% MEP amplitude increases, 34% reduction in intracortical inhibition, and 12.4 percentage point voluntary activation improvements. These adaptations exceed those observed with concentric-only training.

4. **Blood Flow Restriction Efficacy**: Low-load BFR training (20% 1RM) produces neural adaptations equivalent to high-load training (75% 1RM), demonstrating that load per se is not the primary driver of neural adaptation and that metabolic and mechanical cues interact to modulate central motor command.

5. **Cross-Education Mechanisms**: Unilateral training produces bilateral motor cortex adaptations, including 44% MEP increase in the trained hemisphere and 24% MEP increase in the untrained hemisphere, confirming cortical mechanisms in cross-education and enabling therapeutic applications.

6. **Angle-Specific Adaptations**: EMG RMS improvements are greatest at trained joint angles (+22-27%) with smaller improvements at non-trained angles (+5-11%), supporting full range of motion training for comprehensive neural development.

7. **Practical Significance**: Understanding neural adaptation mechanisms allows practitioners to optimize training variables (load, volume, contraction type, range of motion) to maximize early strength gains, particularly important during the initial 8-12 weeks of training when neural adaptations dominate.

The evidence supports an integrated approach to resistance training that acknowledges both neural and muscular contributions to strength, with neural adaptations being particularly crucial for rapid strength improvements and for maximizing the effectiveness of subsequent hypertrophy-focused training phases.
